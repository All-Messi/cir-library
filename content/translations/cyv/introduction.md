<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='text-align:center'><span

<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:center'><span
style='font-size:14.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
.5in'><i><span style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>The
Yahveh Covenant</span></i><span style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;
font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'> presents a modified form of the Old
Testament portion of <i>The Conversational Version</i>. Besides the features of
that translation, the following points come up in this variant of it.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
.5in'><i><span style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>The
Yahveh Covenant</span></i><span style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;
font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'> uses the personal name of the God of
Moses in places where it appears in the Hebrew text. The intent is to highlight
the personal character of Old Testament revelation by using God’s personal name
instead of substituting a descriptor—Lord, Master, God, Almighty, and the like.
There is a difference in feel between listening to a brother talk and listening
to John. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
.5in'><span style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>Where
standard versions may use all caps for LORD (or GOD) when covering the personal
name for the Hebrew God (</span><span lang=HE dir=RTL style='font-size:14.0pt;
line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>יהוה</span><span dir=LTR></span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'><span
dir=LTR></span>, Yahveh), this rendering puts the (presumed modern spelling of
the) actual name in the text. Sometime before Jewish scholars created the
Septuagint Greek translation of the Old Testament (LXX) in Alexandria, Egypt
(285 B. C.? or 150 B. C.?), Jewish people had adopted the custom of not saying
God’s personal name<span dir=RTL></span><span dir=RTL><span dir=RTL></span> </span>at
all. The respected name became the ineffable name replaced by </span><span
lang=HE dir=RTL style='font-size:14.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>אדוני</span><span
dir=LTR></span><span style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'><span
dir=LTR></span>, <i>“my Lord</i>,<i>”</i> and covered in the LXX by the Greek
word </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:
"Times New Roman",serif'>κύριος</span><span style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:
115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>, <i>Lord</i>. New Testament writers
did not challenge that tradition; so only in the Old Testament does the matter
come up—and, of course, in New Testament quotations of Old Testament texts that
contain the name. In the latter instances, New Testament writers quote the LXX
anyway; so </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;
font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>κύριος</span><span lang=EL
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'> </span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>appears
even in New Testament quotations of the Old Testament with <i>Yahveh</i> originally
in the Hebrew text. Restoring the personal name of God in <i>CV </i>is meant to
have a personalizing effect on the God of the Old Testament.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
.5in'><span style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>After

feature of <i>The Yahveh Covenant</i> is a judgment call about <i>“son-of”</i>
expressions. It takes <i>“x son-of y”</i> more as a designation than a description—like
Barjonah (John <b>1</b>:42, <i>“son-of John”</i>), Ben Gurion airport in Israel,
and Ben-Hur, the movie (cp. Exodus <b>31</b>:2). Ben- covers the <i>“son-of”</i>
part of the designations. Examples, of course, are quite numerous: Genesis <b>23</b>:8
(Ephron Ben-Zohar); <b>24</b>:24;<b> 28</b>:5; <b>29</b>:5; <b>34</b>:2; <b>36</b>:32</span><sup><span
style='font-size:10.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>2</span></sup><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>,
35, 38; <b>50</b>:23; <i>etc</i>. The Ben-x pattern stands even in longer genealogical
strings: “</span><span style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:
"Times New Roman",serif'>Korah Ben-Izhar Ben-Kohath Ben-Levi” (Numbers <b>16</b>:1);
cp. </span><span style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>Numbers
<b>25</b>:7; <b>27</b>:1; <b>36</b>:1; Joshua<b> 7</b>:1, 18; <b>17</b>:3;
Judges <b>10</b>:1; <b>18</b>:30; <b>20</b>:28, <i>etc</i>. <i>Ben</i> is
capitalized with hyphenation to the next name in capital form. If <i>Ben-x</i>
is considered one name by English custom, it is capitalized without a hyphen to
the next root, no blank involved and the next element not capitalized: Benjamin,
Benhadad, and the like.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
.5in'><span style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>Less
common than the <i>“son-of”</i> custom of identification is the <i>“daughter-of”</i>
(Bat-) designation found some 48 times in the Hebrew Bible. It is a particularly
noticeable feature of king descriptions at the time of their inaugurations. It
may occur in those cases because the kings were typically polygamists. The
instances of <i>“x Bat-y”</i> (x daughter of y) are Genesis <b>11</b>:29; <b>26</b>:34<sup>2</sup>;
<b>28</b>:9; <b>36</b>:2<sup>2</sup>, 3, 18, 39<sup>2</sup>; <b>41</b>:45; <b>46</b>:20;
Exodus <b>6</b>:23; Leviticus <b>24</b>:11; Numbers <b>25</b>:15; <b>26</b>:59;
Ruth <b>4</b>:17; 1 Samuel <b>14</b>:50; 2 Samuel <b>3</b>:3, 7; <b>11</b>:3; <b>17</b>:25;
<b>21</b>:8<sup>2</sup>; 1 Kings <b>15</b>:2, 10; <b>16</b>:32; 2 Kings <b>11</b>:2;
<b>15</b>:33; <b>18</b>:2; <b>21</b>:19; <b>22</b>:1; <b>23</b>:31, 36; <b>24</b>:8,
18; 1 Chronicles <b>1</b>:34, 36, 50<sup>2</sup>; <b>2</b>:3, 5; <b>3</b>:5; <b>7</b>:14,
31;<b> 8</b>:7; <b>11</b>:18<sup>2</sup>, 20; <b>13</b>:2; <b>20</b>:31; <b>27</b>:1;
Jeremiah <b>52</b>:1; Hosea<b> 1</b>:3.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
.5in'><span style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>A
related feature considers the <i>“father-of”</i> designation as a kind of
genealogy tracer “x Avi-y”: Genesis <b>4</b>:18; <b>10</b>:24; <b>11</b>:27;
Ruth <b>4</b>:17-22; 1 Chronicles <b>1</b>:1-4, 18, 24-26;<b> 2</b>:10-13,
20-21. 23-24, 30, 36-42, 49-52; <b>3</b>:10-15; <b>4</b>:2, 4, 11-12, 17-18,
21, 25;<b> 6</b>:4-14, 20-27, 29, 50-53; <b>7</b>:16, 25; <b>8</b>:29, 33-37;<b>
9</b>:33, 39-43; Nehemiah <b>12</b>:10-11. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
.5in'><span style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>There
is one case of <i>“mother of”</i> designation (x Em-y): 1 Chronicles <b>2</b>:29.</span></p>

language in superscript after some replacement wording more natural to modern
English. The phenomenon occurs especially in conversations during the kingdom
period.</span></p>
