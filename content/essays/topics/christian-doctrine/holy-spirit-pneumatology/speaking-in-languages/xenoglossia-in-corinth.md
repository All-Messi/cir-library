<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:center'><b><span
style='font-size:14.0pt'>XENOGLOSSIA IN CORINTH</span></b></p>

<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:center'><b><span
style='font-size:14.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></b></p>

<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:center'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>Virgil Warren, PhD</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>I. METHOD OF RESEARCH</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  One question about New Testament speaking
in languages is whether there was more than one type. Among evangelicals it is
agreed that speaking in languages was a first-century phenomenon involving
actual human languages. Not everyone agrees that there was a “second type.” </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  A.  Terminological Approach</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        There is a lack of finality in
the vocabulary approach regarding </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>γλῶσσα</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>(tongue,
language) as well as </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>λαλέω</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>(speak)
and </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>διερμηνεύω</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>(interpret,
translate). What a word means elsewhere does not necessarily determine its
usage here, especially if it is a unique matter. The statistically most likely
meaning may be set aside by the peculiarities of a special setting. Inasmuch as
the meaning of an expression is ultimately determined by its context anyway, it
is better to examine the characteristics of the situation than to rely on word usage
elsewhere. Any uniformity that can be found, however, for </span><span lang=EL
style='font-size:12.0pt'>γλῶσσα</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>as a reference to actual human languages does tend to
shift the burden of proof onto someone who claims a non-language character for speaking
in languages. The positive evidence for glossolalia based on word usage becomes
a difficulty for those who call for a special usage not evidenced elsewhere.
One offsetting consideration might be that if there were no other examples of
what was happening among the Christians, referring to it by the closest word
available could explain a special meaning.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        Words get their meanings from
their settings. A word’s meaning, then, is learned from its contexts. Interpreters
must not confuse the <u>reality</u> itself, the <u>perception</u> of it, the
idea about the reality (<u>conception</u>), and the word that represents the
idea about the reality (</span><u><span style='font-size:12.0pt;color:black'>language</span></u><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  B.  Examples of Approach</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        1.   Starting with Cornelius
and moving to Pentecost is illustrated by William L. Hendricks, “Glossolalia in
the New Testament,” in <i>Speaking in Tongues: Let’s Talk About It</i> (ed. by
Watson Mills, Word Books, 1973), pp. 51-52. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        2.   Starting with 1
Corinthians <b>12-14</b> as defining a double possibility and then moving to
Acts is another approach.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        3.   Starting with Acts <b>2</b>
and supposing that other cases are the same seems to be the most natural method.
Only those marks in Acts <b>10-11</b>, Acts <b>19</b>, Mark <b>16</b>:17; 1
Corinthians <b>12-14</b> that would not harmonize with the picture in Acts <b>2</b>
could justify supposing that tongues had more than one type. This procedure
seems best because Acts <b>2</b> is the <u>first example</u> of language
speaking in the New Testament. It is also a <u>clear account</u> of what they
were, because in the record of it a good amount of description accompanies the
phenomenon. The importance of starting with Acts <b>2</b>, then, is that
beginning elsewhere allows for unfalsifiable claims based on ambiguous language
(like that of 1 Corinthians <b>14</b>) and thereby creates a negative burden of
proof, which is always an invalid thinking pattern.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  C.  Experiential Approach</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        A common approach begins with
a personal or observed verbal experience. The elements of the procedure are as
follows:</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        1.   The observation is made
that there is Christian fruit in the life of the person </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              involved.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        2.   The event occurred in
connection with acts done to honor Jesus.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        3.   It cannot be “my own act”
because people cannot do such a thing on their own. It cannot be by Satan’s
power because it honors Christ; so it must be the Spirit’s work.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        4.   The observer consults
scripture to see if the experience fits with what is taught there. The approach,
however, does not well demonstrate <i>sola scriptura</i> because experience
provides the precise information about what occurred. Scripture may not be
precise enough to <u>eliminate</u> a certain possibility; so the fact that it
can be made to fit with scripture does not <u>establish</u> it as true. In the
case of glossolalic languages, it does not mean that an experience can be
identified, say, with languages in a “second sense,” because that second sense
(second kind of languages) cannot be positively established in the first place.
Since everything mentioned in 1 Corinthians <b>14</b> (and elsewhere) fits with
actual human languages, the precise nature of the “second type” comes from
experience rather than scripture.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        “Baptism in the Spirit as
evidenced by the speaking in tongues” is considered a self-authenticating
experience. In other words, it is so clearly real that the people who
experience it accept it as real even though they cannot explain it or answer
objections people may raise about it. The problem is that “self-authenticating”
may mean nothing more than vivid subjectivity devoid of objective evaluation. Furthermore,
it confuses the vivid reality of the experience and the explanation of the
cause and meaning of the experience. The fact of the experience is a different
thing from what identity of it and means behind it. No one denies that those
who have had an experience have had it, sometimes a life-changing one. The
objection is to identifying it with New Testament speaking in languages and concluding
that God dispensed his Spirit to bring that experience about. The person
experiencing it is in no better position than anyone else to explain its source,
significance, and identity with the New Testament phenomenon.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>II. TONGUES IN ACTS <b>2</b></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  A.  Observations that they were
actual languages (xenoglossia)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
-117.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                                         1.   <i>“They
began to <u>speak</u> in <u>other</u> tongues</i> [</span><span lang=EL
style='font-size:12.0pt'>ἑτέραις</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>γλώσσαις</span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>]
<i>as the Spirit gave <u>them</u> utterance” </i>(<b>2</b>:4b).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        2.   <i>“They were <u>hearing
them speak to each one in their own language</u> [</i></span><i><span lang=EL
style='font-size:12.0pt'>διαλέκτῷ</span></i><i><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>]
in which they were born”</span></i><span style='font-size:12.0pt'> (<b>2</b>:6
+ 8; note <b>2</b>:11, </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>γλώσσαις</span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>). The amazement in the audience was produced by seeing
these Palestinian commoners preaching in distant Gentile “dialects/vernaculars.”
</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        3.   <u>No interpreter was
needed</u> for the Parthians, Medes, and the rest, to know that these Jews were
talking about the <i>“mighty works of God”</i> (<b>2</b>:11).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  B.  Observations posed against their
being actual languages</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        1.   <u>The idea that the
miracle was in the hearing rather than in the speaking</u></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              a.   <i>“We <u>hear</u>
them speaking.”</i> </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                    (1)    The text is
explicit, however, that the apostles <u>spoke</u> in other languages. If they <u>spoke</u>
in other languages, the audience would <u>hear</u> them speaking in other
languages. If the miracle were in the hearing, the apostles would not be
speaking in other languages as the text says.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                    (2)    The visible
manifestation was tongues over the <u>speakers</u>. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                    (3)    The Holy
Spirit came on the speakers, not the hearers.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                    (4)    Acts <b>2</b>:4
sets the stage so that <b>2</b>:6, 11 is understood in light of the preceding,
not <i>vice versa</i>.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                    (5)    In the
other cases of language speaking, the miracle clearly occurs with the speakers,
especially in Corinth because “no one understood.”</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                    (6)    One wonders
where the charge of <u>drunkenness</u> would come from if they were all hearing
their own language only. Why would everyone not have been endowed with the
power of interpretation instead? (The charge could have been made because of
the <u>message</u> rather than the <u>sound</u> of foreign languages.) Evidently
some of them could hear languages they did not recognize—perhaps from another
apostle nearby. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                             Speaking <u>in</u>
a language does not require a preposition. In Acts <b>26</b>:14 <i>“speaking to
me in the Hebrew language”</i> is worded </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:
12.0pt'>τῇ</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL
style='font-size:12.0pt'>Ἑβραΐστῇ</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>
</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>διαλέκτῷ</span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>. In 1 Corinthians <b>14</b>:21 <i>“in/with other
languages and with other lips I will speak to this people”</i> is worded </span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ἐν</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>
</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ἑτερογλώσσοις</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>καὶ</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ἐν</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>χείλεσιν</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ἑτέροις</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>[</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ἑτέρων</span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>] </span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>λαλήσω</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:
12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>τῷ</span><span lang=EL
style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>λαῷ</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>τούτῳ</span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              b.   The number of
nations was too large to be addressed by twelve languages.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                    (1)    The number
of language groups could probably have been covered by twelve men speaking
simultaneously in various areas in the temple court, a twenty-seven-acre area
on Mt. Moriah. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                    (2)    The
deliveries could have been made in series so that more than twelve languages
would have been used. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                    (3)    Quite a few
foreign Jews would have known Aramaic, but the comment had to do with languages
wherein they were born. Greek would have served for the vast majority and would
perhaps qualify as a language <i>“wherein they were born.”</i> Since many
Israelites would have known Greek, however, we wonder whether speaking Greek
would have elicited the comment or would have had much sign value.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        c.   <u>Each</u> heard <u>them</u>
speaking in their own language while we know they heard in their own language.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                    Each hearing them
speaking may be regarded as a distributive singular (cp. “Put spoons, knives,
and forks by the plates when you set the table.” See also Ephesians <b>4</b>:2a,
18, 23; <b>5</b>:19; <b>6</b>:14, 22; and Philemon 25?).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        2.   <u>The charge that the
speakers were drunk</u> (<b>2</b>:13) </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              a.   Hearing an unknown
language could elicit that remark as surely as hearing an ecstatic utterance
could, particularly if the mockers were near an apostle that spoke a language they
did not understand.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              b.   Perhaps some
Palestinian Jews, being somewhat unsympathetic toward Hellenists, resented the
presence of foreign elements in the temple precincts.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              c.   If the mockers
understood the message, perhaps they were mocking <u>it</u>—the resurrection,
Jesus as the Messiah, a suffering Messiah. At any rate, a person does not have
to be drunk to be accused of it. Paul was accused of being crazy (Acts <b>26</b>:24);
Jesus was accused of having a demon (John<b> 8</b>:48).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        3.   <u>Other (kinds of)
languages</u> (</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ἑτέραις</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><b><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>2</span></b><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>:4): </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ἑτέραις</span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'> as distinguished from </span><span lang=EL
style='font-size:12.0pt'>ἄλλαις</span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>
supposedly means another as to kind rather than another as to number. John <b>14</b>:16
and 1 Corinthians <b>14</b>:17 are presented as examples. In 1 Corinthians <b>12</b>:10,
28, the text says “kinds of tongues” (</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:
12.0pt'>γένη</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>γλωσσῶν</span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              a.   The words do not,
however, have to differ in this manner—a distinction more characteristic of
classical Greek than the Hellenistic Greek of the New Testament. If
distinguished, </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ἕτερος</span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'> regards the “other” in distinction, disjunction,
contrast to the form—a matter of identity more than kind. </span><span lang=EL
style='font-size:12.0pt'>Ἄλλος</span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'> regards it
as separate. With </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ἄλλος</span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>, so to speak, they face one another, but with </span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ἕτερος</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:
12.0pt'> </span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>do not. The pattern seems
evident from the following passages: 1 Corinthians 1<b>2</b>:9-10 uses them
interchangeably in regard to the same list; Luke <b>5</b>:7; <b>7</b>:41; <b>8</b>:6,
7, 8; <b>11</b>:16, 26 (= Matthew<b> 12</b>:45); John <b>19</b>:37; Acts <b>13</b>:35;<b>
20</b>:15 (=<i> </i><b>27</b>:3); Romans<b> 7</b>:3; Galatians<b> 1</b>:19;
Hebrews <b>5</b>:6; 7:11; <b>11</b>:36.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              b.   A difference even
in kind might address the <u>cause</u> of the languages rather than the <u>kind</u>
of languages. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              c.   The difference in
kind might arise from a Semitic <i>vs</i>. non-Semitic distinction. 1
Corinthians<b> 14</b>:21 (</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ἐν</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ἑτερογλώσσοις</span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>) is instructive and could fit either (a) or (c)
because actual human languages are in view in this reference to the Old
Testament Book of Isaiah (<b>28</b>:11-12). The Septuagint for Isaiah <b>28</b>:11
is </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>διὰ</span><span lang=EL
style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>γλώσσης</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ἑτέρας</span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>. Other <u>kinds of</u> tongues do not have to be
unearthly as well as earthly, angelic as well as human, natural as well as
supernatural. The quotation from Isaiah is about human languages—the languages
of foreigners.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        4.   <u>Same as the ecstatic
utterances in Caesarea</u> (<b>11</b>:15, 17)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              The case of Cornelius’
household does not involve ecstatic utterance, however. That they had no
control over their beginning to speak either in Acts<b> 2</b> or Acts <b>10</b>
is not a necessary inference from the text. Even if they did not, the ecstatic
nature of these speakings is not supported (anymore than at the confusion of
languages in Genesis <b>11</b>:1-9).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>III. LANGUAGE SPEAKING IN 1 CORINTHIANS <b>14</b> </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  A.  Introductory considerations</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        1.   Some elements of the
description in 1 Corinthians <b>14</b> either differ from those of Acts <b>2</b>
or involve matters not specified elsewhere. Most are readily answerable; others
have more cogency to them.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                                      </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                                               Pentecost
                                Corinth</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                             (a)     everyone
understood (<b>2</b>:6)            no one understood (<b>14</b>:2)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                             (b)    tongues
spoken to men (<b>2</b>:6)        spoken to God (<b>14</b>:2)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                             (c)     no
interpreter needed (<b>2</b>:6)           interpreter needed (<b>14</b>:23, 28)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                             (d)    sign
to believers (<b>11</b>:15)               sign to unbelievers (<b>14</b>:22)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                             (e)     strangers
awed (<b>2</b>:7-8)                  think they are mad (<b>14</b>:23)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        2.   Three explanations could
account for these differences.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              a.   <u>A difference in
the circumstance</u>: the hearers in Acts could understand the languages
without supernatural aid, but the hearers in Corinth were having to listen to
people misuse the gift by using it in languages none—or almost none—of them
knew.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              b.   <u>A difference in
the nature of the tongues themselves</u></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                    (1)    Some
commentators view the Corinthian tongues as illegitimate because they were <u>uncontrolled
ecstatic utterance</u> rather than human languages. Paul was content to deal
with the problem pastorally by putting enough regulations on using them that
they could no longer be done unless they were genuine.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                    (2)    Other
writers consider them as structured languages of angels (1 Corinthians<b> 13</b>:1).
Most Pentecostals do not believe that the “second kind of tongues” is ecstatic
gibberish or babble. They suppose they are actually verbalizing in something
that may be called a language. Thus there are three proposed understandings of languages
in the New Testament: actual human languages, angelic languages, and ecstatic
utterance. The last two are only theoretically different from each other.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              c.   <u>A difference in
the use of the word </u></span><u><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>γλῶσσα</span></u><u><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'> (<i>glōssa</i>):</span></u><span style='font-size:
12.0pt'> in Acts </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>γλῶσσα</span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'> means language, but in 1 Corinthians Paul nuances </span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>γλῶσσα</span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>
to mean a language the hearer does not understand. Combined with </span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>γλῶσσα</span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>, </span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>λαλέω</span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>
lends itself to this distinction because it focuses on the speaking in contrast
to </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>λέγω</span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>, which focuses more on the form that can then carry
meaning. Consequently, speaking in a language contrasts with prophesying as
non-communicative speech contrasts with communicative speech.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                    It is clarifying
to say “language(s)” in the expression “speaking in languages/a language.” Part
of what opens the door to supposing non-language utterance is that modern
English seldom uses <i>tongue</i> to mean “language.” <i>Tongues</i> is a
relatively meaningless word, and therefore can get filled with foreign meaning;
current usage reserves it for the mouth organ (whereas Greek uses </span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>γλῶσσα</span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>
for the tongue and for language). From this point forward, we use <i>language</i>
rather than <i>tongue</i> to illustrate the difference it makes when the normal
word <i>languages </i>replaces the ambiguous term <i>tongues</i> throughout,
thereby reproducing the continuity of usage in the Greek text of 1 Corinthians <b>14</b>.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        3.   The method required: understanding
the ambiguous in light of the clear and thereby shouldering the positive burden
of proof</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              If the descriptive
elements in 1 Corinthians were indecisive as to the nature of this language
speaking, proper procedure would still call for understanding these languages
as actual languages, because Acts <b>2</b> sets the framework for determining
their nature. As long as other descriptions fit with actual languages, there is
no reason to bring in a second type. The situation does not seem to be so
imprecise, however, because actual languages are evidently in Paul’s mind as
the following observations below about what was happening in Corinth.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  B.  Arguments for their being actual
human languages, not “angelic” ones</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
-1.0in'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                          1.   <u>The
phenomenon was capable of serving as a sign to unbelievers </u>(<b>14</b>:22;
cp. <b>16</b>:17).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              a.   If </span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>εἰς</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>
</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>σημεῖον</span><span lang=EL
style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>(<i>eis sēmeion</i>)
means “is of significance/is <u>meaningful</u>” as far as an unbeliever knows,
the languages must be actual languages that outsiders could understand. They could
recognize in the message something that addressed the secret yearnings of their
heart (<b>14</b>:25). “Secrets of the heart” could be a prophet’s knowing
things about the hearers without being told (cp. John <b>4</b>:19). More likely
the message addressed the general yearnings of the human heart and fostered
intuitive acceptance. <i>“That God is surely among you”</i> could compare with <i>“you
are the Son of God”</i> in the mouth of Nathaneal (John<b> 1</b>:47-51) or the
Roman centurion at Christ’s crucifixion (Mark <b>15</b>:39).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                    The importance of “sign
to <u>unbelievers</u>” lies in their not needing the gift of interpretation.
Unbelievers could not understand if language speaking were languages of angels
or ecstatic non-language.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              b.   If the sign value
is <u>evidential</u> in the sense that a hearer could recognize something
miraculous was happening (people speaking a language they would not know), the
phenomenon would have to be recognizable as supernatural. This means again that
languages have to be recognizable languages. These “languages” could not have any
proof value </span><span style='font-size:12.0pt;color:windowtext'>unless they
were something God would need to enable a person to do</span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:red'> </span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>(cp.
John <b>3</b>:2). <u>What is not capable of being verified or falsified is not
capable of bearing proof</u>. The nature of languages here had to be different
from what was going on down the road in the mouth of the priestess at the
Delphic oracle or no one could distinguish pagan practice and divine
manifestation. Unbelievers had to be able to appreciate what was occurring. What
occurs in pagan religions cannot mark Christianity.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                    The foregoing
argument assumes that the sign could mark Christianity in contrast to demonic
and parapsychological phenomena. Perhaps Christianity could be marked by some
trait without being distinctively so marked. Supernatural, for example, is
available through satanic as well as divine sources. Consequently, we could
remember that combinationalism takes evidence as a cluster to create cumulative
effect. We could also remember that scripture often does not distinguish
primary proof and confirmatory evidence. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                    Paul would still
not have in mind “languages of angels” here because no one has heard “languages
of angels” so as to identify something as an example of them. When the angels
appeared to people in biblical times, they spoke the languages of the ones they
visited.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                    How can we explain
the combination that languages are for a sign to unbelievers and yet
unbelievers will think people are crazy if they come in and everybody is
speaking in languages (<b>14</b>:23)? The answer is either that they will think
the people are crazy because everybody is talking at once (chaos), that the
speakers are “out of control,” or that the languages are not being translated
(mystery). The likelihood is that these unbelievers will not understand the languages
either.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                    If Jewish hearers
mocked actual languages in Acts <b>2</b>, pagans would surely have done the
same—or more—with non-languages. What people mock does not carry convincing power
for them.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        2.   <u>Paul is talking about “sounds
in the world”</u> (<b>14</b>:7-11), so not that of “angels.”</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        3.   <u>Not knowing the
meaning of the languages would cause a person to be viewed as a “foreigner”</u>
(<b>14</b>:11), not an angel.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        4.   <u>Paul considered Isaiah
<b>28</b>:11-12 applicable to the Corinthian languages</u> (cp. Deuteronomy <b>28</b>:49-50;
Isaiah <b>66</b>:18; Jeremiah <b>5</b>:15). The passage deals with foreigners
(Assyrians) speaking to Israel (<b>14</b>:21). So Paul is talking about languages
of people, not languages of angels.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        5.   <u>The prophet is in
control of himself</u> (<b>14</b>:32). Consequently, these languages are not glossolalic
expression arising from uncontrollable ecstasy that would cause a visitor to
think the people were crazy.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        6.   <u>Languages here are
capable of being made plural</u>: note <b>14</b>:5, 18, 21, 22, 23, 39 as
opposed to singulars in <b>14</b>:2, 4, 13, 14, 19, 26?, 27. What can be made
plural must have boundaries; a language must be a system parallel to other
systems or it cannot be made plural. There is no way to make “gibberish”
plural—“gibberishes.” Consequently, even if we were to understand “languages of
angels” as other than a hyperbole, the “language<u>s</u>” would have to be
systematic articulations. If language speaking is random sounds, it cannot be made
plural.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        7.   <u>Languages could be
interpreted</u>, or translated (<b>12</b>:30; <b>14</b>:5, 13, 26, 27), to give
meaning. The terms </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ἑρμηνεύω</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>(Luke <b>24</b>:27;
John <b>1</b>:42; <b>9</b>:7; Hebrews <b>7</b>:2) and </span><span lang=EL
style='font-size:12.0pt'>διερμηνεύω</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>
</span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>(Luke <b>24</b>:27; Acts <b>9</b>:36; 1
Corinthians <b>12</b>:30; <b>14</b>:5, 13, 27) mean “to translate,” or with the
latter term “to expound” (Luke <b>24</b>:27). What can be translated must have
meaning to be translated. To have meaning, languages must have structure (even
if they are “languages of angels”); because it is by structure that meaning is
carried and conveyed. No structure, no meaning, no translation. Any phenomenon
that does not have analyzable structure cannot fall under Paul’s usage of “languages”
in this chapter. Languages were able to become prophecy. Languages-unto-prophecy
means sounds with agreed-upon significance (<b>14</b>:7-11). So, these languages
in Corinth were not non-linguistic glossolalia.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        8.   <u>In praying in languages,
<i>you</i> are truly giving thanks all right</u> (<b>14</b>:17). The person is
communicating something. (<i>You</i> is a separate pronoun in the Greek text,
hence, emphatic. Subject pronouns are not needed in Greek because finite verbs
have endings that indicate person and number)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        9.   <u>Even if the other
persons were </u></span><u><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ἰδιώται</span></u><u><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'> (<i>idiōtai</i>), they would know what you are saying
and could say “Amen” to it</span></u><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>. An “idiotes”
is a layman, a non-specialist, perhaps a person without a gift—like the gift of
interpretation. Consequently, language speakers are saying something. They are
using a sound-symbol system; otherwise, they could not be saying anything. This
fact stands over against something meant by God back to himself without the
human instruments having any idea of what is passing through their voices. That
alternate view pictures language speaking as something where the speakers do
not mean anything because they do not understand what they are saying. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  C.  Arguments posed against their
being actual human languages</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        1.   They were <i>“languages
of angels.”</i></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              A number of
considerations make clear, however, that languages of angels in 1 Corinthians<b>
13</b>:1 is hyperbole for emphasis, not sober literalism.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              a.   The context of 1
Corinthians <b>13</b>:1 is hyperbolic: remove mountains (<b>13</b>:2; cp.
Matthew <b>17</b>:20; <b>21</b>:21 = Mark <b>11</b>:23; Luke <b>17</b>:6), give
my body to be burned (<b>13</b>:3; note textual question “that I might be proud”),
know all mysteries and all knowledge (<b>13</b>:2), give all my goods to feed
the poor (<b>13</b>:3). A similar heightened expression occurs in Galatians <b>1</b>:8-9:
<i>“Though an angel from heaven should preach any other gospel . . . let him be
accursed.”</i></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              b.   Angels are spirits,
who do not have flesh and bones (Luke<b> 24</b>:3; Hebrews <b>l</b>:4, 14); consequently,
in their own natural form of being, they have no physical tongues to make
sounds with; hence, angels do not communicate by physical means. <i>“The word
spoken by angels”</i> (Hebrews <b>2</b>:2) happened in times of manifestation
to human beings, when angels entered the material realm. In such cases they were
speaking in human languages.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                    The idea that
angels communicate, though true, is not enough. In order for angelic languages
to explain human glossolalia, angels would have to communicate in their own
realm in a way that could be duplicated by flesh-and-bones people. That is what
we have no basis for saying they do, because they are not physical beings.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              c.   Such a view commits
a person to the idea that there is atmosphere in heaven for fleshly tongues to
make sounds with. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              d.   Paul identifies languages
with the temporary and temporal matters of this life in contrast to love, which
never falls; speaking in languages will not occur in “the perfect” (<b>13</b>:8).
Languages of angels cannot be literal; otherwise, they would be in this life
(the “in-part” situation) <u>and</u> in the hereafter (the perfect). But the “in-part”
will be done away when “the perfect” comes. In the face-to-face relationship,
we will be with angels and like them—spiritual; consequently, we cannot now be
receiving something angels do not have.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              e.   Languages of angels
could not have evidential force. But Paul teaches in <b>14</b>:23 that languages
are a sign, or “sign-ificant,” to unbelievers. How would people ever know that
what they witnessed was a language of the angels unless they had previously
known what such a thing was like? And if angels’ realm differs from ours, how
would a human person <u>ever</u> find out what their languages are like? </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              f.    Language of angels
would have to have structure to convey meaning. As a sound-symbol system,
language relies on form to represent meaning. Linguistic analysis can determine
whether an utterance has linguisticality.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              g.   Those who followed
the apostles were not linguists or psychoanalysts who could verify
scientifically that they were dealing with an actual specimen of language. They
had to recognize it as language in some down-to-earth way. They could do that
if they knew the language they were hearing and knew that these people were not
native speakers of it. For this reason, in today’s situation languages cannot
be ancient human languages now dead or a human language from some obscure
tribal people no one here knows. Typical “people on the street” could not render
a verdict in such cases; so they could have no evidential force for them. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              h.   There is no reason
to believe that there was a confusion of languages among the angels as was the
case among men. If not for some punitive reason angelic languages were
confused, we wonder why they would differ from one another?</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        2.   <u>Languages are
addressed to God</u> (<b>14</b>:2, 28) and to oneself (<b>14</b>:28; cp. <b>14</b>:4).
Some have maintained that 1 Corinthians<b> 14</b> refers to prayer language
because of <b>14</b>:2, 14-19, 28. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              a.   More strictly, the
text says, <i>“Those who speak in a language speak to God.”</i> “Address” implies
intentionally directed speech, something not evident in the verse. Languages
were not directed to God intentionally as shown by the following points. The
marks of human language have been noted. “Speak to oneself” might be hyperbole,
though doubtful. That “speaking to oneself” (<b>14</b>:28b) can occur while the
speakers are keeping silent (<b>14</b>:28a). They are verbalizing to themselves
and, as we say, praying silently. Meditation and communion both fit with silently
speaking to themselves. Neither are closely tied to specific wording.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              b.   The reason the language
speaker speaks <u>to God</u> is that “no man understands.” Speaking to God was
by default, not intent. God was the only one left who understood. The absolute “no
one” would not have to include those with the gift of interpretation or those
with the language as their native language. Since it cannot be really absolute,
it may be wise even to suppose that a few speakers of a language were present
on occasion. Perhaps simultaneous languages speaking got started in Corinth as
a way to communicate to small pockets of foreign-language speakers that showed
up at the services. Paul would then have been telling language speakers to
translate so everyone could profit. For all practical purposes, no one was understanding.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              c.   What sense does it
make for God to talk to himself? If speakers in a language have no idea what they
are saying and if the Spirit of God is giving them a message appropriate for <u>listeners,</u>
what intentional purpose could it serve for God? <i>“<u>You</u> are giving
thanks all right”</i> and “blessing” (<b>14</b>:17); if so, God is not originating
the message. If speakers have no idea what they are saying, they are not saying
it. Having God talking to himself removes it from being an interpersonal
experience, and it breaks the principle that miraculous gifts are for other
than the ones who have them. Jesus did not turn the stones into bread, and Paul
did not cure himself of the “thorn in the flesh.”</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                    If spiritual gifts
are not for benefiting the person who has them, speaking in languages is not
for the speaker’s benefit. As a counter comment, the statements in <b>14</b>:4,
28 are raised. <i>“Those who speak in a language edify themselves” </i>(<b>14</b>:4)
is true when the communication loop is completed (as when interpretation takes
place, the hearer knows the language, the language speaker has also the gift of
interpretation, or understands the flow of thought—just not the word-by-word
level). The <u>purpose</u> of languages is not to edify oneself, but to edify
others; however, when communication to others does not take place, the <u>result</u>
is that only the speakers are edified, and they end up speaking only to God (<b>14</b>:2).
The purpose in such cases differs from the result.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              d.   Speaking <u>to oneself</u>,
<b>14</b>:4, 28 is sometimes taken as indicating a second kind of languages (“languages
of angels”) used in a different setting. These languages are for <u>private</u>
use in address to <u>God</u> as in prayer.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                    Paul does not say
in<b> 14</b>:28 that those who speak to themselves do so in a language. They
may be running through their minds truths that edify themselves or praise God. In<b>
14</b>:4 the speaking to God and the edifying of self comes while using languages
out loud. In <b>14</b>:28 it comes in speaking to themselves. Whether aloud or
silently, and whether in a language or not, the effects are the same. (See
comments below under <i>“the speakers do not understand what they are saying.”</i>)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                    Since speaking in
languages is a communication gift, it does not necessarily involve revelation
as well. The message of those who <i>“speaks to themselves and to God”</i> may
be a message originating with themselves, not God.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                    Earlier in the
chapter, Paul may have in mind people who have the gift of interpretation as
well as languages, and so they edify themselves (<b>14</b>:4) because they are
doing for themselves what Paul urges them to do for listeners in <b>14</b>:6—interpret
what they say. Later, Paul recommends praying for the gift of interpretation if
the person does <u>not</u> have it. More likely, however, is the proposal that language
speakers would be edified by the ideas they had in mind, ideas that speaking in
languages enabled them to communicate to others in a language they as speakers
did not know. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                    As to <u>private
usage</u>, Paul deals with a situation occurring “in church.” In so doing, he
does not tell the Corinthians they are using this gift in the wrong place and
should do it at home or alone. Instead, he regulates it as something that can
take place in the gathered community. On other occasions involving public <i>vs.</i>
private, he comes right out and tells them to eat at home (1 Corinthians <b>11</b>:20-22)
or raise questions at home (1 Corinthians<b> 14</b>:35) instead of abusing each
other and creating disturbance during fellowship and worship.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                    <u>In the church</u>
(<b>14</b>:18-19) does not have to contrast with <u>in private</u>. It can
contrast with <u>in evangelism</u>. The point would then be that languages work
well in proclaiming the gospel in cross-cultural settings as a reversal of the
tower of Babel. In a cosmopolitan city like Corinth, occasions for using that
gift might arise often. They could be adapted to worship settings with a mixed
audience, but normally that would not be necessary; gathered worshipers tend to
be local and understand the same language. It is not possible to maintain the
proposition that languages were intended purely for evangelistic purposes since
Acts demonstrates their occurrence in cases where brand new converts spoke in languages
aside from any evangelistic effort. But then again, it is not possible to
maintain that languages were for private use either. Speaking in languages in
places that did not call for them and in ways that were counter-productive, was
prompted by desires inappropriate to Christian service. People’s not
interpreting when they could shows the motivation behind this language
speaking. In Corinth, language speaking seems to have degenerated into
attention-getting behavior in place of benefit to others or glory to God.        </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                    At any rate, even
if “in church” contrasted with “in private,” we do not have to have a second
type of language. Speaking in a real language in private would likewise be
possible. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              e.   <i>Languages</i> is
used parallel to <i>prophecy</i> because there is no communication going on if
the hearer cannot understand them. If the audience comprehends as at Pentecost
or when interpretation is given (<b>14</b>:5), then languages become prophecy.
The relationship between</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:center'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'><img width=253 height=52 id="Picture 1"
src="/cir-library/images/essays/xenoglossia-in-corinth/image001.png" alt=CIR--HS--diag></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>the terms changes from separate circles to one within
another. Speaking in languages is a speaking gift, not necessarily a content
gift. It does not convey a message—prophecy—if the hearer does not understand
it.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        3.   <u>Someone unlearned or
unbelieving will think you are crazy</u> (<b>14</b>:23).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              That observation does
not show they were not real languages. A similar reaction came from skeptical
people in Acts <b>2</b>, where actual languages were spoken. Skeptics accused
them of being <u>drunk</u>, which is comparable to being crazy. In another
incident, Festus told Paul he was crazy even when Festus knew the language and
understood the message (Acts <b>26</b>:24). The Pharisees accused Jesus of
being a demoniac Samaritan when they could not understand what he was telling
them in their own language (John <b>8</b>:48).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        4.   <u>Speakers do not
understand what they are saying</u> (<b>14</b>:14). In <b>14</b>:2 the apostle
says that no one understands. In <b>14</b>:14 he adds, <i>“If I pray in a language,
my spirit prays, but my understanding is unfruitful.”</i> Paul would rather pray
with his understanding also (<b>14</b>:15, 19). These verses should not be
taken to mean that language speakers have no idea what they are saying, with
the inference that what they’re saying comes from God. The following
considerations provide an alternative reconstruction of this matter.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              By “understand” Paul
means the element-by-element, word-for-word level in contrast to the general
conceptual flow. Understanding always occurs at the conceptual level in language
speaking, but not necessarily at the word level. It is <u>language</u>, not <u>concept</u>,
that is at issue in language speaking and interpretation. We take it that Paul
refers to the conceptual level when he uses the word “spirit.” In like manner
he refers to the word level when he says that the <u>understanding,</u> or mind<u>,</u>
is unfruitful (<b>14</b>:14-19). Revelation is <u>concept</u>; languages and interpretation
are matters of <u>language</u>.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              The text does not say
that Paul does not understand when he speaks in a language. Since speaking in languages
is a language gift rather than a revelation or content gift, he surely means
that he did not understand the language, not that he did not understand the
thought.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              Paul goes on say that
the hearer cannot say “Amen” to a prayer spoken in a language because <i>“he
does not know what you are saying.” </i>Indeed, how can “you” say “Amen” to
your own prayer or even be said to be praying? It is no good to say, “I agree
with the spirit/feel/attitude of the prayer”; the one occupying the place of
the “idiotes” could say <u>that</u>. If language speaking should not be done
when the “<u>other</u>” has no understanding, language speaking in prayer to
God should not be done when the <u>speaker</u> does not understand, which would
amount to prohibiting language speaking; but Paul does not do that.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              a.   You are giving
thanks, all right, but you are not edifying others. It is doubtful how proper
it would be to speak of giving thanks if the speakers did not understand that they
were giving thanks—if they did not understand at all what they were “saying.” What
point would there be in prayer if the speaker was not offering the content? Perhaps
it is imaginable how people proclaiming to other people would be doing
something real and beneficial even though they themselves had no idea what God
was saying through them. It would at least be communication. But in the case of
prayer, the speaker and the hearer would be the same person: God. First, what
sense would it make for God to talk to himself? At least what sense would it
make to call it “communication”? Second, what would the text possibly mean by
saying that the <u>speaker</u> is giving thanks (cp. “magnifying God,” Acts <b>10</b>:46)?
The point here is reinforced by the express use of the pronoun <u>you</u> with
the verb “thank”: <i>“<u>You</u> are giving thanks all right, but you are not
edifying the <u>other</u>.”</i> If the pronoun “you” (</span><span lang=EL
style='font-size:12.0pt'>σύ</span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>) contrasts
with “other” (</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ἕτερος</span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>), it stresses the fact that <u>you</u> does
understand the message spoken in a language.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              b.   Edification does
occur despite their not “understanding.” Edification is not simply affective
exhilaration, but cognitive “eye-opening.” Therefore, edification must be
understanding, but at a different level. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                    If “edify themselves”
in <b>14</b>:4 meant affective uplift, the persons nearby would also be edified
by knowing that the supernatural hand of God was penetrating the world right
beside them; yet <b>14</b>:17 says that those hearers are not edified. In <b>14</b>:26,
edification comes from psalms, teaching, revelation, languages, interpretation.
Although singing might involve the emotional side, the emphasis is on cognitive
forms of communication. Edifying others happens when languages are translated to
become prophecy (<b>14</b>:5). The other people in<b> 14</b>:17 are not edified
because they do not know what is being said. The intended use of spiritual
gifts is edification (<b>14</b>:12).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              c.   Paul contrasts
speaking in languages with speaking <u>five words</u> with his understanding. He
seems to contrast the language-speaking type of understanding with the word-level
communication.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              d.   <u>Singing</u> with
the spirit (<b>14</b>:15) is </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ψάλλω</span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'> (<i>psallō</i>), “to sing a psalm,” not </span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ἄδω</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>
</span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>(<i>adō</i>), “to praise” (Ephesians <b>5</b>:19a),
or </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>αἰνέω</span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'> (<i>aineō</i>), “to sing” (Luke <b>2</b>:13). It is
even used in Ephesians<b> 5</b>:19b in apparent contrast to </span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ἄδω</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>
</span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>(“to sing”). </span><span lang=EL
style='font-size:12.0pt'>Ψάλλω</span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'> most
likely means “singing psalms.” If so, a set and known content is involved. Secondly,
</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>τῷ</span><span lang=EL
style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>πνεύματι</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>(<i>tōi
pneumati</i>, “with the spirit”) is comparable to </span><span lang=EL
style='font-size:12.0pt'>τῇ</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>καρδίᾳ</span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'> (<i>tēi
kardiāi</i>, “with the heart”) in Ephesians<b> 5</b>:19. Finally, “singing in
the spirit” may not be singing in languages.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              e.   <u>Except they
interpret/translate</u> (<b>14</b>:5) may mean, not that they have also the
gift of interpretation (contrast above), but that they are explaining the message,
which they know at the conceptual level. The gift of interpretation would normally
be for interpreting someone else’s language. <u>Praying to interpret</u> their
own language (<b>14</b>:13) would presumably be so they could express in their
own language the word-for-word presentation they had just spoken in a language.
Another possibility is that in the case where content is known, “interpret”
would be <u>explaining the meaning</u> rather than <u>translating the language</u>.
“Interpret” (</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ἑρμηνεύω</span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>, <i>hermēneuō</i>) can mean to translate from one
language to another or to explain something differently in the same language.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              f.    Nowhere in
Corinthians or elsewhere does scripture indicate that speaking in languages
necessarily involves a revelation of what people say in those languages. In
fact, <b>14</b>:26 distinguishes the two gifts. Of course, were people to have
both gifts, they could combine them on an occasion like Pentecost (cp. <b>14</b>:30).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              g.   “Speaking
mysteries” (<b>14</b>:2) does not have to mean “hidden things” to the <u>speaker</u>
or the <u>whole world</u>. They were mysteries to <u>listeners</u> who did not
understand the words.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              h.   In witnessing or in
teaching believers, how could language speakers field questions about their
message if they had no idea what they had just said? On Pentecost, for example,
Peter was speaking in languages when he preached what is recorded. How would he
understand the audience’s question when they responded to his presentation? Even
worse, he would not know what answer the Holy Spirit was giving them. It would
apparently be a surprise to him when they come to him then, wanting him to help
them obey what they had heard him say about getting baptized. Similarly in Corinth,
how would speakers answer questions about what they just said (<b>14</b>:35)?</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              i.    In prayer
especially, if speakers had no idea what they were saying, we say again that
God would simply be talking to himself.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        Even if this alternate
reconstruction does not commend itself as <u>the</u> point, it is satisfactory
enough to indicate another way to read the text. If so, the “not understand”
factor falls out as a distinctive mark for a second type of language speaking
found at Corinth.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        We summarize with a list of
approaches to the statement that in prayer the ones that speak in languages are
praying while their understanding is not: (a) idea level <i>vs</i>. verbal
level, as advocated above; (b) emotional level <i>vs.</i> rational level; (c)
enthusiasm vs. content; and (d) intention <i>vs.</i> result.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        5.   <u>The languages are “unknown</u>”
(<b>14:</b>2).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              The word “unknown” in
the Authorized Version does not appear in the original text. Even if it did, it
would not mean the language was unknown to mankind and therefore was angelic
utterance. It could be unknown to the speaker even as “new languages” (Mark <b>16</b>:17)
can be new to the speaker rather than to mankind. Similarly, the “other languages”
(Acts <b>2</b>:4; 1 Corinthians <b>14</b>:21) are other than what the speaker
used normally.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        Our summary comments on
proposals for a second type of languages are these: scriptural statements are
being accommodated to the experience of people who have done what they suppose
is language speaking. Since they did not understand what they were saying, they
suppose <b>14</b>:14 means that. Since the sounds they made were not
understandable to anybody around them, they suppose that unknown languages in <b>14</b>:2
(KJV) means unknown to mankind, and so on. The experience is providing the
precise information for the way the wording of the text is understood. The
assumption is that their experience is what this text is talking about. Since
that is not necessarily the case, the interpretation is not really an
interpretation of the text.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  D.  Summary of main points of
interpretation in 1 Corinthians<b> 14</b></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
-1.0in'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                                1.   Languages
speak to God because no <u>human</u> understands; they speak to God by default.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        2. The speakers do not
understand at the element-by-element level, but they know at the conceptual
level. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
-1.25in'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                                      3.   The
language speakers are accused of being crazy because the hearers cannot
understand what they are speaking—or the speakers are all talking at once.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        4.   Those who speak in
languages edify themselves by conceptual edification.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        5.   They speak mysteries as
far as the audience is concerned.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        6.   “Languages of angels” is
exaggeration for emphasis.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                                      Summary
of Characteristics of “Languages”</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        1.   Languages are actual
human languages (xenoglossia).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        2.   Speakers control <u>when</u>
they use the gift.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        3.   Speakers are in control
of themselves <u>while</u> they use the gift.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        4.   They understand
conceptually what they are saying while they are using the </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              gift.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        5.   The gift of languages is
a supernatural gift. It is not “primed” into existence. A person does not just
decide to do it; it is a gift. Being emotionally “driven” does not enhance the
coming of the gift.         </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        6.   Speakers evidently can
match the language of the one(s) they address.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        7.   Speaking in languages is a
language-speaking ability; it does not necessarily involve a revealed content
as well.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        8.   Speaking in languages is
such that it can serve as a sign to unbelievers. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:center'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>Conclusion</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:center'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  These considerations lead to the
conclusion that the New Testament gives no basis for the idea that there is a
second type of legitimate speaking in languages.</span></p>
