<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;

style='font-size:12.0pt'>  </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            The diagram pictures miraculous built on
non-miraculous. Everything that has been said about the Spirit’s non-miraculous
operation in conversion, guidance, power, intercession, and unification
underlies any miraculous operation. Certain implications are come from this
arrangement of the Spirit’s ways of working. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            (a)  The non-miraculous and indirect
supernatural work of the Spirit applies to everyone who is in Christ. Miraculous
manifestation (as distinguished from answered prayer) does not apply to
everyone. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            (b) Genuine miracle presupposes that the miracle
worker is in Christ. Miraculous manifestation does not occur through those who
are not his, because miracles have evidential purpose; they confirm the claims
of the miracle worker. That means miraculous signs are not done through an office,
but through the person. God does not work through the office aside from the
person in the office; the institutional church is an interpersonal system, not
a legal one like priesthood or kingship. There is no such thing as having the
second layer without the first to build it on. As status must correlate with
walk, walk must correlate with miraculous endowment. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            (c)  New revelation must harmonize with
previous revelation. No legitimate empowerment can do what is <u>contradictory</u>
to or foreign to the purposes of the faith. No special revelation can deny previous
revelation: reconciling people to God (salvation) and enabling them to grow in
that relationship (evangelism and edification).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            A.  Speaking in languages in the New
Testament</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  One question about New Testament language
speaking is whether there was more than one type. Evangelicals agree that
speaking in languages was a first-century phenomenon involving actual human
languages. Not everyone agrees that there was a “second type.” </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  1. Method of research </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        a.   Terminology</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              There is a lack of
finality in the vocabulary approach regarding </span><span lang=EL
style='font-size:12.0pt'>γλῶσσα</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>(tongue, language) as well as </span><span lang=EL
style='font-size:12.0pt'>λαλέω</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>(speak) and </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:
12.0pt'>διερμηνεύω</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>(interpret, translate). What a word means in other
cases does not necessarily determine its usage in a given instance. The
statistically most likely meaning may be set aside by the peculiarities of a special
setting. Inasmuch as the meaning of an expression is ultimately determined by
its context anyway, it is better to examine the characteristics of the
situation than to rely on word usage elsewhere. Any uniformity that can be
found, however, for </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>γλῶσσα</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>as a
reference to actual human language does tend to shift the burden of proof onto
someone who claims a non-language character for speaking in languages. The
positive evidence for glossolalia based on word usage becomes a difficulty for
those who call for a special usage not evidenced elsewhere. One offsetting
consideration might be that if there were no other examples of what was
happening among the Christians, referring to it by the closest word available could
explain a special meaning.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              Words get their meanings
from the settings they address. A word’s meaning we learn from the
circumstances where it occurs. We must not confuse the reality (perception),
the idea about the reality (conception), and the word that represents the idea
about the reality (language).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        b.   Examples</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                       (1)    Starting
with Cornelius and moving to Pentecost is illustrated by William L. Hendricks, “Glossolalia
in the New Testament,” in <i>Speaking in Tongues: Let’s Talk About It</i> (ed.
by Watson Mills, Word Books, 1973), pp. 51-52. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                       (2)    Starting
with 1 Corinthians 1<b>2-14 </b>as defining a double possibility and then
moving to Acts is another approach.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                       (3)    Starting
with Acts <b>2</b> and supposing that other cases are the same seems to be the
most natural method. Only those marks in Acts <b>10-11</b>, Acts <b>19</b>,
Mark <b>16</b>:17, 1 Corinthians <b>12-14 </b>that would not harmonize with the
picture in Acts <b>2</b> could justify supposing that speaking in languages was
of more than one type. This procedure seems best because Acts <b>2</b> is the <u>first
example</u> of language speaking in the New Testament. It is also a <u>clear
account</u> of what they were. The importance of starting with Acts <b>2</b> is
that beginning elsewhere allows for unfalsifiable claims based on ambiguous descriptions
(like that of 1 Corinthians <b>14</b>) and thereby creates a negative burden of
proof, which is almost always an invalid thinking pattern.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        c.   Experience</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              A common approach begins
with a personal or observed verbal experience. The elements of the procedure
are as follows:</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              (1)    The observation
is made that there is Christian fruit in the person’s </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                       life.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              (2)    The event
occurred in connection with acts done to honor Jesus.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              (3)    It cannot be “their
own act” because people cannot do such a thing on their own. It cannot be by
Satan’s power, so it must be the Spirit’s work.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              (4)    The observer consults
scripture to see if the experience fits with what scripture teaches. But the method
does not demonstrate <i>sola scriptura</i> well because experience provides the
precise information about what occurred. Scripture may not be precise enough to
<u>eliminate</u> that possibility; so the fact that it can fit with scripture
does not <u>establish</u> it as true. In the case of glossolalic tongues, it does
not mean that an experience can be identified, say, with tongues in a “second
sense,” because that second sense (second kind of tongues) cannot be positively
established. Since everything mentioned in 1 Corinthians <b>14</b> (and
elsewhere) fits with actual human languages, the precise nature of the “second
type” comes from experience rather than scripture.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        <i> “Baptism in the Spirit as
evidenced by speaking in tongues”</i> people may consider a self-authenticating
experience. It is so clearly real that people who experience it accept it as
real even though they cannot explain it or answer objections people may raise. The
problem is that “self-authenticating” may mean nothing more than vivid subjectivity
devoid of objective evaluation. Furthermore, it confuses the vivid reality of
the experience and the explanation of the cause and meaning of the experience. The
fact of the experience is a different thing from what it is and means. No one denies
that those who have had an experience have had it, sometimes in a life-changing
way. The objection is to identifying it with New Testament language speaking
and concluding that God dispensed his Spirit in that experience. The person
experiencing it is in no better position than anyone else to explain its source,
significance, and identity with the New Testament phenomenon.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  B.  Speaking in languages in Acts <b>2</b></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        a. Observations that they were
actual languages (xenoglossia)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:135.0pt;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify;text-indent:-117.0pt'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              (1)    <i>“They began to
<u>speak</u> in <u>other</u> tongues</i> [</span><span lang=EL
style='font-size:12.0pt'>ἑτέραις</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>γλώσσαις</span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>]
<i>as the Spirit enabled them to speak”</i> (<b>2</b>:4b).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              (2)    <i>“They were <u>hearing
them speak each one in our own language</u></i> [</span><span lang=EL
style='font-size:12.0pt'>διαλέκτῷ</span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>] <i>in
which we were born”</i> (<b>2</b>:6 + 8; note <b>2</b>:11, </span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>γλώσσαις</span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>).
The amazement in the audience came from seeing these Palestinian commoners
preaching in distant Gentile “dialects/vernaculars.” </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              (3)    <u>No interpreter
was needed</u> for the Parthians, Medes, and the rest, to know that these Jews
were talking about the <i>“mighty works of God”</i> (<b>2</b>:11).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        b.   Observations posed
against their being actual languages</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              (1)    <u>The idea that
the miracle was in the hearing rather than in the </u></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                       <u>speaking</u></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                       (a)     <i>“We <u>hear</u>
them speaking.”</i> </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                                [1]    The
text is explicit, however, that the apostles <u>spoke</u> in other languages. If
they <u>spoke</u> in other languages, the audience would <u>hear</u> them speaking
in other languages. If the miracle were in the hearing, the apostles would not
be speaking in other languages as the text says.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                                [2]    The
visible manifestation was fiery tongues over the </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                                         <u>speakers</u>.
</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                                [3]    The
Holy Spirit came on the speakers, not the hearers.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:2.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify;text-indent:-2.25in'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                                [4]    Acts
<b>2</b>:4 sets the stage so that <b>2</b>:6, 11 explains the             preceding,
not <i>vice versa</i>.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                                [5]    In
the other cases of language speaking, the miracle clearly occurs with the
speakers, especially in Corinth because “no one understood.”</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                                [6]    We
wonder where the charge of <u>drunkenness</u> would come from if they were all
hearing their own language only. Why would everyone not have been endowed with
the power of interpretation instead? (The charge could have been made because
of the <u>message</u> rather than the <u>sound</u> of foreign languages.) Evidently
some of them could hear languages they did not recognize—perhaps from another
apostle speaking nearby. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                                Speaking
<u>in</u> a language does not require a preposition. In Acts <b>26</b>:14 <i>“speaking
to me in the Hebrew language”</i> is worded </span><span lang=EL
style='font-size:12.0pt'>τῇ</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>Ἑβραΐστῇ</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:
12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>διαλέκτῷ</span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>. In 1 Corinthians <b>14</b>:21 <i>“I will speak to
this people in/with other languages and with other lips”</i> is worded </span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ἐν</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>
</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ἑτερογλώσσοις</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>καὶ</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ἐν</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>χείλεσιν</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ἑτέροις</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>[</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ἑτέρων</span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>] </span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>λαλήσω</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:
12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>τῷ</span><span lang=EL
style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>λαῷ</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>τούτῳ</span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                       (b)    The
number of nations was too large to be addressed by twelve </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                       languages.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                                [1]    The
number of language groups could probably have been covered by twelve men
speaking simultaneously in various areas in the temple court, a
twenty-seven-acre area on Mt. Moriah. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                                [2]    The
deliveries could have been made in series so that more than twelve languages
would have been used. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                                [3]    Quite
a few foreign Jews would have known Aramaic, but the comment had to do with the
languages where they were born. Greek would have served for the vast majority
and would perhaps qualify as a language <i>“wherein they were born.”</i> Since
many Israelites would have known Greek, however, we wonder whether speaking
Greek would have elicited the comment or would have had much sign value.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:2.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify;text-indent:-2.0in'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                       (c)     <u>Each</u>
heard <u>them</u> speaking in their own language while we know they heard in
their own language.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                                Each
hearing them speaking may be regarded as a distributive singular (cp. “Put
spoons, knives, and forks by the plates when you set the table.” See also
Ephesians <b>4</b>:2a, 18, 23; <b>5</b>:19; <b>6</b>:14, 22; and Philemon 25?).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              (2)    <u>The charge
that the speakers were drunk</u> (<b>2</b>:13) </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                       (a)     Hearing
an unknown language could elicit that remark as surely as hearing an ecstatic
utterance could, particularly if the mockers were near an apostle who spoke a
language they did not understand or if they were not sympathetic to what they
were hearing.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                       (b)    Perhaps
some Palestinian Jews, being somewhat unsympathetic toward Hellenists, resented
the presence of foreign elements in the temple precincts.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                       (c)     If the
mockers understood the message, perhaps they were mocking <u>it</u>—the resurrection,
Jesus as the Messiah, a suffering Messiah. At any rate, a person does not have
to be drunk to be accused of it. Paul was accused of being crazy (Acts <b>26</b>:24);
Jesus was accused of having a demon (John<b> 8</b>:48).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              (3)    <u>Other (kinds
of) languages</u> (</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ἑτέραις</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><b><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>2</span></b><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>:4): </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ἑτέραις</span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'> as distinguished from </span><span lang=EL
style='font-size:12.0pt'>ἄλλαις</span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'> supposedly
means another as to kind rather than another as to number. John <b>14</b>:16
and 1 Corinthians<b> 14</b>:17 come up as examples. In 1 Corinthians <b>12</b>:10,
28 the text says <i>“kinds of languages”</i> (</span><span lang=EL
style='font-size:12.0pt'>γένη</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>γλωσσῶν</span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                       (a)     The
words do not have to differ in this manner—a distinction more characteristic of
classical Greek than the Hellenistic Greek of the New Testament. If
distinguished, </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ἕτερος</span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'> regards the “other” in distinction, disjunction,
contrast to the former—a matter of identity more than kind. </span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>Ἄλλος</span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>
regards it as separate. With </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ἄλλος</span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>, so to speak, they face one another, but with </span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ἕτερος</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:
12.0pt'> </span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>do not. The pattern seems
evident from the following passages: 1 Corinthians <b>12</b>:9-10 uses them
interchangeably in regard to the same list; Luke <b>5</b>:7; <b>7</b>:41; <b>8</b>:6,
7, 8;<b> 11</b>:16, 26 (= Matthew<b> 12</b>:45); John <b>19</b>:37; Acts<b> 13</b>:35;<b>
20</b>:15 (= <b>27</b>:3); Romans <b>7</b>:3; Galatians <b>1</b>:19; Hebrews<b>
5</b>:6; <b>7</b>:11; <b>11</b>:36.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                       (b)    A
difference even in kind might address the <u>cause</u> of the languages rather than
the <u>kind</u> of languages themselves. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                       (c)     The
difference in kind might arise from a Semitic <i>vs</i>. non-Semitic
distinction. 1 Corinthians <b>14</b>:21 (</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:
12.0pt'>ἐν</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL
style='font-size:12.0pt'>ἑτερογλώσσοις</span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>)
is instructive and could fit either (a) or (c) because actual human languages
are in view in this Old Testament reference to the Book of Isaiah (<b>28</b>:11-12).
The Septuagint for Isaiah <b>28</b>:11 is </span><span lang=EL
style='font-size:12.0pt'>διὰ</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>γλώσσης</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:
12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ἑτέρας</span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>. Other <u>kinds of</u> languages do not have to mean
unearthly as well as earthly, angelic as well as human, natural as well as
supernatural. The quotation from Isaiah is about human languages.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              (4)    <u>Same as the
ecstatic utterances in Caesarea</u> (<b>11</b>:15, 17)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                       But the case of
Cornelius’ household does not involve ecstatic utterance. That they had no
control over their beginning to speak either in Acts <b>2</b> or Acts <b>10</b>
is not a necessary inference from the text. Even if they did not, the ecstatic
nature of these speakings does not follow.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  3.   Speaking in languages in Acts <b>10-11</b></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        a.   Observations that they
were actual human languages</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              (1)    They are said to
be the same as what happened on Pentecost (<b>11</b>:15-17). If Acts<b> 2</b> represents
human languages, then Acts <b>10-11</b> does also.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              (2)    <i>“They heard
them . . . magnify God”</i> (<b>10</b>:46). There was discernible content to
their speech. The text does not indicate that this was an involuntary outbreak,
as in a seizure or case of demon possession. The text does not specify how the
Jewish believers could tell that these Gentiles were magnifying God. Peter
could understand because he had also the gift of interpretation. Perhaps it was
Aramaic or even classical Hebrew that Cornelius’ household was enabled to use,
Greek or Latin being their native language. Most Jews would know Greek, but one
wonders whether Roman officials would know Aramaic, and it is not clear that a
Roman centurion would learn Hebrew. At any rate, the believers among the
circumcision, who knew these dialects, could tell that these Gentiles were
glorifying God.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                       As far as
grammar goes, <i>“Heard them speaking in languages and magnifying God”</i> (<b>10</b>:46)
could be taken two ways: magnifying God was done by the language speaking or
they were speaking in languages and also magnifying God as two separate
actions. Hebrew idiom could use two verbs to cover one composite act
(hendiadys): cp. Acts <b>19</b>:6; 1 Timothy <b>2</b>:12; Revelation <b>2</b>:20).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        b.   Opposing observation</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              There was an immediate
outburst rather than a calm conversational response to Peter’s message. But
their enthusiasm for what they were hearing could sufficiently explain their
behavior without resorting to uncontrollable utterance. Besides, the text does
not say that the languages heard were an immediate “outburst.” Paul teaches in 1
Corinthians <b>14</b> that the Spirit of the prophet is subject to the prophet.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  4.    Speaking in languages in Acts <b>19</b>
</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        a.   They did speak in languages
and prophesy, so they were real languages that carried a message. If they did
not know what they were saying, <u>they</u> would not be saying it, especially
in the case of glorifying God.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        b.   Acts <b>19</b>:6 is
evidently speaking of a composite act when it says that they spoke in languages
and prophesied. In both Acts <b>10</b>:46 and <b>19</b>:6, we note that on
Pentecost Peter identifies the language speaking of Acts <b>2</b>—and therefore
<b>10-11</b>—with Joel <b>2</b>, which talks about pouring out the Spirit and
people prophesying. Joel says nothing about speaking in languages; hence, the
prophesying was through the tongue speaking.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        c.   Luke does not elaborate;
so presumably he knew nothing peculiar about </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              this instance.       </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  5.   Speaking in languages in 1
Corinthians <b>14</b></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        a.   Introductory
considerations</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              (1)    Some elements of
the description in 1 Corinthians <b>14</b> either differ from those of Acts <b>2</b>
or involve matters not specified elsewhere. J. Oswald Sanders lists several
differences between Acts <b>2</b> and 1 Corinthians <b>14</b>. Most are readily
answerable; others have more cogency to them.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                                </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                                         Pentecost
                                        Corinth</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                       (a)     all
spoke in tongues (<b>2</b>:4)             not all spoke in tongues </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                                         (<b>12</b>:30)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                       (b)    everyone
understood (<b>2</b>:6)            no one understood (<b>14</b>:2)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                       (c)     tongues
spoken to men (<b>2</b>:6)        spoken to God (<b>14</b>:2)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                       (d)    no
interpreter needed (<b>2</b>:6)           interpreter needed (<b>14</b>:23, 28)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                       (e)     sign to
believers (<b>11</b>:15)               sign to unbelievers (<b>14</b>:22)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                       (f)     strangers
awed (<b>2</b>:7-8)                  think they are mad (<b>14</b>:23)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                       (g)    perfect
harmony (<b>2</b>:1)                   confusion (<b>14</b>:32)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>      </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              (2)    Three
explanations could account for these differences.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                       (a)     <u>A
difference in the circumstance</u>: the hearers in Acts could understand the
languages without supernatural aid, but the hearers in Corinth were having to
listen to people misuse the gift by using it in languages none—or almost
none—of them knew.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                       (b)    <u>A
difference in the nature of the tongues themselves</u></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>  </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                                [1]    Some
commentators view the Corinthian tongues as illegitimate because they were <u>uncontrolled
ecstatic utterance</u> rather than human languages. Paul was content to deal
with the problem pastorally by putting enough regulations on using them that
they could no longer be done unless they were genuine.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                                [2]    Other
writers consider them as structured languages of angels (1 Corinthians <b>13</b>:1).
Most Pentecostals do not believe that the “second kind of tongues” is ecstatic
gibberish or babble. They suppose they are actually verbalizing in something
that may be called a language. So, there are three understandings of speaking
in languages in the New Testament: actual human languages, angelic languages,
and ecstatic utterance. The last two are only theoretically different from each
other.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                       (c)     <u>A
difference in the use of the word </u></span><u><span lang=EL style='font-size:
12.0pt'>γλῶσσα</span></u><u><span style='font-size:12.0pt'> (glōssa):</span></u><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'> in Acts </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>γλῶσσα</span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'> means language, but in 1 Corinthians Paul nuances </span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>γλῶσσα</span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>
to mean a language a hearer does not understand. Combined with </span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>γλῶσσα</span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>, </span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>λαλέω</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:
12.0pt'> </span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>lends itself to this distinction
because it focuses on the speaking in contrast to </span><span lang=EL
style='font-size:12.0pt'>λέγω</span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>, which
focuses more on the form that can carry meaning. So, speaking in a language
contrasts with prophesying as non-communicative speech contrasts with
communicative speech.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                                It is
important to use the English word “language(s)” in the expression <i>“speaking
in languages/a language.”</i> Part of what opens the door to supposing non-language
utterance is that modern English seldom uses <i>tongue</i> to mean “language”; current
usage reserves it for the mouth organ whereas Greek uses </span><span lang=EL
style='font-size:12.0pt'>γλῶσσα</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>for the tongue and for language. <i>Tongues</i> is a
relatively meaningless word, and can get filled with foreign meaning. So, in
the discussion of 1 Corinthians <b>14, we use <i>languages</i> instead of <i>tongues</i>
to illustrate the difference it makes when the no</b>r<b>mal</b> <b>word <i>languages</i>
replaces the ambiguous term<i> tongues</i> </b>throughout, thereby reproducing the
continuity of usage in the Greek text of 1 Corinthians and elsewhere. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              (3)    The method
required: understanding the ambiguous in light of the clear and thereby
shouldering the positive burden of proof</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                       If the
descriptive elements in 1 Corinthians were indecisive as to the nature of that language
speaking, proper procedure would still call for understanding those languages
as actual languages, because Acts<b> 2</b> sets the framework for determining
their nature. As long as other descriptions fit with actual languages, there is
no reason to bring in a second type. The situation is ot so imprecise, however,
because actual languages are evidently in Paul’s mind for the following reasons.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        b.   Arguments for their being
actual human languages, not “angelic” ones</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:135.0pt;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify;text-indent:-117.0pt'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              (1)    <u>The phenomenon
was capable of serving as a sign to unbelievers </u>(<b>14</b>:22; cp. <b>16</b>:17).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                       (a)     If </span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>εἰς</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>
</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>σημεῖον</span><span lang=EL
style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>(<i>eis sēmeion</i>)
means “is of significance/is <u>meaningful</u>” as far as an unbeliever knows,
the languages must be actual languages outsiders could understand. They could
recognize in the message something that addressed the secret yearnings of their
heart (<b>14</b>:25). <i>“Secrets of the heart” </i>could be the matter of a
prophet’s knowing things about the person without being told (cp. John <b>4</b>:19).
More likely the message addressed the yearnings of the human heart and fostered
intuitive acceptance. <i>“That God is surely among you”</i> could compare with <i>“you
are the Son of God”</i> (John </span><b><span style='font-size:12.0pt;
color:windowtext'>2</span></b><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>:47-51; note Mark <b>15</b>:39).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                                The
importance of <i>“sign to <u>unbelievers</u>”</i> lies in their not needing the
gift of interpretation. Unbelievers could not understand if speaking in
languages was speaking in languages of angels or in ecstatic non-language.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                       (b)    If the
sign value is <u>evidential</u> in the sense that a hearer could recognize
something miraculous was happening (people speaking a language they would not know),
the phenomenon would have to be recognizable as supernatural. This means again
that languages would have to be recognizable languages. These “languages” could
have no proof value </span><span style='font-size:12.0pt;color:windowtext'>unless
they were something God would need to enable a person to do </span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>(cp. John <b>3</b>:2). <u>What is not capable of being
verified or falsified is not capable of bearing proof</u>. The nature of languages
here had to be different from what was going on down the road in the mouth of
the priestess of the Delphic oracle or no one could distinguish pagan practice
and divine manifestation. Unbelievers had to be able to appreciate what was
occurring. What occurs in pagan religions and non-religious experimentation cannot
mark Christianity.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                                The
foregoing argument assumes that the sign could mark Christianity in contrast to
demonic and parapsychological phenomena. Perhaps Christianity could be marked
by some trait without being distinctively so marked. Supernatural, for example,
is available through satanic as well as divine sources. Consequently, we could
remember that combinationalism takes evidence as a cluster to create cumulative
effect. We could also remember that scripture often does not distinguish
primary proof and confirmatory evidence. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                                Paul
would still not have in mind <i>“languages of angels” </i>here because no one
has heard <i>“languages of angels”</i> so as to identify something as an
example of them. When the angels appeared to people in biblical times, they
spoke the languages of the people they visited.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                       How can we
explain the combination of ideas that languages are for a sign to unbelievers
and yet unbelievers will think people are crazy if they come in and everybody
is speaking in languages (<b>14</b>:23)? The answer is either that they will
think the people are crazy because everybody is talking at once (chaos), that
the speakers are “out of control,” or that the languages are not being
translated. The likelihood is that these unbelievers will not understand the languages
either.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                       If Jewish
hearers mocked actual languages in Acts<b> 2</b>, pagans would surely have done
the same—or more—with non-languages. What a person mocks is not convincing to
him.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              (2)    <u>Paul is
talking about <i>“sounds in the world”</i></u> (<b>14</b>:7-11), hence, not </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                       that of <i>“angels.”</i></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              (3)    <u>Not knowing
the meaning of the languages would cause a person to be viewed as a “foreigner”</u>
(<b>14</b>:11), not an angel.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              (4)    <u>Paul
considered Isaiah <b>28</b>:11-12 applicable to the Corinthian languages</u> (cp.
Deuteronomy <b>28</b>:49-50; Isaiah <b>66</b>:18; Jeremiah <b>5</b>:15). The
passage deals with foreigners (Assyrians) speaking to Israel (<b>14</b>:21). So,
he is talking about languages of people, not angels.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              (5)    <u>The prophet is
in control of himself</u> (<b>14</b>:32). Consequently, these languages are not
glossolalic expression arising from uncontrollable ecstasy that would cause a
visitor to think the people were crazy.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              (6)    <u>Languages here
are capable of being made plural</u>: note <b>14</b>:5, 18, 21, 22, 23, 39 as
opposed to singulars in <b>14</b>:2, 4, 13, 14, 19, 26?, 27. What can be made
plural must have boundaries; that is, a language must be a system parallel to
other systems or it cannot be made plural. There is no way to make “gibberish”
plural—“gibberishes.” Even if we were to understand <i>“languages of angels”</i>
as other than a hyperbole, the <i>“language<u>s</u>”</i> would have to be
systematic articulations. If language speaking is random sounds, it cannot be made
plural.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              (7)    <u>Languages
could be interpreted</u>, or translated (<b>12</b>:30; <b>14</b>:5, 13, 26,
27), to convey meaning. The terms </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ἑρμηνεύω</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>(Luke <b>24</b>:27;
John <b>1</b>:42; <b>9</b>:7; Hebrews <b>7</b>:2) and </span><span lang=EL
style='font-size:12.0pt'>διερμηνεύω</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>
</span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>(Luke <b>24</b>:27; Acts <b>9</b>:36; 1
Corinthians <b>12</b>:30; 1<b>4</b>:5, 13, 27) mean “to translate,” or with the
latter term “to expound” (Luke <b>24</b>:27). What can be translated must have
meaning to be translated. To have meaning, languages must have structure (even
if they are “languages of angels”); because it is by structure that meaning is
codified and conveyed. No structure, no meaning, no translation. Any phenomenon
that does not have analyzable structure cannot be included under Paul’s usage
of “languages” in this chapter. Languages were able to become prophecy. Languages-unto-prophecy
means sounds with agreed-upon significance (<b>14</b>:7-11). As a result, these
languages in Corinth were not non-linguistic glossolalia.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              (8)    <u>In praying in languages,
<i>you</i> are truly giving thanks all right</u> (<b>14</b>:17). Something is
being communicated by the person speaking. (<i>You</i> is a separate pronoun in
the Greek text, hence, emphatic.)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              (9)    <u>Even if other
persons were </u></span><u><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ἰδιώται</span></u><u><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'> (<i>idiōtai</i>), they would know what you are saying
and could say “Amen” to it</span></u><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>. An “idiotes”
is a layman, a non-specialist, perhaps a person without a gift—like that of
interpretation. The language speakers are saying something. They are using a
sound-symbol system; otherwise, nothing would be “said.” This concept stands
over against something meant by God back to himself without the human
instruments having any idea of what is passing through their voices. That
alternate view pictures language speaking as something where the speakers do
not mean anything because they do not understand what they are saying. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        c.   Arguments posed against
their being actual human languages</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              (1)    They were <i>“languages
of angels.”</i></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  Several considerations make clear that
<i>“languages of angels”</i> in 1 Corinthians<b> 13</b>:1 is hyperbole for
emphasis, not sober literalism.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                       (a)     The
context of 1 Corinthians <b>13</b>:1 is hyperbolic: remove mountains (<b>13</b>:2;
cp. Matthew <b>17</b>:20; <b>21</b>:21 = Mark <b>11</b>:23; Luke <b>17</b>:6),
give my body to be burned (<b>13</b>:3; note textual question <i>“that I might
be proud”</i>), know all mysteries and all knowledge (<b>13</b>:2), give all my
goods to feed the poor (<b>13</b>:3). A similar heightened expression occurs in
Galatians <b>1</b>:8-9: <i>“Though an angel from heaven should preach any other
gospel . . . let him be accursed.”</i></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                       (b)    Angels
are spirits, who do not have flesh and bones (Luke <b>24</b>:3; Hebrews <b>l</b>:4,
14); consequently, in their own natural form of being, they have no physical tongues
to make sounds with; hence, angels do not communicate by physical means. <i>“The
word spoken by angels”</i> (Hebrews <b>2</b>:2) happened in times of
manifestation to human beings, when the angel entered the material sphere. In
such cases the angels were speaking in the languages of people.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                                The
idea that angels communicate, though true, is not enough. For angelic languages
to explain human glossolalia, angels would have to communicate in their own
realm in a way that could be duplicated by flesh-and-bones people. That is what
we have no basis for saying they do because they are not physical beings.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                       (c)     Such a
view commits a person to the idea that there is atmosphere in heaven for
physical languages to make sounds with.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                       (d)    Paul
identifies languages with the temporary and temporal matters of this life in
contrast to love, which never falls; languages will not be part of “the perfect”
(<b>13</b>:8). Languages of angels cannot be literal; otherwise, they would be
in this life (the “in-part” situation) <u>and</u> in the hereafter (the
perfect). But the “in-part” will be done away when “the perfect” comes. In the
face-to-face relationship, we will be with angels and like them—spiritual; so
we cannot now be receiving something angels do not have.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                       (e)     Languages
of angels could not have evidential force. But Paul teaches in <b>14</b>:23
that languages are a sign, or “sign-ificant,” to unbelievers. How would people
ever know that what they witnessed or personally experienced were languages of
angels unless they had previously known what such a thing was like? And if angels’
realm differs from ours, how would a human person <u>ever</u> find out what
their languages are like? </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                       (f)     Languages
of angels would have to have structure to convey meaning. Language is a
sound-symbol system that relies on form to represent meaning. Linguistic
analysis can determine whether an utterance has linguisticality.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                       (g)    Those
who followed the apostles were not linguists or psychoanalists who could verify
scientifically that they were dealing with an actual specimen of language. They
had to recognize it as language in some down-to-earth way. They could do that
if they knew the language they were hearing and could tell that these people
were not native speakers of it. For that reason, in today’s situation languages
cannot be ancient human languages now dead or a human language from some
obscure tribal people no one here knows. The typical “person on the street”
could not render a verdict in such cases; so they could have no evidential
force for him. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                       (h)    There is
no reason to believe that there was a confusion of languages among the angels
as was the case among men. If not for some punitive reason angelic languages
were confused, we wonder why they would differ from one another?</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              (2)    <u>Languages are
addressed to God</u> (<b>14</b>:2, 28) and to oneself (<b>14</b>:28; cp. <b>14</b>:4).
Some have maintained that 1 Corinthians <b>14</b> refers to prayer language
because of <b>14</b>:2, 14-19, 28. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                       (a)     More
strictly, the text says, <i>“Those who speak in languages speak to God.”</i> “Address”
implies intentionally directed speech, something not evident in the verse. Languages
were not directed to God intentionally as shown by the following points. The
marks of human language have been noted. <i>“Speak to oneself” </i>might be
hyperbole, though doubtful. That <i>“speaking to oneself”</i> (<b>14</b>:28b)
can occur while people are keeping silent (<b>14</b>:28a). They are verbalizing
to themselves and, as we say, praying silently. Meditation and communion would
both fit with silently speaking to oneself. Neither are closely tied to
specific wording.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                       (b)    The
reason the language speaker speaks <u>to God</u> is <i>“because human beings
understand.”</i> Speaking to God is by default, not intent. God is the only one
left who understands. The absolute “no one” would not have to include those
with the gift of interpretation or those with the language as their native language.
Since it cannot be really absolute, it may be wise even to suppose that a few
speakers of a language were present on occasion. Perhaps this simultaneous language
speaking got started in Corinth as a way to communicate to small pockets of
foreign-language speakers that showed up at the services. Paul would then have been
telling language speakers to translate so <u>everyone</u> could profit. For all
practical purposes, no one was understanding.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                       (c)     What
sense does it make for God to talk to himself? If speakers in languages have no
idea what they are saying and if the Spirit of God is giving them a message
appropriate for <u>listeners,</u> what intentional purpose could it serve for
God? <i>“<u>You</u> are giving thanks all right” </i>and <i>“blessing”</i> (<b>14</b>:17);
if so, God is not the originator of the message. If speakers have no idea what they
are saying, they are not saying it. Having God talking to himself removes it
from being an interpersonal experience, and it breaks the principle that
miraculous gifts are intended for other than the ones who have them.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                                If
spiritual gifts are not for the benefit of the person who has them, languages are
not for the speaker’s benefit. As a counter comment, the statements in <b>14</b>:4,
28 are raised. <i>“They who speak in a language edify themselves”</i> (<b>14</b>:4)
is true when the communication loop is completed (as when interpretation takes
place, the hearer knows the language, the language speaker has also the gift of
interpretation or understands the flow of thought—just not the word-by-word
level). The <u>intention</u> of languages is not to edify oneself, but to edify
others; however, when communication to others does not take place, the <u>result</u>
is that only the speaker is edified, and the speaker ends up speaking only to
God (<b>14</b>:2). The purpose in such cases differs from the result.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                       (d)    In
regard to speaking <u>to oneself</u>, <b>14</b>:4, 28 are sometimes taken as
indicating a second kind of languages (<i>“languages of angels”</i>) to be used
in a different setting. These languages are for <u>private</u> use in address
to <u>God</u> as in prayer.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                                Paul
does not say in <b>14</b>:28 that the ones who speak to themselves do so in a language.
They may be running the truths through their mind so that they edify themselves
or praise God, as the case may be. In <b>14</b>:4 the speaking to God and the
edifying of self comes while using languages out loud. In <b>14</b>:28 it comes
in people speaking to themselves. Whether aloud or silently, and whether in a language
or not, the effects are the same. (See comments below under <i>“the speakers do
not understand what they are saying.”</i>)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                                Since language
speaking is a communication gift, it does not necessarily involve revelation as
well. The message of the ones who <i>“speaks to themselves and to God” </i>are most
likely message originating with themselves, not God</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                                In the
earlier part of the chapter, Paul may have in mind people that have the gift of
interpretation as well as languages, and so they edify themselves (<b>14</b>:4)
because they are doing for themselves what Paul urges them to do with the
listener in <b>14</b>:6—interpret what they say. Later Paul recommends praying
for the gift of interpretation if the speaker in a language does <u>not</u>
have it. More likely, however, is the proposal that language speakers would be
edified by the ideas they had in mind, ideas that speaking in languages enabled
them to communicate to others in a language they as speakers did not know. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                                As to <u>private
usage</u>, Paul deals with a situation occurring “in church.” In so doing, he
does not say to the Corinthians that they are using this gift in the wrong
place and should do it at home or alone. Instead, he regulates it as something
that can take place in the gathered community. On other occasions involving
public <i>vs.</i> private, he comes right out and tells them to eat at home (1
Corinthians <b>11</b>:20-22) or raise questions at home (1 Corinthians <b>14</b>:35)
instead of abusing each other and creating disturbance during fellowship and
worship.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                                <u>In
church</u> (<b>14</b>:18-19) does not have to contrast with <u>in private</u>. It
can contrast with <u>in evangelism</u>. The point would be that languages work
well in proclaiming the gospel in cross-cultural settings as a reversal of the
tower of Babel. In a cosmopolitan city like Corinth, occasions for using that
gift might arise often. They could be adapted to worship settings with a mixed
audience, but normally that would not be necessary; gathered worshipers tend to
be local and understand the same language. It is not possible to maintain the
proposition that languages were intended purely for evangelistic purposes since
Acts demonstrates their occurrence in cases where brand new converts spoke in languages
aside from any evangelistic effort. But then again, it is not possible to
maintain that languages were for private use either. The use of languages in
places that did not call for them and in ways that were counter-productive was
prompted by desires inappropriate to Christian service. People’s not
interpreting when they could shows the motivation behind this language
speaking. In Corinth, language speaking seems to have degenerated into
attention-getting behavior in place of benefit to others or glory to God.       </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                                At any
rate, even if “in church” contrasted with “in private,” we do not have to have
a second type of language. Speaking in a real language in private would
likewise be possible.    </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                       (e)     <i>Languages</i>
is used alternative to <i>prophecy</i> because there is no communication going
on if the hearer cannot understand them. If the audience comprehends as at
Pentecost or when interpretation is given (<b>14</b>:5), then languages become
prophecy. The </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;
margin-bottom:0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:center'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'><img width=253 height=52 id="Picture 1"
src="/images/essays/supernatural-products-of-hs-book-pt-7-tongues-1/image002.png"></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>relationship between the terms changes from separate
circles to one within another. Speaking in languages is a speaking gift, not a
content gift. It does not necessarily convey a message—prophecy—if the hearers
do not understand it.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        (3) <u>Someone unlearned or
unbelieving will think you are crazy</u> (<b>14</b>:23).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              This observation does
not show they were not real languages. A similar reaction came from skeptical
people in Acts<b> 2</b>, where actual languages were spoken. Skeptics accused
them of being <u>drunk</u>, which is comparable to being crazy. In another
incident, Festus told Paul he was crazy even when Festus knew the language
(Acts <b>26</b>:24). The Pharisees accused Jesus of being a demonic Samaritan
when they could not understand what he was telling them in their own language
(John <b>8</b>:48).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        (4) <u>Speakers do not
understand what they are saying</u> (<b>14</b>:14). In <b>14</b>:2 the apostle
says that no one understands. In <b>14</b>:14 he adds, <i>“If I am praying in a
language, my spirit is praying, but my understanding is not fruitful.”</i> Paul
would pray with his understanding also (<b>14</b>:15, 19). These verses need
not be taken to mean that speakers have no idea what they are saying, with the
inference that what they are saying comes from God. The following
considerations provide an alternative reconstruction of this matter.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              By “understand” Paul
means the element-by-element, word-for-word level, not the conceptual flow of
thought. Understanding always occurs at the conceptual level in language
speaking, but not necessarily at the word level. It is <u>language</u>, not <u>concept</u>,
that occurs in speaking in languages and interpretating languages. We take it
that Paul refers to the conceptual level when he uses the word “spirit.” It is
“spirit” relative to “words” much as Paul speaks of the “spirit” of the Law
relative to the letter of the Law, that is, its intended purpose (Romans <b>2</b>:29).
In like manner, he refers to the word level when he says that the <u>understanding,</u>
or mind<u>,</u> is unfruitful (<b>14</b>:14-19). Revelation is <u>concept</u>; languages
and interpretation are matters of <u>language</u>, the verbiage by which we
convey concepts.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              The text does not say
that Paul does not understand when he speaks in a language. Since speaking in languages
is a language gift rather than a revelation or content gift, he surely means
that he did not understand the language, not that he did not understand the
line of thought.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              Paul goes on say that
the hearers cannot say “Amen” to a prayer spoken in a language because <i>“they
do not know what you are saying.”</i> Indeed, how can “you” say “Amen” to your
own prayer or even be said to be praying? It is no good to say, <i>“I agree
with the spirit/feel/attitude of the prayer”</i>; the one occupying the place
of the “idiotes” could say <u>that</u>. If language speaking should not be done
when the “<u>other</u>” has no understanding, speaking in languages in prayer
to God should not be done when the <u>speaker</u> does not understand, which
would amount to prohibiting speaking in languages; but Paul does not do that.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              (a)     You are giving
thanks, all right, but the other is not edified. It is doubtful how proper it
would be to speak of giving thanks if language speakers did not understand that
that they were giving thanks—if they did not understand at all what they were “saying.”
What point would there be in prayer if the speaker was not offering the
content? Perhaps it is imaginable how people proclaiming to other people would
be doing something real and beneficial even though they themselves had no idea
what God was saying through them. It would at least be communication. But in
the case of prayer, the speaker and the hearer would be the same person: God. First,
what sense would it make for God to talk to himself? At least what sense would
it make to call it “communication”? Second, what would the text mean by saying
that the <u>speaker</u> is giving thanks (cp. “magnifying God,” Acts<b> 10</b>:46)?
The point here is reinforced by the express use of the pronoun with the verb
“thank”: <i>“<u>You</u> are giving thanks all right, but the <u>other</u> is
not edified.” </i>If the pronoun <i>“you”</i> (</span><span lang=EL
style='font-size:12.0pt'>σύ</span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>) contrasts
with <i>“other”</i> (</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ἕτερος</span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>), it stresses the fact that <u>you</u> does
understand the speaking</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              (b)    Edification does
occur despite not “understanding.” Edification is not simply affective
exhilaration, but cognitive “eye-opening.” So, edification must be
understanding at a different level. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                       If <i>“edifies themselves”</i>
in <b>14</b>:4 meant affective uplift, the persons nearby would also be edified
by knowing that the supernatural hand of God was penetrating the world right
beside them; yet <b>14</b>:17 says that the hearers are not edified. In <b>14</b>:26
edification comes from psalms, teaching, revelation, languages, interpretation.
Although singing might involve the emotional side, the emphasis is on cognitive
forms of communication. Edifying others happens when languages, by translation,
become prophecy (<b>14</b>:5). Other people in <b>14</b>:17 are not edified,
because they do not know what you are saying. The intended use of spiritual
gifts is edification (<b>14</b>:12).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              (c)     Paul contrasts
speaking in languages with speaking <u>five words</u> with his understanding. He
thereby seems to contrast the language-speaking type of understanding with the
word level communication.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              (d)    <u>Singing</u>
with the spirit (<b>14</b>:15) is the word </span><span lang=EL
style='font-size:12.0pt'>ψάλλω</span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'> (<i>psallō</i>),
<i>“to sing a psalm</i>,<i>”</i> not </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:
12.0pt'>ἄδω</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>(<i>adō</i>), <i>“to praise”</i> (Ephesians <b>5</b>:19a),
or </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>αἰνέω</span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'> (<i>aineō</i>), <i>“to sing”</i> (Luke <b>2</b>:13). It
is even used in Ephesians <b>5</b>:19b in apparent contrast to </span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ἄδω</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>
</span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>(<i>“to sing”</i>)<i>. </i></span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>Ψάλλω</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:
12.0pt'> </span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>most likely means <i>“singing
psalms.”</i> If so, a set and known content is involved. Secondly, </span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>τῷ</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>
</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>πνεύματι</span><span lang=EL
style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>(<i>tōi pneumati</i>,
<i>“with the spirit”</i>) is comparable to </span><span lang=EL
style='font-size:12.0pt'>τῇ</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>καρδίᾳ</span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'> (<i>tēi
kardiāi</i>, <i>“with the heart”</i>) in Ephesians <b>5</b>:19. Finally, <i>“singing
in the spirit”</i> may not be singing in languages.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              (e)     <u>Except they
interpret/translate</u> (<b>14</b>:5) may mean, not that they have also the
gift of interpretation (contrast above), but that they are explaining the message,
which they know at the general level. The gift of interpretation would be for
interpreting someone else’s language. <u>Praying to interpret</u> their own language
speaking (<b>14</b>:13) would presumably be so they could express cogently in their
own language the word-for-word presentation they just spoke in a language. Another
possibility is that in the case where content is known, “interpret” would be
the ability to <u>explain the meaning</u> rather than <u>translate the language</u>.
“Interpret” (</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ἑρμηνεύω</span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>, <i>hermēneuō</i>) can mean either to translate from
one language to another or to explain something differently in the same
language.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              (f)     Nowhere in
Corinthians or elsewhere does scripture indicate that speaking in languages
necessarily involves a revelation of what is said in languages. In fact, <b>14</b>:26
distinguishes the two gifts. Of course, were a person to have both gifts, they could
be combined on an occasion such as Pentecost (note also <b>14</b>:30).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              (g).   <i>“Speaking
mysteries”</i> (<b>14</b>:2) does not have to mean <i>“hidden things” </i>to
the <u>speaker</u> or the <u>whole world</u>. They were mysteries to <u>listeners</u>
who did not understand the words spoken.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              (h)    In witnessing or in
teaching believers, how could a language speakers field questions about their
message if they had no idea what they had just said? On Pentecost Peter was
speaking in languages when he preached what Luke recorded. How would he understand
the audience’s question when they responded to his presentation? Even worse, he
would not know what answer the Holy Spirit was giving them. It would apparently
be a surprise to him when they come to him, wanting him to baptize them. Similarly
in Corinth, how would speakers answer questions about what they had just said (<b>14</b>:35)?</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              (i)     In prayer
especially, if speakers have no idea what they are saying, we say again that
God would simply be talking to himself.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  Even if this alternate
reconstruction does not commend itself as <u>the</u> point, it is satisfactory
enough to indicate another way to read the text. If so, the <i>“not understand”</i>
factor falls out as a distinctive mark for a second type of language speaking
found at Corinth.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        In summary, we offer a listing
of approaches to the statement that in prayer the language speakers’ spirit is
praying while their understanding is not: (a) idea level <i>vs.</i> verbal
level, as advocated above; (b) emotional level <i>vs.</i> rational level; (c)
enthusiasm <i>vs.</i> content; and (d) intention <i>vs.</i> result.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        (5) <u>The languages are “unknown</u>”
(<b>14</b>:2).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              The word “unknown” in
the Authorized Version does not appear in the original text. Even if it did, it
would not mean the language was unknown to mankind and so be angelic utterance.
It could be unknown to the speaker even as “new languages” (Mark <b>16</b>:17)
can be new to the speaker rather than to mankind. Similarly, the <i>“other languages”</i>
(Acts <b>2</b>:4; 1 Corinthians<b> 14</b>:21) are other than what the speaker
used normally.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        Our summary comments on
proposals for a second type of languages are these: scriptural statements are
being accommodated to the experience of people who have done what they suppose
is speaking in a language, “speaking in tongues,” as they term it. Since they
did not understand what they were saying, they suppose <b>14:</b>14 means that.
Since the sounds they made were not understandable to anybody around them, they
suppose that unknown languages in<b> 14</b>:2 (KJV) means unknown to mankind,
and so on. The experience is providing the precise information for understanding
the wording of the text. The assumption is that their experience is what this
text is talking about. Since that is not necessarily the case, the
interpretation is not really an interpretation of the text.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  d.   Summary of main points of
interpretation in 1 Corinthians <b>14</b></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:1.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify;text-indent:-1.25in'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        (1) Languages speak to God
because no <u>human being</u> understands; they speak to God by default.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:22.5pt;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify;text-indent:-31.5pt'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                 (2) The speaker does
not understand at the element-by-element level, but knows at the conceptual
level. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        (3) The language speakers are
accused of being crazy because the hearers cannot understand the human language
they are speaking—or the speakers are all talking at once.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        (4) The language speakers edify
themselves by conceptual edification.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        (5) They speak mysteries as
far as the audience is concerned.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        (6) <i>“Languages of angels” </i>is
exaggeration for emphasis.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            6.   Ephesians <b>5</b>:18</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  The idea sometimes taken from this
text is that being filled with the Spirit unto speaking in languages is
comparable to being filled with wine. The charge of drunkenness in Acts <b>2</b>
supposedly reinforces the point along with the mention of madness in 1
Corinthians <b>14</b>:23.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  Paul does not mention languages in
this context. Even so, it is not at all necessary to follow the above line of
reasoning any more than it is to make thanksgiving comparable to levity, silly
talk, and filthiness because they appear next to each other in Ephesians <b>5</b>:4.
Understanding contrasts to, not compares with, foolishness in Ephesians <b>5</b>:17.
Likewise, drunkenness replaces or contrasts to, not compares with, being filled
with the Spirit in the next verse. Instead of drunkenness there is sobriety. People
with the Spirit who are speaking in languages are in control of the Spirit’s
operation through them 1 Corinthians <b>14</b>:23.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  7.   Romans <b>8</b>:26-27 says that
the Spirit makes intercession for us with groanings </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        that cannot be uttered.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        Again, speaking in languages is
not part of the context. Paul speaks of groanings that can<u>not</u> be
uttered, but according to this proposal they would be “uttered” by using
angelic language. The passage deals with intercession by the Spirit, not
endowment by the Spirit. The point of the verse is that we do not have to rely
on the adequacy of wording our feelings and intentions, because the Spirit sees
that our real intents “get through.” Admittedly, human language is somewhat
inadequate to express some things, particularly inner feelings and abstract
ideas, but non-language or angelic language would be worse, not better, because
such a thing would not express anything at all from the person. Besides, Paul
would rather speak five words with understanding than five thousand without
understanding, presumably because speaking those five words would be better.
Private use of languages in a “prayer language” would not be better because the
understanding would not be involved in the manner Paul means in 1 Corinthians <b>14</b>:19.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        Even if people were to agree
that on occasion language simply fails us—as with the utterance of pain, they
hardly thereby agree that language-speaking as non-language is then the case or
that non-language is better than language. People do not have to have a
supernatural gift to express “groanings.”</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                                Summary
of Characteristics of “Languages”</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  1.   Languages are actual human
languages (xenoglossia).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  2.   Speakers control <u>when</u> they
use the gift.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  3.   Speakers are in control of themselves
<u>while</u> they use the gift.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  4.   They understand conceptually
what they are saying while they are using the </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        gift.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  5.   The gift of languages is a
supernatural gift. It is not “primed” into existence. A person does not just
decide to do it; it is a gift. Being emotionally “driven” does not enhance the
coming of the gift.               </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  6.   Speaker evidently can match the
language of the ones they address.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  7.   Speaking in languages is a
language-speaking ability; it does not necessarily involve a revealed content
as well.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  8.   Language speaking is such that
it can serve as a sign to unbelievers. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>
