<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:center'><b><span
style='font-size:14.0pt'>TESTING CHRISTIAN EXPERIENCE</span></b></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><b><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></b></p>

<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:center'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>Virgil Warren, PhD</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><b><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></b></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><b><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></b></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><b><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>I. Pattern for Testing Christian Experience</span></b></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><b><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></b></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  In our estimation, <i>scripture does
not clearly teach any exact pattern of supernatural experience during the
church era</i>. The New Testament does not say that (1) such experiences would
cease with the apostles, that (2) they would continue among true believers,
that (3) they would come as “latter rain” after centuries of drought, or that
(4) they would manifest themselves any time the gospel penetrates a new field. There
is no basis for thinking <i>“the normal Christian experience”</i> involves
supernatural experiences. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><b><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            </span></b><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>A.
Take a first-century viewpoint and apply tests.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  We do well to <i>take a
first-century viewpoint and apply tests </i>(1 John<b> 4</b>:1-4).  That keeps
us from unbounded subjectivism or a spirit of denial. It makes us cautious but
not obscurantist. First-century Christians had to test genuine Christian
experience against competing phenomena. Taking a first-century viewpoint and
applying tests sidesteps the moot question about New Testament teaching on the
duration of supernatural experience.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  The question is not whether
supernatural experiences occur, but what we make of our experiences. The issue
is not <i>the raw experience</i> but our <u>interpretation</u> of <i>what it is</i>,
our <u>explanation</u> of <i>what caused it</i>, and our <u>inferences</u>
about <i>what to make of it</i>. In the modern Christian scene, the problem is
not so much <i>deliberate misleading</i> as it is <i>sincere misreading </i>that
ends up misleading. It is not so often bad motives as it is misunderstanding
scripture and misinterpreting experience. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            B. Use objective tests for subjective
experiences.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  Gideon used objective <u>signs</u>
with cakes and chevron stew to verify that <i>“it is you that are talking with
me”</i> (Judges <b>6</b>:17). On the road to Damascus, Paul <u>asked for
identification</u>, evidently because God does not let other entities claim to
be God’s agents (Acts <b>9</b>:5; <b>22</b>:8; <b>26</b>:15).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  <u>Let other people help</u> us test
our experiences. Other people have not had our experience, but that does not
mean we alone can test it. The crux of the matter is identifying our experience
with <u>the word</u>. Determining what certain biblical examples were like is
an exegetical matter, and other people stand on equal footing with us in that
regard. They cannot deny we had an experience, but they can discern whether it
should be equated with New Testament precedents. In some ways, it is hard for us
to be “objective” about our own experiences.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><b><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>II. Tests for Genuine Christian Experience</span></b></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            A. Definitional test</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  Before anything else, we need to
determine what certain Christian experiences really were. <u>Revelation</u> was
more than an idea coming forcibly to mind. <u>Prophesying</u> was more than “for-telling”
what spontaneously struck someone. Some Christians apparently suppose that
since they have God’s Spirit, he has planted ideas that come to mind on
Christian matters, especially if they are new to them, if they are reading the
Bible and praying, if they have first asked God to show them how to understand
or what to do. That procedure may be nothing more than supernaturalizing what “pops
into my head” from the “tacit dimension.” The origin of an idea is hard to
account for, but we do not identify the Spirit with the creative urge that is
part of being created in the Creator’s image. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  For me to suppose that my experience
I have had a divinely originated experience, I must distinguish it from
experiences that people have on matters that are not even religious or
Christian. That responsibility lies with me because other people have not had
my experience, so they cannot analyze its aspects.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  <u>Speaking in languages</u> means
speaking in human languages (xenoglossia), not ecstatic utterance
(glossolalia). Paul evidently uses <i>“languages of angels”</i> (1 Corinthians <b>13</b>:1)
as an exaggeration for emphasis akin to other heightened expressions nearby: <i>“move
mountains</i>,<i>” “give my body to be burned [note textual issue]</i>,<i>” “know
all mysteries and have all knowledge</i>,<i>” “give all my belongings to feed
the poor”</i> (<b>13</b>:2-3). <b>(a)</b> To the Galatians Paul makes another
hyperbolic statement about angels: <i>“If an angel from heaven should preach to
you anything else . . . let him be accursed” </i>(<b>1</b>:8-9). <b>(b)</b> Angels
are spirits, who do not have tongues to make audible languages (Luke <b>24</b>:3;
Hebrews <b>1</b>:4, 14). Languages are sound-symbol systems would mean the
physicality both of angels and their realm. Besides, <b>(c) </b>if we have
never heard angels speak, we cannot identify our verbal experience with their
speaking. Finally, (d) even angelic languages would have structure to convey
meaning. The nature of <u>interpretation of languages</u> follows from the
nature of languages and the nature of revelation.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  New Testament material on <u>baptism
in the Holy Spirit</u> does not seem to indicate what Pentecostals take it to
have been in the apostolic setting. The issue is too complex to treat here, but
we can stress a point about procedure. We cannot settle the experiential
question without first settling the exegetical one; otherwise, we let personal
experience dictate doctrine. On this question, the issue between Pentecostals
and non-Pentecostals is not that one group believes the Bible and the other one
believes experience, or that one wants the Spirit and the other does not. The
problem is identifying experience with revelation. For our part, we think <i>“baptism
in the Spirit”</i> is an alternate expression for <i>“gift of the Spirit,”</i>
normally meaning indwelling presence. It is simply and significantly
relationship with God the Spirit. Any supernatural manifestation associated
with baptism in the Spirit is a non-uniform effect pursuant to the relationship
itself.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  <u>Being slain in the spirit</u> comes
from misunderstanding Paul’s Damascus road encounter. The phenomenon accommodates
scripture to experiences that are unidentifiable as to cause and pointless as
to effect.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  <u>Dreams, visions, and trances</u>
recur in both testaments. The previous items involve more prominently
interpreting the New Testament as to what the <i>experience </i>was. These
items involve interpreting <i>individual cases of the experience </i>to make
sure they are a divine variety of these things.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            B. Doctrinal test</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  Since God is consistent, anything he
reveals later fits with what he revealed before, especially in successful confrontation
with opposing agencies. No experience can authenticate a doctrine or practice that
is at odds with God’s revelation (Deuteronomy <b>13</b>:1-5; John <b>16</b>:13-15;
Galatians<b> 1</b>:8-9; Hebrews <b>9</b>:26-28; 1 Peter <b>3</b>:18; Jude 3).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  There are some cautions here. (a)
The doctrinal test assumes <u>correct understanding</u> of previous revelation.
(b) The doctrinal test applies only when there has been <u>previous revelation
on this matter</u>. (c) The doctrinal test assumes that the items on which
someone else is in error are <u>sufficiently important</u> that God would take
his Spirit from someone.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            C. Moral test (John <b>9</b>:31, 33; Mark <b>9</b>:39)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  The moral test does not require
moral perfection before we can have clear supernatural experiences from God. Jesus
observed that it is not natural for us to speak against God soon after being someone
God relates to in a special way.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            D. Practical tests</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  1. Are we in control of ourselves (1
Corinthians <b>14</b>:32)? The Spirit gives control; he does not throw us out
of control.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  2. Do our experiences draw attention
to us (2 Corinthians <b>2</b>:7-9; Matthew <b>4</b>:3-4) more than glorify God
(Matthew <b>5</b>:16; <b>9</b>:8; <b>15</b>:31; Luke <b>7</b>:16;<b> 13</b>:13;
<b>17</b>:15; Acts<b> 4</b>:12; <b>21</b>:17-20) or benefit other people? Do we
sense a desire for attention? Insofar as it is perceivable to other people, do
our experiences border on exhibitionism? Are they for making money (Acts<b> 3</b>:6;
<b>8</b>:18-24; <b>16</b>:16-18)?</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  3. What are the results of our
experiences? Do they always succeed (Deuteronomy <b>18</b>:20-22)? Do they
stand the test of time?         </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            E. Control test</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  What is not distinctive to
Christianity cannot distinguish Christian experience. Many experiences found in
Christianity happen outside it as well. We must assume either that the beliefs
involved are not very important or that the experiences are not distinctively
Christian (hence not supernatural?). Conversely, we must differentiate the true
from the fake in these other arenas. What looks like our own experience may not
be the same; it may intend to deceive the very elect.                                                                                    </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  Testing and evaluating must balance
healthy caution with willing openness. We do not want to be so gullible that we
believe every experience or so skeptical we could never be corrected. Because
we want certainty and security, we may be prone to accept any experience that
looks close enough; because we fear excesses, we may overreact against anything
special. Discernment and caution must prevail.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><b><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>III. Alternatives to Genuine Christian Experience</span></b></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            The Holy Spirit does not correspond with
the subjective, the affective (feelings, emotions), the intuitive, the
mysterious, or the miraculous. He is a person, so his guidance and empowerment
occur primarily through interpersonal means. The alternatives to a (A) <u>divine
reconstruction</u> of our experiences are frequently impersonal.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            B. Psychological </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  Here belong <i>psychosomatic
phenomena</i> that illustrate the impact of mind on body and <i>vice versa</i>.
<i>Altered states of consciousness</i> involve curious subjective experiences. <i>Illusion
</i>and <i>warped perception </i>do not compare with Holy Spirit phenomena, but
to the person with mental issues they may not be distinguishable. <i>Hallucinations</i>
are not to be confused with visions, seizures with baptism in the Spirit or
being <i>“slain in the Spirit,”</i> catatonic states with trances, <i>etc</i>.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            C. Paranormal</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  The word “paranormal” refers to
phenomena that psychic researchers study: telekinesis, precognition, telepathy,
apports, materialization, clairvoyance, clairaudience,
extra-bodily-experiences, hypnotism, poltergeist phenomena, psychometry (See,
for example, E. Garth Moore. <i>Try the Spirits: Christianity and Psychical
Research. </i>N. Y.: Oxford University Press, l977). Paranormal may not differ
from demonic or psychological, but theoretically it may. Some people can do
other people cannot. Since humans have them, they are not supernatural; they
are “normal.” Since not all human persons have them, they are “<i>para-</i>normal”:
perfect pitch, total permanent recall, abilities available under hypnosis, <i>etc</i>.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  We need to know what happens outside
the Christian setting. Literature about <u>psychological and psychic research</u>
challenges us to distinguish those things from special Christian experience. Most
of us do not realize the range of human potential and natural possibility,
which sets us up for supernaturalizing our experiences. The medical profession
continues to gain appreciation for <u>mind-body interdependency</u> and the
extent of control mind can exert over body. Curious experiences have occurred
in Roman Catholicism, pagan religions, New Age circles, Satanism, Hinduism,
Buddhism, and so on. We wonder whether these are unusual natural phenomena that
people, when they experience them, interpret in terms of their differing world
views. If so, such occurrences no more mark Christian experience than they
legitimize any other.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            D.  Demonic </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  Appealing to the demonic to explain
Christian experiences is questionable. Someone with the promise not to be snatched
out of Christ’s hand (John <b>8</b>:28; <b>10</b>:28-29) would not likely be
oppressed or possessed by demonic agencies. But Christians subject themselves
to that danger in efforts at worship that try to get rid of personal identity,
to become zero, to let go of themselves. Such efforts belong more to Eastern
mysticism than Christian worship. When people create a vacuum by removing their
personal wills, they invite whatever is “out there” to come in—demons, “extra-terrestrials.”
They leave the interpersonal realm of Christianity and relationship to God by
losing touch with their personal identity.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            <i>Tests eliminate certain phenomena</i>
from being divine or at least distinctively so. Tests do not identify which
alternative a case is, and they do not need to. The concern is to avoid <i>mis</i>identifying
what we experience.<i> Tests keep us from supernaturalizing our experiences.</i>
They keep us from misreading what may be common to people simply as human
beings. <i>Tests keep us from confusing divine and demonic</i>. The underlying
test is the interpersonal one.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            <b>If we cannot distinguish an experience
from other possibilities, we do not consider it divine</b>. Don’t strain to be
a <i>prophet</i> because you think your situation needs one.  Don’t try to add
authority to your ideas by saying, <i>“God told me.”</i> Don’t require <i>God’s
special leading</i> because you want special help, and don’t require God’s
special leading to be <i>knowable special leading</i>. Don’t require more <i>certainty</i>
than God intends to provide. Take ambiguous experiences as data to be
considered rather than as signs to be followed. Affirming the lesser claim is
the safer policy.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            In summary, past objective revelation <i>tests</i>
current objective revelation. Objective criteria <i>test</i> subjective
experience, and subjective experience <i>confirms</i> objective revelation. <i>New</i>
revelation and special divine guidance must be distinguishable from alternatives.
Normal and normative subjective Christian experience is interpersonal, not impersonal.
Experiences are not ends in themselves; they are given for interpersonal
purposes beyond themselves in evangelism and edification. </span></p>
