<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>II. Observations on the Data</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  A.  Except for Ephesians<b> 5</b>:18,
the filling imagery for the Spirit occurs only in Lucan materials. Luke is
virtually alone in using the expression <i>“filled with the Spirit.”</i> Paul
is alone in using the expression <i>“earnest</i> (and “seal”) <i>of the Spirit.”
</i>John the Baptist is alone in using the term <i>“baptism in the Holy Spirit.”</i>
Terminology peculiar to one writer does not likely refer to something
distinctive. He is not likely the only writer to be talking about that part of
the picture. Other writers probably talk about the same thing with different
word pictures. So, we do not look for a different thing every time we see a
different expression. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  B.  Except in Luke<b> 1</b>:15, </span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>πίμπλημι</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:
12.0pt'> </span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>refers to an on-the-spot drive
to action.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  C.  </span><span lang=EL
style='font-size:12.0pt'>Πληρόω</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>(<i>plēróō</i>) connotes a more stative idea, although
Acts <b>5</b>:3 may be a </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        response to a situation.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  D.  Ephesians <b>5</b>:18, being
present imperative, is a characteristic filling rather than a special one. This
passage is the only one in the imperative. Since a person can be commanded to
be filled, the person determines whether it occurs.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        Ephesians <b>5</b>:18 should
not be taken to mean that being filled with the Spirit is comparable to being
drunk. In fact, being filled with the Spirit and being drunk are
opposites—replacing one another—as in <b>5</b>:4, <i>“Let there be no
filthiness, nor silly talk, nor levity (which are not fitting), but instead let
there be thanksgiving.”</i></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  E.   There is seemingly a conceptual
connection between indwelling and filling in the characteristic sense. The
first pictures ongoing personal association; the second pictures that
association as complete—“full.” “Being filled the Spirit” connects the imagery
with actions prompted by that complete “filling.”</span></p>
