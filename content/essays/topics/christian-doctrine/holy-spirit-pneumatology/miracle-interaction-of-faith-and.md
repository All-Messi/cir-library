<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:center'><b><span
style='font-size:14.0pt'>THE INTERACTION OF FAITH AND MIRACLE</span></b></p>

<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:center'><b><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></b></p>

<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:center'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>Virgil Warren, PhD</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            <i>The distinctive purpose of New
Testament miracles was to <u>enhance faith</u></i>. That contrasts with doing
miracles to <u>entertain</u>, to <u>satisfy curiosity</u> (cp. Herod in Luke <b>23</b>:8-9),
to <u>escape limitations</u> in the natural order, to <u>make money</u>, to <u>get
attention</u>, or even to <u>heal everyone</u>. Jesus cursed the fig tree
(Matthew <b>21</b>:18-22 = Mark <b>11</b>:12-14, 20-26) to <u>teach</u> his
disciples a valuable lesson. Miracles were not sheer displays of supernatural
power, but manifestations that verified Jesus’ proclamation and claims.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            New Testament miracles were for <i>enhancing
faith in Jesus of Nazareth as the promised Messiah</i>. That Messianic purpose was
relatively specific within the general purpose of miracles, which were for
proving the claim of the miracle worker whatever it was. Jesus used the healing
of a paralytic to confirm his claim to forgive sins (Mark <b>2</b>:1-12). He
appealed to his coming resurrection to prove his right to expel moneychangers
from the temple (John <b>2</b>:13-22). He used the predicted resurrection as the
“sign” of his messiahship (Matthew <b>12</b>:38-40 = Luke <b>11</b>:29-30;
Matthew <b>16</b>:4; cp. Romans <b>1</b>:1-4, <i>etc</i>.). He healed on the Sabbath
with the side benefit of showing that Jewish compunctions against Sabbath breaking
had <i>“gone to seed”</i> (John <b>5</b>:2-17; <b>9</b>;1-<u>16</u>-<u>24</u> -<u>40-41</u>).
Paul and Barnabas <i>“recounted what signs and wonders God had worked through
them among the Gentiles”</i> in order to show that their not circumcising
Gentile converts accorded with God’s will (Acts <b>15</b>:12-13).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            One important implication of miracles for
faith enhancement is that <i>the recipient’s faith is not necessary for the
success of a miracle</i>. That is obvious in <u>cases where Jesus or the
apostles took the initiative</u> without being asked: the resurrection of the
widow of Nain’s son (Luke <b>7</b>:11-18), the healing of a lame man at the
Pool of Bethesda (John <b>5</b>:2-9) and the lame man at Gate Beautiful (Acts <b>3</b>:1-10),
and the feeding of the five thousand (Matthew <b>14</b>:13-21 = Mark<b> 6</b>:32-44
= Luke <b>9</b>:10-17 = John <b>6</b>:1-13) and the four thousand (Matthew <b>15</b>:32-39
= Mark <b>8</b>:1-10). A particularly striking example is the healing of
Malchus’ ear in the Garden of Gethsemane (John <b>18</b>:10-11). That the
recipient’s faith is not necessary for the success of a miracle is obvious in <u>cases
that did not benefit anyone personally</u>: walking on the water (Matthew<b> 14</b>:28-32),
for example. It is also obvious in <u>cases where God’s supernatural power
overrode man’s ability</u> to enhance or destroy, as judgment miracles show—striking
Elymas blind (Acts <b>13</b>:8-11), striking Ananias and Sapphira dead for
lying (Acts<b> 5</b>:1-11), or exorcising demons (Matthew<b> 17</b>:14- 18, <i>etc</i>.).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            Since the recipient’s faith is not
necessary for the success of a miracle, <i>a supposed faith healer cannot blame
his failure on the recipient’s lack of faith</i>. Since faith is a degree
matter, insufficient faith would always be a plausible-sounding reason for
failure. But once a miracle worker attempts to perform a miracle, he shows by
doing so that all required conditions are in fact present or there would be no
reason to proceed. There is only one example of a failed miracle in the New
Testament (Matthew <b>17</b>:14-20 = Mark <b>9</b>:14-29), and that failure is
blamed on the <u>miracle workers’</u> lack of faith (Matthew <b>17</b>:19-20).
Peter’s partial failure at walking on the water is linked to his losing
confidence once he started out from the boat (Matthew<b> 14</b>:28-31). Lack of
faith is a warning most often associated with unanswered prayer rather than
with failed miracle.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            But if a recipient’s faith is not
necessary for the success of a miracle, we may ask why Jesus asked for faith <u>before</u>
a miracle was performed: Matthew <b>8</b>:13 (= Mark <b>5</b>:36); <b>9</b>:2 (=
Mark <b>2</b>:5); <b>9</b>:22 (= Mark <b>5</b>:34; <b>10</b>:52; Luke <b>7</b>:50;
<b>8</b>:48;<b> 17</b>:19; <b>18</b>:42; <i>“your faith has made you whole”</i>);<b>
9</b>:28-29; <b>12</b>:58 (= Mark <b>6</b>:5); Mark<b> 9</b>:23-24; <b>11</b>:23-24
(cp. Matthew <b>17</b>:20; <b>21</b>:21; 1 Corinthians<b> 13</b>:2); Luke <b>8</b>:50;
Acts<b> 14</b>:9. Why was Jesus not able to do many mighty works in Nazareth
because of their unbelief (Matthew <b>8</b>:58)? At Lystra how did Paul see that
the crippled pagan had faith to be healed (Acts <b>14</b>:9)? Before his
miracles Jesus often said, <i>“According to your faith let it be done to you”</i>
(Matthew <b>8</b>:13; <b>9</b>:29) or <i>“Your faith is great; let it be done
to your as you desire”</i> (Matthew <b>15</b>:28). Afterward he says several
times, <i>“Your faith has made you whole” </i>(Matthew <b>9</b>:22 = Mark<b> 5</b>:34
= Luke <b>8</b>:48; Mark <b>10</b>:52 = Luke <b>18</b>:42; Lk. <b>17</b>:19;
cp. Luke <b>7</b>:50). If faith does not contribute to the success of the
miracle, why do the accounts keep saying such things?</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            <i>Faith may have been a <u>condition</u>
for healing. </i>Saying that the recipient’s faith was not necessary to make
the miracle succeed means that faith did not help <u>cause</u> it. Faith is not
like mind over matter as if believing something strongly enough makes it
happen. In Jesus’ ministry, faith may well have been required as (1) a <u>condition</u>
for doing a miracle, but a miracle may be <u>conditioned on faith</u> without
being <u>dependent on faith</u>. Presumably Jesus’ not being able to perform
many miracles in Nazareth was due to the people’s lack of faith, which he
typically required as a condition. He did not customarily perform miracles in a
hostile environment. The home-town people did not believe Jesus was who he
claimed to be.  </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            Jesus’ calling for faith also (2) kept his
miracles from degenerating into something less significant than his messiahship
and related claims. They were not for astounding people, for getting attention,
or distinctively even for relieving their suffering. That they were not
specifically for relieving human suffering showed when Jesus left a place
without healing everyone that wanted to be cured (Matthew<b> 8</b>:14-18).
After healing many people one evening, he went out early the next morning to
pray; when the disciples found him and told him that everyone was looking for
him, he said, <i>“Let’s go into the next towns so I may preach there too, for <u>to
this end I came forth</u>”</i> (Mark<b> 1</b>:32-<u>38</u> = Luke <b>4</b>:40-<u>42</u>).
Performing exorcisms and healings (a) gave evidence for his claims, but at the
same time (b) allowed his miracles to have positive value in and of themselves,
which in turn (c) enabled him thereby to express compassion for people at the
same time he was trying to convert them. The miracles focused attention and
helped create trust in his motives. Since he had chosen to use healing miracles
and exorcisms to prove his claims, he needed to guard against people’s interest
in them for their beneficial value alone. His saying, then, that people’s faith
made them whole was in a conditional sense rather than a causal one; it was
only because of the faith expressed in asking, <i>etc</i>., that they ended up
healed; and they ended up healed, not by their faith, but by Jesus whom they
asked and by his choice to grant their request.                </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            In cases where the requester and the
recipient were not the same, it was most specifically the faith of the people
who interceded that was decisive: the centurion who approached Jesus on behalf
of his servant (Matthew <b>8</b>:5-13 = Luke<b> 7</b>:1-10), the father at the
foot of the Mount of Transfiguration who asked Jesus to exorcise the demon from
his epileptic son (Matthew <b>17</b>:14-18 = Mark <b>9</b>:14-27 = Luke <b>9</b>:37-43),
the sister of Lazarus whom Jesus resurrected from the dead (John <b>11</b>:1-45);
and Jairus, the father of the twelve-year-old girl (Matthew <b>9</b>:18-19, 23-26
= Mark <b>5</b>:21-24, 35-43 = Luke <b>8</b>:40-42, 49-56), </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            The full picture combines interacting
truths: (1) the miracle worker may have called for preliminary faith as a
condition of his performing the miracle or at least to contextualize the
healing in the Messianic enterprise; but when he went ahead and attempted the
miracle, its occurrence did not result from the recipient’s faith. (2) When the
miracle succeeded, the recipient’s faith was increased. The role of healing
miracles then was <u>faith enhancement</u>; the benefit moved the degree of his
faith from what it was to what it became. Where there was no faith in the first
place particularly, healing enhanced faith by initiating it.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            <u>How</u> the faith of the recipient is
involved in some instances of healing may call for comment. The Acts includes
two unusual instances of healing that take place indirectly through symbols. In<b>
5</b>:15-16 people put their sick along the road so Peter’s shadow would pass
over them. Strictly speaking, the text does not say that any of them were
healed in this effort, but there is also no necessary reason for supposing that
they were not healed. In<b> 19</b>:11-12 napkins were sent out from Paul to
heal certain people. In the gospels a woman with continuous menstrual bleeding
was healed by touching Jesus’ garment (Matthew <b>9</b>:18-25 = Mark <b>5</b>:22-43
= Luke <b>8</b>:41-56). Matthew gives a general statement in<b> 14</b>:36 that
people were asking him just to let them touch his clothes.  </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            A certain trust level is evident in all
these cases. Those who lined up along the roads or were carried there had
enough faith to “try” being healed by Peter’s shadow. The woman with the issue
of blood took the initiative in approaching Jesus as he walked along in the
crowd. Perhaps because of the kind of malady she had, she was hesitant about
asking for help from a man in public. Those who asked just to touch Jesus’
clothes had enough faith at least to ask. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            On the other side of some episodes lies
the question of how the will of the <u>miracle worker</u> was involved. It is
not evident that the woman with the bleeding problem was healed without Jesus’
will. Since he knew the hearts of people in other situations (John <b>6</b>:64),
we suppose that he realized what was transpiring. He did not ask the question
to find out who touched him but to protect against her interpreting the miracle
in impersonal terms, to connect the miracle with his claims about himself, to
change the miracle from a secret happening to a matter of public record, or to
elicit a public expression of her faith. The people who asked to touch Jesus
let him know what they wanted, so he was not unaware. Paul surely chose to “bless”
the aprons and handkerchiefs that other people carried from his body to the
sick or demon-possessed. The account does not indicate whether he knew who was
receiving these items, so we cannot say for sure that he did; but we need not
think that third parties took things he happened to touch and carried them away
without his awareness as if psychic energy was transported through these
articles. Cases like these add physical symbols to the phenomenon of healing
from afar as when Jesus’ healed the centurion’s servant (Matthew <b>8</b>:5-13
= Luke <b>7</b>:1-10) and the nobleman’s son (John <b>4</b>:46-54) and
exorcised the demon from the Syro-Phoenician woman’s daughter (Matthew<b> 15</b>:21-28
= Mark <b>7</b>:24-30).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            <i>Finally, New Testament miracles had an
interpersonal aspect</i>. They were not examples of <b>psychic power</b>. The
recipient had enough <u>faith</u> to <u>choose</u> to approach; in almost all
cases he <u>asked</u> for the benefit; Jesus <u>chose</u> whether to heal. The
miracles were not just <b>energy transfers</b> that could occur aside from the
will of the benefactor as perhaps the woman thought who had menstrual bleeding,
as the people may have thought in trying to position themselves so Peter’s
shadow would fall on them, and as the popular belief ran in connection with
troubling of the water in the Pool of Bethesda (John <b>5</b>:2-7). Miracles
were not just magic results that came from a<b> ritual</b> as perhaps Simon the
sorcerer supposed when he asked Peter to give him the power that whomever he
laid his hands on would receive the Holy Spirit (Acts <b>8</b>:18-24). Nor were
miracles caused by a <b>verbal formula</b> as the seven sons of Sceva found out
when they tried to exorcise a demon by invoking the name of Jesus (Acts <b>19</b>:13-16).
New Testament miracles were matters of power (or authority), but it was power
or authority resident in a <u>person</u> and subject to his will to use it. The
power did not lie in a form or in a term or in psychic energy: <i>“I know <u>Jesus</u>
and I know <u>Paul</u>, but who are <u>you</u>?”</i> (Acts <b>19</b>:15). The
interpersonal character of New Testament miracles may explain why they almost
never benefited the miracle worker (although Jesus evidently ate with the
crowds at the feeding of the five thousand and the four thousand and with the
disciples in Galilee after the resurrection). </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            New Testament miracles were matters of “faith,”
which, as the term implies, means persons were involved. The <u>faith</u> of <u>persons</u>
was frequently a condition for miraculous benefits, and the <u>faith</u> of <u>persons</u>
was always the purpose of miracles because they were done to enhance faith.</span></p>
