<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  B.  Acts <b>8</b> and the duration
of miraculous charismata</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>  </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        1.   A proposal implying
cessation</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              While himself able to do
miracles, Philip did not pass on miraculous gifts to the Samaritans. After
Peter and John came down from Jerusalem, they prayed and laid hands on the
believers to that end. Simon the sorcerer saw that <i>“by the laying on of the
apostles’ hands the Holy Spirit was given”</i> (<b>8</b>:18). The inference has
been that “apostles” should be read in contrast to Philip. When the Twelve
passed off the scene, there would be no perpetuation of gifts beyond those who
received them second hand—as Philip evidently did (Acts <b>6</b>:6).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        2.   Suggested caution in this
regard</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              a.   We need not read the
statement restrictively. It may be non-restrictive. </span><span lang=EL
style='font-size:12.0pt'>Δίδοται</span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'> is
present tense in an indirect discourse clause after a leading verb in past
tense. Luke’s point does not have to be a generality statement; the present
tense could mean progressive action rather than characteristic action: <i>“But
Simon, seeing (having seen) that through the laying on of the apostles’ hands
the Spirit <u>was being given</u> . . .”</i> What Simon was watching at the
time is more likely the point; he would probably not have known about other
situations; hence, Luke would not be reporting that Simon had seen a generalization.
</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                    The generality may
have lain in Luke’s awareness, and he worded the instance with Simon as part of
the generality <u>Luke</u> knew. What Simon saw was part of the pattern even
though he may not have been able to do more than guess that such was the case.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              b.   Peter does not tell
Simon that he could not give the gift he requested—the ability to pass on gifts
to third parties. Instead, he condemns the motivation behind the request.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              c.   The reason for
Philip’s not passing on the gifts may not have been that he could not. Dispensing
these gifts through the apostles openly legitimized the Samaritan campaign and
identif</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ι</span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>ed Samaritans as part of the same body as the Jews.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              d.   Even if Philip
could not pass gifts on by the imposition of hands, it does not follow that they
would disappear with the apostles. They could continue by answered prayer (1
Corinthians <b>14</b>:13). The inference may suggest too mechanical a process
for obtaining these charismata. These are abilities dispensed by a person, not
transfers of energy through someone’s hands.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  C.  Other passages suggest a distinctive
operation of the Spirit through the apostles with the further suggestion that
these charismata would not be permanent—at least in the range that the apostles
had them. The peculiarity of persons unites with the peculiarity of purpose
handled in the next heading.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        1.   Acts <b>8</b> may imply
that <u>only the apostles could pass on gifts to others</u>, with the further
inference that the gifts would cease when the apostles died. There is no case
of imparting miraculous gifts except by an <u>apostle</u>. The case of Timothy
(1 Timothy<b> 4</b>:14; cp.<b> 1</b>:5, 18) is not clear since (a) Paul
indicates in 2 Timothy <b>1</b>:6 that he had laid hands on Timothy as well,
perhaps at the same time as the presbytery did. (b) “Gift” may not mean
supernatural gift here (see earlier statements on “gift”); it may indicate the slot
of ministry they were setting Timothy aside for (1 Timothy<b> 4</b>:14 + 2
Timothy <b>1</b>:6; cp. Ephesians <b>4</b>:8-16). Finally, (c) 1 Timothy <b>4</b>:14
says <i>“<u>with</u> the laying on of the hands of the presbytery,” </i>not <u>by</u>
their hands. The phraseology sounds more like accompaniment (<u>with</u> the
presbytery) than agency (<u>by</u> the presbytery).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        2.   2 Corinthians <b>12</b>:12
speaks of <i>“the signs of an apostle”</i> with the inference that something
about them was distinctive.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              Although <i>apostle</i> can
be a term equivalent to <i>evangelist</i>, Paul’s usage here is part of his
authentication as a “primary” apostle. So, we are inclined to take <i>“signs of
an apostle”</i> as something distinctive as to <u>range</u>, <u>manner of
receiving</u>, or <u>kind</u>. The apostles received their miraculous signs
directly from Christ while others typically may have received them indirectly. The
Corinthians could not have told from the exercising of Paul’s gifts how he
received them, but his comment to the Corinthians (<b>12</b>:12) about signs may
have had the same force as his comment to the Galatians (<b>1</b>:11-12) about
the message. The difference is that Galatians represents a claim while
Corinthians represents an appeal.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        3.   Ephesians <b>2</b>:20
speaks of apostles and prophets as being the foundation of the church
historically. The charismatic offices put in that foundation may be so pictured
because they were not perpetuated.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        4.   In Hebrews <b>2</b>:3-4
the writer presents himself as a second-generation Christian—whether to
identify with his readers or to indicate that he was, in fact, a second-generation
Christian. He assigns the charismata to first-century believers.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        5.   Daniel <b>9</b>:24, <i>“To
seal up vision and prophecy,”</i> refers to the cessation of prophecy. The
passage relates seemingly to the finality of special revelation, so it would be
no longer progressive as before. If it is not progressive, it does not require
continued miraculous manifestation to verify new revelation. Maybe the text
means the “fulfillment” of all vision and prophecy.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        6.   Responses to the
proposition that miraculous gifts were passed on only through the laying on of
the apostles’ hands</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              (a)<i>   “Let the ones
speaking in a language pray that they may interpret”</i> (1 Corinthians <b>14</b>:13).
Prayer appears to be a second method of obtaining at least individual gifts. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              (b)  <i>“Desire
earnestly the greater gifts”</i> Paul says to the Corinthians at a time when no
apostle was among them (1 Corinthians <b>12</b>:31). This could be an idiomatic
use of the imperative mood as in John <b>2</b>:19: <i>“Destroy this temple and
in three days I will raise it up.” </i>But it sounds more like Paul’s comment
to Timothy that <i>“the one that desires the work of an elder desires a good
thing”</i> (1 Timothy<b> 3</b>:1).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              (c)   The Romans had
gifts, but no one had evidently been among them to dispense gifts (Romans <b>12</b>:4-8).
On the other hand, Paul wanted to go to Rome to impart some spiritual gift to
the Christians there (<b>1</b>:11). The gifts listed in<b> 12</b>:4-8 may not
represent supernatural gifts; or if they do, they could have come to Rome by converts
migrating from elsewhere. Romans <b>1</b>:11 may refer to earlier times when
they had not yet received such enablements. The situation is not clear.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              (d)  In Galatians <b>3</b>:5
Paul refers to someone who was <i>“supplying [the Galatians] the Spirit”</i>
and working miracles among them. Was this person “supplying” them the Spirit in
the sense of passing on gifts to them or by ministering to them? In this case,
there may also be an implication that the Judaizing teacher(s) were <u>not</u>
ding miracles of passing on miraculous gifts so that such gifting would not be
confirming their false teaching: <i>“Does the one who is proving you with the
Spirit and working miracles among you, do it by the works of law or by the
hearing of faith”</i> (Galatians <b>3</b>:5)?</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              (e)  There is no record
of anyone but an apostle ever passed on miraculous gifts, but that may be due
to the brevity of the accounts. The New Testament was largely written before
that second generation passed off the scene.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              (f)   1 Timothy <b>4</b>:14
(see above)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              (g)  Philip the
evangelist worked miracles, but the imposition of the apostles’ hands in Acts <b>6</b>:6
was not necessarily for more than appointment to the benevolent work at hand
that the original “seven” were selected by the church to do. The same goes for
Stephen (<b>6</b>:8).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  D.  The lack of apologetic necessity
(sufficiency of the written word)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        Miraculous manifestation has
served to authenticate the divine origin of new revelation (John <b>3</b>:2, <i>e.g</i>.).
The inference is that <u>once its authenticity was established</u>, there was
no <u>necessity</u> for such signs. The record of the authentication suffices. The
testimony of believable witnesses of authentication replaced it (John <b>20</b>:30-31;
Hebrews <b>2</b>:3-4; <i>etc</i>.). Belief by others contemporary or later called
for sufficient evidence, not overwhelming evidence.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        Miracles are not <u>necessary</u>
except for new revelation or special guidance, but there is no exact
correlation between the two. Miracles may be <u>useful</u> at other times for
efficiency, effectiveness, or relief of suffering. Even in new revelation,
miraculous confirmation may not be necessary; God may substitute <u>providence</u>
for miracle to establish his purposes and confirm special revelation.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        1.   In keeping with this last
point, is Jesus’ comment to Thomas, <i>“Blessed are they who have not seen and
have believed”</i> (John <b>20</b>:25). God is more glorified by an open heart
than by someone he has to “make” believe by stupendous display.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        2.   God’s not manifesting
himself openly to unbelievers is partially due to that principle as well. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        3.   In Luke<b> 16</b>:31 Jesus
has Lazarus comment that if people refuse to believe Moses and the prophets, they
will not believe even if someone arises from the dead (cp. 1 Corinthians <b>14</b>:21).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        4.   The scripture depreciates
craving after signs (Luke <b>1</b>:18-20; <b>10</b>:17-20;<b> 11</b>:29-30 =
Matthew <b>12</b>:38-42; I Corinthians <b>1</b>:22). </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        5.   Too much emphasis on
miracles is counterproductive because it diverts attention from God to God’s
effects on individuals’ benefits (cp. 1 Corinthians <b>12</b>:29-<b>13</b>:13).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        6.   Finally, “sign” does not always
mean something miraculous (1 Corinthians <b>14</b>:21; Luke <b>2</b>:12, <i>e.g</i>.);
so the objection to seeking signs generalizes to continually needing
reassurance because of weak faith or reluctance to believe.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        The record of authentication
replaces miraculous authentication. Once the new revelation is authenticated,
it does not have to be re-established time after time. Nevertheless, that pattern
is not absolute because miracle had also the function of benefit, as in
healing. Even authentication of messengers could be appropriate under special
circumstances such as in pioneer fields or from time to time when the gospel is
under concentrated attack (cp. Elijah on Mount Carmel, where no new revelation
was being given; it was re-authentication of previous revelation). The Old
Testament era differed in that it had progressive revelation. The canon was not
closed as long as there was a succession of prophets. Now the faith is <i>“once
and for all time delivered to the saints” </i>(Jude 3). </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  E.   The finality of the gospel</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        Since scripture teaches that
the revelation through Christ is final (Daniel<b> 9</b>:24; Jude 3, <i>e.g</i>.),
no further revelation will replace the church age as the Christian dispensation
replaced the Mosaic economy. So again, perpetuation of miracle is a moot point.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        While we can admit the
tendency of that thought, some considerations apply. We can agree that “closing
the canon” does not eliminate continuous revelation for <u>guidance</u>. Those
gifts available to <u>enhance ministry</u> might continue and be available upon
request (prayer), the enhancement being as much to aid the servant as to
convince observers. Miracles could <u>re-authenticate former revelation</u> as
in Elijah’s contest with the Baal prophets.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  F.   The testimony of history</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        Two classic volumes by Henry
Barclay Swete trace the work of the Spirit historically: <i>The Holy Spirit in
the New Testament: A Study of Primitive Christian Teaching</i> and <i>The Holy
Spirit in the Ancient Church: A Study of Christian Teaching in the Age of the
Fathers</i>. The consensus among Protestant historians has been that the charismata
passed out of use with claims for the gifts appearing <i>seriatim</i>. Roman
Catholicism has taken a “classical Pentecostal” stance, a position at one with
the doctrine of apostolic succession. Montanism emphasized supernatural gifts
during the second century and later. Albigenses, Waldenses, Jansenists, and
others made such claims as well.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        For doctrinal reasons, we do
well to exercise caution about supernatural phenomena, because miracle
authenticates the claim of the miracle worker (Mark <b>2</b>:6-9, <i>e.g</i>.).
Such phenomena would either (a) mean that the theological differences have been
immaterial (which seems surprising), (b) be demonic (which seems harsh), (c) be
fake (which may apply), (d) be psychosomatic, (e) parapsychological, or (f)
mistaken identity. The correlation between doctrine and miracle could lessen
the historical objection to perpetual charismata if we felt free to write off
most of church history as doctrinally aberrant, a rather extreme stance. Surely
some in sub-apostolic times were as spiritually mature and doctrinally correct
as the Corinthian Christians, who had such gifts among them.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        One approach to temporary
presence of gifts adopts the idea of the “latter rain,” which teaches that near
the end time miracles will return. Passage invoked are Joel <b>2</b>:23;
Jeremiah <b>5</b>:24; Deuteronomy<b> 11</b>:14; (cp. Joel <b>2</b>:28-29; Acts <b>2</b>:17-21;
James <b>5</b>:7). Even if true, that belief does not validate current claims
unless these are the “latter days,” something not yet evident.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  G.  The purpose of the gospel</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        The gospel is not for
deliverance from human limitation. <i>“Jesus wants you well”</i> is not a New
Testament promise. Underlying many differences in Holy Spirit studies is the
relationship of God (hence, Holy Spirit) to the world. God seems to operate
first and usually through normal interpersonal and natural processes, and
specially through direct intervention when the others are not sufficient for his
purposes.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  H.  John <b>20</b>:29</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        Jesus told Thomas that those
are blessed who believe without <u>seeing</u>. The inference is that people are
more blessed who believe without <u>seeing miracles</u>. Taken together with
Jesus’ depreciation of miracles relative to salvation, that observation is
probably true; but it is a different thing from dismissing supernatural
manifestation altogether. Such certainly happened in the appearance itself. In
the case of Thomas, his disbelief was rather extreme, countering the testimony of
several trustworthy eyewitnesses he had known for years. It bordered on
obscurantism; that may be why Jesus said what he did and may imply a difference
from disbelief in less certain circumstances.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  I.    The distinctively apostolic
and prophetic roles were part of launching the church.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        Ephesians <b>2</b>:20 pictures
the household of God as built on the <u>foundation</u> of the apostles and
prophets with Christ as the cornerstone. From that figure we might argue that
supernaturally enabled prophecy belonged to the apostolic age—the foundation. The
<u>foundation figure</u> in Ephesians <b>2</b> does have a historical thrust in
contrast to the theological thrust of 1 Corinthians <b>3</b>:10-15. <u>Prophets</u>
in Ephesians <b>2</b> are New Testament prophets rather than Old Testament ones
(<b>3</b>:5).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        Excepting one interpretation
of 1 Corinthians <b>13</b>, the observations above are argue <u>against</u>
continuous miracle. All the arguments for ending supernatural manifestation
with the passing of the apostles turn on (1) the <u>finality of the gospel
revelation through Christ</u> plus (2) <u>the correlation between miracle and
new revelation</u>. That thought lies behind raising points to show that no one
but apostles could pass on the gifts, the argument from <i>“the perfect” </i>in
1 Corinthians <b>13</b>, and the tendency to read <u>restrictively</u> rather
than positively passages like Hebrews <b>2</b>:3-4; Ephesians <b>2</b>:20;
Daniel <b>9</b>:24; 2 Corinthians <b>12</b>:12 (<i>“signs of an <u>apostle</u>”
vs</i>. non-apostle; and Acts <b>8</b>:14-24 (<i>“the laying on of the <u>apostles’</u>
hands” vs</i>. Philip’s hands). </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        We conclude that the New
Testament evidence for <u>cessation of miracle</u>s is tentative, though
theological and historical considerations may foster that conclusion if not
made absolute. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        The following entries
represent arguments for <u>continuation of miracles</u>.                  </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  J.   Mark<b> 16</b> and the duration
of miraculous phenomena</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        1.   The statement itself</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              The text deals with the apostles’
work and with Jesus’ promises that signs would follow those who believe through
their ministry. <i>Believers</i> in <b>16</b>:17 corresponds with the <i>believers</i>
of <b>16</b>:16. So, the text does not deal explicitly with duration. That is an
(a) inference from the nature of the case, (b) information from elsewhere in
the New Testament or from history, or (c) personal experience with such
phenomena.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              The promise in <b>16</b>:17-18
finds adequate fulfillment in <b>16</b>:20; so, Jesus does not necessarily look
beyond what Mark’s gospel states as fulfilling that promise. Since the writer
states a fulfillment, we must entertain the possibility that he states it to indicate
its fulfillment. <i>“Their ministry”</i> does not mean just the ones they
converted. It should include all those laboring under their direction during
their ministries. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        2.   Some have suggested an
inference: miraculous manifestation lasts as long as the apostolic commission,
including the conditions for salvation. But the cases are not the same.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              a.   The purpose of God’s
operation in history is salvation, not miraculous display. Miracles are aids in
establishing gospel truth. Once that establishment has happened, God’s pleasure
decides on the need for subsequent miraculous manifestation. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              b.   Granting such power
is <u>God’s doing</u>; so we wait to see whether he grants it. Obedience to God
is <u>our doing</u>, and we follow it until he withdraws the command or the
opportunity to obey it.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
.25in'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        c.   1
Corinthians <b>13</b> makes clear that miracle is secondary and non-germane to
Christianity like salvation is.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              d.    It would be about
as appropriate to argue that the apostles would carry out the Great Commission
all the time in the Christian era because they are the ones Jesus was talking
to.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        3.   An <i>ad hominem</i>
consideration is valuable.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              If languages are
practiced <u>as a sign</u> on the basis of Mark<b> 16</b>, why not snake
handling and public poison drinking? They would be more objective proof that
something miraculous is occurring. Since drinking poison and snakebite cure are
listed among the signs, </span><span style='font-size:12.0pt;color:windowtext'>there
ay be</span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'> insight here into the motivation
and manner of using miraculous gifts. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              They are not something we
seek out deliberately; they are protections in response to a need that calls
them forth. In Paul, we can compare the case of snakebite in Acts <b>28</b>:1-6,
of exorcism in Acts <b>16</b>:16-18ff., of language speaking in 1 Corinthians <b>14</b>:18-19,
of healing in 2 Timothy <b>4</b>:20 (cp. Luke<b> 10</b>:17-20). He was not
enamored with seeking opportunities to display miracles even before unbelievers,
much less before believers. Rather he responded to situations that came up
while he was proclaiming the gospel. <i>“For many days”</i> he put up with the
maiden in Philippi that had the spirit of divination before he exorcised the
demon (Acts <b>16</b>:16-18). Mark’s prediction sounds quite like Jesus’
empowerments at the Mission of the Seventy: <i>“I have given you authority to
treat on snakes and scorpions and over all the power of the enemy; and nothing
will hurt you in any way.”</i></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        4.   Most textual critics think
Mark <b>16</b>:9-20 represents material not in the original gospel. We make no
attempt here to bring that consideration to bear on the question at hand because
no impropriety belongs to anything in this pericope. The section might be
genuine even though manuscript evidence for its integrity is weaker than
preferred.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  K.  The presence of the occult</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        As there has been a
correlation made between miracle and new revelation, there has been a
correlation between miracle and the presence of the demonic. The latter has
been done with opposite conclusions. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        On the one hand, so it is
said, <i>“No miracle today, no demonic possession today.” </i>On the other hand,
some have said, <i>“Demonic possession today; so supernatural gifts today</i> (exorcism).<i>”
</i>Both positions beg the question either of current manifestation or of
current demonic activity.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        Christians often apply to
demonic activity Christ’s promise that <i>“no one will snatch you out of my
hand” </i>(John <b>10</b>:28). Surely Satanic forces cannot possess Christians
contrary to their will. As to possessions already in place with a disbeliever, we
may invoke the observation that <u>whatever is available through miracle is
available through answered prayer</u>, a principle that applies to all kinds of
needs taken care of by miracle. The effect of that realization is the
separation of demonic activity and divine miracle. Moreover, exorcism is never
called a spiritual gift in the New Testament. The guardian-angel possibility may
apply here (Matthew <b>4</b>:6; Psalm <b>18</b>:10; <b>26</b>:53; Acts <b>12</b>:7-15;
Hebrews<b> 1</b>:14; cp. Psalm <b>91</b>:11-12). In this promise, Jesus more likely
has in mind Jewish and Roman opponents of the day; they would not overthrow
Christ or his followers: even death—by execution, as it turned out—would not
prevail against him or the building of his church (Matthew <b>16</b>:18).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  L.   The application of all the
promises to God’s people</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        Claiming God’s promises
presupposes that they apply to the ones who claim them. That fact means
reinvestigating texts about duration. There is no reason to set aside the question
about who a statement addresses—one of the most basic principles of
interpretation. Its generalization must then be by the nature of the case or
some other direct statement.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  M. John <b>14</b>:12 says, <i>“They
will do greater things than these.”</i></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        1.   What is Jesus’ time
reference?</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        2.   Is there anything
miraculous that is greater than resurrection or healing from </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              Leprosy or blindness
from birth?</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        3.   Jesus was speaking
directly only to the apostles; how do we generalize his</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              promise beyond them?</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        4.   What is the nature of the
“greaterness”? Is it greater as to kind or degree?</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              In Hebrew idiom
especially, greater degree may express better kind (cp. majestic plural, which
uses plural number for greater degree). <b>(a) </b>Jesus told his audiences
that their righteousness needed to exceed the righteousness of the scribes and Pharisees
(Matthew <b>5</b>:20); to do that, they would have to switch <u>kinds</u> of
righteousness. The Pharisees had carried their <u>kind</u> of righteousness
about as far as imaginable. <b>(b)</b> In Matthew <b>11</b>:11 Jesus said that
no one was greater than John the Baptist; yet the least in the kingdom was
greater than he—denial plus affirmation of the same point! The greatness of
those in the kingdom could not have been a more extreme degree of the same kind;
that greatness had to be in another <u>kind</u> of category that was greater
than John’s. <b>(c)</b> Paul says in 1 Corinthians <b>12-<u>13</u>-14</b> that
love is greater than supernatural gifts—a different <u>kind</u> of thing. <b>(d)</b>
When Jesus’ disciples returned from the Mission of the Seventy, he told them
that having their names written in heaven was more important than exorcising
demons, healing, or performing signs (Luke <b>10</b>:17-20). So, doing
something greater than Jesus’ miracles is not necessarily doing greater miracles
than his.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  N.   Romans <b>11</b>:29 says, <i>“The
gifts and calling of God are not repented of.”</i></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        Paul’s subject is not the
duration of sign gifts. He is talking about the role God gave Israel. Besides,
the translation can be progressive present instead of characteristic present: <i>“The
gifts and calling of God</i> [to Israel]<i> are not being repented of</i> [in
calling the Gentiles into fellowship with him].<i>”</i> God has not changed his
mind about Israel; they are still responsible for standing for the one true God
and serving as a light to the nations in that regard.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        Arguments for permanent
manifestation follow one or two lines: they argue for progressive <u>revelation</u>
or for continued miracle for other purposes: to authenticate special <u>guidance</u>
with a closed canon, to <u>benefit</u> as with answered prayer, to assist in <u>ministry</u>
as with the “ministry” gifts, or to <u>re-affirm</u> <u>previous revelation</u>.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        We may ask again why duration
of charismata is even an issue—why we would expect any difference in the level
of supernatural manifestation in the first century and today. Several items we
have noted relate to such a change. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        1.   In the New Testament era,
we do not have the progressive revelation that occurred in the Old Testament
era, and miraculous manifestation corresponds largely with the new revelation.
Christians have a closed canon. God may consider sufficient the record of
original confirmation.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        2.   God created us in his
image to live in the natural realm, so supernatural enablement is exceptional
rather than typical. The kingdom of God is not about miracles but personal
relationship. Even in the first century, miraculous manifestation was the
exception instead of the rule. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        3.   The continued presence of
miracles with Christians can distract even Christians themselves; so once
miracles have served their purpose, they can disappear to avoid the idea that they
are integral to the faith.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        4.   Everything available
through miracle is available through answered prayer; so, not having miraculous
endowments does not leave us orphans in evangelism, edification, and personal growth.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:center'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>Conclusion Re Method</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:center'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  The biblical, historical, and
theological arguments for and against continued miraculous phenomena do not
seem decisive. Rather than attempting to establish either view, we do better to
operate as early Christians did—where there was no such discussion—and apply
the tests.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  That procedure is safer because it
does not force us into a position where we cannot remain open to what God may
want to do. We dare not put themselves in a situation like that of
first-century Pharisees, who could not even in principle allow for Jesus’
claims as vindicated by his miracles: claiming to be the Son of the Blessed was
itself blasphemy (Matthew <b>26</b>:63-65; Mark <b>14</b>:61-64; Luke <b>22</b>:70)!
Our interpretation of Acts <b>8</b>, 1 Corinthians <b>13</b>, and other texts,
may be as incorrect as the Pharisees’ understanding of texts about the Messiah
and his mission. We do not want to lock ourselves into some idea that requires
us to assign all manifestations to the power of Beelzebub (Matthew <b>12</b>:24
ff.). That charge put the Pharisees in danger of committing blasphemy.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  This procedure is safer also
because, though scripture might give adequate evidence for the cessation of
miracles, we may not be skillful enough to see it there or convince others that
it is there. Taking the suggested approach does not make us vulnerable any more
than it did first-century Christians, and it buys time to arrive at what is not
clear.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  As a final point, the biggest
concern is not about modern miracles, but about the doctrinal claims and special
guidance that claimants may use such signs to validate. The challenge comes
from a call for unity based on <u>experiences</u> rather than on <u>doctrine</u>.
The implication can be that the doctrinal questions are irrelevant that
distinguish Christian groups. Even more unsettling is the appearance of some of
these experiences in quasi-Christian groups, to say nothing of non-Christian
religions. The approach to miracles must be such that it can deal with those
demonstrations by which other religions try to establish <u>their</u> claims.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in'><b><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></b></p>

<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:center'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>Addendum on the Imposition of Hands</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:center'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  The question arises as to whether we
should lay hands on people to give them gifts. If we do not have that authority
or power, nothing will “happen.” If there seems to be such a need for ministry
or witnessing gifts, we can invoke prayer. By putting the matter in the
framework of prayer, we make possible the receiving of benefits, but we avoid “putting
God on the spot” or <i>“presuming on God’s grace.”</i> We would be acting
“presumptuously” (Deuteronomy <b>18</b>:20-22) because we have no commission so
to such a thing. </span></p>
