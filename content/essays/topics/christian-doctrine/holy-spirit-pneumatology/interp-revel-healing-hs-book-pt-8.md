style='font-size:12.0pt'>            D.  Miraculous healing (See also documents

with Oil” as well as “Evaluation of Current Manifestation.”)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:1.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify;text-indent:-1.0in'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  1.   <u>Instantaneous</u>. The
closest thing to an exception is Jesus’ two-step miracle in healing a blind man
(Mark <b>8</b>:22-26).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  2.   <u>Always successful</u>. One
exception was the apostles’ failure during Jesus’ ministry to heal the lunatic
boy at the foot of the Mount of Transfiguration. Another was Peter’s failure to
succeed completely in walking on the water. In both cases the failure was
assigned to the lack of faith in the person attempting the miracle. (See fuller
treatment under “Evaluation: Tests.”)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  3.   Usually <u>performed on those
outside the Christian community</u> </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        a.   The case of Trophimus
implies that miracles were not usually performed on fellow Christians; Paul
left him behind at Miletus sick (2 Timothy <b>4</b>:20).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        b.   Epaphroditus was sick to
the point of death, but Paul did not heal him presumably. He was in Rome
visiting Paul with an offering from the Philippian church when he took sick
(Philippians <b>2</b>:25-27).     </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        c.   Timothy’s stomach ailment
was not removed miraculously by Paul or anyone else (1 Timothy<b> 5</b>:23). Instead,
Paul recommended using medicine. (Paul’s own thorn in the flesh was not removed,
even in response to threefold prayer; 2 Corinthians <b>12</b>:7-9. However,
this is a case of prayer, not miracle.)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        Exceptions are the resurrection
of Dorcas (Acts <b>9</b>:36-41), the resurrection of Eutychus (Acts <b>20</b>:7-12),
and the healing of Peter’s mother-in-law during Christ’s ministry (Matthew <b>8</b>:14-15;
Mark<b> 1</b>:29-30; Luke<b> 4</b>:38-39). This pattern looks away from the
health-and-wealth, “full faith” gospel.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  There are no cases of miracle
workers using miraculous powers on themselves. That is an underlying issue in
the stones-to-bread temptation; Jesus refused to use miracle for
self-preservation (Matthew<b> 4</b>:2-4; Luke<b> 4</b>:2-4). The closest things
to an exception are the feeding of the five thousand and the four thousand;
presumably Jesus himself and his disciples ate with the multitudes. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  James <b>5</b>:13-18 is probably not
talking about miraculous healing, but about answered prayer. Both healings and
answered prayer were done with anointing and the laying on of hands. Healings
involve oil only in James <b>5 </b>and Mark <b>6</b>:13. (See separate essay “James
<b>5</b> and Anointing with Oil.”)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  In Isaiah <b>53</b> the word <i>disease</i>
is not mentioned in the context. <i>Heal</i> has many applications; so the use
of it does not require disease to be included, even though Matthew <b>18</b>:16-17
does cite Isaiah <b>53</b> in connection with Jesus’ healings. </span></p>
