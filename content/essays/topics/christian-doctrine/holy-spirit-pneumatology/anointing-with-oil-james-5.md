<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:center'><b><span
style='font-size:14.0pt'>JAMES 5 AND ANOINTING WITH OIL</span></b></p>

<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:center'><b><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></b></p>

<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:center'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>Virgil Warren, PhD</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            If people get sick, James <b>5</b>:14-15
urges them to call the elders of the church to pray over them and anoint them
with olive oil in the name of the Lord. This text raises two issues: (1)
whether the procedure is <u>restrictive or positive</u> (cp. commandment <i>vs.</i>
advice), which amounts to <u>precedent <i>vs.</i> example</u>; and (2) whether
it is <u>miracle or answered prayer</u>. In the first issue, “restrictive”
commandment means that specifying this way eliminates other ways; “positive”
commandment means this is a good way to deal with it. If the commandment is
non-restrictive, it must be an “example” (one way of approaching God for
healing) rather than a “precedent” to be followed always and henceforth. These
variables affect the propriety and necessity of using olive oil today for healing,
of calling a plurality of church elders, and of accompanying the procedure with
prayer.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>I. Example and Precedent</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            What James describes is evidently example
rather than <u>set form</u>. He does not lay down a necessary formula for getting
the results. A “formula” would require that the sick person do the calling,
that he call the <u>elders</u> rather than general church members, that he call
elder<u>s</u> rather than one of them, that they must <u>anoint with oil</u>,
that they use <u>olive</u> oil rather than some other oil or ointment, that
they do so explicitly <u>in the name of the Lord</u>, and that they <u>pray</u>.
James does not mention <u>laying on hands</u>, which occurs in some accounts of
New Testament healings.      </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            The following considerations tell against James’
instructions as a <u>permanent set form</u>. (1) The <u>number</u> of New
Testament healing cases is too small to set a precedent for anointing with oil.
Of the many miraculous and non-miraculous healings, only two texts mention
olive oil: this text and the Mission of the Twelve in Mark’s account (<b>6</b>:13).
Not much of a pattern can be deduced from two cases (although both involve sets
of cases). </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            Furthermore, (2) the New Testament examples
are <u>not uniform</u>. No account of a specific healing mentions anointing. Many
include laying on of hands, which is not mentioned here. If the examples are
not uniform, they do not set precedent. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            (3) <u>The nature of the case</u> allows the
effect to come in various ways. Christian processes are not formulas, because
they occur with a personal God rather than a legal or natural operation.
Christianity has no magic words or rituals that activate impersonal forces. Human
requests are not causes; they pass through the will of a Person who answers
freely by promise (cp. Romans <b>5</b>:15-16 &gt; Exodus <b>33</b>:19).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            The lack of uniformity means that James <b>5
</b>does not provide a biblical basis for “extreme unction” or “last rites.” James
envisions more than terminal illness. In fact, he pictures a standard situation
that does <u>not</u> involve death-bed circumstances: <i>“the Lord will raise
them up.”</i></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>II. Miracle <i>vs</i>. Answered Prayer</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            Although Mark <b>6</b>:13 connects oil
with miraculous healing, it seems not to be the kind of healing James <b>5</b> enjoins.
</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  (1) James would be implying that
every congregation had elders with the gift of healing, which is uncertain from
the evidence available on the diversity of gifts in the New Testament. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  (2) Instances of miraculous healing
do not always include prayer (Mark <b>2</b>:1-12; Acts<b> 3</b>; <i>etc</i>.). </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  (3) Most importantly, the context in
James <b>5</b> deals primarily with <u>prayer</u>. In fact, the text identifies
<i>“the prayer of faith”</i> as what saves the sick. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  (4) The author associates his
exhortation with Elijah’s <u>prayer</u> that it not rain for three and a half
years in Israel and then that it would rain. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  (5) James does not mention imposition
of hands. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  (6) The promise of healing is no
stronger here than with answered prayer: <i>“Whatever you ask in prayer, believing,
you will receive” </i>(Matthew <b>21</b>:[21-]22; cp. <b>17</b>:19-20; Mark <b>10</b>:23-24;
John <b>15</b>:16b; <b>16</b>:23-24). Whereas miracles are uniformly successful
(to provide evidential value), answered prayers are not. Nevertheless,
statements about answered prayer are made in absolute terms that assume all the
appropriate considerations: </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  that the request harmonizes with
God’s will (John<b> 14</b>:13-14; <b>15</b>:7; 1 John<b> 5</b>:14), </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  that requesters are doing their
part, </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  that their motives for asking are
right (James <b>4</b>:3),</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  that they are obeying God in other
respects (1 John<b> 3</b>:22),</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  that they are treating other people
right (1 Peter <b>3</b>:7), </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  that they are sincere (Luke <b>18</b>:8;
<b>20</b>:46-47), </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  that they have sufficient faith
(Matthew <b>21</b>:21; James <b>1</b>:6-7), </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  that they are forgiving others
(Matthew<b> 6</b>:15), and </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  that receiving the request would not
reduce their growth potential (Acts <b>4</b>:29-31; 1 </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        Corinthians <b>10</b>:13b; <b>12</b>:8-9).
</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>James’ promise that God <i>“will raise them up”</i>
does not move the process from prayer to miracle. With either answered prayer
or express miracle, the process cannot be uniform, or no Christian would ever
die. For that reason, it is important to note cases where people were not
healed by prayer (2 Corinthians <b>12</b>:7-10), and miracle was not even
attempted (1 Timothy<b> 5</b>:23; 2 Timothy <b>4</b>:20).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>III. The Purpose of Using Oil</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            James does not explain the reason for
olive oil; we can only infer the reason from other anointings. In Jewish
religion and culture, anointing was a medicine, a cosmetic, and a symbol of consecration.
</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  (1) The Samaritan illustrated <u>medicinal
usage</u> when he put oil and wine in the traveler’s wounds (Luke <b>10</b>:34;
cp. Isaiah <b>1</b>:6; note Revelation <b>3</b>:18). </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  (2) <u>Cosmetic usage</u> appears in
Esther<b> 2</b>:12 when Esther was being prepared to enter the house of King
Ahasuerus (cp. Song of Solomon <b>1</b>:3; Judith <b>10</b>:3-4; <b>16</b>:8). By
extension, pouring oil on the head or feet of guests was a <u>gesture of</u> <u>hospitality</u>.
In two similar instances, a woman poured oil on the head or feet of Jesus while
he was reclining at dinner (on the head according to Matthew <b>26</b>:6-13 =
Mark <b>14</b>:3-9; on the feet in Luke <b>7</b>:36-50; John <b>12</b>:1-8 + <b>11</b>:2).
In response to criticism from his disciples, Jesus interpreted her act as a
preparation for his soon <u>burial</u> (Matthew <b>26</b>:8-13 = Mark<b> 14</b>:4-9;
John <b>12</b>:4-8; cp. Mark <b>16</b>:1). A third extension of cosmetic usage signaled
the <u>end of mourning</u> (2 Samuel <b>12</b>:20; <b>14</b>:2; Matthew <b>6</b>:17).
Mourners indicated mourning by not putting on the customary oil. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  (3) <u>Appointment usage</u> was
with <i>kings</i> (Judges <b>9</b>:15; 1 Samuel<b> 10</b>:1; <b>16</b>:3; 1
Kings <b>1</b>:39; 2 Kings <b>9</b>:6; <b>11</b>:12; Psalm<b> 89</b>:20),
including the <i>Messiah</i> as king (Psalm<b> 2</b>:2;<b> 45</b>:7 = Acts<b> 4</b>:26-27;
cp. Acts <b>10</b>:38); <i>priests</i> (Exodus <b>38</b>:41; <b>29</b>:7;
Leviticus<b> 8</b>:12;<b> 21</b>:10), <i>prophets</i> (1 Kings <b>19</b>:16;
Isaiah <b>61</b>:1 = Luke<b> 4</b>:18; cp. Genesis <b>20</b>:7; 1 Chronicles <b>16</b>:22;
Psalm <b>105</b>:15), and more generally <i>highly favored ones</i> (Zechariah <b>4</b>:14).
Setting aside to a specific usage—sanctification—included the dedication of <i>objects
</i>for temple ritual (Exodus <b>30</b>:33; Leviticus<b> 8</b>:10-11). Relative
to privileged appointment and hospitality, anointing with oil symbolized <u>blessing</u>
and joy (Psalm<b> 23</b>:5; <b>92</b>:10), and by extension it symbolized the <u>Holy
Spirit</u> (Acts <b>10</b>:38; 1 John<b> 2</b>:20, 27). When “Messiah” (the “Anointed
One”) is appointed to office, his anointing is not with oil but with the
Spirit—as at his baptism (Matthew <b>3</b>:16 = Mark <b>1</b>:10 = Luke <b>3</b>:22
= John<b> 1</b>:22-24).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  (a) In keeping with <u>cosmetic</u>
usage, anointing with oil in James <b>5 </b>could indicate confidence in being
healed by <i>preparing to re-enter public life</i>. (b) In <u>medicinal</u>
usage, anointing may symbolize the <i>healing</i> that will take place. It is
doubtful that oil is for therapeutic value, because James is speaking of
sickness in general, not of external wounds in particular, where the oil itself
might facilitate healing. (c) The oil may symbolize the <i>blessing</i> that healing
brings. Finally, (d) it may serve as a symbol of the <i>Holy Spirit</i> who
blesses with healing. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>IV. The Propriety of Contemporary Usage</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            If the anointing in James <b>5</b> connects
with cosmetic usage, it was a cultural practice modern western societies no
longer follow. Since it is example rather than precedent, again anointing with
oil need not be standard practice or us. If James <u>is</u> speaking of miracle
<i>vs</i>. answered prayer, the propriety of contemporary usage depends on a
person’s conclusion about the duration of miracle. Even then, anointing would
not have to be precedent to guarantee success.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            In the final analysis, the use of olive
oil in requests for healing appears to be a matter of taste, a matter of
opinion, something not forbidden yet not required.</span></p>
