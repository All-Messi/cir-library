<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;

style='font-size:4.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></b></p>

<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;
margin-bottom:0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:center'><b><span
style='font-size:14.0pt'>SUBJECTIVE EXPERIENCE AND OBJECTIVE REVELATION</span></b></p>

<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;
margin-bottom:0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:center'><b><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></b></p>

<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;
margin-bottom:0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:center'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>Virgil Warren, PhD</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><b><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></b></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>Introduction</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            <b>Every perversion of Christianity has
been some kind of departure from its interpersonal nature and purpose</b>. The
difficulties that have arisen in teaching, practice, and experience have
resulted from <i>depersonalizing God or people</i> and from <i>de-interpersonalizing</i>
<i>Christian experience</i>. Christianity addresses persons in relationship
both vertically and horizontally because that is the most encompassing, most
basic, and most complex reality we participate in. The Christian faith is
distorted if it degenerates into impersonal experience.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            This study on Christian experience is
difficult to focus because (1) natural and supernatural appear to overlap in people
created in God’s image. (2) A given experience with God can contain elements
natural and supernatural. (3) An experience combines objective occurrence and
subjective response.  </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            So we are defining subjective experience
as experience caused by supernatural agency done directly to the person,
whether perceivable by other people or not. It deals with what is not mediated
through the experience of another or conveyed to us indirectly by natural means.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            Our topic includes (a) what should be the
experience of <u>all</u> Christians, (b) what might happen to <u>some</u>
without being normative for all, or (c) what was originally possible for some
but conceivably not experienced thereafter.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><b><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>  </span></b><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>I. Reasons
for Concern on This Issue</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            In one direction . . . </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            A.  <u>We fear dry, lifeless, powerless
religion that comes from misemphasizing objective revelation</u>. When
Christian leaders commit suicide, fail in their marriages and in raising their
children, get involved in significant long-term sin—when we find ourselves all
too vulnerable, we consider whether something is missing that could prevent
such things. Perhaps these problems come from making Christianity too rational.
</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            B.  <u>We fear intolerance that comes from
misemphasizing propositional revelation</u>. Christian groups continually
divide over doctrinal issues. While Christ’s body divides over theoretical,
eschatological, and formal issues, the rest of mankind may not even get
acquainted with Christ himself. Our attitude may turn them away or disillusion
them early on. When our energies turn inward on ourselves, we have little left
to direct outward on the world. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            In the other direction . . . </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><b><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></b></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            C.  <u>We fear doctrinal and practical
excesses that come from misemphasizing subjective experience</u>. Common
subjective experience surely cannot set aside doctrinal differences like those
between the wide range of Christian and quasi-Christian groups.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            D.  <u>We fear false expectancies and
disillusionment that come from misemphasizing subjective experience</u>. Scripture
does not teach a “name-it-claim-it,” “health-and-wealth,” “faith-formula”
Christianity. Faith is not mind over matter. We hesitate to agree that not
having special experiences means we are failing to receive everything God
offers us, that we are not striving for them earnestly enough, that we have something
wrong with our faith, that we have unforgiven sin, or that even our salvation
is in question.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            E.   <u>We fear diversion that comes from
misemphasizing subjective experience</u>. Christ warned his audiences (cp.
Matthew <b>12</b>:38-45, <i>etc</i>.) and his apostles (Luke <b>10</b>:20) not
to get pre-occupied with signs and wonders. Much of what goes under the name “renewal”
are products of modern pop culture with its growing anti-intellectualism,
anti-traditionalism, individualism, emotionalism, even thrill-seeking. This
yearning for experience may represent another form of self-centeredness excused
and encouraged by dressing it in religious garb.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            From one misemphasis, we get pre-occupied
with the medium more than the Message—with the Bible more than with God who
speaks there, with information more than use, with head more than heart. From
the other misemphasis, we get pre-occupied with private effects, which become
the religious equivalent of stimulation dependency—the religious “high.”</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            These fears need not become real, because
they are extremes; so we ask how we should understand the basic, normal,
normative Christian experience without getting into excesses. Frequently truth
lies between extremes, because there is an element of truth in them that causes
them to arise. We look for the larger whole that incorporates whatever truths
lie in the full spectrum of possible Christian experience.  </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            That larger reality is <b>persons in
relationship</b>. Interpersonalism integrates whatever non-interpersonal things
<u>are</u> involved, measures the relative importance of issues, distinguishes
potential from normative, identifies what is irrelevant and false, and guides
our expectations for personal experience.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>II. Christian Experience Is Interpersonal Experience.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            A.  Christian experience <u>combines</u>
objective and subjective.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  In respect to objective revelation
and subjective experience, the normative Christian experience is not either-or.
Since the normal Christian experience with God is interpersonal, there is both
a subjective and objective aspect to it. <u>It is both-and</u>. Christianity
has the character of <i>event</i>; it is something that <i>happens</i> and
continues to happen. Objective plus subjective is not an <b>I-idea</b> connection.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  Of importance here is the role of
scripture and proclamation. Word revelation is not the reality itself, and
propositional revelation does more than lead to ideas, understanding, and
belief. The goal of salvation is personal relationship with God through Christ.
The role of scripture must fit with the goal of salvation. Scripture points to
him of whom it speaks: <i>“Beyond the sacred page [we] see thee, Lord.”</i> The
Bible is the medium, not the Message.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  Furthermore, there seems to be no
biblical basis for saying that in conversion or otherwise God operates only
through the word, statements, propositional revelation. The descriptive content
of Christianity is for all practical purposes available entirely through the
Bible. But there is more to conversion and growth than understanding words and
the concepts they refer to; there is also motivation and affection, which are kinds
of content themselves—contents that can be conveyed in other ways besides
propositionally.  </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  Consequently, besides operating <b>(a)</b>
<u>through the word</u>, God operates also<b> (b) </b><u>through the church</u>,
that is, through other Christians who first appropriate the word into their own
lives. In both cases God is working through the word. He also operates <b>(c) </b><u>through
circumstance</u>. He can plant events in our experience that help prepare us
for responding favorably to the word, that strengthen us for greater tasks, and
that direct our lives in varying degrees of specificness according to his will.
This last operation is <i>aside from</i> <i>the word</i>, though not <i>contrary
to</i> <i>the word</i> or <i>in place of the word</i>. It is possible, but not
inherently necessary. It is indirect operation, not direct supernatural
operation on the person parallel to the message. How frequently God intervenes
this way we do not know because he intervenes invisibly. Finally, with
Christians God operates <b>(d)</b> <u>through the relationship itself</u>. The
previous three activities are <i>indirect</i>; this one is <i>direct</i>. It is
direct in the sense that the relationship is direct. It is direct relationship,
but <i>not perceivable</i> relationship. We know about the relationship by
promise, not by perception (see below). But knowing we are in real relationship
with an all-knowing person has a guiding and empowering effect on our Christian
experience.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            B.   Christian experience combines
objective <u>person</u> and subjective <u>person</u>.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  As to objective revelation and
subjective experience, the normative Christian experience is not just any kind
of both-and. <u>It is an interpersonal kind of both-and</u>, not too subjective
or too objective. Christian experience is natural subjective response by a
person to natural objective influence from another person. It is not <b>I-it</b>
relationship, but <b>I-he </b>(communing) and <b>I-you</b> (worshiping).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  1.  Normative Christian experience
is not impersonal.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        Holy Spirit matters belong
here because Christ promised to send the Spirit and return to us so we would
not be orphans (John <b>14</b>:16-18). Feeling like orphans means feeling that
God is distant, unaware, uninvolved. We conceive of God the Father as being in
heaven and Christ the Son with him, but we have the Holy Spirit here with us
and in us (John<b> 14</b>:17). The omniscience of God and the presence of his
Spirit mean that we are not alone in the world.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        We tend, however, to
depersonalize this Spirit present with us and in us. That is somewhat
understandable because (a) he has no personal name or term of address in
scripture (except “Comforter/Advocate/Paraclete”). (b) Scripture never seems to
address him in second person (“you”). (c) Some versions even mistranslate
pronoun references to him in a few places (Acts<b> 8</b>:16 ASV; Romans<b> 8</b>:26
KJV). (d) The Greek word for “spirit” is <i>neuter gender</i>, which people
sometimes get confused with <i>impersonal “sex” reference</i>. But he is a
person because he does what persons do and is treated like persons are treated. 
</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        <u>The Spirit is not a <b>power</b>,
but a person who has power</u>. He is not God’s energy or an impersonal power
that functions like a force field or an electrical charge. He does not produce
in us the kinds of experiences that forces produce.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        <u>The Spirit is not a <b>feeling</b>,
but a person we feel close to</u>. Feelings belong to Christian experience, but
they are the kinds of feelings that come from personal interactions, not
impersonal ones. Being filled with the Spirit is not a “tingle,” a “buzz,” a
surge of “ecstasy,” an experience of “enthusiasm.”</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        <u>The Spirit is not an <b>attitude</b>,
but a person who influences our attitudes</u>.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        <u>The Spirit is not a <b>principle</b>,
but a person who communicates principles to us</u>.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        <u>The Spirit is not an <b>atmosphere</b>,
but a person who fosters among us a positive interpersonal atmosphere</u>.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        The Spirit’s effects must be
distinguished from the Spirit himself. The effects that come from him are the
kinds that come from interpersonal influence and come in an interpersonal way. We
do not correlate the Spirit with emotion, intuition, miracle, or mystery just
because we cannot fully explain him or his workings.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  2.  Normative Christian experience
is not essentially miraculous.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        The tendency to <i>depersonalize
the Spirit</i> relates to the tendency to <i>supernaturalize Christian
experience</i>. Supernaturalizing means interpreting something natural as
supernatural or caused supernaturally. It comes from one of two sources. The
first is <u>the concept of natural depravity</u>, the belief that each person
biologically inherits a deformed nature caused by Adam’s disobedience. Correcting
that deformity requires supernatural operation. In traditional theology one
aspect of that re-ordering, “re-creating” act is <b>(a)</b> <u>the Spirit’s
supernatural “regeneration” of our deformed nature itself</u>. Although this
kind of regeneration would be done by a person, the operation itself would be
impersonal. What the Spirit would do he would be doing <i>to</i> our nature. He
would not be using personal influence but impersonal power; he would not be
addressing the willing but the capacity for willing. Some theologians speak
also of “a second definite work of grace”: <b>(b) </b><u>the Spirit’s
supernatural “sanctification” that removes the vestiges of inbred sin</u>. This
concept underlies the doctrine of “entire sanctification,” which says that a
sanctified Christian can in principle avoid all sin and lead a life of perfect
love. Conceiving of conversion and growth as essentially supernatural
activities moves Christian concerns into the category of <i>nature</i> (“ontic
being,” hypostasis) instead of keeping them in the categories of <i>action</i>
and <i>relationship</i>.  </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        We have not seen adequate
biblical basis for the doctrine of natural depravity (<i>vs</i>. psychological
depravity). As a result, we have not understood Holy Spirit operations in
conversion and growth as supernatural ones. Interpersonal operations are not
stimulus-response or supernatural. Consequently, we do not include in
subjective experience those kinds of experiences that would be part of
supernatural conversion and development. If something is miraculous, it is not
interpersonal <i>per se</i>. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        Relationship to a supernatural
Person does not make the relationship itself supernatural, and the relating
does not mean supernatural experiences are produced. Human relationship with
God occurs through the capacity we share with him. The image of God in us makes
their relationship possible. Since personhood is something we and God have in
common, it is not above our nature, supernatural, or miraculous.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  3.   Normative Christian experience
is not necessarily perceivable.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        Besides experiences that
derive from <i>the Augustinian-Calvinistic view of mankind</i>, a second source
of supernaturalizing experience is supposing the Spirit’s work is perceivable. Christian
<u>experience with</u> the divine is a different thing from <u>perception of</u>
the divine. We have real relationship with the Father, with Christ, with the
Spirit; but that real relationship is not perceivable in a sensory or
extra-sensory manner. God “manifests” himself to us so we can “perceive” him
and identify who he is. He must move into our realm before we can “sense” him. That
was a reason for the incarnation (John<b> 1</b>:18). No one has ever seen God
in his own nature (Exodus <b>33</b>:20; John <b>1</b>:18; <b>6</b>:46;
Colossians <b>1</b>:15; 1 Timothy<b> 1</b>:17; <b>6</b>:16; Hebrews <b>11</b>:27;
1 Peter <b>1</b>:8; 1 John<b> 4</b>:12, 20). Samson did not know the Spirit had
left him (Judges <b>16</b>:20). If he could have perceived the Spirit himself,
he would have noticed the absence of the Spirit. We <u>will be</u> like Christ
and see him as he is (1 John <b>3</b>:2). We have a <u>direct</u> relationship
that we know about <u>indirectly</u> through promise in the word.  </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        <u>We need a sense of God’s
presence</u>, and we should seek to have it; but we seek an <i>interpersonal
sense of presence</i> rather than a <i>sensory or extra-sensory perception of
presence. </i><u>We gain that sense through activities</u> that reinforce
awareness of his real presence: fellowship with the faithful, Bible reading,
prayer, fasting, meditation—the “spiritual exercises.” Finite awareness of
omniscient presence is conscious response to communication through indirect
means, not involuntary response to direct stimulation.  </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        This approach does not deny
what is <u>possible</u>, as if the Spirit could not give someone a “buzz.” Rather,
it observes what is <u>natural</u> and <u>normative</u> in Christian
experiences, because they correlate with the nature and purpose of the faith. We
are also not arguing that the Spirit no longer does what he used to do. We
simply find no biblical basis for saying that supernatural subjective
experiences <u>ever</u> happened to every Christian. We should not then expect
them or claim to have them if we cannot distinguish them as divine. Consequently,
we must pass to tests for distinguishing Christian experiences from their
alternatives.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>III. Pattern for Testing Christian Experience</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  In our estimation, <i>scripture does
not teach any pattern of supernatural experience for the whole church age</i>. The
New Testament does not say that (1) such experiences would cease with the
apostles, that (2) they would always continue among true believers, that (3)
they would come as “latter rain” after centuries of drought, or that (4) they
would manifest themselves any time the gospel penetrates a new ethnic group. There
is no biblical basis for believing that “the normal Christian experience”
involves supernatural experiences.  </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><b><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            </span></b><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>A. 
Take a first-century viewpoint and apply tests.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  We do well to <i>take a
first-century viewpoint and apply tests</i> Christians then would have had to
apply (1 John <b>4</b>:1-4). That approach keeps us from both unbounded
subjectivism and a spirit of denial. It makes us cautious but not obscurantist.
First-century Christians had to test genuine Christian experience against
competing phenomena. Taking a first-century viewpoint and applying tests
sidesteps the moot question about New Testament teaching on the duration of
supernatural experience.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  The main question is not whether
supernatural experiences occur, but what we make of them. The issue is not <i>the
raw experience</i> itself but our <u>interpretation</u> of <i>what it is</i>,
our <u>explanation</u> of <i>what caused it</i>, and our <u>inferences</u>
about <i>what to make of it</i>. In the modern Christian scene, the problem is
not so much <i>deliberate misleading</i> as it is <i>sincere misreading</i>
that ends up misleading. It is not so often bad motives as it is
misunderstanding scripture and experience. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            B.  Use objective tests for subjective
experiences.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  Gideon used objective <u>signs</u>
with cakes and chevron stew to verify that <i>“it is you that is talking to me”</i>
(Judges <b>6</b>:17). On the road to Damascus, Paul <u>asked for identification</u>
because God does not let demonic entities claim to be his agents (Acts<b> 9</b>:5;<b>
22</b>:8; <b>26</b>:15).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  <u>Let other people help</u> test
our subjective experiences. Other people have not had our experience, but that
does not mean we alone can evaluate it. The crux of the matter is identifying
our experience with <u>the word</u>. Determining what certain biblical examples
were like is an exegetical matter, and other people stand on equal footing with
us in that regard. They cannot deny we had an experience, but they can discern
whether we should equate it with New Testament signs. In some ways, it is harder
for us to be “objective” about our own experiences.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'> IV. Tests for Genuine Christian Experience</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            A.  Definitional test</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  Before anything else we must
determine from scripture what certain Christian experiences really were. <u>Revelation</u>
was more than having an idea come forcibly to mind. <u>Prophesying</u> was more
than “for-telling” what spontaneously struck someone. Some Christians
apparently reason that since they have God’s Spirit, ideas that come to their
minds on Christian matters the Spirit has planted there, particularly if the
idea is new to them, if they are reading the Bible and praying, if they have
first asked God to show them how to understand or what to do. This whole
procedure may be nothing more than supernaturalizing what “pops into my mind”
from the “tacit dimension.” The origin of an idea is hard to account for, but
we do not identify the Spirit with the creative urge that is simply part of
what it means to be in God’s image.  </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  For me to suppose that I have had a
divinely originated subjective experience, I must be able to distinguish it
from experiences I have on matters that are not even religious or Christian. The
responsibility for that lies especially with me because other people have not
had my subjective experience; so they cannot analyze some aspects of it.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  <u>Speaking in tongues</u> means
speaking in actual human languages (xenoglossia), not ecstatic utterance
(glossolalia). Paul uses the expression “tongues of angels” (1 Corinthians <b>13</b>:1),
but as an exaggeration for emphasis akin to other heightened expressions in the
context: <i>“move mountains,” “give my body to be burned </i>[note textual
issue],<i>” “know all mysteries and all knowledge,” “give all my belongings to
feed the poor”</i> (<b>13</b>:2-3). To the Galatians Paul makes another hyperbolic
statement about angels: <i>“If an angel from heaven should preach to you
anything else . . . let him be accursed” </i>(<b>1</b>:8-9). Angels are
spirits, who do not have tongues (Luke <b>24</b>:3; Hebrews <b>1</b>:4, 14) to
make audible languages between themselves. Languages as sound-symbol systems
would require the physicality of angels and their realm. Furthermore, if we
have never heard angels speak, we have a problem identifying our verbal
experience with their speaking. Finally, even angelic languages would have
structure to convey meaning. The nature of <u>interpreting tongues</u> follows
from the nature of tongues and the nature of revelation.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  New Testament material on <u>baptism
in the Holy Spirit</u> does not always indicate a necessary, direct,
supernatural, visible outpouring of the Spirit beyond “the gift of the Spirit”
to all at conversion. The issue is much too involved for treatment here, but we
can stress a point about procedure. We cannot settle the experiential question
without first settling the exegetical one; otherwise, we let personal
experience dictate Christian doctrine. On this question the issue is not
believing the Bible or believing experience, wanting the Spirit or not. The
problem centers on identifying personal experience with New Testament
phenomena. For my part, “baptism in the Spirit” is an alternate expression for “gift
of the Spirit,” normally meaning indwelling presence. It is simply and
significantly interpersonal relationship with God in the person of the Spirit. Any
supernatural manifestation associated with baptism in the Spirit is a
non-uniform effect based on the relationship itself.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  <u>Being slain in the spirit</u>
rests on a misunderstanding of Paul’s Damascus road experience and perhaps some
other indecisive texts. The phenomenon accommodates biblical material to
ambiguous experiences unidentifiable as to cause and pointless as to effect.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  <u>Dreams, visions, and trances</u>
recur in both testaments. The previous items involve understanding the New
Testament as to what the <i>experience as such</i> was. These items involve
interpreting <i>individual experiences </i>to make sure that they are divine in
cause and nature.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            B.  Doctrinal test</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  Since God is consistent, anything he
reveals later harmonizes with what he has established before, especially in
confrontation with opposing agencies. No experience subjective or otherwise can
authenticate a doctrine or practice incompatible with God’s revelation in
scripture (Deuteronomy <b>13</b>:1-5; John <b>16</b>:13-15; Galatians<b> 1</b>:8-9;
Hebrews <b>9</b>:26-28; 1 Peter <b>3</b>:18; Jude 3).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  There are cautions here. (a) The
doctrinal test assumes <u>correct understanding</u> of previous revelation. (b)
The doctrinal test applies only when there has been <u>previous revelation on
the matter at hand</u>, or when (c) the prophet has been wrong on some claim;
nothing else that prophet says should be respected either. (d) The doctrinal
test assumes that the items on which someone errs are <u>sufficiently important</u>
that God would take his Spirit from him on that account.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            C.  Moral test (John <b>9</b>:31, 33; Mark
<b>9</b>:39)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  The moral test does not mean that people
must be morally perfect before they can have supernatural experiences from God.
But Jesus observed that it is not natural for a person to speak against God
soon after relating to God in a special way.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            D.  Practical tests</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  1.  Are we in control of ourselves
(1 Corinthians<b> 14</b>:32)? The Spirit gives control; he does not throw us
out of control.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  2. Do our experiences draw attention
to us (2 Corinthians <b>2</b>:7-9; Matthew<b> 4</b>:3-4) more than glorify God
(Matthew <b>5</b>:16; <b>9</b>:8; <b>115</b>:31; Luke <b>7</b>:16; <b>13</b>:13;
<b>17</b>:15; Acts<b> 4</b>:12; <b>21</b>:17-20) or benefit other people? Are
we trying to get attention? Do our experiences border on exhibitionism? Are
they used for making money (Acts<b> 3</b>:6;<b> 8</b>:18-24;<b> 16</b>:16-18)?</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  3. What results come from our
experiences? Do they always succeed (Deuteronomy <b>18</b>:20-22)? Do they
stand the test of time? </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            E.  Control test</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  What is not distinctive to
Christianity cannot distinguish Christian experience. Some experiences inside
Christianity take place outside it as well. We must assume either that the
beliefs involved are not very important or that the experiences are not
distinctively Christian. Conversely, we must differentiate between the true and
the fake in these other arenas. What looks like our experience may not be the
same thing; it may be meant to deceive the very elect (Matthew <b>24</b>:24).  </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  Testing and evaluating must balance
healthy caution and willing openness. We do not want to be so gullible that we
believe every experience (“spirit”) or so skeptical we cannot be corrected. Because
we want certainty and security, we may be prone to accept any experience that
looks close; because we fear excesses, we may overreact against anything
special. We must be discerning (1 John<b> 4</b>:1) and cautious (1
Thessalonians <b>5</b>:19).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'> V. Alternatives to Genuine Christian Experience</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            The Holy Spirit does not correspond with
the subjective, the affective (feelings, emotions), the intuitive, the
mysterious, or the miraculous. He is a person; so his guidance and empowerment
occur first of all through interpersonal means. The alternatives to a . . . </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            A.  <u>Divine reconstruction</u> of
experiences are frequently impersonal ones.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            B.  Psychological  </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  Here belong <i>psychosomatic
phenomena</i> that illustrate the significant impact that the mind and mental
attitude have on the physical body and <i>vice versa</i>. <i>Altered states of
consciousness</i> involve some curious subjective experiences. <i>Illusion </i>and
<i>warped perception </i>do not compare with Holy Spirit phenomena, but to the
person with mental difficulties they may not be distinguishable. <i>Hallucinations</i>
are not to be confused with visions, seizures with baptism in the Spirit or
being “slain in the Spirit,” catatonic states with trances, and so on.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            C.  Paranormal</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  <i>Paranormal</i> refers to a wide
range of phenomena studied by psychic researchers: telekinesis, precognition,
telepathy, apports, materialization, clairvoyance, clairaudience,
extra-bodily-experiences, hypnotism, poltergeist phenomena, psychometry. Paranormal
may not differ from demonic or psychological, but theoretically it may. Some
people have natural capacities other people do not have. Inasmuch as human
persons have them, the capacities are not supernatural; they are “normal.” Inasmuch
as not all human persons have them, they are not normal, but “<i>para-</i>normal.”</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            We need to be aware of what happens in
arenas outside the Christian setting. Literature about <u>psychological and
psychic research</u> challenges us to distinguish these things from special
Christian experience. Most of us do not realize the range of human potential
and natural possibility, which sets us up for supernaturalizing Christian
experiences. The medical profession continues to gain appreciation for <u>mind-body
interdependence</u> and the extent of control the mind can exert over the body.
Curious experiences occur in quasi-Christian groups and pagan religions. We
wonder whether these are unusual natural phenomena that people interpret in
terms of their differing worldviews. If so, they no more mark Christian
experience than they legitimize any other viewpoint.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            D.  Demonic </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  Appealing to the demonic to explain
Christians’ experiences is probably overdone. Someone who has the promise not
to be snatched out of Christ’s hand (John <b>8</b>:28; <b>10</b>:28-29) would
not likely be unconsciously oppressed or possessed by demonic agencies (cp. 1
John <b>5</b>:18). Nevertheless, Christians do subject themselves to this
danger in extreme efforts to worship by getting rid of personal identity (self),
to becoming zero, to letting go of themselves. Such efforts belong more to
Eastern mysticism than Christian worship. When people create a vacuum that
removes their personal wills, they may be inviting in whatever entities are “out
there”—demons, “extra-terrestrials” (?). People leave the <u>inter</u>personal character
of Christianity and relationship to God by losing touch with their own personal
identity.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            <i>Tests eliminate certain phenomena</i>
from being divine or at least distinctively divine. Tests do not identify which
alternative a given case is, and they do not need to. Our concern is to avoid <i>mis</i>identifying
what we have experienced. <i>The tests help keep us from supernaturalizing our
experiences. </i>They keep us from misinterpreting what may be natural
experience common to many or all people simply as human beings. <i>Tests keep
us from confusing divine and demonic</i>. The underlying test is the
interpersonal one.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            The safeguard is this: <b>if you cannot
distinguish an experience from other possibilities, do not consider it divine</b>.
Don’t strain to be a <i>prophet</i> just because you think your situation needs
one. Don’t try to add authority to your ideas by saying, “God told me.” Don’t
require <i>God’s special leading</i> just because you want special help, and
don’t require God’s special leading to be <i>knowable special leading</i>. Don’t
require more <i>certainty</i> than God intends to provide. Take ambiguous
experiences as data to consider rather than as signs to follow. Affirming the
lesser claim is the safer policy.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            In summary, past objective revelation <i>tests</i>
current objective revelation. Objective criteria <i>test</i> subjective
experience, and subjective experience <i>confirms</i> objective revelation. <i>New</i>
revelation and special divine guidance must be distinguishable from alternative
possibilities. Normal and normative subjective Christian experience is
interpersonal rather than impersonal. Experiences are not ends in themselves;
they are given for interpersonal purposes beyond themselves in edification and
outreach. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;
margin-bottom:0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:center'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>Conclusion</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            Like human experience generally, Christian
experience is faith experience (2 Corinthians <b>5</b>:7). <i>“Walking by
faith, not sight”</i> means conducting our lives by trust more than direct
experience. A trust situation is an interpersonal one because “I” is trusting “you.”
In the Christian application of this principle, we trust the promise of God to
save us, to be with us, to guide and empower us, to protect us from more than
we can handle (1 Corinthians <b>12</b>:13). Even though experiences like Christ’s
appearance to the eleven would certainly <u>help</u> us, we do not <u>need</u>
the direct basis for the certainty that Thomas wanted (John <b>20</b>:24-29). Jesus
told him that people who believe without seeing are more blessed than those who
believe by direct experience. Scripture was written to give adequate reason to
believe through credible testimony like that of Thomas’s fellow apostles (John<b>
20</b>:30-31; cp. Luke <b>16</b>:37-41). God evidently deems that <u>sufficient</u>;
anything else he may decide to do goes beyond what is <u>necessary</u>.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            We approach subjective experience and
objective revelation in this way, remembering that <b>every perversion of
Christianity has been some kind of departure from its interpersonal nature and
purpose</b>.</span></p>
