<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;

style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  1.   Investigate the <u>activities</u>
of the Father, Son, and Holy Spirit (as well as statements about them) in
passages where they are mentioned together, thus observing interaction in real
circumstances.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  2.   Examples of <u>relational activities</u>
are often clearer than <u>abstract statements</u> of relationship. That
procedure minimizes problems created by figurative language, the various
meanings that can be attached to words like “one,” and the possibility of accommodation
to our mode of thinking and being.</span></p>

style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  4.   The trinity should be
understood in terms of interpersonalism.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        a.   Interpersonalism captures
both distinctness and oneness in one picture.    </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        b.   Interpersonal trinity
shows how both difference and oneness relate to each; it shows the <u>sense</u>
in which they are three and one. The distinctness and relationship between the
Father, Son, and Spirit is an interpersonal one: the Son <u>prays</u> to the
Father and the Spirit is <u>another</u> comforter.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>      </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  The trinity is not just any kind of
threeness-in-oneness; it is <u>interpersonal</u> threeness-in-oneness and <u>contemporary</u>
threeness-in-oneness. The point is important because it can take into account
the somewhat overlapping character of even personal identity. Who we “are”—in a
psychological sense at least—is the sum total of all our aspects of being,
action, and <u>relationship</u>. Interpersonal threeness-in-oneness can also
take into account the <i>plural</i>, <i>reciprocal</i>, <i>transactional</i>,<i>
complementary</i>, and <i>compenetrating </i>features of personal relationship.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  Other three-in-one situations are
significantly inadequate, because they lack this interpersonal element even
though they do have contemporary threeness-in-oneness. That is the problem with
emanationism, tritheism, manifestative/economic trinity. They do not involve
the kind or degree of distinction, on the one hand, or oneness, on the other,
that characterizes interpersonal oneness.  </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  Speaking of interpersonal
threeness-in-oneness avoids the verbal entanglements with saying “the Father,
Son, and Spirit are <u>separate</u>,” “they are <u>together</u>,” “they are <u>one</u>,”
and so on. Such statements have at least one term that can carry more than one
meaning; so we can spend a lot of time sorting through those options to clarify
which they mean. The result is a cacophony of confusion filled with subtle
distinctions that leave us cold. “Trinity” ends up dying a death of a thousand
qualifications. Non-interpersonal models do not get at interpersonal gestalt,
and they do not naturally incorporate complementariness.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  Texts like those above in B and C
show that <u>during</u> history Father, Son, and Spirit have interpersonal
threeness-in-oneness. <u>Before</u> and <u>after</u> history they have interpersonal
threeness-in-oneness. Strictly speaking, we cannot show that they have <u>eternal</u>
threeness-in-oneness even though that makes the best sense given what we know
about personal and interpersonal identity. Of course, it cannot be shown either
that somehow they become one person at some time in the future or were one at
some time in the infinite past.</span></p>

style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  The trinity is not just any kind of
threeness-in-oneness; it is <u>contemporar</u>y, <u>interpersonal</u>
threeness-in-oneness. The point is important because it accounts for the
somewhat overlapping character of even personal identity. Who we “are”—in a
psychological sense at least—is the sum total of all our aspects of being,
action, and <u>relationship</u>. Interpersonal threeness-in-oneness also
accounts for the <i>plural</i>, <i>reciprocal</i>, <i>transactional</i>,<i>
complementary</i>, and <i>compenetrating </i>features of personal relationships.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  Other three-in-one situations are
significantly inadequate because they lack this interpersonal element even
though they have contemporary threeness-in-oneness. That is the fundamental
problem with emanationism, tritheism, and manifestative/economic trinity. They
do not involve the kind or degree of distinction, on the one hand, or oneness,
on the other, that characterizes interpersonal oneness. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  Speaking of interpersonal
threeness-in-oneness avoids the verbal entanglements associated with saying
things like “the Father, Son, and Spirit are <u>separate</u>,” “they are <u>together</u>,”
“they are <u>one</u>.” Such statements have at least one term that can carry more
than one meaning; so people spend a lot of time sorting through those options
to clarify which they mean. The result is a cacophony of confusion filled with
subtle distinctions that leave the hearer cold. “Trinity” ends up dying a death
of a thousand qualifications. Non-interpersonal models do not get at
interpersonal gestalt, and they do not naturally incorporate complementariness.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  Texts like those above in II and III
show that <u>during</u> history Father, Son, and Spirit have interpersonal
threeness-in-oneness. <u>Before</u> and <u>after</u> history they also have
interpersonal threeness-in-oneness. Strictly speaking, we cannot show that they
have <u>eternal</u> threeness-in-oneness even though that makes the best sense
given what we know about personal and interpersonal identity. On the other hand,
it cannot be shown either that somehow they become one person sometime in the
future.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>I. Passages raised as difficulties for the above view</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  The Gospel of John has the most
Christological material of any gospel. Revelation, Hebrews, and Philippians
also have “high Christology.”</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  The texts in John are often taken as
disallowing distinctness between Father and Son; yet <b>14</b>:16 is perhaps
the clearest text in the whole Bible on the distinctness between Father, Son,
and Spirit.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  1.   John <b>10</b>:30: <i>“The
Father and I are one”</i></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        The statement means “same” in
kind and purpose; we are “at one” (note: <b>10</b>:31-39). The Son equals the Father
in that he does the Father’s works, is in the Father and vice versa, and the
Father sanctified and sent him (cp. 1 John <b>5</b>:8). The following verse
brings up the Son of God, which involves distinction from the Father.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  2.   John <b>14</b>:7-10; <b>15</b>:24:
<i>“Seeing me is seeing the father.”</i></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        The preceding and succeeding
contexts mention all three (<b>14</b>:7, 9). <i>“The one that has seen me has
seen the One who sent me.”</i> There must be distinction between sender and
sent, yet he says seeing the sent is seeing the sender. So, that expression is
a way of saying, <i>“. . . is as good as.”</i> Sending the Son was for revealing
the Father (cp. John <b>1</b>:18). In seeing what the Son is like, we can see
what the Father is like—insofar as deity can be demonstrated in the material
realm. We best read the passage with an emphasis on <u>me</u> rather than on <u>seen</u>:
<i>“Have I been so long time with you, and you have not seen <u>me</u>? If you
have seen <u>me</u> you have seen [what the Father is like].”</i> In <b>15</b>:23
Jesus says, <i>“Hating me is hating the Father <u>also</u>,”</i> implying distinction
between them. (Is there any connection between “seeing God” in this context and
Jesus’ comment in Matthew<b> 5</b>:8 that the pure in heart will see God?)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  3.   John <b>14</b>:12 + 18;<b> 14</b>:28;
<i>“I go away . . . and I come to you,”</i> taken to mean the </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>coming of the Spirit is the next form (spiritual) of
the Son (physical)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  Compare <b>14</b>:3 with <b>14</b>:18:
</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                              a. = the
Son’s second coming, not the Spirit’s coming.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                       or</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                              b. = the
Spirit’s representative coming for the Son</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                       or</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                             *c. = the
Spirit’s representative coming is the proleptic presence (earnest) till the Son’s
second coming. He comes representatively through the Spirit (on the day of
Pentecost) until he comes personally in the end (parousia).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  The Father will send the Spirit in
Christ’s name (<b>14</b>:26).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  4.   John 14:10, 11, 20: <i>“The
Father is in the Son, and the Son is in the Father.”</i></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        Note <b>10</b>:28; <b>14</b>:20;
<b>17</b>:21-24. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        A-in-b-in-a means close
identification (via love?), not “identicalness.” There is mutuality and
reciprocity. In a rougher analogy, interpersonal influence and love are like
interpenetrating gases blown into the same container.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  These texts in John are often taken
as disallowing distinctness between Father and Son; yet <b>14</b>:126 is
probably the clearest text in the Bible of the distinctness between Father,
Son, and Spirit.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  5.   Deuteronomy <b>6</b>:4: <i>“The
Lord our God is one Lord.”</i></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        a.   It must mean “solicity”
(uniqueness), not singleness. Singleness does not afford a reason to love him; a
person may love God whether God is single or triune. <i>“God is not a unit but
a union.”</i></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        b.   <i>“One”</i>: see Ezekiel
<b>7</b>:5; Job <b>23</b>:13; compare </span><span lang=HE dir=RTL
style='font-size:14.0pt'>יָחִיד</span><span dir=LTR></span><span lang=HE
style='font-size:14.0pt'><span dir=LTR></span> </span><span lang=HE dir=RTL
style='font-size:14.0pt'>בֶּן</span><span dir=LTR></span><span lang=HE
style='font-size:14.0pt'><span dir=LTR></span> </span><span style='font-size:
12.0pt'>(<i>“only son”</i>; Genesis </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              <b>22</b>:16)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  6.   Isaiah<b> 43</b>:11: <i>“I,
even I, am Yahveh; and there is no savior besides me.”</i></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            It is easier to understand passages about
oneness in light of passages about prayer, sending, intercession, and so on,
than it is to understand these activities really as instances of singularity.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            J.   General Observations</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  1.   The trinity is not a
mathematical absurdity; <i>they</i> is not three in the same sense </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>as <i>they </i>are one. The threeness and the oneness
are not at the same level; the threeness is within the oneness.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        a.   <u>Three</u> as to
individual personalities</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        b.   <u>One</u></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><u><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'><span style='text-decoration:none'>&nbsp;</span></span></u></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              (1)    Uniqueness: from
all others proposed as gods (<u>kind</u> in contrast </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                       within a
category)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              (2)    Commonality: of
the same nature and purpose (kind in comparison within a category). In
Galatians <b>3</b>:28 Paul says there is no male or female, bond or free; but
all are one in Christ. <i>One</i> here does not mean “united” or “the same one,”
but everyone is viewed as the <u>same</u> (kind) in Christ.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              (3)    “Loneness”: only
Yahveh is in the category of deity: he is the “only </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                       One”</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              (4)    “Corporateness”: a
whole is bigger than the sum of its parts (unity within the category). John <b>17</b>
records Jesus’ prayer that his disciples would be one as he and the Father are
one. In such a context “one” means “united.” They are “at one.”</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              (5)    Unicity:
complementariness; John <b>17</b> records Jesus’ prayer that his disciples be
one as he and the Father were one. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  2.   Most models neglect the unity
at the expense of the distinctness or <i>vice versa.</i></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  3.   Understanding of the trinity is
through divinely chosen models.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        The reason for sticking with
divinely chosen models is that to do otherwise might construct something that
does not match the reality at crucial points. The idea of a son who is later a
father who is later a grandfather is a model that gets three and one, but it
fails to have them at the <u>same</u> time. That is the mark of
Father-Son-Spirit in John<b> 14</b>:16 and other places.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  4.   Knowledge of the trinity is
partial.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  With the trinity there is distinction
during time, before time, and after time. The oneness of a bucket of water
(before time) from which a cup of water is later taken (during time) does not
explain John<b> 10</b>:30, where Jesus says that he and the Father are one and
says it during the incarnation, when supposedly a monarchianist must admit
there was separateness. That same thing describes their relationship before
time. So, the question becomes one of where we get the idea that their relationship
was different before time from what it was <u>in</u> time. We also have to ask why
there is some problem with their being distinct <u>before</u> as they were <u>in</u>
time.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            K.  Reasons for interest in the trinity
discussion </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  1.   Desire to understand what God
has actually revealed about himself</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  2.   Missions to pagans, Jews,
Muslims, and unitarians: We must understand the issue sufficiently to articulate
it to non-Christians and remove unnecessary stumbling blocks to their accepting
the Christian message.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  3.   Christian unity: some groups
within Christendom as well as individuals in more “mainline” movements object
to the traditional formulation of trinitarianism. We have to decide whether the
alternatives they adopt are sufficiently objectionable to warrant separation,
division, and withdrawal of fellowship.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;
margin-bottom:0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:center'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>Concluding Summaries</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;
margin-bottom:0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:center'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            The trinity involves an <u>interpersonal</u>
threeness-in-oneness. The distinctness between the Father, Son, and Holy Spirit
takes place within that larger oneness. That threeness-in-oneness is simultaneous.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            1.   They have the <u>same nature</u>: deity;
that is, they are not of different kinds or different degrees of a kind—homoousia
(of the same kind, </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ὁμοούσια</span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>) rather than homoiousia (of a similar kind, </span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ὁμοιούσια</span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            2.   They serve <u>complementary roles</u>:
they are not three clones, doing the same thing, </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  working in parallel, or working
against one another.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            3.   They have <u>different rank</u>: they
have order within the whole rather than being of </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  equal authority.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            4.   They are objectively <u>distinguishable</u>.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>There is (1) plurality, (2) contemporaneousness, (3)
complementariness, and (4) compenetration.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  a.   John <b>14</b>:16 shows the
distinction between Father, Son, and Spirit at least </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        during time. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  b.   John<b> 1</b>:1-2 and other
passages show the distinction between Father and Son </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        before the incarnation. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  c.   Revelation shows the
distinction between Father and Son after the ascension. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        It is difficult to prove the <u>eternal</u>
pre-existent and eschatological distinction between them. It is also difficult
to <u>dis</u>prove the createdness of the Son, but createdness is not
positively said about him.</span></p>
