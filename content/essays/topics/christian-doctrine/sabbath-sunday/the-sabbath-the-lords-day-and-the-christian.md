<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;

style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>Introduction</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            Sabbath observance corresponds with
national Israel. God instituted the Sabbath as a memorial of the exodus because
Israel became a nation at the exodus. Sabbath observance does not apply to
those outside of Israel, before Israel, or after Israel, hence, not to
Christians.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            The agenda for Sabbatarians has been to
show that the Sabbath applied outside of Israel. It was supposedly observed
before Israel, outside of Israel, and after Israel because it was a memorial of
the creation, an ever-relevant event to memorialize.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>I. Why Christians Remember <u>Sunday</u></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            The expression <i>“the Lord’s Day”</i> in
Revelation <b>1</b>:10 shows that by the time John wrote Revelation, a specific
day had gained enough prominence to be called his day. The day when Christians
normally met for worship would be the day previous observations identified as
the first day of the week.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            A.  Christ’s resurrection occurred on
Sunday (note Matthew<b> 28</b>:1).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            B.  Many of Jesus’ appearances took place
on Sunday (Luke <b>24</b>:13ff; John<b> 20</b>:11ff).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            C.  The church was established on Sunday
(Leviticus <b>23</b>:15-21 re Pentecost).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify;text-indent:-.5in'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        D.  The practice of early
Christians was to meet on Sunday (1 Corinthians <b>16</b>:1; <b>11</b>:17ff;
Acts<b> 20</b>:7).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  Sunday is different from the Sabbath
day, when Jews gathered for worship; the earliest Christians were Jews. These
two points show an intentional difference between day seven and day one.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            E.   The triumphal entry evidently
happened on Sunday.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'> II. Arguments Against the Christian Observance of the
Old Testament Sabbath</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            A.  New Testament objection: <i>“Don’t let
anyone judge you about food . . . or Sabbath day, which are shadow of things to
come”</i> (Colossians<b> 2</b>:16-17).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            B.  No basis for the Christian practice</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  1.   Sabbatarians think of the Sabbath
as memorializing creation so that it applies to all generations everywhere
throughout history. Three texts do connect the Sabbath with God’s creation
rest: Genesis <b>2</b>:3, noted above; Exodus <b>20</b>:11; and <b>31</b>:17. These
passages, however, do not connect Sabbath with creation as a <u>memorial</u> of
the creation. The <u>manner of observance</u> is the reason th</span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:windowtext'><span class=msoIns><ins
cite="mailto:Virgil%20Warren" datetime="2024-12-26T02:12">es</ins></span>e<span
class=msoIns><ins cite="mailto:Virgil%20Warren" datetime="2024-12-26T02:13"> t</ins></span><span
class=msoIns><ins cite="mailto:Virgil%20Warren" datetime="2024-12-26T02:14">e</ins></span><span
class=msoIns><ins cite="mailto:Virgil%20Warren" datetime="2024-12-26T02:13">xts</ins></span></span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'> connect it with the creation: a <u>weekly</u> <u>rest</u>
on the <u>seventh day</u>. The <u>meaning</u> of the Sabbath comes from its
connection with the exodus, the “Independence Day” for national Israel
(Deuteronomy <b>5</b>:15; Exodus <b>13</b>). Participants were to “remember”
that founding day.<span class=msoIns><ins cite="mailto:Virgil%20Warren"
datetime="2024-12-26T02:16"> </ins></span></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        So there is no adequate </span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:windowtext'><span class=msoDel><del
cite="mailto:Virgil%20Warren" datetime="2024-12-26T02:15">positive</del></span></span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'><span class=msoDel><del cite="mailto:Virgil%20Warren"
datetime="2024-12-26T02:15"> </del></span>evidence from the creation account <u>for</u>
connecting Sabbath observance with creation and so with all humanity permanently.
For comments <u>against</u> the invalidity of the “memorial of creation” view,
see “The Sabbath as a Memorial of the Exodus.”</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        2.   Invalidity
of the “pre-Israel” argument  </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        a.   Genesis <b>2</b>:3
appears at a <u>place</u> in Genesis before the founding of Israel, but it does
not refer to a <u>time</u> before the founding of Israel. The <u>placement</u>
of the comment about God’s hallowing the seventh day is to be distinguished
from the <u>time</u> when the Sabbath was to begin being observed. The Genesis
comment is not a <u>command</u> to observe the Sabbath then but an <u>explanation</u>
about observing it on that day. Moses is writing here from the time viewpoint
of the exodus from Egypt. For his contemporaries, he inserts a comment about Sabbath
at the point where he records the event that served as the pattern for observing
it. The memorial of the exodus from slavery was analogous to God’s earlier rest
from his creation work. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              Furthermore, Genesis<b>
2</b>:3 does not say that God blessed the <u>Sabbath</u> day and hallowed it;
it says he blessed the <u>seventh</u> day and hallowed it. Reading the verse
should stress the word <i>seventh</i> in contrast to other days of the week. Moses’
comment explains why God used the <u>seventh</u> day as the exodus memorial,
why it was <u>weekly</u> rather than annual, together with observing it as a <u>rest</u>
instead of a celebration or feast. Genesis <b>2</b>:3 says that God blessed the
<u>seventh</u> day, but Exodus<b> 20</b>:11 says that God blessed the <u>Sabbath</u>
day. The point evidently is that, having identified the seventh day as the rest
day, Moses can later call it a “Sabbath day,” a rest day.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              Genesis<b> 2</b>:3 deals
with the <u>placement</u> of explanation rather than the time of initial
observance, and it gives an explanation about <u>form</u> rather than meaning. We
do not need to take it as evidence that people observed the Sabbath from the
creation onward. Indeed, there is no example of such an observance before the
exodus.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        b.   Exodus <b>16</b>:23-30 is
before the giving of the Law, but after the beginning of Israel as a nation. The
constitution of Israel as a nation technically occurred at the crossing of the
Red Sea, where, by leaving Egypt, they became a separate nation from the one where
they were a subdivision, a slave class (1 Corinthians <b>10</b>:2: <i>“baptized
to Moses in the cloud and the sea”</i>).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  3.   Invalidity of the Gentile
approach (Exodus <b>20</b>:10)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        Gentiles present in Palestine
were to observe the holidays in keeping with Jewish regulations. The Sabbath law
never applied to Gentiles elsewhere. The Sabbath was a sign between Israel and
God (Exodus <b>20</b>:10;<b> 31</b>:12-17). Those living among the Jews were to
observe the holidays along with the native population. That is a different
thing from Gentiles as Gentiles being called on to observe the Sabbath in
foreign territories.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  4.   Invalidity of the “forever”
approach</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        a.   <i>Forever</i> is a “universal”
word and as such lies within the frame of reference where it stands (Exodus <b>31</b>:16-17).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        b.   Examples: slave legislation
(Exodus <b>21</b>:6); slaves serve “forever” (Philemon 15). See <i>What the
Bible Says About Salvation</i>, pp. 125-26.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  The following diagram summarizes the
argument for universal observance of the Sabbath throughout history.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            C.  After Pentecost, A.D. 30, no one
commended, commanded, or practiced the Sabbath as a Christian observance.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  Rest is appropriate for everyone. It
gives a psychological break and allows people to balance <u>purpose/goal
orientation</u> with <u>existential relationship</u> vertically and
horizontally. Having a break from routine—even a regular <u>set</u> break for a
whole day every week—is a different thing from infusing that “day off” with a
religious significance like memorializing the creation, the exodus, or the
resurrection of Christ.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  Cessation from labor is associated
with God, who rested from creation (Genesis <b>2</b>:3); with Israel, who
rested from Egyptian slavery (Deuteronomy<b> 5</b>:15); and Christians, who
rest from this life’s labors and trials (Hebrews <b>3</b> &lt; Psalm <b>95</b>).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>III. Argument Against Christians Observing Sunday
After the Manner of a Rest</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            A.  No precedent for observing the Lord’s
Day after that fashion</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            B.  No commandment to do so</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            C.  Not governed by the same minutia of
legislation as Sabbath was (and people </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  advocating the Sabbath-Sunday
correlation so observe it)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  1.   Death for desecration: Numbers<b>
15</b>:32-36; Exodus <b>31</b>:4</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  2.   Extreme “no-work” clauses: fire-building
was prohibited (Exodus<b> 35</b>:3), baking also (Exodus <b>16</b>:23); people
were to stay home (Numbers <b>16</b>:29). The prohibition against building
fires meant needing to eat “leftovers” and not cooking meals on Saturday. Those
provisions gave women a break as well as men, who worked in the fields, with
the herds, in business, and in other labors <u>outside</u> the home.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            D.  Sunday and Sabbath memorialize
different things.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  1.   The Sabbath memorializes the exodus,
the beginning of national Israel (Deuteronomy <b>5</b>:15).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        If the Sabbath was a memorial
of the exodus (Deuteronomy<b> 5</b>:15), it was not observed prior to what it memorialized.
If it was a memorial of the exodus, any observance after the exodus would not
qualify as a pre-Israel or Gentile observance. As Paul notes in 1 Corinthians <b>10</b>:1-2,
Israel became a nation at the exodus, not at the giving of the Law a few months
later. Israel became a separate national identity at the Red Sea, not at Sinai.
At the border of Egypt, Israel ceased being a slave class in another nation.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  2.   The Lord’s Day memorializes the
resurrection (perhaps also Pentecost, the beginning of the church). </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        Sabbath pertains to national
Israel. So, it is not observed outside, before, or after national Israel. That
generalization implies the lack of validity for arguments <u>before</u> Israel
based Genesis <b>2</b>:3 and the pre-Israel argument from Exodus <b>16</b>, for
arguments <u>outside</u> Israel based on Gentile observance in the land of
Israel, and for arguments <u>after</u> Israel based on Sabbath as a memorial to
creation and based on the “forever” argument.</span></p>
