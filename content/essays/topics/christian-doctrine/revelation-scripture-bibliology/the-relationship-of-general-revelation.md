<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;

<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;
margin-bottom:0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:center'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;
margin-bottom:0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:center'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>Introduction</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;
margin-bottom:0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:center'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            Interpreters commonly take Romans<b> 1</b>:18ff
as an instance where Paul lays out the theistic argument. Then they conclude
that it is possible to establish the existence of the one infinite, true God
and certain of his attributes.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            This study proposes that Paul is not presenting
an argument for God’s existence based on general revelation. Rather, he
declares from <u>special revelation</u> the lostness of all people, against
whom God’s wrath is revealed. Romans <b>1</b>:18ff views collective humanity
from the creation till the coming of the gospel. He presents (1) a collective
anthropological viewpoint (2) in a revelational framework (3) from a total
historical perspective.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            Since people originally had special
revelation about God, the most Paul intends in <b>1</b>:20 is to say that
general revelation was sufficient to <u>confirm</u> that original special
revelation and to keep people from relinquishing the knowledge of God they had received
originally. Humankind is corporately guilty, and then individually guilty
because no person is no less a sinner than anyone else.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            Whatever possibility there may be for
establishing a theistic philosophy from general revelation, Paul does not set
forth proof to that end, but to confirm the truth of the special revelation
given originally, along the way, and now again in the gospel era.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'> I. Anthropological viewpoint of Romans <b>1-3</b></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            A.  The usual view: pagan, moralists, Jews:
<b>1</b>:18-<b>3</b>:20</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  F. F. Bruce presents the common way
of viewing Romans <b>1</b>:18-<b>3</b>:20 when he outlines it under the heading
<i>Sin and Retribution: The Universal Need Diagnosed</i>: </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  1.   The pagan world (<b>1</b>:18-32)
</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  2.   The moralist (<b>2</b>:1-16) </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  3.   The Jew (<b>2</b>:17-<b>3</b>:8)
</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  4.   All people found guilty (<b>3</b>:9-20).<span
style='position:relative;top:-3.0pt'>1</span> </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>I. B. Grubbs handled the section in similar fashion
under the heading <i>Universal Need of the Salvation Offered in the Gospel
Evinced from the Sinfulness of the Whole Race</i>: </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  1.   The moral condition of the
Gentile World (<b>1</b>:18-32) </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  2.   General considerations
applicable alike to Jews and Gentiles, preparatory to </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        the special reference to the
moral condition of the Jews (<b>2</b>:1-16) </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  3.   Direct reference to the Jews
embodying evidence of their sinfulness (<b>2</b>:17-29) </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  4.   Comparison of Jews with
Gentiles, showing the former to be superior as to privileges enjoyed, yet not as
to their moral condition before the Law.<span style='position:relative;
top:-3.0pt'>2</span> </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>Sanday and Headlam in the <u>International Critical
Commentary</u> did the same, only in two steps rather than three.<span
style='position:relative;top:-3.0pt'>3</span> Most commentators approach the
first section of Romans in this manner.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            These commentators have in common the same
perception of Paul’s movement of thought from <b>1</b>:18-<b>3</b>:20. Using
Bruce’s terminology, we may diagram the <u>argument</u> in this way.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;
margin-bottom:0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:center'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>pagans’    +    moralists’    +    Jews’    =   universal</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;
margin-bottom:0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:center'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>need                 need               need              need</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            B.  The suggested view</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            F. F. Bruce said with respect to this portion
of Romans, <i>“Paul’s aim is to show that the whole of humanity is morally
bankrupt, unable to claim a favorable verdict at the judgment bar of God,
desperately in need of His mercy and pardon.”</i><span style='position:relative;
top:-3.0pt'>4</span> There can be no question that Bruce was correct in his
analysis of the point <u>that</u> Paul is making in these three chapters, but the
question is about the <u>basis</u> on which Paul makes that point. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            While the usual outline is partially
accurate, the text seems to require a different progression of thought by different
diagram:</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:8.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;
margin-bottom:0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:center'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'><img width=242 height=196
src="/cir-library/images/essays/the-relationship-of-general-revelation/image001.jpg"></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:4.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>Under this arrangement Paul begins with people collectively
(<b>1</b>:16) as the objects of God’s displeasure because of unrighteousness
and ungodliness (<b>1</b>:18). Then he moves to moralists whether Jews or
Greeks (<b>2</b>:10) who might claim exception to that characterization. Finally,
he comes to Jews specifically (<b>2</b>:17ff) and removes all doubt as to
whether they can claim exception. Under that pattern, chapter <b>3</b>:9-20 is
not a conclusion inductively reached, but a reaffirmation of Paul’s original
proposition defended against objectors. He does not conclude that all people are
separated from God because parallel parts of them are individually separated
from him. Rather, on the basis of the universal call of the gospel (<b>1</b>:16-17),
he affirms that everyone is separated from God and defends that proposition
against any who seek exemption by claiming to be better than others. The <i>“God’s
righteousness”</i> (<b>1</b>:17) revealed in the gospel is the concept—even the
very expression—he returns to in<b> 3</b>:21ff.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            The usual analysis of Romans <b>1-3</b> is
inductive while the suggested one is deductive. Under the inductive approach
Paul deals separately with pagans, moralists, and Jews under the theme of
lostness because of sin. Under the deductive view he begins with the fact that
all people, barbarians and Greeks (<b>1</b>:14), Greeks and Jews (<b>1</b>:16),
need <i>“God’s power to <u>salvation</u>” </i>(<b>1</b>:16), <i>“God’s
righteousness”</i> (<b>1</b>:17). What Paul declares in <b>1</b>:18-32 Greeks
and Jews would want to limit to barbarians; so, to discriminating Greeks and
Jews he says beginning in <b>2</b>:1, <i>“You that judge other people are no
better than they are.”</i> Of Jews particularly he says, <i>“You live
inconsistently with the Law you believe and teach others”</i> (<b>2</b>:21). Not
even they can escape God’s displeasure.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            <b>(1)</b> Jews that snub uncircumcised
Gentiles are themselves “uncircumcised” because of transgression (<b>2</b>:25),
so they are no better than the Greeks that did not receive the Law. It is not
receiving the Law but doing it that makes people righteous (<b>2</b>:26-27,
12-15). So, Jews cannot claim superiority over Greeks. <b>(2)</b> Greeks in
turn that claim to be “wise” and condemn foolish barbarians likewise condemn
themselves with their own condemnation; they practice the same gross immorality
and idolatry present among the most depraved barbarians. (Note the parallelism
in <b>1</b>:14: Greeks-barbarians, wise-foolish.)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  1.   Positive evidence from the text
</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            The first evidence for the deductive
arrangement comes from the text itself. Paul begins establishing the universal
perspective as far back as<b> 1</b>:8 when he thanks God for the Romans’ faith
as known throughout the world. In <b>1</b>:13 he expresses his desire to have
fruit among them, even as among the rest of the Gentiles. He is debtor to <i>“Greeks
and barbarians”</i> (<b>1</b>:14). From the Jewish perspective <i>“Jew and
Greek”</i> is a complete list (<b>1</b>:17). The gospel is then directed to all
humanity as the power of God unto <u>salvation</u>, because the gospel reveals
God’s righteousness to all people. If the gospel is the news of salvation for
all people, then all people must need to be unsaved. If it reveals the
righteousness of God that the righteous have by faith (<b>1</b>:17b), then all people
are <u>unrighteous</u> in need of <u>salvation</u>. This fact of
unrighteousness is <u>declared</u> (<b>1</b>:16-17), then <u>described</u> (<b>1</b>:18-32),
then <u>defended</u> (<b>2</b>:1-<b>3</b>:20). Having established a universal
perspective in <b>1</b>:8-17, Paul announces that <i>“God reveals his
displeasure from heaven against everyone’s ungodliness and unrighteousness.”</i>
Everybody <i>“sees heaven,”</i> not just pagans. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  2.   Negative evidence from the text</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            Negatively the reader may observe that
Paul nowhere says he is talking about “pagans”; he is talking about people. The
unrighteousness by which they hinder the truth may vary in degree, but it is
unrighteousness still.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  3.   Paul’s avowed revelation
framework</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            What Paul writes in Romans <b>1-3</b>
issues from a <u>special revelation</u> framework. A means of obtaining God’s
righteousness has been revealed in the gospel; <u>that</u> is the good news. It
is good news because it communicates something everybody needs to know: there
is salvation now. Salvation for everybody implies the lostness of everybody
otherwise.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            Paul himself would not have known that all
people are lost had it not been for the special revelation in the gospel. He
had previously thought that people could save themselves by keeping the Law. When
the gospel came, it showed that such was not the case, but that everyone is a
sinner and therefore lost. Not only, then, does the <u>Roman epistle</u> begin with
a deductive framework by reference to the gospel, but <u>Paul’s whole life</u>
was a deductive result of special revelation. Since Paul begins with a
revelational framework, he deals with a deductive matter, and the content of
that revelation involves precisely the fact that everyone is lost; otherwise,
not everyone would need the salvation that gospel revelation announces.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  4.   The blurring of categories</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            Another observation that supports the
suggested arrangement of thought is the blurring of categories between pagans,
moralists, and Jews. Under an inductive pattern we would not expect such a
phenomenon, but it fits with a pattern of narrowing categories. <b>(a)</b>
While talking about all people (large square), a presenter may mention
something peculiar to Jews because Jews (small square) are a part of all people.
While talking generally about </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;
margin-bottom:0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:center'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'><img width=108 height=111
src="/cir-library/images/essays/the-relationship-of-general-revelation/image002.jpg"></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:4.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:4.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>moralists (intermediate square), Paul may speak of
Jews, who are included among them. <b>(b)</b> Conversely, while dealing with
the inner squares, one would not speak of something from which they are exempt.
<b>(c)</b> Thirdly, the transition from larger to smaller squares can be more
blurred, and there may be a wavering back and forth as the author moves toward
a more restricted reference. The reason is that the edge of the moralist square
is, so to speak, superposed over the all-people square.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            The blurring of these categories appears
in the fact that some of the sins listed in <b>1</b>:18-32 were committed by
moralists and Jews as well as barbarians. In such instances, the reader’s mind
moves to the center of the large box, which is also within the box for Jews and
moralists. The point of the sin list becomes this: some sins are common to everybody
while others are limited to some, but the definition of sinners does not depend
on how many sins they have committed, which ones, gross or not so gross. Sinners
are sinners because they have sinned.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            Sexual immorality (<b>1</b>:26) was not
peculiar to pagans; neither was covetousness, envy, deceit (<b>1</b>:29),
boasting (<b>1</b>:30), lack of mercy (<b>1</b>:31). On the other hand,
covenant breaking is more connected with Jews than pagans. So, pagans are not
the only ones condemned in <b>1</b>:18-32; all people come under God’s displeasure.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  5.   The historical perspective</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            The historical perspective that the next
section handles, also makes it evident that there is no basic distinction
between Jews and Gentiles from a collective viewpoint, because the Jews’
ancestor Abraham came out of an idolatrous pagan situation (Joshua <b>24</b>:2)
to become father of the faithful.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            B.  Relationship to the question of
theistic philosophy</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            If Paul’s anthropological viewpoint is
collective rather than individual as he begins in <b>1</b>:18, his approach is
deductive rather than inductive. Since his approach is revelational (<b>1</b>:16-17),
it is also deductive. If his approach is deductive, it is not primarily philosophical
but revelational. Paul does not first establish a natural theology and put a
revealed theology on top of it. Rather, he begins with the revelation that everyone
needs the salvation proffered in the gospel and describes the character of lost
people as evidenced by their deeds, defending that proposition against
disclaimers by showing that they are basically no different than the people
they contrast themselves to.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>II. Historical perspective</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>        A. The general pattern</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            A second matter of importance is that
Romans <b>1</b>:18ff stands in a historical framework that briefly
recapitulates the whole history of God’s dealing with people and the general
pattern of recurring apostasy among them collectively. Paul is not speaking of
a characteristic process that goes on in<i> </i><u>individuals at any time</u>,
but of the process that has occurred during the <u>whole history</u> of <u>people
collectively</u> after special revelation was given in the beginning and
repeatedly thereafter.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            There are several aorist verbs in<b> 1</b>:18-32
that form a historical sequence in the past. Paul does not use a series of
timeless present indicatives as if speaking of a characteristic recurring
process with each individual. In <b>1</b>:19 <i>“God manifested it to them”</i>;
in <b>1</b>:21 <i>“having known God, they did not glorify him as God but became
vain”</i>; <i>“they became fools”</i> and <i>“changed the glory of God into a
likeness. . . .”</i> So, God gave them up to uncleanness to dishonor their
bodies (<b>1</b>:24-25), to vile passions contrary to nature (<b>1</b>:26-27),
to a reprobate mind to do things that are not fitting (<b>1</b>:28-32).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            As a historical pattern, then, Paul begins
in the <u>present</u>: the righteousness of God is being revealed in the gospel
(<b>1</b>:16-17). He passes to a <u>timeless</u> recurring pattern: God continually
reveals his displeasure from heaven against unrighteousness; the knowledge of
God is constantly manifest among humankind; the invisible things about him are
always clearly seen. He moves to the <u>past</u>: they knew God; they did not
glorify him as God; they became vain, foolish, and darkened; they changed the
glory of God; they refused to have him in their knowledge; God gave them up. Finally,
he returns to the <u>present</u>: they know the ordinance of God; they sin;
they consent to sin (<b>1</b>:32-<b>2</b>:1ff).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            B.  Presence of an original special
revelation</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            God gave special revelation in the
beginning. The Old Testament records that fact. Since Romans <b>1</b>:18-32 is
a historical summary of mankind’s collective apostasy from the time of creation
and their being given up by God subsequently, the interpreter is justified in
prefixing revelation to humankind’s general apostasy of which Paul speaks. As a
matter of history, God did deal with people directly in the beginning before
their defection. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            We need not bring that fact to the
passage, because Romans <b>1</b>:21 refers to that original special revelation,
<i>“They knew God”</i>; in <b>1</b>:25 they exchanged that truth about him for
a lie; <b>1</b>:28 says they refused to have God in their knowledge. Of course,
we might interpret that knowledge and truth about God—which they refused to
keep—as a knowledge of his eternal power and deity (<b>1</b>:20) and
incorruptibleness <b>(1</b>:23) as perceived solely from what God made (<b>1</b>:20).
It would be a knowledge that could have caused them to glorify him, be thankful
(<b>1</b>:2), and worship him (<b>1</b>:25).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            But Paul seems to be thinking along
another line. <i>“The invisible things of him . . . are clearly seen . . . that
they may be without excuse . . . because . . . they became vain in their
reasonings . . . ” </i>(1:21-22). Paul’s core statement is that their knowledge
of God from general revelation was sufficient to make them without excuse for <u>becoming
vain</u> in their reasonings. His point is the very reverse of being without
excuse for <u>not leaving their vain</u> reasonings, for not enlightening their
senseless heart, for not leaving their foolishness, for not exchanging the
likeness of an image for the glory of the incorruptible God. Instead, they were
without excuse for exchanging the truth of God for a falsehood, for refusing to
have God in their knowledge, for becoming vain, darkened, foolish, and
idolatrous. General revelation made them without excuse for leaving the special
revelation God had given them.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            The people Paul speaks about were not
originally vain, foolish, darkened, ignorant idolaters. They knew better at
first. Paul does not specify that their originally knowing better came from
general revelation. God did not continually reinforce the original revelation
by his personal presence with every person or with every generation after Adam
went out from his presence. The descendants of earliest people turned aside
from their knowledge of God originally given, passed down in race remembrance,
and confirmed by the world around them. General revelation was clear enough
that these people should not have supposed that some other view of God was
better than the one they had received. Nature is easier to explain in terms of
the God they had known than in terms of what they substituted for him. Confirmatory
evidence in a revelational deductive framework greatly differs from primary
proof in an empirical inductive framework. Paul refers to the former and leaves
the possibility of the latter untouched in this context.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            In light of Paul’s point that the created things
sufficed for keeping people from becoming vain, the manifestation meant in <b>1</b>:19b
is special revelation. <i>“To make manifest”</i> refers to special revelation
in other passages. Romans<b> 3</b>:21 is especially to the point, <i>“But now
apart from the Law a righteousness of God <u>has been manifested</u>, being
witnessed to by the Law and the prophets.”</i> Since <b>3</b>:21 returns to the
thought of <b>1</b>:17, no difference of meaning exists between </span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>φανερόω</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:
12.0pt'> </span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>(<i>phaneroō</i>, “manifest”) in
<b>3</b>:21 and </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ἀποκαλύπτω</span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'> (<i>apokalyptō</i>, “reveal”) in<b> 1</b>:17.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            In <b>1</b>:19a what is it, then, that is
manifest among humankind? <i>“The known about God”</i> that is <i>“manifest
among people”</i> refers to the awareness of God that they manifest even in
their perverted worship. Pagan idolatry, though a perversion of God’s real
nature, witnesses to the fact that “deity” is something everyone knows about. There
is no knowledge more universally manifest than the concept of deity (<b>1</b>:19a).
The reason is that God personally manifested himself to people in the beginning
(<b>1</b>:19b).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>             The knowledge of God is <u>manifest</u>
among people because God <u>manifested</u> it to them. <i>Manifested</i> is
aorist indicative, not continuous action; so the word is better suited to
special revelation than to general revelation (continuous action). The text
does not say that the knowledge of God is manifest among people because God <u>manifests</u>
it to them through general revelation; because general revelation is continuous
everywhere.<span style='position:relative;top:-3.0pt'>5</span></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            Furthermore, Paul says, <i>“the knowledge
of God <u>is manifest</u>”</i> (</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>φανερόν</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ἐστιν</span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>, <i>phaneron estin</i>). He does not say, <i>“the
knowledge of God <u>is (being) manifested</u>”</i> (</span><span lang=EL
style='font-size:12.0pt'>φανεροῦται</span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>, <i>phaneroutai</i><u>).</u>
The knowledge of God is <u>obvious among people</u> in their behavior, not that
the knowledge of God is <u>being manifested to them</u> (in general
revelation). The whole of<b> 1</b>:19 means that people’s knowledge about God
is obvious from the way they live. Something so obvious now originally resulted
from special manifestation by God in the past. If that manifestation occurred
at a certain time, then it stands in contrast to another time or to a
continuous process. If so, Paul is referring to original special revelation
rather than to continuing general revelation.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            If this construction captures Paul’s
meaning in <b>1</b>:19, then Paul himself appeals to special revelation rather
than to general revelation as the first explanation for the manifest knowledge
of God among people. Having appealed first to special revelation for that
explanation, Paul can be appealing to general revelation in <b>1</b>:20 only as
confirmatory evidence. His point in the appeal says nothing about the
possibility of constructing an argument for the existence of the one true God
from the data of general revelation <u>alone</u>.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            The whole of <b>1</b>:18-32 may be put
together as follows. In the present time, special revelation in the gospel
declares the righteousness of God, the sinfulness of people, and the means of
reconciling them to God (<b>1</b>:16-17). Whereas <u>special revelation</u>
always reveals <u>God’s righteousness</u>,<span style='position:relative;
top:-3.0pt'>6</span> <u>general revelation</u> reveals especially <u>God’s power</u>
and that continuously (<b>1</b>:20). <u>Social revelation</u><span
style='position:relative;top:-3.0pt'>7</span> reveals particularly <u>God’s displeasure</u>
(<b>1</b>:18).<span style='position:relative;top:-3.0pt'>8</span> The manifest
knowledge of God among people even in its perverted form witnesses to an
original disclosure of God’s nature in special revelation. General revelation
in nature has surrounded people with confirmatory evidence that should have
kept them from turning aside to less adequate views of God than they originally
had (<b>1</b>:19-20). So, knowing God from special revelation conformable to
general revelation, people had no excuse for their apostasy and so God gave
them up. The following diagram summarizes the description.<span
style='position:relative;top:-3.0pt'>9</span></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;
margin-bottom:0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:center'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'><img width=325 height=202
src="/cir-library/images/essays/the-relationship-of-general-revelation/image003.jpg"></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><u><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'><span style='text-decoration:none'>&nbsp;</span></span></u></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            B.  Relationship to theistic philosophy</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            The central point of Romans <b>1</b>:20-21
is that the <i>“made things”</i> render people inexcusable for becoming vain. Such
a use of general revelation differs from constructing a theistic proof since it
appeals to the creation to <u>confirm</u> what people had known through special
revelation before they became vain. Theistic philosophy is based on general
revelation alone.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;
margin-bottom:0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:center'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>Conclusion</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;
margin-bottom:0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:center'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            What view we take of Romans <b>1</b> must
contribute to the conclusion that all have sinned (<b>3</b>:23). Paul
characterizes the human race historically as having turned from the true
knowledge of God they had originally (chapter <b>1</b>) and defends that
characterization against anyone who would claim exception (chapter <b>2</b>). So,
in chapter <b>2</b> those who come on the scene after the apostasy, Paul
condemns for inconsistency between behavior and (1) <u>conscience</u> or (2) <u>revelation</u>
depending on whether the individual is a pagan or Jew. No individual in the
present situation can claim exception to the lost condition of people
collectively, the historical explanation of which eventuation Paul traces in
Romans <b>1</b> from the beginning to the present. Anyone along the way who has
drifted from special revelation stands condemned on the same principle as those
who originally did so.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            The Christian framework is deductive
because special revelation has taken place. A non-revelation framework is
limited to induction. In light of Romans <b>1</b>, we suggest then, that Paul’s
opposition to reason in other portions of his writings is not (1) because
reason should not be used, or (2) that the use of reason conflicts with
subservience to God, or (3) that revelation is not reasonable or rational, or
(4) that reason is incapable of establishing the existence of one infinite,
good God. While reason might be able to establish a <u>true</u> knowledge of
God—albeit quite limited, reason establishes an <u>inadequate true</u>
knowledge of him and so is aside from Paul’s purposes as a missionary of the
good news about <u>salvation from sin</u>. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            The plight of people without revelation is
not necessarily (1) that they lack the capacity to construct a valid argument
for the existence of the one true infinite good God or (2) that they are
ontically incapable of appreciating, assenting, or actively committing themselves
to the fact established by rational empiricism.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            Other reasons hinder this: (1) lack of
motivation to construct an argument that puts the center of attention outside themselves
and (2) the willful refusal to accept what is true because of its implications.
The complexity of the argument affords many opportunities to question, to
suggest other possibilities, to be irrational.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            The problem for people without revelation
is that they do not have the proper conclusion before they begin; so they have
no way of knowing that their conclusion is wrong when they get to it. After they
have constructed their own argument from their own facts and have arrived at their
own conclusion, they are as unwilling as Christians are to give up that
conclusion. They believe it is true even when they see the contrasting proper
conclusion and the argument is laid out for them. Being defensive of their own view,
they do not focus their attention on what a theistic philosopher is saying. They
fix half of their attention on constructing a rebuttal. They feel that they and
God’s spokesmen are on equal footing and that they themselves are as capable of
discovering truth as anyone else. The difficulty does not lie in the ability to
manipulate rationally the facts common to both them and the theist. The problem
is that they lack the theist’s guidelines for putting them on the correct path.
The reason theists can get to the conclusion is that they know what it is. The
difference between “revelation people” and pagans is the revelation that
Christians have; but in constructing theistic philosophy, the whole point is to
do it without the aid of revelation, which puts a Christian back on common
ground with the pagan. To operate otherwise is to change the process from
philosophy to theology, from general revelation to special revelation.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            Three possibilities exist for theistic
philosophy and the use of general revelation in Romans <b>1</b>. (1) From
general revelation it is possible to <u>construct</u> a true, albeit
inadequate, quite limited, theistic philosophy. (2) From general revelation it
is possible to <u>confirm</u> special revelation, but not to establish theism
on general revelation as primary proof. (3) While it may be possible to
construct a true, but inadequate, quite limited, theistic philosophy from
general revelation as primary proof, it is not Paul’s intention to do so in
Romans <b>1</b>. This study has chosen the third alternative because it
represents the least truth claim.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>could be an
ingressive aorist signifying the beginning of continuous general revelation,
but that possibility does not seem likely since the next verses use present
tenses rather than ingressive aorists.         </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            <span style='position:relative;top:-3.0pt'>6</span>The
righteousness of God was revealed in the original special revelation in the
fact that God judged the sins of Adam, Eve, and Cain.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            <span style='position:relative;top:-3.0pt'>7</span>“Social
revelation” here refers to the recompenses God decrees on those who turn from
him: he “gives them up.” While social disorder and strife could be considered
part of general revelation, it is here made a separate category from special
revelation through direct means and from general revelation indirectly through
nature. Since people are not a part of impersonal nature, revelation “through people”
differs from revelation through nature. Distinguishing a third mode of
revelation seems helpful for conceptualizing the gamut of God’s means for
making himself known.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            <span style='position:relative;top:-3.0pt'>8</span>Every
religion by some system of sacrifice, appeasement, and the like, implies people’s
gnawing dread of estrangement from God and a desire to be in favor with him.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            <span style='position:relative;top:-3.0pt'>9</span>As
regards people who lived after the apostasy had occurred and therefore had no
access to unperverted special revelation, Paul says that God overlooks it (Acts
<b>17</b>:30; cp. Acts <b>14</b>:16) and judges them by consistency between the
way they live and their conscience where it is leading them in conformity with
a revelation they no longer have (Romans <b>2</b>:12-16). Special revelation is
now coming again, and people are to repent (Acts <b>17</b>:30-31).</span></p>
