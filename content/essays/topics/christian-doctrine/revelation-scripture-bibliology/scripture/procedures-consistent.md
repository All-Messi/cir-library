<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='text-align:center'><b><span

<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:center'><span

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt;
line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>            On the one
hand, a totally reliable text in principle represents the guiding
characteristic of “scripture.” On the other hand, a certain amount of
variability may happen in the manner by which biblical writings originated and passed
down. Two assumptions underlie the suggestions below. (a) We need not invoke the
supernatural as long as natural means can accomplish the purposes. God does not
generally do for people what they can do for themselves. (b) The concern is
reliability of the <u>information</u>, not prescription of <u>forms</u>
(words).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt;
line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>           1.    <u>Use
of source material</u>. Writing a plenarily inspired book has nothing to do
with the manner in which the information came to God’s spokesman. If there were
accounts he could access, God need not reveal such materials to him. References
to resources no longer extant appear in Joshua through 2 Chronicles, showing
that resource usage occurred. The following works make a relatively full list.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>            Some
above references indicate sources written by inspired men: the Prophecy of
Ahijah the Shilonite, The Visions of Iddo the Seer, and so on. Others were
typical chronicles of the nation’s activities. They need not have had more than
providential oversight because such information could be collected, organized,
and written without supernatural aid. Many sources could have been incorporated
into the canonical literature that cites them insofar as they made any lasting
contribution.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>            A
second use of resources is the adoption and adaptation of non-Israelite
materials in hymnody and legal codification. Snatches of Ugaritic poetry are
reminiscent of certain Old Testament psalms. David, Moses, Solomon, and others
could have adapted whole pieces to the worship of Yahveh by deleting the pagan god
and omitting inappropriate portions. The psalms become appropriate in the new
setting. In modern times Christians have done that with <i>“You’ll Never Walk
Alone”</i>; <i>“What the World Needs Now Is Love, Sweet Love”</i>; <i>“Bridge
Over Troubled Waters”’</i>; <i>“No Man Is an Island”</i>; <i>“Cleanse Me”</i>; <i>“You
Light Up My Life”</i>; <i>“I Believe in Music”</i> (insert “Jesus”); <i>“I
Believe in Love”</i> (Mac Davis). We would expect a limited amount of such
borrowing even as there is a limited amount of it today.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>            Legal
codes offer another possibility of adaptation. Sacrificial categories similar
to those in Leviticus have shown up among other Near Eastern cultic documents. Some
civil regulations are likewise common to other peoples. Such matters do not
detract from their rightness, appropriateness, or divine authority any more
than elders in the church would be non-essential simply because they were part
of the Jewish synagogue form.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>            2.   <u>Use
of amanuensis</u>. Baruch as Jeremiah’s scribe brings up another consideration for
the variability and looseness of God’s oversight in producing the Old Testament
scripture. Depending on the degree of freedom given to assistants, an author’s
work might vary in style in different parts. Such matters pertain to longer
books and to authors that may have produced several literary pieces. The use of
amanuenses applies especially to New Testament criticism since they varied from
epistle to epistle, the author himself penning one or another as well.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>            3.   <u>Various
editions of a work</u>. The Book of Jeremiah went through at least three
editions. The information behind Joshua through 2 Chronicles existed in at
least two levels, the present Old Testament books being more abbreviated
editions of those histories.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>            4.   <u>Editorial
activity</u>. Up to this point the procedures have dealt with the originating,
or essential, author. Editorial activity comes after the fact; yet the product
is viewed as the original author’s work. The alterations are minor deletions
due to space limitations, clarification through rewording and punctuation
changes, and the like.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>            In
Old Testament studies, editorial activity may explain a number of supplementary
glosses for <b>(a) </b><u>updating</u> obsolete place names (Genesis <b>10</b>:12;
<b>14</b>:2, 3, 7, 8, 15, 17; <b>16</b>:14; <b>23</b>:2; <b>35</b>:19; <b>36</b>:1;
Joshua <b>15</b>:9, 10, 13, 25, 49, 54, 60; <b>20</b>:7; <b>21</b>:22; Judges <b>1</b>:10,
11, 23; 2 Chronicles <b>1</b>:27) and archaic vocabulary. Extensive vocabulary
modernization may be questionable, however, since many such terms were not
altered as, for example, in the headings of the psalms. The Septuagint does not
render <u>correctly</u> <i>“To the Choir Master”</i> in Psalm <b>44</b>, <i>“To
the Lilies” </i>in Psalm <b>80</b>, <i>“According to the Maidens”</i> in Psalm <b>46</b>.
</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>            Included
under editorial activity are <b>(b)</b> <u>explanatory notations</u>. References
that presuppose a much later viewpoint likely indicate editorial activity on
the original record. One case may be Judges <b>18</b>:20, <i>“He and his sons
were priests to the tribe of Dan until the day of the captivity of the land.” </i>We
think of the fall of the Northern Kingdom several centuries later, in 722 B.C. Several
places speak of something that continues <i>“to this day.”</i> Those of special
importance are Genesis <b>35</b>:20; Deuteronomy <b>3</b>:14; Joshua <b>15</b>:63;
Judges <b>1</b>:21; 1 Samuel <b>27</b>:6; 1 Kings <b>8</b>:8; <b>9</b>:21;<b>
12</b>:19; 2 Kings <b>8</b>:22; <b>16</b>:6; <b>17</b>:23, 41. Explanatory
notes are Deuteronomy <b>2</b>:10-12, 20-23. The different times of <i>“to this
day”</i> may reflect different editorial viewpoints (1 Kings <b>12</b>:19
before fall of Israel; 2 Kings <b>17</b>:23, 41 several generations after the
fall of Israel). The problem remains, however, of why such ambiguous notations
would have been added since they would become confusing later to people that
did not know the scribe that inserted them. They might have marginal notes on a
personal copy preserved during the time of the Babylonian captivity. Later
copyists might think the insertions came when the original scribe checked back
through the work and found them missing.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>            A
more satisfactory explanation is that <i>“to this day”</i> is comparable to <i>“beyond
the Jordan,” </i>which does not represent the same viewpoint in all cases and
so means simply <i>“with reference to the Jordan.” “To this day”</i> could
indicate <i>“for a long time.”</i></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>            Finally,
<b>(c) </b><u>organization of material</u> may be left up to editorial
activity. Not only were the Psalms collected and ordered that way, but we can
imagine that a lifetime of prophetic oracles might be organized into final form
by a disciple (Elisha) or scribe (Baruch). Isaiah, Jeremiah, Ezekiel, and Amos are
anthologies of their writers’ “articles.” Collecting and editing them would not
need to be by their respective authors. (Isaiah <b>1</b>, however, seems to introduce
the whole book). We would expect that those in responsible leadership would
undertake that editorial activity.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>            (5) <u>Not
everything preserved</u>. Not everything written by inspired men has been
preserved, as is evident from the different “Histories” of prophets. Just
because the writing is inspired does not mean it is preserved.</span></p>
