style='font-size:12.0pt'>            B.  Eternal pre-existence</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  The <u>eternal</u> pre-existence of
Christ is difficult to prove. It is an inference from having first shown that
he is personal deity. It is difficult to <u>dis</u>prove the idea that the
Father, Son, and Spirit were originally one person, but that for a time the Son
and then the Spirit became a second and third person somehow set off from the
original one. The nature of personhood would seem to exclude the separating idea;
but it is possible that we make that inference because <u>our</u> personal nature
is like that. The following information, however, relates to the eternal
pre-existence of the Son.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  1.   Christ-Son-Word-Jesus is never
said to be created, especially interesting since scripture says he created all
things material and spiritual (John <b>1</b>:3; 1 Corinthians<b> 8</b>:6;
Colossians <b>1</b>:16). </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  2.   <i>“Firstborn”</i> [</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>πρωτότοκος</span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>,
<i>prōtotokos</i>]</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify;text-indent:-1.25in'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                                      a.
<i>“Firstborn of all creation,”</i> or <i>“firstborn of every creature”: </i>Colossians
<b>1</b>:15-18; cp. Psalm <b>8</b>:3-6 (Hebrews <b>2</b>:6-18)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              <i>“Firstborn”</i> means
that Christ has position over the whole creation just as a firstborn <b>(1)</b>
had position over the whole family in ancient Semitic culture. As the
firstborn, he presides over the creation, not just over one of the realms that
the Gnostics imagined and made him the ruler over. (See “Gnosticism and the
Book of Colossians.”) Firstborn could parallel Christ’s role also because he <b>(2)</b>
preceded the creation he made even as the firstborn preceded the rest of the
siblings he oversaw as head of the family. Being created is not the same thing
as incarnation. Christ became a man in the incarnation, but he did not come
into existence then. Carrying the meaning of firstborn beyond these two points
allegorizes the comparison between him and the firstborn son of the family. <b>(3)</b>
In Old Testament practice, the firstborn was dedicated to God. Christ as firstborn
was that kind of person. Perhaps that element as well pertains to the firstborn
terminology of Colossians (Luke<b> 2</b>:23-24 &lt; Exodus <b>13</b>:2, 12,
15).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              Paul means that during
the incarnation Jesus was “fully deity” in contrast to partially so, as with
the Gnostic notion of a mixture of spirit (<i>a la</i> deity) and flesh like
the archons that ruled the hierarchy of aeons in the pleroma. In the Christian
system, Jesus was the functional equivalent of the whole pleroma (fullness) in
Gnostic thought.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              <i>“The beginning of the
creation of God”</i> (Revelation <b>3</b>:14) means the “source” of the
creation and presider over it rather than the first or most important part of it.
In other texts, the Christ is called <i>“the beginning and the end”</i>
(Revelation <b>2</b>:6; <b>22</b>:13). In the Old Testament, Reuben, Israel’s
firstborn, was <i>“the beginning of my virility”</i> (cp. Deuteronomy <b>21</b>:17;
Psalm <b>78</b>:51; <b>105</b>:36).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        b.   Compare Romans <b>8</b>:29
(<i>“firstborn among many brothers”</i>); Colossians <b>1</b>:18 (<i>“firstborn
from the dead”</i>) as well as Luke <b>2</b>:7; Hebrews <b>11</b>:28;<b> 12</b>:23.
So “firstborn” is associated with incarnation and resurrection (Colossians <b>1</b>:18;
Revelation<b> 1</b>:5).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  3.   <i>“Only</i> <i>(begotten)”</i>
[</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>μονογενής</span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>]: <i>monogenēs</i> is a Johannine term for Messiah: John
<b>1</b>:14, 18; <b>3</b>:16, 18; 1 John <b>4</b>:9. (Note Luke<b> 7</b>:12; <b>8</b>:42;<b>
9</b>:38 and especially Hebrews <b>11</b>:17 [Genesis <b>22</b>:2].) The import
of the word is “unique.” He is the only one of his <u>kind</u> (</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>γένος</span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'> [<i>genos</i>])
rather than only one begotten (</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>γεννάω</span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'> [<i>gennaō</i>])—unless we mean that he is the only
One begotten in the flesh the way he was engendered by the Holy Spirit (Luke <b>1</b>:35).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>II. Deity </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            The question here is what order of being
Christ is. Strong Christology appears in the New Testament books of John,
Philippians, Hebrews, and Revelation.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;

<b>14</b>:16-17, 25-26;<b> 15</b>:26-27; <b>16</b>:7-15; Matthew <b>28</b>:19;
Acts <b>2</b>:29-36; <b>7</b>:55; 1 Corinthians <b>12</b>:4-6; 2 Corinthians <b>1</b>:21-22;
<b>13</b>:13 [14]; Ephesians <b>4</b>:4-6; 1 Peter<b> 1</b>:2; 1 John <b>5</b>:4-8)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;

style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  2. Ethical nature: sinlessness (John

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            A.  <i>“Kenosis,”</i> the emptying: Philippians
<b>2</b>:5-11</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  <i>“Fullness of the godhead bodily”</i>
(Colossians <b>2</b>:19) shows that in becoming a man Jesus gave up nothing in
the way of deity; he was “fully” deity. Jesus himself makes a comparable
statement when he tells Philip that seeing him is (as good as) seeing the
Father (John <b>14</b>:9). The <i>“emptying”</i> was not an emptying of deity,
but the giving up of the free exercise of his prerogatives as deity. The image
of God in people means that incarnation can take place without compromising
deity or prostituting humanity. The “overlap” between divine and human means
that incarnation adds the physical (which does not pervert or take away from
the divine) and ceases to exercise the prerogatives and distinctive abilities
or degree of them in the divine. Note the diagrams under VI below.</span></p>

style='font-size:12.0pt'> IV. Resurrection (empty-tomb resurrection, bodily
resurrection)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            Besides the gospel accounts of the
resurrection, note Romans <b>1</b>:3-4; 1 Corinthians <b>15</b>:4, 12-34. Jesus’
resurrection is not a synonym for <u>immortality of the soul</u> (Acts <b>17</b>:31-32;
1 Corinthians <b>1</b>:23) or a parable for continuing influence. It is not a <u>parable</u>;
it left behind an empty tomb.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            The nature of the resurrection body has
been the subject of some discussion. Some call for seeing Jesus’ resurrected
body as something exactly like the pre-crucifixion body. Thomas was challenged
to touch Jesus, Mary Magdalene was able to hold onto him in the garden, and he
told his disciples in a resurrection appearance in Galilee that he had flesh
and bones; he ate with them. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            On the other hand, he “materialized”
before them inside the locked upper room, and vanished just as quickly. Both
movements do not occur in the pre-crucifixion existence. Was the resurrection the
resuscitation of a dead corpse?</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            The fact that they were not demonstrated
does not prove they could not have been, however. Philip may have “disappeared”
from the Ethiopian eunuch (Acts <b>8</b>:39). Jesus’ resurrection <u>appearances</u>
may have had a different form than the state his body had between appearances. He
walked on water during his ministry and ascended to heaven bodily after the
resurrection. Did he just not do these things before or was his body different
afterward? Could he have gone through the wall in the condition he was in
during the upper-room appearance?</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>V.  Ascension (bodily ascension): Mark <b>16</b>:19;
Luke <b>24</b>:51; Acts <b>1</b>:9-11</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>VI. Doctrine of the “two natures” (dyophysitism <i>vs</i>.
monophysitism)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>          </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                      <img width=213
height=222 src="/cir-library/images/essays/the-christ/image002.jpg"></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            If we get rid of the idea of omnipresence
as immensity, we know of nothing the Son had to give up to become incarnate. Immensity
would have to be given up in favor of location in a physical body. Omnipresence
may be an extension of omniscience (Psalm <b>139</b>).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            On the one extreme, overemphasizing
Christ’s deity detracts from the value of the incarnation for us. It changes
the normal human experience into something not so extreme as what we endure in
our physical situation. That tendency found its ancient expression in Docetic Gnosticism,
where Jesus was only a phantom; that would hardly enable him to be tempted in
all points such as we are (Hebrews <b>4</b>:15). On the other extreme,
overemphasizing Christ’s humanity detracts from the strong testimony in the New
Testament regarding his hypostatic deity (Hebrews <b>1</b>:1-4).</span></p>
