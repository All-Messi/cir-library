<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='text-align:center'>            <b><span
style='font-size:14.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>THE
DISTINCTION BETWEEN COVENANTS AND SOME</span></b></p>

<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='text-align:center'><b><span
style='font-size:14.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>RELEVANT
APPLICATIONS: A SKETCH</span></b></p>

<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='text-align:center'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>Virgil
Warren, PhD</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:center'><b><span
style='font-size:14.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>&nbsp;</span></b></p>

<p class=MsoListParagraph style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:0in;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:.75in;text-align:justify;text-indent:-.5in'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>I.<span
style='font:7.0pt "Times New Roman"'>&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; </span></span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>Central
thesis</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:0in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;text-align:justify;text-indent:.5in'><span style='font-size:
12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>The Old Covenant
was a <u>different</u> covenant from the New Covenant, was a <u>different kind</u>
of covenant, and was <u>preparatory for</u> the new. The Old Covenant was <u>legal</u>
while the New Covenant is <u>interpersonal</u>.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:0in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;text-align:justify;text-indent:.5in'><span style='font-size:
12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>The former (L) was
added to promise (IP) for a time to define and sensitize to sin till Christ
(IP) would come for universal, permanent operation leading to (perhaps
conceivable millennial “kingdom” before) the eternal state.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:0in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;text-align:justify;text-indent:.5in'><span style='font-size:
12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>A map of salvation
history starts with concrete operational (garden), followed by rules and
regulations (law), leading to principled operation (gospel). The literature of
the covenants corresponds with and expresses the nature of the covenants
themselves.  </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:0in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;text-align:justify;text-indent:.5in'><span style='font-size:
12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:0in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:
115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>II.  Preliminary distinctions</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:0in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:
115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>            A.  A covenant has to do
with what persons <u>do</u>. It is not concerned directly with the <u>nature</u>
of things (except, of course, insofar as nature enables doing) and <u>history</u>
(records of past action) and prediction (promises about future action).  
[personal action]</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:0in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:
115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>            B.  The <u>code-content</u>
distinction notes that different codes have many of the same elements of
content. Having shared elements does not mean being the same code, and it does
not mean that the people involved are under both codes. They are under only the
one that has “jurisdiction” over them.  [what personal actions]</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:0in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:
115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>            C.  Covenants may differ
as to <u>kind</u> of content and operation. An interpersonal “covenant” has
just as much in it, yea more, than can realistically be codified in a legal
one. Laws have difficulty addressing inner factors like motive and attitude,
genuine repentance.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:0in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:
115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>            D.  The <u>legal-interpersonal</u>
distinction brings in the difference between a legal content driven by
authority and force and an interpersonal content driven by influence. That
distinction leads to a difference in hermeneutical and theological method. 
[interpersonal action]</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:0in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:
115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>            E.   Finally, there is
the specification-spirit distinction, which involves understanding the spirit
of the specifics that is necessary for generalization to unaddressed issues
especially across cultures (cultural diversity) and over time (cultural
drift).  [adequate interpersonal action]</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:0in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:
115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:0in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:
115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>III. Approaches to the Covenant(s)
issue</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:0in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:
115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>            A.  In the Sermon on the
Mount, Jesus guards against a conceivable misperception when he says he did not
come to destroy the law (Matthew<b> 5</b>:17). His concern was with
contemporary misapplications of Mosaic provisions as well as with filling out
more the inner attitudes and motives that needed to be drawn out from legal
enactments, which tend to address overt, observable, actionable behavior.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:0in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:
115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                  Some earlier
gnostic-like thinkers were inclined to trash the Old Testament in light of
(what they wanted to make of) the New.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:0in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:
115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:0in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:
115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>

<span style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>            B.  Two
separate covenants could have the twist that there are two peoples of God—Jews
and Christians. But they comprise one fold (John <b>10</b>:16; Ephesians <b>4</b>:4).
Two covenants could even be over an unrelated set of constituency (constitution
for America and Canada). Spiritualizing the Old Testament in terms of the New
Testament was another attempt to account for two covenants. Finally, the two
covenants could be for two successive systems. All three possibilities share
the fault that both can be of the same kind—legal particularly.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:0in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:
115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>            C.  As with the previous
entry, the amendment approach does not remove the “second” from the legal
category the first is in. It also means that everything in the original not
specifically amended out is still binding. Something of an effort to overcome
this difficulty lies in seeing corresponding aspects of the new as alterations
of the old. Baptism comes in the “room” of circumcision, the Lord’s Supper
replaces the Passover feast, the sacrifice of Christ replaces the paschal lamb,
Sabbath gets moved to Sunday, and so on.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:0in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:
115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                  There must have
been some rationale for the specifics that were explicitly dropped either by
perception of the apostolic writers themselves or by God’s expressed revelation
to the apostles; hence, the next entry.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:0in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:
115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                                             </span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'><img
width=191 height=130 id="Picture 3" src="/cir-library/images/essays/the-distinction-between-covenants-and-some/image003.png"></span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>   </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:0in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:
115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>            D.  During the time of
the Mosaic Covenant, Jeremiah prophesied that God would make a new covenant
(different) that was not like the previous one (different kind). To be in the
covenant, a person had to know God (interpersonal) and have his law written on
the heart (Jeremiah <b>31</b>:31-34 &gt; Hebrews <b>8</b>:6-13). It is not,
then, a matter of replacing one set of realities with better realities, but of
seeing shadows and figures of later realities.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:0in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:
115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                  The difficulty was
not so much with the law’s content as such. It was not false but inadequate. It
could not actually remove an infraction of its own directives: The law was
perfect, but it could not make perfect: we are sold under sin (Romans <b>7</b>:12-15).
Repentance has no value under law.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:0in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:
115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                  From Habakkuk <b>2</b>:4
came Paul’s burst of illumination that “having life” had to be based on
trust/faith in the offended to forgive, because “nobody’s perfect.”
Righteousness in theory could come from “continuing to do all” (Deuteronomy <b>27</b>:26,
Galatians <b>3</b>:10), so God would “owe it” to a person to regard him as
righteous because he was (Romans <b>4</b>:4). Perfectionism is the only
provision law—Moses’ law or any law—can provide.  Consequently, another basis
for relationship to God must be found. Paul’s code word for that basis is
“faith,” an interpersonal term.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:0in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:
115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                                      </span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'><img
width=240 height=145 id="Picture 4" src="/cir-library/images/essays/the-distinction-between-covenants-and-some/image004.png"></span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>     </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:0in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:
115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>            E.   Moving to an
interpersonal basis for being viewed as righteous—identity with the Righteous
One is really a return to basics that preceded law. That is Paul’s point in
Galatians <b>3</b>:19 by emphasizing the word “promise,” another interpersonal
term. The law succeeded promise by 430 years and did not annul promise, which
preceded it. Law and promise are parallel kinds of things: <i>“if it is by law,
it is not by promise”</i> (Galatians <b>3</b>:18).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:0in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:
115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                  In salvation
history the law was a temporary add-on to define and sensitize to sin (Romans <b>5</b>:20;
Galatians <b>3</b>:19), to get people ready for Christ (Galatians <b>3</b>:24-25).
When the school master is here, we cease to be under a “tutor.” When something
perfect comes, in-part things disappear (1 Corinthians <b>13</b>: 9).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:0in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:
115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                  </span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'><img
width=374 height=137 id="Picture 5" src="/cir-library/images/essays/the-distinction-between-covenants-and-some/image005.png"></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:0in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:
115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:0in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:
115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>IV. Major issues impacted by the
interpersonal character of the New Covenant</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:0in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:
115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>            A. Those who function
under the New Covenant prioritize influence (IP) over authority (law) and force
(nature) and form (metaphysics).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:0in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:
115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>            B.  The Christian system
implies an interpersonal <u>hermeneutical and theological method</u> for
reading and applying the relevant canonical literature. That method stands in
contrast to legal and hypostatic reading of texts.        </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:0in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:
115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                  The confidence is
that all people are persons and therefore are capable of understanding and
applying the implications of the central concerns of the New Covenant.  It involves
a realistic, simple “theology from below.”*</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:0in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:
115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>            C.  The nature of the
Christian Covenant with God has implication for the relationship of <u>Christians
to the state</u>.  [church to the outside<u>]</u></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:0in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:
115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                  1.   The state has
a <u>restrictive role</u> whereas religion has a <u>positive role</u> within
the whole social process. The divine intent for human government is not to <i>“bear
the sword in vain”</i> (Romans <b>13</b>:1-7); hence, legitimized operation by
authority and force.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:0in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:
115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                  2.   Interpersonal
commitment to Christ takes precedence over political requirement (Acts <b>4</b>:19;
<b>5</b>:29).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:0in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:
115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                  3.   Those in
personal relationship to God through Christ are free to participate in the
political and national process as long as the expectancies and responsibilities
of the state do not conflict with commitment to God. In contrast, a
“one-kingdom” approach calls for participation in the spiritual kingdom only
and obedience to human law only for expediency. But that is not necessary,
because the role of human government <i>per se</i> is divinely appointed and
approved. Christians can be members of any set as long as its demands do not
conflict with the higher order (two kingdom approach).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:0in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:
115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                        Those
personally committed to Christ can provisionally hold office in the political
process, be members of the police system, and participate in “just war”
(non-aggression).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:0in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:
115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                  4.   Since
Christians participate in an interpersonal system, they should not expect or
aspire to accomplish the purpose of Christ through the mechanisms of the state.
<u>Liberation theology</u><i> </i>is foreign to the character of Christian
system. Christians operating in the state to restrict unfairness by authority
and force do so as members of the state rather than as members of the church.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:0in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:
115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                  5.   There can be
no such thing as the <u>union of church and state</u>. Since the New Covenant
has no legitimizing commission beyond an interpersonal purpose, the authority
and force of state operation as directed at the whole of society cannot be
employed.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:0in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:
115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>            D.  The nature of the
Christian covenant with God has implications for the relationship of <u>Christians
to culture</u>. [church to the outside]</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:0in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:
115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                  1.   The body of
Christ as the continuation of Christ’s influence in the world means that the
church has a <u>prophetic role</u> in relation to culture. The people of God
serve to provide critique of the culture and the dominant perspectives of the
social environment. It stands above culture to call it individual by individual
to a lifestyle more in conformity with God’s intentions and ultimate purpose
for humankind. As such, the function of the faith contrasts with the role of
reinforcing societal norms (a sociological definition of religion).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:0in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:
115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                  2.   The prophetic
role under Christ in turn eliminates <u>restructuring</u> the <u>content of the
faith</u> to fit the culture.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:0in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:
115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                  3.   Prioritizing
personal relationship to Christ likewise eliminates operating alternatively in
two parallel systems, one at home in private and another in the public
marketplace as dictated by society or the state. When expectancies are in
conflict, the person operates by the One he loves the most (Matthew <b>6</b>:24;
Acts <b>4</b>:19). Thus comes civil disobedience.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:0in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:
115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                  4.   The
interpersonal nature of the Covenant eliminates stratification of social
relations at least in attitude, because personhood is common to all persons
regardless of economic level, caste category, sex, race, nationality, societal
level.  Interpersonal love is the great equalizer of people and the manner of
relating among them. Unity in diversity can be, because personhood exists
across irrelevant differences. The Covenant is inclusive of all types of people
who agree to relate to God on his terms.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:0in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:
115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>            E.   The method and means
of <u>kingdom extension</u> come wholly from influence without appeal to
authority and force.  [church to the outside]</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:0in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:
115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                  1.   The Great
Commission characterizes the Christian enterprise in the world as an
educational process. Eliminated, then, is extension by military means and
authoritative requirement. For Christians operating toward the outside, what
cannot be done by persuasion cannot be done—neither by threat, pressure,
manipulation, shunning or any other anti-interpersonal means.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:0in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:
115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                  2.   The social
character of the New Covenant means that Covenant processes are person on
person <i>vs</i>. category on category, group on group. In consequence, New
Covenanters do not stereotype or act accordingly.  [church to the outside]</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:0in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:
115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                  3.   The church is
not to be denominated into parallel and competing parts.  Its very purpose is
to create oneness in the world (Ephesians <b>2</b>:11-22).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:0in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:
115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>            F.   <u>Proclamation</u>
of the gospel is in terms of personal relationship, which makes it
automatically understandable and relevant to all hearers. It can be
demonstrated as well as said, so that is sensed as well as understood.  [church
to the outside]</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:0in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:
115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>            G.  The <u>apologetic</u>
for the faith is at one with the interpersonal circumstance of its
proclamation. <i>“Try it, you’ll like it”</i> comes on top of <i>“Doesn’t this
sound good?”</i></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:0in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:
115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>            H.  Interpersonal
hermeneutics in the context of state and culture leads to the possibility of <u>implied
cultural limitation</u> on certain New Testament directives that seem
culturally informed rather than inherently necessary given the revealed nature
of mankind and God’s purpose for him.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:0in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:
115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                  This possibility
has given rise to discussion points in the modern church: arranged marriages,
braided hair, hair length, veil wearing and other measures of modesty, table
fellowship (Gentiles, Samaritans, unbelievers); forms of deference behavior of
wives toward husbands, children toward parents, Christians toward rulers. Are
some of these matters rooted in human perception or divine intent?</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:0in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:
115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                  Law is static in
regard to items it addresses. So matters of dress and diet would remain
unchanged over time and across cultures. But we know dietary laws were repealed
(Mark <b>7</b>:19), and the church as such evidently has no custom of
veil-wearing (1 Corinthians <b>11</b>:10). So what was the rationale for their
removal? The answer seems to be that they and items like them have no necessary
basis in interpersonal relationships. That is relevant because the covenant
itself is interpersonal.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:0in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:
115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>            I.    An interpersonal
covenant establishes the manner of Christian life and worship.  [within the
church]</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:0in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:
115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                  1.   The
non-quantification of Christian observance</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:0in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:
115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                        How often do
we fast (twice a week?), how many times do we forgive (seven times in a day?),
how frequently do we pray (three times a day?), how much do we give (a tithe of
all we possess?), is there a festival cycle to observe (Sabbath-keeping, new
moons, <i>etc</i>.)? None of the observances are quantified in the New Covenant
literature, and there is no mechanism for adding to the word of God as by
amendment.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:0in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:
115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                  2.   Christian
living is done to a Person, not to a rule abstracted from the Person who
established the expectancy. It is loving him that leads to doing his will and
living out his values.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:0in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:
115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                  3.   Christian
living is according to what is true rather than what is legal in the world
outside the Covenant.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:0in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:
115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>            J.   The interpersonal
context in the Covenant naturally minimizes or even eliminates ceremonial
observances. Baptism and the Lord’s Supper most easily lend themselves to
ritual performances. However, they are to be done as performative acts by which
one person says something to Another.  [within the church]</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:0in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:
115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>            K.  The interpersonal
nature of the covenant has implications for <u>leadership</u>.  [within the
church]</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:0in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:
115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                  1.   It tends
toward <u>minimizing organizational structure</u> as “authority flow.” The
church does as much as possible within itself by influence before appealing to duly
appointed authority (elders), remembering that the church is a voluntary
society.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:0in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:
115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                  2.   That minimized
organizational structure tends in turn toward <u>local leadership</u> under,
and limited to, the concerns and teaching of the Word. The Word serves as the
functional equivalent and perpetuation of the apostles, the only general
authority the New Testament witnesses besides that of the Lord himself. The New
Testament was written by apostles and those who served as extensions of the
apostolic office; hence, apostolicity as the primary New Testament canonical
principle.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:0in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:
115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                  3.   The <u>theory
of leadership</u> is that of responsibility reinforced by authority as needed.
It is something that a person is <u>called</u> to rather than what he competes
to seize. The operation, again, is that of <u>influence</u> before authority
and never by compulsion. Leadership in the interpersonal “kingdom” does not
bypass the personhood of the ones led.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:0in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:
115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                  4.   What can be
done by influence can be done by any person—man, woman, young, or old.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:0in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:
115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>            K.  Worship is <u>I-Thou</u>.
The general guidelines are decency and order (1 Corinthians <b>14</b>:10), what
is appropriate and under control. Worship is the voluntary self-expression by a
person to God as a present superior second person. Whatever can be done under
this <u>limitation</u> a person has <u>freedom</u> to do. Worship gets its
character from the interpersonal nature of the setting where it takes place.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:0in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:
115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'> [within the church]</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:0in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:
115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                  </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:0in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:
115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>            In short, virtually
everything we can identify as weaknesses of the church ancient and modern
involves some kind of deficiency in the application of the interpersonal
character of the New Covenant.</span></p>
