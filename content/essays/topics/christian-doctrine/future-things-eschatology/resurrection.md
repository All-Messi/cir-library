<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;

style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            D.  Comparing cases</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        1.   <u>Comparing biblical
resurrections and our resurrection</u> is not necessarily helpful, because all biblical
resurrections happened soon after death. The longest time difference was four
days with Lazarus. Matthew <b>27</b>:52 makes brief reference to many bodies of
the saints that had fallen asleep were raised, entered the holy city, and
appeared to many. Presumably they were recognizable in their appearance or
there would have been no purpose in appearing or any knowledge that they were
dead saints. Those resurrections do not help us answer how resurrection can
occur after thousands of years when bodily materials have completely disintegrated,
been shifted around, and been re-assimilated elsewhere. Besides, except for
Jesus Christ, resurrected people arose only to die again or re-enter their
previous intermediate condition as far as we can tell. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            Those resurrected and those translated at
the return of Christ end up in the same condition (1 Corinthians <b>15</b>:51-52);
hence, we can say that <b>k = l</b>. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            The transfigured condition of Elijah and
Moses is not necessarily the same as their (and our) normal intermediate state;
hence, <b>h ≠ j</b>. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            Samuel’s called-up condition may equal
that of Moses and Elijah (<b>g + h</b>), but that is indeterminate. At least he
was recognizable. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            Our intermediate condition evidently does
not equal the resurrected condition; otherwise, resurrection would not bring
anything additional; yet the intermediate condition has a heavenly body (2 Corinthians
<b>5</b>:2) just like the resurrected body has (1 Corinthians <b>15</b>:40,
48-49). They are both incorruptible and eternal (2 Corinthians <b>5</b>:1-2, 4;
1 Corinthians <b>15</b>:42, 50-54). So, <b>j ≠ k = l</b>.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            The <u>intermediate state</u> must be more
than immortality of the soul. Assuming that 2 Corinthians<b> 5</b> speaks of
the intermediate state, we learn that in that condition we are not <i>“unclothed”</i>
or <i>“naked”</i> (<b>5</b>:3-4). That probably means we are not (exactly)
disembodied spirits. We are <i>“absent from the body”</i> and <i>“at home with
the Lord”</i> (<b>5</b>:6, 8-9) with a <i>“heavenly habitation”</i> (<b>5</b>:2).
So, <b>i ≠ j</b>.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            Most clearly, <u>resurrection</u> is not
to be confused with the Greek notion of the immortality of the soul. Greeks
objected to resurrection even though they believed in immortality (Acts <b>17</b>:31;
1 Corinthians<b> 1</b>:23). Their objection could not have been simply to <u>Christ’s</u>
resurrection as if his and ours were enough different to make our resurrection—though
not Christ’s—comparable to immortality. In 1 Corinthians <b>15</b> Paul equates
Christ’s resurrection with ours as to kind (at least in the final analysis),
using Christ’s as a reason to believe in ours. So, <b>i ≠ k/l</b>.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            So, <b>g ? h ≠ i ≠ j ≠ k/l</b>.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        2.   It is not decisive to
compare <u>Christ’s resurrection and our resurrection</u>. Although people
sometimes say that Christ’s resurrection is “proof and prefigure” of our
resurrection, we do well not to press the “prefigure” to too detailed a level. He
had not been dead more than thirty-eight hours when he arose. Furthermore, his
resurrection had to have apologetic force, which does not transfer to our
resurrection. He needed to be visible, audible, touchable, able to eat physical
food so the disciples could recognize him and </span><span style='font-size:
12.0pt;color:windowtext'>be thoroughly convinced he was the same one they knew before
death.</span><span style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#00B050'> </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            His risen condition was not the same
during the whole time prior to his ascension to the Father. At times he could
pass through material buildings while immediately afterward he could be
touched, could handle material things, and yet later ascend to heaven; so we
might say </span><span style='font-size:12.0pt;color:windowtext'>there was</span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'> <b>d</b> and <b>d<span style='position:relative;
top:-3.0pt'>1</span></b>. Nevertheless, at some level his and our resurrections
are comparable—at a level higher than mere continuity of identity—because Paul
combines Christ’s resurrection with ours in 1 Corinthians <b>15</b>:12-13. Christ’s
appearances to the Twelve may not have been exactly the same as the one to Paul
later near Damascus, but they must have been enough alike for Paul to call it a
<u>resurrection</u> appearance (before in the Sanhedrin in Acts<b> 23</b>:6-10;
cp. <b>24</b>:20-21) and to list it with the other appearances (1 Corinthians <b>15</b>:5-8)
in a way that authenticated him as a primary apostle (1 Corinthians <b>9</b>:1-2).
</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            Christ’s pre-incarnate and incarnate
states were not alike (<b>a ≠ b)</b>, and his regular incarnate and
transfigured states were different (<b>b ≠ c</b>). </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            How the transfigured appearance and
ascended conditions compare is indeterminate (<b>c ? d/d<span style='position:
relative;top:-3.0pt'>1</span></b>). Evidently there was a difference between
some appearance conditions and the ascended condition (<b>d<span
style='position:relative;top:-3.0pt'>1</span> e</b>). Therefore, <b>a ≠ b ≠ c ?
d/d<span style='position:relative;top:-3.0pt'>1</span> ? f ? e</b>. In other
words, we have more questions about these conditions than we have answers. When
we try to relate </span><span style='font-size:12.0pt;color:windowtext'>his
resurrection to ours</span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>, even more questions
arise.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            In comparing our and Christ’s final
condition, we must take care since we will not be deity in the resurrection. Whether
there is an ontological difference between spiritual deity and supernatural
personal, spiritual beings we cannot say since we cannot know the nature of
various identities in the spiritual realm. (Could the difference be only a
matter of authority, ontic being becoming an irrelevant category?) Nevertheless,
John can say that we will be like him and see him as he is (1 John <b>3</b>:2).
</span></p>

style='font-size:12.0pt'>            In summary, the subsequent diagram
attempts to capture the more important facets of resurrection both in regard to
variant human conditions and in regard to Christ’s and our</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            Furthermore, the differences discussed
above presuppose hypostatic considerations. The variation between God, angels,
and humans in our final, intermediate, and resurrected states might be more a
difference in “power” or authority than form and substance of being, and our
final resurrection may be more a matter of reidentification with our earthly
origins than with any hypostatic component. The resurrected condition might
approach that of Jesus in his “translated” form as comparable to, but not
identical with, the appearance form of Moses and Elijah. The form Samuel had
when called by the spirit medium of Endor affords another possibility of state
along with the condition of Enoch and Elijah after their translation.</span></p>
