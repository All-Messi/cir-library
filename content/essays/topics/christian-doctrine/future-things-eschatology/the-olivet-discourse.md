<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>            Two related questions may be
asked regarding Matthew’s account of the Olivet Discourse. (1) Who or what is
the <i>“abomination of desolation” </i>in <b>24</b>:14? (2) If some events are
still future, where is the dividing line between the ones now fulfilled and the
ones still to come? All three synoptists record this sermon: Matthew <b>24-25</b>;
Mark <b>13</b>; and Luke <b>21</b>.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>            Since answering the second
question determines the answer to the first, we need to establish the dividing
line between past and future events first. A harmony of the accounts helps
track the progress of thought. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>            Luke seems to present the clearest
break between past and future events. Beginning with his account sets a method
for analyzing the discourse. At <b>21</b>:24 he says, </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-left:.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:18.0pt'><i><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>“</span></i><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>[The
inhabitants of Jerusalem and Judaea] <i>will fall by the sword and be led away
captive into all the nations, and Jerusalem will be trodden down by the
Gentiles until the times of the Gentiles are fulfilled.”</i></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><i><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></i></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>From Jesus’ point in time, the
destruction of Jerusalem plus its consequences provides the nearest adequate
fulfillment; so referring to that cluster of events is the preferrable
interpretation unless contextual considerations dictate otherwise. From A.D. 70
onward, the Jewish people were literally carried captive into all nations, and
Jerusalem was in the hands of Gentiles until the Six-Day War in June 1967.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>            If Luke <b>21</b>:24 is the
chronological dividing line, then <b>21</b>:20 speaks of A.D. 70 when it says armies
would surround Jerusalem. Comparing the accounts at this point shows that Luke <b>21</b>:24
parallels Matthew <b>24</b>:15 and Mark <b>13</b>:14. Matthew and Mark’s accounts
use the expression <i>“abomination of desolation standing in the holy place”</i>
(Matthew), or <i>“standing where it shouldn’t”</i> (Mark). <i>“Holy place”</i>
evidently means Jerusalem, not the Holy of Holies, because the parallelism
aligns it with <i>“Jerusalem”</i> in Luke’s account. It would be too late
anyway for the residents in Jerusalem to flee to the hills after the Roman army
had made it all the way in to the temple area. By metonymy, the Holy Place gave
name to the city in which God promised to put his presence.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>            Matthew adds that Daniel the
prophet had referred to the <i>“abomination of desolation” </i>(<b>24</b>:15). Daniel
speaks four times about an abomination that makes desolate:<b> 8</b>:13; <b>9</b>:27;
<b>11</b>:31; <b>12</b>:11. Daniel <b>11</b>:31 is not the reference since that
part of Daniel’s prophecy was fulfilled by the desecrations of the temple by
Antiochus Epiphanes over 160 years before Christ. Daniel <b>9</b>:24-27
apparently predicted the Roman destruction of Jerusalem, so this reference must
be the one Jesus meant. The Daniel <b>12</b>:11 reference may also refer to
this destruction, although many interpreters believe it refers to the end times
or to the <b>11</b>:31 reference. So far, the data fits the requirements of
having an adequate fulfillment that we can identify with a prophecy of Daniel about
an abomination of desolation.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>            Several points confirm
identifying the abomination of desolation with A.D. 70. Matthew <b>24</b>:21
speaks of <i>“such anguish as has not been since the world until <u>now</u>.”</i>
<i>“Now”</i> implies that the desolation Jesus meant was relatively soon.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>            Secondly, Jesus continues, <i>“.
. . no, nor will ever be,”</i> as if subsequently there would be a long period
of time when such could occur but would not. If these earlier verses of Matthew
<b>24</b> refer to a premillennial tribulation, we wonder whether that comment
would be an understatement. During a millennial reign of Christ on earth, no
such opposition to God’s people could occur. The post-millennial, Gog-and-Magog
conflict does not seem to involve much anguish for the saints, because fire
from heaven devours the forces of evil when they encamp against the saints and
surround Jerusalem (Revelation <b>20</b>:7-10). The miseries of the Roman
destruction that Josephus records suffice as the worst ever (Matthew<b> 24</b>:21).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>            Those events after Luke <b>21</b>:24
are broadscale while the abomination of desolation is localized in Judaea and
Jerusalem, a fact that may be accounted for by the events of A.D. 70 as
distinguished from a great tribulation before the millennium. Mark <b>13</b>:27
(<i>“whole earth”</i>) contrasts with Luke<b> 21</b>:23: <i>“great distress on
the land</i> (</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>γῆ</span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'> [<i>gē</i>] = </span><span lang=HE dir=RTL
style='font-size:14.0pt'>אֶרֶץ</span><span dir=LTR></span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'><span dir=LTR></span>) <i>and wrath to this people</i>
(</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ὁ</span><span lang=EL
style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>λαός</span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'> [<i>ho laos</i>] = </span><span lang=HE dir=RTL
style='font-size:14.0pt'>הָעַם</span><span dir=LTR></span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'><span dir=LTR></span>; </span><span lang=EL
style='font-size:12.0pt'>ὁ</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>λαός</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:
12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>τῆς</span><span lang=EL
style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>γῆς</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>[<i>ho
laos tēs gēs</i>] = </span><span lang=HE dir=RTL style='font-size:14.0pt'>עַם־הָאֶרֶץ</span><span
dir=LTR></span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'><span dir=LTR></span>).” </span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>Γῆ</span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'> [<i>gē</i>]
does not mean <i>“earth”</i> in this case since the context is limited to
Palestine. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>            Although (1) the division in
Luke is clearly after <b>21</b>:24, the division in Mark and Matthew is not as obvious.
Whatever is done, however, must fit with, and be governed by, Luke’s more
simplified account since the parallel passages are so obviously identical in
expression with his. The second step is (2) to notice that Matthew <b>24</b>:29
(= Mark<b> 13</b>:24; Luke <b>24</b>:25a) says <i>“immediately.” </i>At least
part of the preceding context, which has no parallel in Luke, must refer to
events still future. As far back as <b>24</b>:27 the references seem to be
identified with events still future since nothing is known of a coming of
Christ in A.D. 70 that was comparable to lightning visible from one horizon to the
other. Since verse 27 is introduced by <i>for</i>, it explains the previous verse,
which denies a private return. In turn,<b> 24</b>:26 begins with the word <i>therefore</i>,
falling back on verses 23-25. As we move back from Matthew <b>24</b>:28, there
is no break until the beginning of verse 23.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>            Working from the other
direction, we find Matthew <b>24</b>:21 connecting with <b>24</b>:20 and <b>24</b>:22
falling back on<b> 24</b>:21 in that it mentions <i>“those days.”</i> In
Matthew’s account, the break between events associated with the destruction of
Jerusalem and those still unfulfilled seems to occur before 23. <i>Then</i> (</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>τότε</span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'> [<i>tote</i>])
may mean simultaneous with, or after, what precedes. Here it means <i>“at that
time.”</i> </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>            Verses 26-28 can speak of
the end time with 23-25 introducing the subject because Jesus predicted that
false messiahs would arise at the A.D. 70 destruction. These messiahs would try
to fulfill the Old Testament as understood in current Messianic expectancy. Jesus
tells his disciples not to be deceived into thinking that any of these
claimants was Jesus returning to <i>“restore the kingdom to Israel.”</i> His
return would be visible in the sky, not like his first coming. The claim of a
private return is common both to the end of the Jewish age and the end times. Since
Matthew <b>24</b>:21 identifies an element common to both times, that element
bridges between A.D. 70 materials and the latter days. That common element
enables Jesus to pass from one time to the other without indicating a time
distinction. Immediately after the tribulation of <u>those</u> days refers to
the tribulation days when the Son of Man does come visibly from east to west. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>            Therefore, <i>immediately</i>
forms no problem of Christ’s coming immediately after the destruction of
Jerusalem because <i>those days</i> identifies the time of his actual coming,
which is later, visible to all the earth, and accompanied by heavenly
disturbances. Christ’s second coming will involve a stupendous display of
divine intervention, not only in the worldwide visibility of the returning
Christ, but in the astronomical disturbances that will accompany it. No one
should have any difficulty distinguishing the actual return from anything that
would go on at the destruction of Jerusalem. <i>Those</i> indicates a more
distant time (cp. <i>that</i> <i>vs</i>. <i>this</i> in <b>24</b>:37 + 34) at
the end times in contrast to <i>these</i>, which fall within the apostles’
generation and include the destruction of Jerusalem, only forty years away.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>            <i>“Look, here is Christ”</i>
does occur twice in this discourse, but the two occurrences need not be </span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:windowtext'>the same so as</span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'> to contest the above division point in the text. Jesus
moves temporarily to the subject of his actual personal return so the disciples
will not be misled by claims about Messiah coming when they see the fall of
Jerusalem drawing near. It would be natural for political-military messiahs to
arise at such an obvious threat to Israel as the advancing Roman armies would
signal. The destruction of Jerusalem may be viewed as similar to, and typical
of, the Great Tribulation to come, because both events share some features. Having
a common feature—false messiahs—makes it all the more possible for Jesus to
move from speaking about the near destruction to speaking about his return. As
the destruction of Jerusalem removed the formal Jewish religious opposition to
the church, so the end of the tribulation removes the anti-Christian opposition
to the kingdom of God, and the Magog destruction is prelude to the full reign
of God in the eternal state.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>            In summary, Jesus seems to
be talking about the destruction of Jerusalem up through Matthew <b>24</b>:22. Since
a time of anguish accompanies Christ’s second coming—a fact known before A.D.
70, someone might say at the fall of Jerusalem that Messiah had come privately.
To preclude such a suggestion, Jesus introduces verses 23-28 to explain the actual
manner of his real return. He continues in <b>24</b>:29ff talking about events
when he does return. Consequently, <i>“the tribulation of those days”</i> is
the tribulation of those days when he does come—a tribulation (<b>24</b>:28)
followed by cosmological disturbances (<b>24</b>:29) and his actual appearance
(<b>24</b>:30-31). Furthermore, the destruction of Jerusalem would seem to be
the most prominent, or at least the initial, topic of discussion. The questions
asked by the apostles came in response to Jesus’ comment about the destruction
of Jerusalem.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>            At least three questions come
up under this interpretation of the Olivet Discourse. <b>First </b>is the fact
that the <i>immediately</i> of Matthew <b>24</b>:29 is <i>“immediately after
the <u>tribulation of those days</u>.”</i> A reader’s first impulse is to take
the reference back to <b>24</b>:21, but under the above arrangement the tribulation
of <b>24</b>:21 refers to A.D. 70. How can Jesus be speaking of events two
thousand years hence as <i>“immediately after”</i>? E</span><span lang=EL
style='font-size:12.0pt'>ὐθέως</span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'> (<i>eutheōs</i>)
might mean <i>“suddenly”</i> (cp. Mark<b> 13</b>:36, </span><span lang=EL
style='font-size:12.0pt'>ἐξαίφναις</span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'> [<i>exaiphnais</i>])
rather than <i>“immediately.”</i> But lexicons do not attest such a meaning
though it might fit in Revelation <b>4</b>:2 and Matthew<b> 8</b>:3. Nevertheless,
we hesitate to justify an interpretation by a rare meaning even when examples
occur.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>            <i>“Immediately”</i> could
be relative, but that also seems strained. A better alternative takes <b>24</b>:29
as a reference to a tribulation implicit in the coming of false Christs and the
Son of Man, a tribulation known from elsewhere to precede Christ’s second
coming, which brings to an end the <i>“Great Tribulation”</i> consummated in
the battle of Harmagedon. Matthew<b> 24</b>:28 says, <i>“The vultures will
gather where the carcass is.”</i> A negative connotation attaches to this
cryptic aphorism, and the idea of dead bodies and vultures suggests a conflict.
(</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt;color:windowtext'>Ἀετοί</span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:windowtext'>, <i>aetoi</i></span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'> is better translated “<i>vultures”</i> here than <i>“eagles”</i>
because they are gathering around corpses. <i>“Eagles”</i> might still work
here because the eagle was the mascot for the Roman army.)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>            Mark <b>13</b>:24 parallels
Matthew <b>24</b>:29, but Mark does not say <i>immediately</i>. Neither does he
parallel Matthew <b>24</b>:26-28. Mark simply says <i>“in the days after that
tribulation,”</i> that is, the future tribulation of <b>13</b>:21-22, the only
one he mentions. Matthew interjects another tribulation like in kind but
different in time. Whereas the cosmological disturbances are <u>immediately
after</u> that interjected<i> “great tribulation</i>,<i>” </i>they are merely <u>after</u>
the “Jerusalem tribulation,” Matthew and Mark thus referring to different
tribulation at this point.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>            One confirmation of the
above construction comes from Luke<b> 17</b>:22-37, because in Luke <b>17</b>:37
Jesus uses the same expression about vultures gathering. It appears in a
section that equals parts of the Olivet Discourse. Luke<b> 17</b>:23-24 matches
Matthew <b>24</b>:23-27; Luke <b>17</b>:26-27 parallels Matthew <b>24</b>:37-39.
Luke <b>17</b>:28-30, 32 adds the example of Lot to the example of Noah. Luke<b>
17</b>:31, 33-35 is comparable to Matthew <b>24</b>:40-41. The upshot of all
this is that Luke <b>17</b> places the <i>“vultures”</i> statement in the
context of the second coming and in a series of parallels with the latter
section of Matthew <b>24</b>. That portion has already been connected in this
study with the second coming, the dividing line having been placed at Matthew <b>24</b>:29.
In the Olivet Discourse, Luke omitted the parallel to Matthew <b>24</b>:27-28,
because he had already included it in a statement to the Pharisees. Between the
parallel to Matthew<b> 24</b>:27 and <b>24</b>:28, Luke <b>17</b> inserts a
whole statement about <u>when</u> in a context about <u>where</u> (<i>“Look,
here” </i>+ <i>“the vultures”</i>). In Matthew <b>24</b>:23-28 the writer inserts
a statement about <u>where</u> in a context about <u>when</u> (<i>“beginning
until now” </i>+ <i>“immediately after”</i>). The reason this can happen is
that the <u>where</u> of both the Jerusalem destruction and the second coming
is the same, though the times differ.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>            Another confirmation for
inferring a tribulation from Matthew <b>24</b>:28 comes from Ezekiel<b> 39</b>:1-7. 
Ravenous birds have a similar role at the overthrow of Gog and Magog (cp.
Revelation <b>19</b>:17-18, clearly premillennial). Gog and Magog show up in
Revelation <b>20</b>:7-10 at the final conflict before and after the
millennium. Perhaps the conflicts before and after the millennium are of the
same sort and are blurred together in Matthew, who does not interject the
millennium period.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>            The <b>second problem</b>
with this approach is that Matthew says in <b>24</b>:34, <i>“This generation
will not pass away till all these things will be accomplished.”</i> Unless we
spiritualize<b> 24</b>:23-31 beyond recognition, a lot in these verses seem not
to have happened in A.D. 70.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>            Several solutions have been suggested,
including the idea that all the previous things would be accomplished before
(1) the generation at the second coming would pass away. Generation has been
taken in its extended meaning, “family,” that is, (2) the Jewish people. These
do not seem natural.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>            Luke <b>21</b>:35 says, <i>“.
. . it will come on all of them that live all over the earth.” </i>Nothing
during that generation seems to have affected people the world over. Luke <b>21</b>:32
says, <i>“All things be accomplished,”</i> which may mean that during (3) that
generation everything would be accomplished for establishing the basis for the
full reign of God in fellowship with mankind and in eliminating the previous
ineffectual system in A.D. 70.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>            Matthew <b>24</b>:32 (+ Mark’s
parallel) begins with a short parable about a fig tree, primarily applicable to
the destruction of Jerusalem, although there are also signs of his coming as
well. Matthew <b>24</b>:34-35 becomes a reference to the part about the
destruction. Christ’s words would not pass away, even though Jerusalem would.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>            <i>“Heaven and earth will
pass away . . . but of that day and hour</i>” is taken with the second coming. Although
Jesus did not know the time of his return, he did know at least generally when
Jerusalem would be destroyed. After all, it was only forty years away. His
knowledge of it—as soon—is implied in Matthew<b> 23</b>:36 and Luke <b>23</b>:28-29.
Consequently, in the expression <i>“of that day and hour” </i>the emphasis is
on <u>that</u> day and hour as opposed to <u>this</u> generation—far as opposed
to near—rather than on “day-and-hour” (exact time) to “generation” (general
time). The rest of the discourse consists primarily of parables dealing with
the uncertainty of the time of his return (<b>24</b>:42, 44, 50; <b>25</b>:13).
The Parable of the Talents also includes a lengthy time before the master’s
return, perhaps even in contrast to one generation.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>            The <b>third question</b> for
the above interpretation is how to understand the prophecy in Matthew <b>24</b>:14
(= Mark <b>13</b>:10): the gospel would be preached in the whole world as a
testimony to all the nations before the end would come—if <i>“end”</i> means
the A.D. 70 destruction. The answer lies in statements that Paul makes in
Romans <b>1</b>:8; Colossians <b>1</b>:6, 23; and 1 Timothy <b>3</b>:16. He
affirms that the gospel had already been preached in all the <i>“world”</i> (</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>κόσμος</span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>
or </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>κτίσις</span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>) by that time. In Acts <b>24</b>:5 </span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>οἰκουμένη</span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>
(<i>oikoumenē</i>) appears in Tertullus’ oration against Paul before Felix
regarding Paul’s <i>“insurrections among Jews throughout the world.”</i> </span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>Οἰκουμένη</span><span lang=EL
style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>appears in Acts
<b>11</b>:28 regarding a famine over the whole world. (See Romans<b> 16</b>:25-26
as well.) The sense in which these four references say the gospel was already
preached throughout the world is the sense in which we may understand it in the
Olivet Discourse.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>            The reason for the gospel’s
being preached to the whole then-known world before the destruction of
Jerusalem was to show that Jesus was not another fly-by-night Messianic
claimant whose movement died with him. The disintegration of false Messiah’s
movements is what anyone would expect because <i>“Messiah abides forever”</i>
(Psalm<b> 110</b>:4; <b>89</b>; Isaiah <b>9</b>:7; Ezekiel<b> 37</b>:25; Daniel
<b>7</b>:14; John <b>12</b>:34; 1 Corinthians <b>1</b>:23; Galatians<b> 5</b>:11;
cp. Isaiah <b>8</b>:14; <b>28</b>:16 + Luke<b> 2</b>:34; Romans<b> 9</b>:32; 1
Peter<b> 2</b>:8). The fact that the enemies of a <i>“Messiah”</i> could kill
him meant to them that he was a pseudo-Messiah. The apostles’ claim that Jesus
Messiah had resurrected does make sense of the fact that his movement spread rather
than collapsed after his death. That is Gamaliel’s argument in Acts <b>5</b>:33-41.
When previous leaders like Judas and Theudas were killed, their movements died;
but the pattern did not hold for the followers of Jesus Messiah. That is the
reason Gamaliel cautioned his fellow Pharisees to be careful lest they be
working against God. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>            Since the Olivet Discourse
is given from the Jews’ perspective, it testifies not only to the Gentile
nations to whom the gospel is preached, but to the Jewish nation as well. Christianity
is here to stay. Preaching the gospel to the whole world made it clear that
Jesus was the legitimate Messiah and therefore gave rationale to God’s allowing
the Jewish state to fall permanently. Practically speaking, that political nation
was no longer the agent of light and salt to the rest of the nations—the <i>“Gentiles.”</i>
Preaching the gospel to all the nations is not then a sign of the second
coming, but a sign of the validity of the first coming. That process was to
antedate the destruction of Jerusalem, not the return of Christ.</span></p>
