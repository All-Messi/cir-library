<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            Within prophecy generally, apocalyptic has
several distinguishing characteristics. As to content, apocalyptic deals with
eschatological reference, angelic emphasis, cataclysm, universal-cosmic scope,
pessimism in people, divine sovereignty, transcendence, the concept of final comfort.
As to form, apocalyptic uses animal symbolism and numerology.  </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            In chronological order the apocalyptic
passages in scripture are Isaiah <b>24-27</b> (+ <b>6</b>); Ezekiel <b>33-48</b>
especially; Daniel <b>2</b>, <b>7-12</b>; Zechariah generally (although in many
places the book is non-apocalyptic prophecy); the Olivet Discourse in Matthew <b>24-25</b>,
Mark<b> 13</b>, and Luke <b>21</b>; 1 Thessalonians <b>4</b>:1-<b>5</b>:11; 2
Thessalonians <b>2</b>:1-12; and the Book of Revelation generally.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            The above listing yields some observations.
(1) Several apocalyptic sections do not include animal symbolism: Isaiah’s “little
apocalypse,” the Olivet Discourse (except in the one aphoristic statement about
“vultures”), and Paul’s apocalyptic sections. (2) By far the greatest amount of
animal symbolism occurs in Daniel and Revelation. (3) The six most frequently
used animals are the lion, dragon, eagle, horse, lamb, and sheep, although some
seventeen animals appear in the symbolism. (4) Both domesticated and wild
animals come into the scenes. (5) Not all animals in apocalyptic sections are
symbolic: horses in Zechariah <b>10</b>:3, 5; <b>12</b>:4<span
style='position:relative;top:-3.0pt'>2</span>; <b>14</b>:15, 20; camel, mule,
and donkey in <b>14</b>:15.  </span></p>

style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        <b>20</b>:1-3, 7-10).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        a.   The dragon is Satan (<b>12</b>:9),
a person; but the scarlet-colored beast is a composite of person, place, and
things; that is, the “beast” itself (<b>20</b>:11), seven hills (<b>20</b>:9),
and an evil political system (<b>20</b>:10-11a). The heads are both mountains
and kings (<b>20</b>:9-10).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        b.   Two different animal
words are used. One is a “dragon” (</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>δράκων</span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>, drakōn); the other is a beast (</span><span lang=EL
style='font-size:12.0pt'>θήριον</span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>, thērion).
</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>Δράκων</span><span lang=EL
style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>indicates
undomesticated animals generally (mammal, <b>13</b>:2; snake, Acts<b> 28</b>:4-5).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  5.  The sea beast (<b>13</b>:1-10)
equals the scarlet-colored beast (<b>17</b>:3-18).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        a.  The sea beast came up out
of the sea (<b>13</b>:1); the harlot who sat on the beast (<b>17</b>:3) was
sitting on many waters (<b>17</b>:2).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        b.  Both had seven heads and
ten horns (<b>13</b>:1; cp. <b>17</b>:3).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        c.   The sea beast had names
of blasphemy on his heads (<b>13</b>:1, 6); the scarlet-colored beast was “full
of names of blasphemy” (<b>17</b>:3). Blasphemy is associated only with these
two beasts.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        d.   The sea beast had <u>one
head smitten</u> but the <i>death-stroke was healed</i> (<b>13</b>:3); the
scarlet beast was, <u>is not</u>, and <i>will come out of the abyss</i>.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        e.  Only the sea-beast section
(<b>13</b>:8) and the scarlet-beast section (<b>17</b>:8) contain the phrase <i>not
written in the (Lamb’s) book of life</i>, except for references near the
beginning of the book and at the actual judgment scenes near the end of Revelation.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        f.    After mentioning the
healed death-stroke, the text says that “the whole earth” marveled at the beast
(<b>13</b>:3). After stating that the scarlet beast was, is not, and is to
come, the angel says that “they who dwell on the earth” will marvel at the
beast (<b>17</b>:8).  (</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>Μέλλω</span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'> + infinitive may be a New Testament Greek formula for
near future <i>vs</i>. distant future: “be about to”; hence, the beast <i>“was,
is not, and is about to come.”</i> The normal Greek future verb is undifferentiated
future.)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  6.   The sea beast (<b>13</b>:1-10)
equals the beast with an image (<b>14</b>:9-12) because they </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>both have an image made of them (<b>13</b>:14-15; <b>14</b>:9).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  7.   The sea beast (<b>13</b>:1-10)
equals the beast in <b>11</b>:7.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        a.   They both overcome the
saints (<b>11</b>:7; <b>13</b>:7), the only two places in </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>Revelation where saints are said to be overcome.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        b.   The sea beast has been
shown to equal the scarlet beast. These three beasts arise voluntarily. No key
is used to free them to accomplish God’s purposes (<b>11</b>:7; <b>17</b>:8,
10).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        c.   Both have authority over
peoples, tribes, tongues, and nations (<b>11</b>:9; <b>13</b>:7).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
.5in'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>The first difficulty in this
identification is that no other single beast (</span><span lang=EL
style='font-size:12.0pt'>θήριον</span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'> [<i>thērion</i>])
ascends from the abyss. The </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>θήριον</span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'> of <b>11</b>:7, however, cannot be a separate beast
because that would make at least four beasts in Revelation. The text seems to
allow for only three beasts when all identifications have been made. The </span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>δράκων</span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>
does not come out of the abyss after the millennium because it is released from
its prison, the abyss. But the dragon’s release from the abyss does not seem to
fit the <b>11</b>:7 statement exactly for the following reasons:</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:135.0pt;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify;text-indent:-117.0pt'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              (1)    It is a </span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>θήριον</span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>
(undomesticated animal) rather than a </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:
12.0pt'>δράκων</span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'> [<i>drakōn</i>] that is to
come up, or arise.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              (2)    The </span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>θήριον</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:
12.0pt'> </span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>from the sea seems to triumph
over the saints <u>after</u> his coming up, whereas the dragon does not succeed
against God after the millennium.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              (3)    The </span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>θήριον</span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>
of <b>11</b>:7 seems to <u>arise freely</u> from his “abyss,” whereas </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>Satan <u>is released</u> from the abyss after the
millennium.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              (4)    The beast and the
false prophet are thrown directly into the lake of </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>fire and sulfur immediately after their defeat by the
King of Kings.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              (5)    The </span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>δράκων</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:
12.0pt'> </span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>is distinguished from the sea
beast of whom the scarlet beast is the presumed equal, and it is said of the
scarlet beast also (<b>17</b>:8) that he comes up from the abyss.                      </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              The difficulty here may
be overcome by making the abyss of <b>11</b>:7 the same as the sea of <b>13</b>:1
from which the sea beast arises.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              (1) The Septuagint used </span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ἄβυσσος</span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>
(<i>abyssos</i>) to translate </span><span lang=HE dir=RTL style='font-size:
14.0pt'>תְּהוֹם</span><span dir=LTR></span><span lang=HE style='font-size:12.0pt'><span
dir=LTR></span> </span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>(“the deep”) in Genesis <b>1</b>:2.
“The deep” </span><span style='font-size:12.0pt;color:windowtext'>in that
passage means</span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'> the sea, because dry land
has not yet appeared. There are considerable affinities between the creation
and consummation accounts.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              (2)    In Romans <b>10</b>:7
Paul calls Christ’s grave an abyss as a parallel to Deuteronomy <b>30</b>:11-14,
where the sea is mentioned. </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>Ἄβυσσος</span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'> occurs elsewhere in the New Testament only in Luke<b>
8</b>:31, where Legion request Jesus not to send them into the abyss; yet they
run into the sea.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
.5in'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>A second difficulty comes from identifying
the sea beast (<b>13</b>:1-10) with the beast in <b>11</b>:7: coming out of the
abyss is future in<b> 17</b>:8, four chapters after the sea beast arises.
Since, however, the scarlet beast equals the sea beast, events signified in <b>17</b>:1ff.
are probably an amplification of <b>13</b>:1ff.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            In summary, one beast is called a dragon,
a second is called the earth beast and false prophet, and a third is called the
sea beast, beast (<b>11</b>:7), beast with an image, and scarlet-colored beast.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;

style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        <b>Isaiah 6                             Ezekiel

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;

style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                                                     <b>1</b>:5,13<span

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>VII. General Observations and Conclusions</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  A.  Elements are sufficiently
repetitious to imply either (a) that </span><span style='font-size:12.0pt;
color:windowtext'>the repetition is</span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>
intentional or (b) that the visions were similar. Two possibilities exist in
such a case: either the later author gives a similar description because it is
as appropriate to his own subject as it was to the previous event from which he
draws his terminology, or he hints that he speaks of the same thing as his
predecessor.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        The second alternative seems
especially likely when (1) several parallels can be drawn between the two
prophecies; (2) when the previous prophecy was not fulfilled by the time the
second author wrote; (3) when the thing prophesied is not something that would
recur frequently; (4) when the parallels themselves are not common things; and
(5) when notable expressions do not appear elsewhere in scripture.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        Care must be taken in
identification, however. The dragon of Revelation<b> 12 </b>has seven heads, ten
horns, and (seven) diadems. The sea beast of Revelation <b>13 </b>also has
seven heads, ten horns, and (ten) diadems. Nevertheless, they are not the same
thing, because the dragon gives the sea beast his power (<b>13</b>:2). Though
related, they are not identical.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  B.   Animal symbolism comes mostly in
Daniel and Revelation.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  C. In these animal symbols,
something out of character is almost always present.  Frequently the “beasts”
are composites of different animals, as with John’s description of the “locusts”
in Revelation <b>9</b>. Sometimes animal and human features combine, as with
the strong angel of Revelation <b>10</b>. Animal and mineral combine in Daniel’s
fourth beast, which had iron teeth and brass claws (nails, <b>7</b>:19). Daniel’s
fourth beast and Ezekiel’s living creatures combine various animal
characteristics with both human and mineral elements. </span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:windowtext'>Some</span><span style='font-size:
12.0pt'> examples have something just plain “odd,” such as the three ribs
between the bear’s teeth in Daniel <b>7</b>:5 or unrealistic colors like the
pale green horse of Revelation <b>6</b>.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  D.  Time wise, animal symbolism
(indeed apocalyptic itself) comes primarily from the time of the seventy-year
exile (Ezekiel, Daniel, Zechariah) and the first seventy years of the apostolic
age. Apocalyptic appears almost exclusively in Jesus’ Olivet Discourse during
his final week. Even there, practically no animal symbolism comes up. Pauline
apocalyptic also lacks this rather “exotic” touch. Perhaps </span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:windowtext'>this pattern is because</span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'> animal symbolism is used mostly in the narration of
apocalyptic <u>visions</u>, where the author is presumably in a state of
ecstasy observing visual enactments before his mind’s eye.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        The apocalyptic imagery of subsequent
apocalyptic follows previous use of symbolism, perhaps even builds on it. The “locusts”
of Revelation<b> 9</b> may reflect the symbolism in Joel. Sometimes imagery is
reminiscent of pre-apocalyptic, or non-apocalyptic, notably the serpent
symbolism for Satan (cp. Genesis <b>3</b>) and the Lamb symbolism for Christ
(cp. sacrifice laws).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        Generally speaking, there does
not seem to be much development in apocalyptic animal symbolism. Composites
appear from Ezekiel onward. Good-connotation symbolism comes from that time as
well; Isaiah has good connotations associated with the seraphs, who elsewhere
appear in apocalyptic contexts.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
-.75in'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              E.   On
the whole, more can be said about elements of continuity in apocalyptic than
can be said about development.  </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        1.   Generally animal
symbolism carries bad connotation, two-thirds of the time, according to the
enumeration in Chart IV.  </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        2.   Horns usually indicate
kings; beasts frequently indicate whole kingdoms.  </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        3.   Undomesticated animals
predominate two to one in the examples above.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        4.   Domesticated animals do
not always carry good connotations, as one might expect. Only five of the
twelve domesticated animals give positive images. Conversely, seven of the
twenty-four wild animals carry good connotations; so undomesticated animals do
tend to connote bad things, but only in keeping with the predominance of evil
connotation for animal symbolism generally. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
