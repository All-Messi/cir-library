<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;

style='font-size:12.0pt'>         </span></b><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>In
the chart above , “kingdom of heaven” occurs in all the Matthew references
except the five places where “(K of G)” appears after the entry: <b>6</b>:33; <b>12</b>:28;
<b>19</b>:24; <b>21</b>:31, 43. “His kingdom” appears in <b>16</b>:28, and “Father’s

of God” in the passages indicated.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span

times where Matthew parallels the other synoptics and shows “kingdom of heaven”
where they say “kingdom of God.” Additionally, there are three places (shown in
brackets) that are not clearly parallel occasions, but they have similar
wordings except for “kingdom of heaven/God.”</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>         Matthew <b>19</b>:23-24 uses both
designations for the kingdom in the scope of two verses in a repeated statement

style='font-size:12.0pt'>         </span></b><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>A couple
of reasons can explain Matthew’s practice. Perhaps (1) he was trying to
emphasize the transcendent, interpersonal, spiritual nature of the kingdom in
contrast to the earthly, political kingdom the Jews had been expecting the Messiah
to establish. Another suggestion has been that (2) he was using “heaven” as a
substitute for “God” in much the way the prodigal son spoke of “sinning against
<u>Heaven</u>” (Luke <b>15</b>:18, 24). Such usage would not necessarily have
been to avoid offending the sensitivities of contemporary Judaism in the use of
the divine name. It could simply have become a habitual way of speaking. The
other gospels show no concern to avoid the term “God,” although they—as well as
the rest of the New Testament writings—do follow current custom by using the
Greek word <i>Lord</i> (</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>κύριος</span><span

style='font-size:12.0pt;color:windowtext'>),</span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>
which had become ineffable in Jewish custom. The sense that Matthew’s gospel
was aimed more deliberately at a Jewish audience may have made this practice
