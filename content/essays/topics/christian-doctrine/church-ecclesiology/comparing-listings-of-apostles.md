<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-45.0pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  In reference to Acts<b> 1</b>:13,
English readers will find <i>“Judas, the brother of James”</i> (KJV) and <i>“Judas,
the son of James”</i> (ASV, <i>etc.</i>). The difference is a translation matter,
not a manuscript variant. The Greek text simply reads <i>“Judas of James.”</i> The
words <i>“the <u>son</u> of”</i> or <i>“the <u>brother</u> of”</i> are added in
English to clarify the shorthand expression in the original.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-45.0pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  The shorthand expression <i>“X of Y”</i>
virtually always means <i>“son of,”</i> although if context could clarify it, the
format could indicate <i>“wife of,” “father of,” “brother of,”</i> or some other
relationship. The standard relationship, however, is son-to-father when the
idiom occurs.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-45.0pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  The reason translators like those of
the King James Version (the New King James reads <i>“son of”</i>) thought it
might be <i>“brother of”</i> here is that there were two prominent brothers in
the Jerusalem church named Judas and James. They authored the two New Testament
letters that bear those names. But those were half-brothers of Jesus, as New
Testament scholars generally agree. The Judas and James of Acts <b>1</b>:13
would surely not refer to them since the next verse mentions Jesus’ brothers as
present in the upper room just before Pentecost. There would be no reason to
mention them in<b> 1</b>:14 after naming them in <b>1</b>:13.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-45.0pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  We have no evidence for a
James-Judas set of brothers among the apostles. There is a Judas <i>“not
Iscariot”</i> (John <b>14</b>:22) that Luke includes in his apostle lists as <i>“Judas
of James”</i> (Luke <b>6</b>:14-16; Acts <b>1</b>:13), who in turn corresponds
to Thaddaeus (Lebbaeus) in Matthew <b>10</b>:2-4 and Mark<b> 3</b>:16-19. The
other brother combinations among the apostles are clearly labeled brothers
aside from the phrase <i>“X of Y.” </i>The text either says directly they are
brothers, or their fathers had the same name (as with Matthew Levi and James of
Alphaeus—Mark <b>2</b>:14;<b> 3</b>:18). So, we know of three sets of brothers:
Peter-Andrew, James-John, and Matthew-James of Alphaeus. Making Judas a “brother”
of James would at best create a three-way set of brothers between <i>“Judas of
James”</i>/Thaddaeus Lebbaeus, Matthew Levi, and James of Alphaeus. But for the
reasons in the previous paragraph, Judas <i>“brother of”</i> James would not
refer to the James-Jude who wrote New Testament books with those names.</span></p>
