<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;

style='font-size:14.0pt'>PRELIMINARY CONCEPTS IN ORGANIZATIONAL THEORY</span></b></p>

<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;
margin-bottom:0in;margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:center'><b><span
style='font-size:14.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></b></p>

<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;
margin-bottom:0in;margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:center'><span

<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;
margin-bottom:0in;margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:center'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>Influence and Authority</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>Definitions of Influence and Authority</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            Physical force, legal authority, and
personal influence are three means of controlling persons. The distinctness,
compatibility, and relationship of the latter two are especially important in a
study of church organization. <u>Authority</u> is the legal right to compel the
behavior only of status inferiors. <u>Influence</u> is the personal ability to
impel the behavior even of status superiors. A study of organizational
structure concerns itself with the pattern of authority flow.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>Natural
Leadership and Formal Leadership</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><u><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>Distinctions</span></u><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>.
Influence and authority are related respectively to the concepts of natural and
formal leadership, distinguished as follows: without authoritative direction
from above, <u>natural leadership</u> earns prestige as it rises spontaneously
from the bottom in a freely responding homogeneous group. Authoritative
direction originating from above, <u>formal leadership</u> has an ascribed
prestige that settles from the top over the general membership. The process of
natural leadership is carried out through example in the medium of personal
influence. The process of formal leadership is carried out under law in the
medium of impersonal authority. The two approaches differ in the level of
origination and in the medium and process of operation.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><u><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>Compatibility</span></u><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>.
While formal leadership is distinguished by authority initiated from above, it
is not comprehended thereby. In many instances authority reinforces a more
basic manner of proceeding. An official leads others most effectively when he
is sensitive to their needs and open to their suggestions so that frequently he
may only be initiating authoritatively what others have initiated personally
through suggestion. Even appointment to office may involve a selection process
by general membership, a procedure which merely makes overt, or “form-alized,”
the simple consensus of recognition in natural generation of leaders. Leaders
may be equally charismatic under either system.<span style='position:relative;
top:-3.0pt'>1</span></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><u><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>Relationship</span></u><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>.
The relationship between natural and formal leadership is illustrated in this
comparison of “above” <i>vs. </i>“below” in origination and personal vs.
authoritative in procedure. At least theoretically the whole leadership system
may be included in formal leadership so that the latter adds to without
necessarily detracting from. In practice the two approaches work together, as
in the home where the husband has <u>authority over</u> the wife and children
(Ephesians <b>5</b>:22-6:4; Colossians <b>3</b>:18, 20), but does not ignore
the needs and desires of these, who are his <u>personal equals</u> (Galatians <b>3</b>:28;
1 Corinthians <b>7</b>:1-5; Colossians <b>3</b>:19, 21). Interestingly, the
divine pattern for the home is the model for the church as exemplified by
Christ himself who both <u>loves</u> the church (Ephesians <b>5</b>:25) and <u>rules</u>
the church (Ephesians<b> 1</b>:20-23).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;
margin-bottom:0in;margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:center'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>Fig. 9. Natural and Formal Leadership</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;

style='font-size:12.0pt'><img width=154 height=161

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            Unlike the inherited offices of priest and
king in the Old Testament period, all positions in the church require
charismatic leaders, who first possess qualifications germane to their tasks,
rather than official leaders, who may hold office by birthright, purchase, and
the like.<span style='position:relative;top:-3.0pt'>2 </span>In the church,
formal leadership always includes natural leadership and is never given until
after service and personal maturity. Authority derives ultimately from ability
in the person of God and is delegated to worthies in government, church, and
home. In the church, authority is always for the edification of the saints, not
their destruction (2 Corinthians <b>10</b>:8 = <b>13</b>:10). It is for the
edification of others, not the aggrandizement of self (2 Corinthians <b>12</b>:14).
Because this thesis deals primarily with organizational pattern, it will trace
the lines of authority from Christ downward. The authority aspect should not,
however, be mistaken for the whole so as to give the false impression of a
rigid, formal institution led by authority-conscious men. Authority is always “added,”
and “after,” and is for others.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><u><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>Identifying Formal Leadership</span></u><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>. The peculiar extra-personal origin of formal
leadership establishes a method for identifying the leadership of the New
Testament as formal leadership: the fact that it is given from “outside the
person” shows that it is more than natural leadership, which arises from “within
the person”—from the power of his personality or the degree of his ability.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>Function and Office</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><u><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>Office Distinguished by </span></u></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><u><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>Supervisory Authority</span></u><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>. In the following analysis authority and influence
are related to a distinction between <u>authoritative offices</u> and <u>non-authoritative
functions</u>. The former inherently involve supervision of other people’s
activities while the latter do not, although there may be special cases where
supervisory authority is delegated from above <u>through</u> such functions. Any
responsibility includes the “right” to fulfill it, but many responsibilities
are such that they are individually fulfilled. Functions, then, are positions
of individual <u>service</u> only. Offices add to service the <u>authority</u>
of oversight.<span style='position:relative;top:-3.0pt'>3</span></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            Any community is potential for as many as
three classifications of its citizenry: <u>general members</u> possessing basic
life status in the community, <u>ministers</u> responsible to the group for
certain functions, and <u>leaders</u> holding offices with supervisory
authority over the general constituency. Marks of each status are as follows:</span></p>

style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

style='font-size:12.0pt'>Ideally the status marks accumulate from left to
right. Characteristic “life qualities” carry into “function” and “office,”
sometimes forming part of their respective qualification requirements. A
selection process and an appointment procedure combine with the possibility of
financial support to distinguish functions and offices from general membership.
“Office” differs from “function” only in its peculiar qualifications and the
added responsibility for supervising the common task of the community to the
good of all. Authority <u>passes through</u> an <u>O</u>ffice; it <u>ends in</u>
a <u>F</u>unction as in Fig. 11. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            Church organization deals with authority
flow. More exactly it deals with supervisory authority flow. The following
brief treatment of church polity, therefore, is organized in terms of office
vs. function.<span style='position:relative;top:-3.0pt'>4</span></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><u><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>Method of identifying office</span></u><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>. The supervision peculiarity of offices establishes a
methodology for identifying them among the ministries of the church. One first
seeks out all those statuses that involve authority and then leaves the rest as
functions only. The “givenness” of New Testament leadership establishes it as
formal leadership. Within that formal leadership the supervisory- authority
element differentiates formal office from formal function.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>New Testament De-Emphasis </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>on Organization <i>per se</i></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><u><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>Emphasis on Natural Leadership</span></u><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>. From the standpoint of the nature of the church, the
de-emphasis on organization arises from (1) the emphasis on natural leadership,
individual responsibility, and the church as a voluntary society. <u>Christ</u>
himself was made head of the church because of ability and service (Philippians
<b>2</b>:5-8 + 9-11), and he laid down that same principle for all leaders in
the kingdom (Luke <b>22</b>:24-34; Matthew <b>19</b>:23-<b>20</b>:16). The <u>apostles</u>,
being charismatic leaders, led by ability more than by authority.<span
style='position:relative;top:-3.0pt'>5</span> Elders also were to lead on the
basis of character (1 Timothy <b>3</b>:1-7; Titus <b>1</b>:5-9). Many problems
among early Christians were therefore solved without a “showdown” in which
authority was finally invoked<span style='position:relative;top:-3.0pt'>6</span> so
that there is not a continuous display of where the authority lies. Emphasis on
personal purity of life, personal initiative in service, and interpersonal
trust rather than motivation by fear, make authority less prominent in the
functioning of a community with voluntary membership.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><u><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>Abbreviated Records</span></u><span style='font-size:
12.0pt'>. From the standpoint of information about the church, de-emphasis on
organization comes from (2) the abbreviated records. The New Testament does not
preserve a detailed history of “all the acts of all the apostles,”<span
style='position:relative;top:-3.0pt'>7</span> but selects prominent persons and
major events to stand for the total pattern of expansion and formation. The
brevity, orderly arrangement, and scope<span style='position:relative;
top:-3.0pt'>8</span> of Luke’s summary imply that it epitomizes the whole
process of establishing the church in the world. His omission of any word about
ordering the churches of the second missionary journey, for instance, does not
suggest that they were not organized or were organized differently from the
previous churches (Acts <b>11</b>:23), as the presence of bishops in Philippi
shows (Acts <b>16</b>:11-40; Philippians <b>1</b>:1-2).<span style='position:
relative;top:-3.0pt'>9</span> Rather, the omission derives from a selective
manner of narrative style that expects the reader in recurring situations to
supply repetitious elements on the principle of generalization. The amount of
historical material on organization results partly from Luke’s terse manner of
presentation.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><u><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>Simplicity of the Pattern</span></u><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>. Finally, from the standpoint of the modern expectations
about the church, the amount of material on organization results from (3) the
relative simplicity of the pattern. Focusing at the wrong level of magnitude
sometimes makes it hard to find a city on a road map because the letters of the
name are smaller than anticipated. In much the same way modern ecclesiastical
systems may unconsciously influence a person to set his mind at the wrong level
of complexity as he searches for the New Testament system; consequently, he may
find <u>less</u> than expected and construe it as <u>none</u>.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            The New Testament lays moderate emphasis
on church organization, interweaving its presentation with other, dynamic
factors. As a result, a person must take care lest he confuse the writers’
de-emphasis with disinterest, and conclude either that there was no form or
that form was non-germane, hence variable, in Christianity. The uniqueness of
the church is not in the accomplishing of its purposes purely on the basis of
interpersonal power, but in the ordering of personal influence before
impersonal authority in the accomplishment of those purposes.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;
margin-bottom:0in;margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:center'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>Local and General</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>Definitions of Local and General</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            A local office is one filled by persons
from within the church where they exercise authority. A general office has at
least potential responsibility over a number of churches. A more precise
definition of “local” is difficult and at the same time helpful to establishing
positively a normative pattern for church unity. “Local” is used here to mean
the <u>perceived basic unit</u> of composition as distinguished from the <u>smallest
discernible unit</u>. The task is to determine which level of grouping in the
New Testament is to be viewed as the basic unity of organizational composition:
the local church.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            The household church is the smallest scope
of reference in the word </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ἐκκλησία</span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>. Paul alone uses it in this sense: (1) in <u>Ephesus</u>
the household church of Aquila and Prisca (1 Corinthians <b>16</b>:19); (2) in
Rome another one sponsored later by the same couple (Romans<b> 16</b>:5; note
also <b>16</b>:14, 15); (3) in <u>Laodicea</u> the household church of a woman
named “Nympha” (Colossians <b>4</b>:15);<span style='position:relative;
top:-3.0pt'>10</span> (4) in <u>Colossae</u> the church in the house of
Philemon (Philemon 2).<span style='position:relative;top:-3.0pt'>11</span></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            Although the <u>household church</u> is
the <u>smallest unit</u> denominated </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:
12.0pt'>ἐκκλησία</span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>, the next larger scope
of its reference, <u>the city</u>, seems to be the <u>basic unit</u>. The first
reason lies in the usage of </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ἐκκλησία</span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'> itself: “church” is uniformly singular with reference
to cities<span style='position:relative;top:-3.0pt'>12 </span>despite the fact
that (a) a <u>plurality</u> of household churches existed in some cities.<span
style='position:relative;top:-3.0pt'>13</span> (b) In certain places where no
household churches are specifically mentioned, the Christian community was
obviously larger than what could have congregated in one house even if it had a
spacious inner courtyard.<span style='position:relative;top:-3.0pt'>14</span> The
big cities Jerusalem, Antioch, Ephesus, and Corinth are among those that have
the singular “church” used of them. (c) Wherever examinable, “church” is
distributive (“every church,” “no church,” “churches”) at the city level rather
than above, or below, that level.<span style='position:relative;top:-3.0pt'>15</span> The
usage of words shows that the New Testament writers perceived “local” to be at
the city order of magnitude.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            The second reason city seems to be the
perceived basic unit is that it is the lowest level with organization. What
lacks organization cannot by definition be the basic unit of organization. <u>Elders
are uniformly mentioned with cities</u><span style='position:relative;
top:-3.0pt'>16</span> rather than with a subunit household church or a higher
territorial section. Ephesus is important at this juncture, for Paul calls to
Miletus “the elders of the church in Ephesus” (Acts <b>20</b>:17). The text
does not <u>say</u> “the elders of the church<u>es</u> in Ephesus”
(distributive); neither does it <u>mean</u> “church elders in Ephesus”
(generic), for Paul proceeds to address the overseers of a unit called “the
flock” (specific; <b>20</b>:28-29).<span style='position:relative;top:-3.0pt'>17</span> Luke
speaks of “appointing elders in every church” (Acts <b>14</b>:23) whereas Paul
speaks of “appointing elders in every city” (Titus <b>1</b>:5).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            In the New Testament, church seems to have
been coextensive with community. Local churches, defined as the basic units of
composition and operation, must have varied greatly in size according to the
size of the population cluster they pervaded. In more populous centers a sense
of the church’s city-wide solidarity could have been provided for by occasional
or regular meetings in public facilities like the stadium, amphitheater, or the
temple area in the case of Jerusalem (Acts <b>2</b>:46; <b>5</b>:12, 42). The
household churches served as functional equivalents of modern cell groups,
cottage prayer meetings, encounter groups, Sunday School classes, <i>etc</i>.,
where close associations in worship, fellowship, and service could give the
personal attention necessary in edification, exhortation, and evangelism. Smaller
cities may not have had subunits. That the city was perceived as local is seen
(1) in the constant usage of “church” with city and (2) in the placement of the
lowest organizational position (elder) at the city level. In confirmation of
these two arguments, (3) all New Testament correspondence addressed to <u>a
church</u> was addressed to <u>a city</u>.<span style='position:relative;
top:-3.0pt'>18</span> Though varying greatly in size, these local units were <u>universally
parallel</u> to one another in that they were <u>uniformly the basic units</u>
of composition and operation.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>General Ministry and General Organization</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            Since “organization” is being used to mean
the pattern of authority flow, it follows that non-authoritative functions
involved in general <u>ministry</u> are not parts of a general <u>organization</u>
exercising control over local leaders. General ministry is a functioning from
place to place serially, rather than a presiding over many places
simultaneously. One might labor in several congregations at one time, but he
would still be operating in a cycle, rather than over a circle. General <u>ministry</u>
does not require general <u>authority</u> since <u>general</u> ministry may be
exercised under <u>local</u> authority as one moves from place to place in
educating and exhorting the <u>established churches</u>.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            In pioneering <u>new</u> work, the general
minister can lead on the dynamic basis of ability under the jurisdiction of the
word, depending on the inherent power of the gospel he demonstrates and
proclaims to bear fruit in the lives of those who hear God’s word and see his
example. He can stand as a natural leader among God’s increase, carrying a
personal ascendancy rather than an official authority. His moving new converts
on to develop their own leadership is through the same means by which he was
God’s instrument for converting them in the first place: the authority of the
word, to which they have already committed themselves; and his ability as a
minister to lead equals by personal influence. As conversion does not involve a
minister’s authority over the hearer, so also growth in Christ does not
necessitate it, especially when he can appeal to the written authority.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            Since the functions of general ministry
may be fulfilled without invoking the principle of authority, the biblical
interpreter must determine whether a given general function has the authority
dimension added so as to make it part of church organization in the strict
sense.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>Organization of the Church and </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>Organization of the Churches</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            How many churches existed in the first
century is not known, and how even the known churches were patterned is not
mentioned in many cases. This lack of information about congregations does not
preclude an inquiry into the <u>form</u> of the <u>universal</u> church for two
reasons: (1) The essential point in the section on “Basic Structure” is the
absence of any general authoritative office between the twelve apostles and the
groups of local elders; hence, the local leaders functioned under the apostles
directly.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            (2) Organization is by nature a deductive
matter, rather than an inductive one, because authority flows down rather than
up. Coming to a conclusion about the form of the church requires <u>sufficient</u>
evidence, but not exhaustive evidence about all the local units in the
organization.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;
margin-bottom:0in;margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:center'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>Summary</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            With respect to leadership, influence and
authority are the <u>means</u> of which natural and formal leadership are the <u>modes</u>;
“function” and “office” label positions characterized by them. While the
influence-natural-function set differs from the authority-formal-office set,
the two systems are made compatible in Christian leadership by placing the
former before the latter. Since the church is a voluntary society, the New
Testament emphasizes natural leadership by requiring that a man first be able
to influence before he is given the authority to direct. Church offices are
therefore distinguished from functions by the added feature of supervisory
authority.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            Local church means the perceived basic
unit of composition as distinguished from the smallest one. In the first
century the local church was coextensive with population cluster as is evident
from three observations: (1) “church” is uniformly singular with cities; (2)
eldership, the lowest office, was uniformly at the city level; (3) all
correspondence addressed to a church was addressed to a city. Larger cities had
a series of household units that collectively comprised the city-wide local
church.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;
margin-bottom:0in;margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:center'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>ENDNOTES</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            <span style='position:relative;top:-3.0pt'>1</span>Note
in this context the applicability of Lawrence Richard’s insights on “Motivating
the Learner,” in <i>Creative Bible Teaching</i>, pp. 128-36. Paul’s forbearance
of rights may be observed in 1 Corinthians <b>9</b>:12; 2 Thessalonians <b>3</b>:9;
Philemon 8, where he would rather exhort and beseech as an equal than command
as a superior.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            <span style='position:relative;top:-3.0pt'>2</span>In
the following passages one can see leadership selected on the basis of germane
qualifications: Philippians <b>2</b>:5-11 (Lord); Acts <b>1</b>:21-22 (apostle);
<b>6</b>:3 (the seven); 1 Timothy<b> 3</b>:1-7; Titus <b>1</b>:5-9 (elders); 1
Timothy <b>3</b>:8-13 (deacons). Examples of non-germane bases for leadership
may be gleaned from Paul’s opposition to false leaders in Corinth: bodily
appearance (2 Corinthians <b>10</b>:9-10); speech (1 Corinthians<b> 1</b>:17; <b>10</b>:6:
2 Corinthians <b>11</b>:6); <i>“riding in on someone else’s shirt tail” </i>(2
Corinthians <b>10</b>:14-16; cp. Romans <b>15</b>:20); “bossiness” (2
Corinthians <b>11</b>:20); ancestry (2 Corinthians<b> 11</b>:18 + 22; cp. Luke <b>3</b>:8);
letters of commendation (2 Corinthians <b>3</b>:1).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;position:relative;top:-3.0pt'>            3</span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>In common parlance “office” frequently covers both “function”
and “office” as used above. Since the following analysis attaches particular
significance to this distinctive feature, it seems advisable in these
preliminary remarks to establish a consistent use of separate terms that point
up the presence or absence of supervisory authority.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            <span style='position:relative;top:-3.0pt'>4</span><u>Offices</u>:
Lord, apostle, elder; <u>functions</u>: evangelist, prophet, deacon(ess). Other
classifications include <u>supernaturally endowed</u> (Lord, apostle, prophet)
vs. <u>ordinary</u> (evangelist, elder, deacon); <u>general</u> (Lord, apostle,
evangelist, prophet) vs. <u>local</u> (elder, deacon); <u>outreach</u> (Lord,
apostle, prophet, evangelist, elder) vs. <u>internal</u> (deacon), the first
category in each pair being inclusive of the later category.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            <span style='position:relative;top:-3.0pt'>5</span>For
an example of how closely Paul associated apostleship and ability displayed in
service, note that in his second epistle he defends to the Corinthians his
apostleship by appealing to his service for Christ (<b>11</b>:16-<b>12</b>:13),
knowledge (<b>6</b>:6; cf. <b>11</b>:6), experience with Christ (<b>12</b>:1-6),
suffering for Christ (<b>11</b>:23-27), concern for the saints (<b>11</b>:28-29),
trust- worthiness (<b>4</b>:2) and other basic Christian virtues (<b>6</b>:1-10).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            <span style='position:relative;top:-3.0pt'>6</span>Certain
exceptions include 2 Corinthians <b>13</b>:1-10; 1 Timothy <b>1</b>:18-20.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            <span style='position:relative;top:-3.0pt'>7</span>J.
W. McGarvey in <i>New Commentary on Acts of Apostles</i> suggests that the
anarthrous title </span><i><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>Πράξεις</span></i><i><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span></i><i><span lang=EL style='font-size:
12.0pt'>Ἀποστόλων</span></i><span style='font-size:12.0pt'> is meant to
indicate this fact (pp. vii-viii). The lack of articles may simply indicate
qualitative reference, however.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            <span style='position:relative;top:-3.0pt'>8</span>Gamaliel
argues that the Jews should be careful about what they did to the apostles
because, unlike previous Messianic claimants, Jesus of Nazareth did not begin a
movement that died with him: Christianity was not another “fly-by-night,”
Zealot uprising (Acts <b>5</b>:33-42). God gave the Jewish nation forty years
to recognize this fact before he allowed the Romans to destroy Jerusalem and
the temple, which stood for the whole Mosaic system (Hebrews<b> 8</b>). Luke
seems to have published his Acts only some three years before the Roman armies
began making their way up from the coast to Jerusalem in A.D. 66. The scope of
Acts corresponds, then, to the period of time for establishing the church to
the extent that Jerusalem could be cut off. For a treatment of the dates
proposed for the writing of Acts, see Donald Guthrie, <i>The Pauline Epistles</i>,
Vol. I: <i>New Testament Introduction</i>, pp. 307-15. An account of the Jewish
war appears in Josephus <i>Wars</i> <b>3</b>:1:1-<b>7</b>:10:1.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            The publication of Acts also corresponds
with the time when Christianity was no longer considered by the Romans as a
branch of Judaism, as the imminent Neronian persecutions imply.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            <span style='position:relative;top:-3.0pt'>9</span>On
the equation of bishop and elder see Chapter III.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            <span style='position:relative;top:-3.0pt'>10</span>See
<i>in loco</i> the critical apparatus in the Kurt Aland Greek text for
manuscript support of various readings on the name <i>Nympha</i>.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            <span style='position:relative;top:-3.0pt'>11</span>Other
examples of the household church seem to occur where “church” is not used to
designate the group: (5) Mary the mother of John (Acts<b> 12</b>:5-17). Peter
naturally went there after his release from prison; many were gathered for
prayer on his behalf. He then sent word of the deliverance to “James and the
brethren,” who were elsewhere, while he himself “went to another place”—all
this in connection with the fact that “fervent prayer was made by <u>the church</u>
to God for him.”</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            (6) A person wonders whether the house of
Stephanas in <u>Corinth</u> maintained a church as part of their ministry (1
Corinthians<b> 16</b>:15-16), and whether Gaius of 3 John 1 had one in his home
since he was in a position to house itinerant Christians like John. Paul may
have household units in mind when he expresses a deep concern for false
teachers “who overthrow whole houses” (Titus <b>1</b>:11).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            <span style='position:relative;top:-3.0pt'>12</span>The
singular <i>church</i> refers to <u>Jerusalem</u> (Acts<b> 5</b>:11; <b>8</b>:1;
<b>11</b>:22; <b>12</b>:5; <b>15</b>:4, 22), <u>Caesarea</u> (Acts <b>18</b>:22;
one might follow the <u>T</u>extus <u>R</u>eceptus, which adds in <b>18</b>:21
the statement about Paul’s intention to go to Jerusalem so as to imply that the
church to which he “went up” was Jerusalem rather than Caesarea; “down from”
could substantiate the TR since it might not be an appropriate expression for
travel from Caesarea to Antioch as shown in Acts <b>14</b>:25; <b>15</b>:2), <u>Antioch</u>
(Acts <b>11</b>:26; <b>13</b>:1; <b>14</b>:27; <b>15</b>:3), <u>Laodicea</u>
(Colossians <b>4</b>:15, 16), <u>Ephesus</u> (Acts 20:17), <u>Philippi</u>
(Philippians <b>4</b>:15), <u>Thessalonica</u> (1 Thessalonians <b>1</b>:1; 2
Thessalonians <b>1</b>:1), <u>Corinth</u> (1 Corinthians <b>1</b>:2; <b>14</b>:4,
5, 12, 19, 23, 28, 35; 2 Corinthians <b>1</b>:1), <u>Cenchrea</u> (Romans <b>16</b>:1).
The list could be enlarged by inference: <u>Colossae</u> and <u>Hierapolis</u> are
compared with Laodicea (Colossians <b>4</b>:13-16; note <b>1</b>:2). <u>Lystra</u>,
<u>Iconium</u>, and <u>Derbe</u> might be added on the comparison of Titus <b>1</b>:5
(“in every city”) and Acts <b>14</b>:23 (“in every church”; Acts <b>16</b>:5;
Galatians <b>1</b>:22).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            The plural “churches” appears <u>within
territorial reference</u>: Judaea (Galatians <b>1</b>:22; 2 Thessalonians <b>2</b>:14;
cf. Acts <b>9</b>:31), Syria and Cilicia (Acts<b> 15</b>:41); Asia (1
Corinthians <b>16</b>:19; the seven churches in Revelation <b>1</b>:4, 11, 20<span
style='position:relative;top:-3.0pt'>2</span>; <b>2</b>:7, 11, 17, 23, 29; <b>3</b>:6,
13, 22; <b>22</b>:16), Galatia (Acts <b>14</b>:23 “every church”;<b> 16</b>:5;
1 Corinthians <b>16</b>:1; Galatians <b>1</b>:22), Macedonia (2 Corinthians<b>
8</b>:1). It appears once <u>within ethnic reference</u>: Gentiles (Romans <b>16</b>:4).
The other plurals are <u>without limiting reference</u>: Romans<b> 16</b>:16 (“churches
of Christ”); 1 Corinthians<b> 4</b>:17; <b>7</b>:17; <b>11</b>:16 (“churches of
God”); <b>14</b>:33, 34; 2 Corinthians <b>8</b>:1, 18, 19, 23, 24; <b>11</b>:8,
28;<b> 12</b>:13; Philippians<b> 4</b>:15 (“no church”).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            <span style='position:relative;top:-3.0pt'>13</span>Rome
had at least three such churches (Romans<b> 16</b>:5, 14, 15). That Paul does
not use the singular “church” in the salutation of Romans seems insignificant
since he does so in the cyclical letter of “Ephesians” (<b>1</b>:1) and in
Philippians (<b>1</b>:1) and Colossians (<b>1</b>:2). “Ephesus” and “Philippi”
elsewhere have “the church of” used with them (Acts <b>20</b>:17; Philippians <b>4</b>:15).
If the church of Nympha was in Colossae (Colossians <b>4</b>:15-16) rather than
in Laodicea, then two household churches met there. Philemon, who had a church
in his home (Philippians <b>2</b>), was a resident of Colossae as shown by the
fact that his slave Onesimus was from that city (Colossians <b>4</b>:9).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            <span style='position:relative;top:-3.0pt'>14</span>Already
by Acts<b> 4</b>:4 the number of believers in Jerusalem was nearly ten
thousand—more than could gather in the house of Mary, for instance (Acts<b> 12</b>:1-19).
At an early date Antioch contained a large concentration of Christians (Acts <b>11</b>:19-26;
<b>15</b>:30-35). The response to the gospel in Ephesus was so tremendous
during the twenty-seven months Paul ministered there that $8,000 worth of books
on magic (the occult?) were burned publicly and the silversmiths feared for the
reputation of heathen artwork (Acts <b>19</b>). Because the Lord had “much
people” in Corinth, Paul stayed for a relatively long ministry of eighteen
months.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            One wonders whether the presence of a
number of household churches in big population centers is behind expressions
like <i>the <u>whole</u> church</i> on certain occasions of general assembly: Acts
<b>6</b>:2; <b>14</b>:27; <b>15</b>:22; <b>20</b>:28; Romans <b>16</b>:23; 1
Corinthians <b>14</b>:23. Such gatherings may have taken place in the stadium
or amphitheater, or in the temple area in Jerusalem (Acts <b>2</b>:46; <b>5</b>:12,
42).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            <span style='position:relative;top:-3.0pt'>15</span>(a)
The “seven churches of Asia” (Revelation <b>1</b>:11, 20<span style='position:
relative;top:-3.0pt'>2</span>; <b>2</b>:7, 11, 17, 29; <b>3</b>:6, 13, 22; <b>22</b>:16)
were specifically Ephesus, Smyrna, Pergamum, Thyatira, Sardis, Philadelphia,
and Laodecia (Revelation <b>1</b>:11; <b>2</b>:1, 8, 12, 18;<b> 3</b>:1, 7,
14). (b) As respects organizing the churches in Galatia, Luke says Paul and
Barnabas “appointed elders in <u>every church</u>” (Acts<b> 14</b>:23), whereas
Paul tells Titus in a comparable situation in Crete to “appoint elders in <u>every
city</u>” (Titus <b>1</b>:5). This equal scope of reference in city and church
may also be the point in another episode: (c) after the Jerusalem conference
Paul took Silas and “went through Syria and Cilicia, confirming the <u>churches</u>”
(Acts <b>15</b>:41); <i>“as they went through the <u>cities</u>, they delivered
the decrees . . . So the <u>churches</u> were strengthened”</i> (Acts <b>16</b>:4-5).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            <span style='position:relative;top:-3.0pt'>16</span>Elements
of organization are associated with <u>Jerusalem</u> in Acts <b>15</b>:2, 4, 5,
6, 22, 23; <b>16</b>:4; <b>21</b>:18 (+ 15); with <u>Lystra</u>, <u>Iconium</u>,
<u>Antioch of Pisidia</u>, <i>etc</i>., in Acts <b>14</b>:23 (+21); with <u>Ephesus</u>
in Acts <b>20</b>:17, 28; 1 Timothy<b> 3</b>:2; <b>5</b>:17, 19 (+ <b>1</b>:3);
with <u>Philippi</u> in Philippians <b>1</b>:1; with cities of Crete in Titus<b>
1</b>:5, 7. For evidence that eldership was the lowest office, see Chapter III.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            <span style='position:relative;top:-3.0pt'>17</span>In
1 Peter<b> 5</b>:1-4 the apostle addresses <u>the Dispersion</u> in Pontus,
Galatia, Cappadocia, Asia, and Pontus, and says <i>“<u>the elders</u> that are
among you . . . tend <u>the flock</u>.” The letter being general by multiple or
cyclical distribution, Peter does not mean the flock that equals the whole
territory of Asia Minor, but “the elders that are among you tend the flock [in
the place where the letter is now being read].”</i> This understanding
distributively rather than corporately, telling the sick to “call <u>the elders</u>
of the church” (<b>5</b>:14-15) and the members generally not to show respect
of persons in their assembly (<b>2</b>:1ff.).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            <span style='position:relative;top:-3.0pt'>18</span><u>Corinth</u>
in 1 Corinthians <b>1</b>:2 and 2 Corinthians <b>1</b>:1; <u>Philippi</u> in
Philippians <b>1</b>:1 + <b>4</b>:15; <u>Colossae</u> in Colossians <b>4</b>:16;
<u>Thessalonica</u> in 1 Thessalonians <b>1</b>:1 and 2 Thessalonians <b>1</b>:1;
<u>Ephesus</u> in Revelation<b> 2</b>:1; <u>Smyrna</u> in Revelation <b>2</b>:8;
<u>Pergamum</u> in Revelation<b> 2</b>:12; <u>Thyatira</u> in Revelation<b> 2</b>:18;
<u>Sardis</u> in Revelation <b>3</b>:1; <u>Philadelphia</u> in Revelation <b>3</b>:7;
<u>Laodecia</u> in Revelation <b>3</b>:14; Colossians<b> 4</b>:16; Ephesians<b>
1</b>:1 (but see p<span style='position:relative;top:-3.0pt'>46</span>, ‡*, B* <i>in
loco</i>). Philemon is addressed secondarily to a household church meeting in
the home of its primary recipient.</span></p>
