text-indent:-.5in'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>Salvation and Unity</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
.5in'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>“Individual unity with Christ” refers to
the interpersonal relationship with Christ made possible by his moral
perfection. Individual salvation both precedes and pervades the subsequent <u>horizontal</u>
group aspects of Christian unity.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
.5in'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>Gestalt psychology has popularized a
helpful concept that brings into focus the place and necessity of unity: <i>“the
whole is more that the sum of its parts.”</i><span style='position:relative;
top:-3.0pt'>1</span> Collective unity as expressed in structure-function is one
corporate quality that lies beyond the sheer sum of parts. “Collective unity
through Christ” moves back far enough from “individual unity with Christ” to
see all as one in the state of salvation. Collective unity combines with
functional and organizational unity to form the dynamic composite of being,
action, and relationship that stands distinct from, and parallel to, “the world”
and is called “the church”: saved people (collective unity) cooperating
(functional unity) in divine pattern (organizational unity). As the whole is
more than the sum of its parts, the church is more than the sum of the saved.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
.5in'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>While church unity is within the context
of salvation, the purpose of this study is not to deal with soteriology as
such, except to draw out its restrictive implications for the form of the
church–implications that negatively preclude denominationalism without in
themselves positively determining the precise pattern for
non-denominationalism.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span

<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:center'><u><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>General Structure-Function Characteristics:  Being</span></u></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:center'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>Historical</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            Various combinations of denominational
characteristics are often reflected in, and reinforced by, an accompanying
separate organization. This study outlines the original pattern of the church
as the normative apostolic precedent recorded authoritatively in New Testament
history. The positive presentation for non-denominational unity is rooted in
the realm of <u>historical</u> origins, whereas the negative presentation
against denominationalism would be argued on the principle of consistency from <u>theological</u>
and <u>practical</u> considerations. In consequence of these three approaches,
the New Testament impresses on the modern church a need for present unity on
earth in conformity with the fact of a common origin in history and a common
destiny in eternity.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:center'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>Visible</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
.5in'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>Visible unity is more at issue than the
invisible oneness into which studies of Christian unity sometimes retreat
leaving the visible church divided without blame.<span style='position:relative;
top:-3.0pt'>2</span> The distinction, valid in itself, offers no avenue either
for making indifferent, or for justifying, division in the visible church. The
saved are of course one in their being saved, but such essential unity is only
the <u>basis-in-being</u> on which the New Testament boldly builds an
additional unity of <u>function-in-form</u>. Though the church is people, it is
pattern as well. Though it is first invisible, it is also to manifest itself.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:center'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>Universal</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
.5in'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>The kind of organizational oneness in the
visible, universal church is the full and proper subject of this thesis. It
goes beyond the question of local autonomy and structure to the larger question
of whether there can be any present earthly headquarters, one or several, in
the united church of Christ catholic.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:center'><u><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>Functional Unity in Christ:  Action</span></u></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:center'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>Common Approaches to Christian Unity<span
style='position:relative;top:-3.0pt'>3</span></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><u><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>Doctrinal Reductionism</span></u></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><u><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'><span style='text-decoration:none'>&nbsp;</span></span></u></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
.5in'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>Erasmus gave classical expression to the
concept that the ultimate basis for Christian unity lies in the most elemental
articles of the Christian faith. Aside from them there is nothing required for
Christian fellowship and church unity.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><u><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>Organizational Union</span></u></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
.5in'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>Richard Hooker may be cited as a prominent
exponent of the view that the church is primary over the individual believer. Unity,
therefore, results <u>from</u> the church rather than <u>in</u> the church. Roman
Catholicism has been an advocate of this approach along with individual leaders
in other denominations.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><u><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>Personal Conversion</span></u></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
.5in'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>In addition to the doctrinal and the
institutional, there is the experiential basis for unity that arose among the
pietists of England and Holland. The common salvation of the saved is viewed as
the decisive factor that provides for, establishes, and seeks to express itself
in, the unity of the church.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:center'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:center'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:center'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>The Approach Herein Presupposed</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><u><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>Formal Approach</span></u></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><u><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'><span style='text-decoration:none'>&nbsp;</span></span></u></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
.5in'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>The formal approach to the visible unity
of the church may be expressed in one general principle: “What distinguishes
Christians form the world is what unites them together.” What takes individuals
out of the world in the first place becomes the <u>minimum basis</u> for
uniting them together in the church. Everything that distinguishes Christians
from the world becomes the <u>maximum requirement</u> for uniting them
together.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
.5in'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>Within this broad framework lie four sets
of distinctions that guide the church in its growth from minimum toward maximum
unity. (1) All distinctives are important, but all are not equally central to
the Christian system.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
.5in'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>(2) The conditions for church membership
are not the same as the qualifications for church leadership. To become a
Christian, a person does not have to be <u>full-grown</u>; but after he has
been <u>born</u> in <u>justification,</u> he must be <u>growing</u> in <u>sanctification</u>
toward naturally leading in the church.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
.5in'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>(3) Ignorance differs from rejection and
unbelief differs from disbelief. Some Christians may need to know the way of
the Lord more perfectly. This is the work of <u>Christian education</u> in its
broadest and most practical sense: <u>perfecting</u> Christian unity. Others
may reject added truths or may apostatize from previous beliefs or
righteousness. This is the work of <u>church discipline</u> discreetly
exercised in connection with “first and second admonitions”: <u>preserving</u>
Christian unity. So nothing is perfected or preserved by denominations that
could not be effectively accomplished without them.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
.5in'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>(4) Earnest uncertainty about a truth is
different from blatant opposition to it.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
.5in'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>Beyond the minimum basis for unity there
is the tension to move toward perfect oneness. Failure to come to agreement
must be viewed as temporary lack of progress in a context of mutual respect of
persons and common submission to the will of God.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
.5in'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>What distinguishes Christians from the
world is what unites them together. Therefore, <u>only</u> Christian
distinctives are necessary for Christian unity, and <u>no additional</u> ones
may be required. <u>All</u> are necessary so that an emphasis on <u>some</u>
cannot be the basis for a division of the church. No denomination can be
founded on less or more than all Christ teaches and commands his one church. Founding
a new denomination is not an orthodox means of maintaining orthodoxy.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><u><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>Material Application</span></u></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
.5in'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>Certain material elements of these formal
guidelines will be implicit in the assertions of the thesis. Conversion takes
place in response to doctrinal matters, and on the conditions of responsive
faith God saves. He saves from sin, adds to the church, and gives minimum
church unity on the same conditions and at the same time. Doctrine, experience,
and organization are therefore <u>temporally simultaneous</u> as well as <u>logically
sequential</u> in that order. The outworking of Christian unity comes in (1)
the common acceptance of the Bible as authority, (2) doctrinal agreement<span
style='position:relative;top:-3.0pt'>4</span>, (3) practical cooperation, and
(4) organizational oneness.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:center'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>Fig. 4.  Formal Framework of Church Unity</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;

<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;

<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;
margin-bottom:0in;margin-left:-4.5pt;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:center'><u><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>Organizational Unity Under Christ:  Relationship</span></u></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:-4.5pt;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;
margin-bottom:0in;margin-left:-4.5pt;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:center'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>Summary of Corporate Ecclesiastical Patterns</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:-4.5pt;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:-4.5pt;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify;text-indent:40.5pt'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>From an organizational standpoint the forms of the
universal church are divisible into three general groups according to the
presence or absence of earthly “headquarters” and local congregations: (1)
local congregations and earthly headquarters; (2) no local congregations and no
earthly headquarters; (3) local congregations and no earthly headquarters.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:-4.5pt;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:-4.5pt;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:-4.5pt;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><u><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>Local Congregations and</span></u></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:-4.5pt;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><u><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>Earthly Headquarters</span></u></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><u><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'><span style='text-decoration:none'>&nbsp;</span></span></u></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
.5in'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>“<u>World-Churchism</u>.” Two variations
exist in the category characterized by local congregations and earthly
headquarters. The first of these may be called “world-churchism” because there
is numerically one ecclesiastical organization on earth. Very generally this
concept of the church is represented by the next diagram. The Earthly
Headquarters may represent a continuation of the original “apostolic college.” Under
this central person or group of officials are different levels of jurisdiction
with <u>S</u>ervice <u>A</u>gencies and degrees of responsibility over the <u>L</u>ocal
<u>C</u>ongregations. To this pattern belongs notably the Roman Catholic Church
(in its doctrinal position on the church) as well as the goal of some
ecumenicists for the “Coming Great Church.”<span style='position:relative;
top:-3.0pt'>5</span> The unity here is organizational and is expressed in
organized action.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><u><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>Denominationalism</span></u><span style='font-size:
12.0pt'>. A second variation in this same category is denominationalism, or
pluralism. “Denominationalism” means here “world-churchism” multiplied into a
number of parallel systems whose polity may be episcopal or presbyterian.<span
style='position:relative;top:-3.0pt'>6</span></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;

style='font-size:12.0pt'>Fig. 6. Pattern of Denominationalism</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:center'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'><img width=319 height=180
src="/cir-library/images/essays/chapter-i/image007.png"></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>These denominations do not necessarily view themselves
individually as the only saved people. Either their differences are considered
sufficient to keep them from being organically united, or are insufficient to
impress them with the need for it. Unity in such cases is invisible, and is
expressed visibly through different degrees of interdenominational co-operation
on areas of common ground.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><u><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>No local congregations and</span></u></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><u><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>no earthly headquarters</span></u></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
.5in'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>Emil Brunner sets forth a view that seems
to be gaining momentum in modern attitudes toward the church: the <u>Ecclesia</u>
itself is aside from, other than, and without reference to, </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:center'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>Fig. 7.  Pattern of “No-Churchism”</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:center'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'><img width=181 height=98
src="/cir-library/images/essays/chapter-i/image008.png"></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>organization.<span style='position:relative;
top:-3.0pt'>7<i>e.g</i>.</span> <u>Ecclesia</u> is indeed people (</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ἐκκλησία</span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>)
in vertical communion and horizontal fellowship (</span><span lang=EL
style='font-size:12.0pt'>κοινωνία</span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>), in
service (</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>διακονία</span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>), and the like; but it does not include form in the
organizational sense. Unity is, therefore, not formal, as in world-churchism. It
is more than invisible, as in interdenominationalism; it is simply and
significantly dynamic.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><u><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>Local Congregations and</span></u></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><u><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>No Earthly Headquarters</span></u></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
.5in'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>The following schematic shows the
relationship of Christ, the Apostles, <u>L</u>ocal <u>C</u>ongregations, and <u>S</u>ervice
<u>A</u>gencies in an organizational pattern that has no earthly headquarters.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:center'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'><img width=236 height=125
src="/cir-library/images/essays/chapter-i/image009.png"></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><u><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>The dynamic nature of non-</span></u></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><u><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>denominational authority</span></u><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>. There are various forms of interpersonal control: physical
force, personal influence, and legal authority. Of these three, the pattern for
the flow of authority is the peculiar emphasis in the organizational aspect of
unity. Non-denominationalism has a system of authority that flows in such a way
that the universal church is one in it while relying as little as possible on
it.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
.5in'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>Describing as a corporate “organization” a
series of autonomous congregations with no earthly headquarters, does not
accommodate the term as long as Christ and the apostles are in the pattern. Nevertheless,
a person’s historical context of denominational complexity may cause him to
misconstrue the simplicity of that original pattern as no real pattern at all. The
church is a high-trust, low-fear system that relies more on personalized power
than on legal authority,<span style='position:relative;top:-3.0pt'>8</span> but
that does not mean it has no pattern of authority.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
.5in'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>Misconstruing simplicity and
overemphasizing trust use their common conclusion in opposite ways. The former
moves church organization into the realm of spontaneous generation and social
evolution, automatically changing authoritative precedent to mere example so
that a search for the divine pattern of the church and its unity ceases.<span
style='position:relative;top:-3.0pt'>9</span> Modern Christianity is thus made
responsible for developing its own expression of unity according to its
perception of what is appropriate to the nature of the body of Christ. Overemphasizing
trust, on the other hand, reacts unduly against organization and seeks to
express unity only in the exercise of interpersonal love. A New Testament
picture of the church combines a strong reliance on inner motivation with a
recognition of simple authority.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><u><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>Local autonomy and responsibility</span></u><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>. Non-denominationalism involves a universal
organizational pattern in which each congregation is autonomous <u>under</u>
Christ. Congregations and agencies on earth are under the apostles in heaven by
virtue of their submission to the New Testament, which preserves all the
apostolic truth the church needs to know. The local church stands legally
independent of all <u>outside controls</u> even while it is responsibly
sensitive to <u>outside concerns</u>.<u> </u>From <u>within</u>, the churches
regulate their own personnel, programs, and properties. In principle any group
of congregations that fully practice local autonomy is organizationally non-
denominational.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
.5in'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>Local autonomy is here viewed in light of
the total cause of Christ.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
.5in'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
.5in'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>The autonomy is not absolute. The
authority of Christ as solitary Head of the church must be recognized; and the
Scriptures, rightly divided, accepted as the all-sufficient rule of faith and
practice.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
.5in'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>The principles of autonomy must be applied
to the relationship that exists between the local church and other churches.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
-.25in'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
.5in'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>The principles of autonomy must be applied
to the relationship that exists between one or a number of local churches to an
extra-congregational ministry or organization.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
-.25in'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
-.25in'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>      Decisions in that realm where
Christ has left the congregations free are to be made in the light of the
relevant law of love and expediency. We are not to think of freedom here as
being absolute.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
31.5pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'> Local autonomy does not relieve the
brethren of like precious faith in various sections of the world of the
obligation of mutual concern in the Lord.<span style='position:relative;
top:-3.0pt'>10</span></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;position:relative;top:-3.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
.5in'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>The congregations, then, are both legally
free and morally responsible.<span style='position:relative;top:-3.0pt'>11</span></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;position:relative;top:-3.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
.5in'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>Extra-congregational service agencies are
not part of the church’s visible organization except in the non-legal sense
that they exist as non-prescriptive entities under biblical jurisdiction. Individual
Christians incorporate under the <u>state</u> to form non-profit publishing
houses, educational institutions, benevolent services, and missions agencies
voluntarily supported by individuals and congregations.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
.5in'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>World-churchism is correct in its
contention that the church should be one even organizationally, the position
taken by ecumenical and Catholic writers. On the other hand, full weight must
be given to Brunner’s emphasis on the dynamic aspect of the <u>Ecclesia</u>,
the position evangelicals tend to take. Non-denominationalism includes both an
organizational and dynamic unity. This combination is made possible by the
simplicity of its organizational pattern.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify;text-indent:.5in'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify;text-indent:.5in'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;
margin-bottom:0in;margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:center;
text-indent:-.5in'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>Qualifying Distinctions</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><u><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>Attitude and Status</span></u></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
.5in'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
.5in'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>Denominational attitude and status have no
necessary correlation. Parochial exclusivism ingrains itself in groups natural
as well as formal and tends toward fragmentation in either. Freedom is not the
cause; structure is not the cure. The heart of the problem lies in the hearts
of the people. The “mark of the Christian” is an outward behavior springing
from an inner disposition regardless of the environment in which it grows.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
.5in'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
.5in'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><u><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>Division and Separateness</span></u></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
.5in'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
.5in'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>Separateness differs from division as
working privately differs from working competitively. But it does not seem
correct to consider the denominations in America, for instance,<span
style='position:relative;top:-3.0pt'>12</span> as merely separate groups while
they have among them mutually exclusive formative principles. The total state
of affairs in denominational Christendom does not measure up to the New
Testament standard for oneness either in kind or in degree.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
.5in'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
.5in'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><u><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>Unity and Union</span></u></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
.5in'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
.5in'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>Unity is not synonymous with union. “Union”
emphasizes organizational <u>form</u> without sufficiently stressing that
general oneness of <u>quality-in-form</u> which grows out of personal and
doctrinal matters. Unity <u>must</u> begin from within and <u>will</u> appear
outwardly.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
.5in'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
.5in'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><u><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>Unity and Uniformity</span></u></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
.5in'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
.5in'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>Unity views synthetically the harmonious
inter-relationship that characterizes a whole comprised of even diverse,
interdependent parts. Uniformity views analytically the formal, parallel
likeness of independent parts. There are parallel features in the autonomous
congregations of the New Testament, but the unity of the universal church is
more than the sum of certain formal likenesses discoverable in these largest of
its constituent parts. Non-denominationalism describes the whole of the church,
focusing not on the uniformity of parts, but on the form <u>and</u> character
of its total unity in history.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
.5in'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
.5in'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><u><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>Causing and Curing</span></u></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
.5in'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
.5in'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>People are not guilty for what they
inherit, but they are responsible for what they perpetuate. Christians must be <u>concerned</u>
about division, and must make every effort to <u>cure</u> what predecessors
have <u>caused</u>. Although guiding principles inhere in the apostles’
handling of incipient division, most New Testament injunctions address causal
factors and their inappropriateness among God’s people. This examination of the
basis and nature of New Testament unity seeks to increase sensitivity to the
problem of division, but does not suggest a program for unifying today’s
divided Christendom.<span style='position:relative;top:-3.0pt'>13</span></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;position:relative;top:-3.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;position:relative;top:-3.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:center'><u><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>Summary</span></u></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
.5in'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
.5in'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>The <u>essential</u> basis of Christian
unity lies in the gift of fellowship with God by virtue of salvation from sin
and separation. From a <u>functional</u> standpoint unity progresses from the
point of the common salvation to the full extent of the Christian distinctives.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
.5in'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>In the question of <u>organizational</u>
church unity, the proper antitheses are not (1) organizational unity vs.
functional unity (Catholicism vs. Emil Brunner’s concept), or (2) visible unity
vs. invisible unity (Catholicism <i>vs.</i> denominationalism), but (3)
hierarchical unity <i>vs.</i> non-hierarchical unity. It is a question of the <u>form</u>
of organizational unity <i>per se</i>.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
.5in'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>Non-denominationalism refers to a kind of
Christian oneness: an essential, functional, historical, universal, visible,
organizational oneness without such denominational characteristics as
exclusivistic attitude, private doctrinal emphases, restrictive Christian
fellowship, distinguishing names, or hierarchical structure.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
.5in'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>The church, then, is saved people serving
together in divine pattern.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
.5in'><span style='font-size:12.0pt;position:relative;top:-3.0pt'>5</span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>The present blueprint of the World Council, however,
makes it a confederation of churches, which retain individual sovereignty,
rather than a federation, where individual sovereignty is not retained. W. A.
Visser’t Hooft affirms the confederate goal of the Council in “The Significance
of the World Council of Churches,” pp. 177-95. Others view the present
confederate character of the “Council” as a provisional instrument for a full
organic unification. See Walter Marshall Horton, <i>Toward a Reborn Church</i>,
pp. 91-92; or Lesslie Newbigin, <i>The Household of God</i>, p. 14.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
.5in'><span style='font-size:12.0pt;position:relative;top:-3.0pt'>6</span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>Even free church fellowships with congregational
autonomy may be denominational for all <u>practical</u> purposes. So contends
Paul M. Harrison, <i>Authority and Power in the Free Church Tradition: A Social
Case Study of the American Baptist Convention</i>, pp. 60, 79, 86-87, 92, 117,
183. The civil courts of North Carolina have even ruled in the case of Southern
Baptist churches that congregations with the Southern Baptist Convention, at
least under certain conditions, forfeit property rights on withdrawal from the
Convention. See attorney Luther Doniphan Burrus’ article, “Judicial Recognition
of the Unique Congregational Polity of New Testament Churches of the
Restoration Movement,” <i>SR</i>, Vol. II, No. 2 (1956), pp. 7-8. Similarly, on
December 3, 1953, the Court of Appeals of New York reversed Kings County Court,
giving the General Council of Congregational Churches properties of all its
affiliated congregations. As a result, the General Council merged with the
Evangelical and Reformed Church and was eventually carried into the United
Church of Christ in July, 1961. For a useful sketch of the major denominational
patterns, see J. Paul Williams, <i>What Americans Believe and How They Worship</i>.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
.5in'><span style='font-size:12.0pt;position:relative;top:-3.0pt'>12</span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>A general idea of the current extent of pluralism in
America may be gained from perusing the <i>Yearbook of American Churches: 1972</i>,
ed. by Constant H. Jacquet, Jr. The <i>Yearbook</i> reports data on 236
religious bodies. America’s contribution to world-wide denominationalism is
evident in <i>North American Protestant Ministries Overseas</i>, published by
Missions Advanced Research Communication Center. See also <i>World Christian
Handbook: 1968</i>, ed. by H. Wakelin Coxill and Kenneth Grubb; or <i>Handbook
of Denominations in the United States</i>, by Frank S. Mead.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
.5in'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>That the denominations of Christendom are
not only separate, but divided, seems obvious from practical matters such as
their mutually exclusive polities, closed communion; disagreement on the
action, design, candidate, and administrator of baptism; the inability of
ministers to be approved for service as readily in one denomination as in the
next. Were the denominations merely separate, these problems would not exist.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
.5in'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>Baptism, communion, and polity are also
singled out by Matthew Spinka, <i>The Quest for Church Unity</i>, pp. 55-57. As
a result of mutually exclusive views on these matters, Spinka is rather
cautious about the full success of Christian unity (pp. 83-85).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
.5in'><span style='font-size:12.0pt;position:relative;top:-3.0pt'>13</span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>Beginning with local autonomy does imply a consequent
pattern of <u>organizational</u> unification–a unification through dissolution
of super-congregational authority and a strengthening of extra-congregational
communication and cooperation, congregations being the common element
throughout Christendom. <u>Practical</u> and <u>doctrinal</u> unification are
realized together in a pattern of reciprocal reinforcement: progressive growth
of fellowship and functioning is proportionate to an increasing doctrinal
agreement centered around salvation by faith only through Christ alone. Strictly
speaking, then, non-denominationalism involves an unusual form of the
unification Earle E. Cairns styles “organic reunion” as distinguished from, and
more than, “confederation” and “co-operation”; see <i>Christianity Through the
Centuries: A History of the Christian Church</i>, pp. 485-92, or <i>Christianity
in the United States</i>, pp. 168-74.</span></p>
