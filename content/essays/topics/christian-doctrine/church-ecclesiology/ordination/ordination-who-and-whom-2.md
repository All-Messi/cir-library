<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:center'><b><span
style='font-size:14.0pt'>ORDINATION: WHO AND WHOM</span></b></p>

<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:center'><b><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></b></p>

<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:center'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>Virgil Warren, PhD</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            Ordination to ministry involves three considerations:
(1) the understanding of New Testament offices and ministry, (2) the New
Testament meaning of ordination, and (3) the meaning attached to ordination by
society today and by the state.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>I. Who should be ordained?</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            From the standpoint of the <u>church</u>,
the New Testament does not evidence an understanding of ordination that
corresponds with the traditional clergy concept. Ordination does not validate a
person’s ministry. It does not bestow the right to preach, “administer
sacraments,” pronounce absolution. The only ordination observed in the New
Testament is an appointment to specific tasks. If done, ordination today simply
commends an individual to the general Christian community. It indicates that in
the ordaining congregation believes the person has the character, abilities,
and skills appropriate to the calling. That has been sufficient to qualify him
also for the rights vouchsafed to religious leaders by the state. So ordination
has been a matter of expediency rather than necessity.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            From the standpoint of the <u>state</u>,
the only purpose for ordination is to authorize ministers to perform weddings
in the name of the state. (Some states do not require ordination for this role;
being regarded as a minister can be sufficient. There is a <u>benefit</u> to
ordination, namely, exemption from federal income tax on housing allowance; but
that is not a <u>reason</u> for ordination.) It is appropriate and helpful if a
minister can meet this traditional expectancy because it provides a natural
opportunity to identify marriage with Christian values and with the Christian
community. People who labor in the Lord even full-time but do not function in
this role have no reason to be ordained in the traditional sense of the term.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            As to </span><span style='font-size:12.0pt;
color:windowtext'>commendation to the general church</span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>, there is some overlap between New Testament official
ministry and the government’s recognition of ordination. The office of
evangelist is the only New Testament office that correlates with what the
government recognizes as an object of ordination. Lord, apostle, and prophet
are specially gifted general offices that have no successors (except possibly
for prophets if special revelation and/or guidance continues). Elder and deacon
are local offices not normally practiced as vocations. Other responsibilities
that are general in scope are regarded as some aspect of the evangelist office.
In other words, when we combine our understanding of ministry with the
government’s understanding of it, ordination in the traditional sense naturally
applies only to <i><u>men</u> in a <u>general</u> office who labor in the
ministry of the <u>word</u>.</i> It applies then to evangelists, preachers, and
missionaries—different manifestations of the New Testament evangelist.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            Moving official ordination beyond <u>men</u>
in <u>general</u> offices for the ministry of the <u>word</u> passes any
natural limit on who could be ordained. It would allow for ordaining (1) all
those vocationally involved in Christian work—with or without formal
preparation. We could ordain <u>all members of churches’ multiple staffs</u>
(Christian education directors, music ministers, children’s ministers,
ministers of worship, business directors, secretaries, custodians) as well as <u>all
participants in parachurch agencies</u> (Christian college faculty regardless
of subject area, house parents in Christian orphanages, publishing personnel)
and everyone whose livelihood comes from <u>church-related services</u>
(traveling musicians, artists, church builders). That pushes the practice
beyond its intended scope. Obviously, we cannot add (2) all those who “minister.”
In theory that would make every Christian eligible for ordination. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            The issue in traditional ordination is not
whether someone is making a <u>vital contribution</u> to the body. Every member
has a vital role (Romans <b>12</b>:4-8; 1 Corinthians <b>12</b>:12-27;
Ephesians <b>4</b>:15-16): those also serve who attend the baggage (1 Samuel <b>30</b>:24).
The issue is <u>what kind of vital contribution</u> a person is making. If we “ordain”
in the traditional sense, then we need to ordain “ministry” in the traditional
sense—even though for our own purposes we do not view ministers in exactly that
sense. If we broaden the term from technical to general usage, it should not
include any purpose or benefit associated with the state—wedding performance
and housing-allowance deduction. If we want to recognize those set aside to
every church role—a good practice already done informally in other connections,
then we should probably call it something else.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            A final question about ordination is
whether women may be ordained. The answer, of course, depends on a person’s
understanding of the relevant New Testament texts cross-culturally adjusted. Until
recently, the standard position among evangelicals has been that God has
assigned men the final responsibility of official ministry of the word in mixed
settings. “Preachers” typically do more than speak publicly even though
preaching includes testimony, encouragement, and “prophesying”—public roles
performed by men and women alike in the New Testament. Preachers also teach the
word in sermons and elsewhere and administer the church program either under
the local leaders or with them. Their standard responsibilities include work
that God has given men. In keeping with this judgment about New Testament
leadership and these expectancies of “the minister,” ordination of women would
not be appropriate.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>II. Who ordains?</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            In the free-church tradition the local
church is the only entity with any ecclesiastical standing. There are no
general offices above the congregation that can speak to or for it. Consequently,
ordination is done by local churches rather than by a synod, session,
association, or presbytery. Congregational government allows for parachurch
agencies like Bible colleges, seminaries, evangelistic associations, publishing
companies, benevolence organizations, and mission agencies. But these
organizations are not part of the church organizationally speaking; they are
entities (usually) formed by groups of individuals to carry on a particular kind
of work. They are typically constituted under boards of trustees recognized by
the state to operate with the benefit of tax-exempt status.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            In conclusion, when a person seeks
ordination, several questions are implied: </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            (1)  What do you mean by “ordain”?</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            (2)  Why do you want to be ordained?</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            (3)  What New Testament office do you want
to be ordained to?</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
-.75in'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              (4)  Does
ordination in the traditional sense apply to the ministry you intend to </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
-.75in'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                    perform?</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
-.25in'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  (5)  In the case of
women, does ordination in the traditional sense apply to the roles </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
-.25in'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        the New
Testament includes in the work you propose to do?</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
.5in'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>As far as the New Testament examples are
concerned, appointment was not a generalized status in contrast to “the laity”;
it was for a specific responsibility. Unfortunately, the complicating factor in
ordination today is the financial advantage that untaxed housing allowance and
deductible ministerial expenses bring into the picture.</span></p>
