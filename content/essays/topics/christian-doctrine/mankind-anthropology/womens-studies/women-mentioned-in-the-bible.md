<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:center'><b><span
style='font-size:14.0pt'>WOMEN MENTIONED IN THE BIBLE</span></b></p>

<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:center'><b><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></b></p>

<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:center'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>Virgil Warren, PhD</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
.5in'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>To maintain a balanced approach in the
current women’s studies debate, we should keep in mind what women did with
divine approval in both Testaments. The following is a suggestive list.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify;text-indent:-31.5pt'><u><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>Anna</span></u><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>: a “prophetess”
in Jerusalem at the time of Jesus’ presentation in the temple. She <i>“gave
thanks to God and spoke of him to all that were looking for redemption in
Israel”</i> (Luke <b>2</b>:36-38).                                   </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><u><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>Apphia</span></u><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>: Philemon
wife? (Philemon 2)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><u><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>Chloe</span></u><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>: a
Christian woman with a household named after her (1 Corinthians <b>1</b>:10-11)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><u><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>Deborah</span></u><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>: a “judge”
during the judgeship period in Israel (Judges <b>4-5</b>; Hebrews <b>11</b>:32-34)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><u><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>Dorcas</span></u><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>: a
woman in Joppa who did benevolent work among the Christians there (Acts <b>9</b>:36-43)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify;text-indent:-.5in'><u><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>Euodia</span></u><span style='font-size:12.0pt'> and <u>Syntyche</u>:
two women in the Philippian church that were at odds with one another (Philemon
<b>4</b>:2)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify;text-indent:-.5in'><u><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>Huldah</span></u><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>: a “prophetess”
during the time of King Josiah, who consulted her about the Book of the Law that
Hilkiah found (2 Kings <b>22</b>:14; 2 Chronicles <b>34</b>:22)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify;text-indent:-.5in'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>Isaiah’s <u>wife</u>: called a “prophetess” (Isaiah <b>8</b>:1-3).
The passage suggests that the wife of a prophet was automatically called a
prophetess. Obviously, this “prophetess” that Isaiah had relations with was not
a temple prostitute or some other female religious figure associated with the
temple cultus.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify;text-indent:-.5in'><u><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>Jezebel</span></u><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>: a
woman mentioned in Revelation <b>2</b>:18-29 who had a prominent position as a
false teacher in Thyatira. She called herself a “prophetess.” She was misusing
the role and either spiritually or perhaps sexually causing people in the
church to commit religious fornication on the analogy of Jezebel, the wife of
Ahab, king of the Northern Kingdom in the Old Testament (1 Kings <b>16</b>:31; <b>18</b>:4-19;
<b>19</b>:1-2; <b>21</b>:5-25; 2 Kings <b>9</b>).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><u><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>Julia</span></u><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>: wife
or sister of Philologus, members of the church in Rome (Romans <b>16</b>:15)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify;text-indent:-.5in'><u><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>Junia(s)</span></u><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>: possibly
the wife of Andronicus if the name is feminine; otherwise, “Junias” is a man’s
name, which would make him a brother or son of Andronicus (Romans <b>16</b>:7)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><u><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>Lois</span></u><span style='font-size:12.0pt'> and <u>Eunice</u>:
Timothy’s mother and grandmother (2 Timothy <b>1</b>:5; cp. <b>3</b>:14-15)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify;text-indent:-.5in'><u><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>Lydia</span></u><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>: a businesswoman
in Philippi, who housed Paul, Silas, and Luke. She had a household named after
her (Acts <b>16</b>:13-15).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify;text-indent:-.5in'><u><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>Mary of Bethany</span></u><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>:
evidently a sister of Lazarus who told people what Jesus had done in raising
her brother from the dead (John <b>11</b>:45)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify;text-indent:-.5in'><u><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>Mary Magdalene</span></u><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>:
bore the news of the empty tomb (John <b>20</b>:1-2) and announced Jesus’
resurrection (John <b>20</b>:18 = Mark <b>16</b>:9-11 = Luke <b>24</b>:1-10,
22-23).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><u><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>Mary of Rome</span></u><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>:
an otherwise unknown laborer in the Roman church (Romans <b>16</b>:6)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify;text-indent:-.5in'><u><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>Nympha(s)</span></u><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>: a
member of the church in Laodecia (or perhaps Colossae), who had a church in
her/his home (Colossians <b>4</b>:15).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><u><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>Philip’s four daughters who prophesied</span></u><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'> (Acts <b>21</b>:9)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify;text-indent:-.5in'><u><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>Phoebe</span></u><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>: a <i>diakonos</i>
(</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>διάκονος</span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>) of the church in Cenchreae and a <i>prostatis</i> (</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>προστάτις</span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>)
of many, including Paul (Romans <b>16</b>:1-2). She was perhaps the bearer of
the Roman letter. Phoebe is the only woman involved in interchurch ministry by
herself. She is called a <i>diakonos</i> of the church in Cenchrea while at the
same time presumably she is traveling to do some “helping” work (<i>prostatis</i>)
in Rome.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify;text-indent:-.5in'><u><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>Priscilla</span></u><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>: wife
of Aquila (Acts <b>18</b>:2, 18; 2 Timothy <b>4</b>:19). This husband and wife
had a church that met in their home when they were working with Paul in Ephesus
(1 Corinthians <b>16</b>:19) and later in Rome (Romans <b>16</b>:3-5). They
together took Apollos aside and <i>“taught him the way of the Lord more
perfectly”</i> (Acts <b>18</b>:24-26).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>Samaritan woman (see “Woman at the Well”)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify;text-indent:-.5in'><u><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>Sherah</span></u><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>: a
woman said to have fortified three villages (1 Chronicles<b> 7</b>:24)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><u><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>Tryphaena and Tryphosa</span></u><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>: two women who labored in the Roman church (Romans <b>16</b>:12)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify;text-indent:-.5in'><u><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>Virtuous Woman [Excellent Wife] of Proverbs <b>31</b></span></u><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>: said to consider a field and buy it (16) with her earning;
she plants a vineyard (16) and sells clothing she has made (24)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify;text-indent:-.5in'><u><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>Woman at the Well</span></u><span style='font-size:
12.0pt'>: Although he was breaking convention in doing so, Jesus talked with
this disreputable woman and used her contacts as a means of entry into the Samaritan
village of Sychar (John <b>4</b>:1-42).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:center'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>General References</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            The <u>widows</u> mentioned in 1 Timothy<b>
5</b>:3-16 evidently had some responsibilities in the church in return for
being supported by the Christian community.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            The <u>women</u> referred to in 1 Timothy <b>3</b>:11
were perhaps deacons’ wives who served with their husbands in the ministering
role. Some commentators suppose that they were not the “wives” of the deacons
but “women” who served as deacons. There is perhaps something of a parallel to
the first option in 1 Corinthians <b>16</b>:15-16, where the <i>“household of
Stephanas”</i> set themselves to minister to the saints; Paul tells the
Corinthians to subject themselves to people like them. Together with the
example of Isaiah’s wife being called a “prophetess,” these phenomena imply
that service to God was thought of as a family affair.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            There was a circle of <u>women</u> that
accompanied Jesus and the apostles during Christ’s ministry. They ministered to
Jesus and the Twelve out of their means. Some of them were relatives of Jesus
and his disciples.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            There are several women who <u>prophesied</u>
or were called “prophetesses” in the Old and New Testaments: Huldah, Isaiah’s
wife, Anna, Jezebel (a false prophetess), Philip’s four daughters, plus the
general prediction in Joel <b>2</b>:28 (= Acts <b>2</b>:18) about daughters
prophesying, and the comments Paul makes in 1 Corinthians<b> 11</b>:2-16; <b>14</b>:26-36.
That praying and prophesying was a public activity in the gathered Christian
community because Paul deals with the veil-wearing issue in connection with the
matter.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            Women were <u>teachers</u> of children and
younger women (Titus<b> 2</b>:3-5).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            A woman could <i>“rule the household” </i>(1
Timothy <b>5</b>:14).</span></p>
