<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>editorial question                                                                Ephesians
<b>5</b>:21</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>Does <i>“defer to one another in the fear of Christ”</i>
belong with the preceding or succeeding context?”</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            The United Bible Society’s text of the Greek
New Testament puts the injunction with the preceding material in the third
edition, but with the succeeding context in the third edition (corrected). Grammatically
the participle <i>“submitting”</i> parallels participles in the preceding two
verses—participles that grammatically modify the subject of the finite verb in <b>5</b>:18.
A free-standing participle can be a command; but clear cases of that usage are
infrequent in the New Testament (Mark <b>5</b>:23; Romans <b>12</b>:9<span
style='position:relative;top:-3.0pt'>2</span>, 10?, 11?, 12<span
style='position:relative;top:-3.0pt'>3</span>?, 13<span style='position:relative;
top:-3.0pt'>2</span>? 16<span style='position:relative;top:-3.0pt'>3</span>?,
17<span style='position:relative;top:-3.0pt'>2</span>?, 18, 19; <b>13</b>:11; 1
Corinthians <b>8</b>:24; Ephesians <b>5</b>:21?; Titus <b>2</b>:7; 1 Peter <b>2</b>:18;<b>
3</b>:1). Moreover, nothing grammatical calls for invoking that usage in
Ephesians<b> 5</b>:21 because of the parallel between “deferring” and the preceding
participles of<b> 5</b>:19-20: <i>“. . . speaking to one another . . . singing
and making melody . . . giving thanks . . . deferring to one another . . .”</i>
Grammatically, <i>“deferring”</i> ties with the preceding flow of thought by
being parallel to the three preceding participles. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            Conceptually, <b>5</b>:18-21 forms a
context for the household instruction in <b>5</b>:22-<b>6</b>:9; but it forms a
conceptual setting for the whole unit of household instruction, not just for
the part that addresses marriage (<b>21</b>-33). Those particularly interested
in getting away from a hierarchical element in marriage want to connect the
sentence with what follows, but only the marriage part of what follows. That connecting
seems to them to establish an egalitarian arrangement for the home since they also
understand <i>“one another” </i>as full individual reciprocation (see below). </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            However, making that connection
grammatically and conceptually does not de-structure the home. For one thing,
(a) structure and interpersonalism are not incompatible. Structure supplements
specialized interpersonal situations like marriage, church, parenting; it does
not preempt it. For another thing, (b) in this passage Paul’s obvious point is
the very opposite of de-structuring—that is, anarchy in the technical sense: <i>“nobody
in charge.”</i> That should be clear from his admonition to children to obey
their parents, and the parenting section is equally part of this unit on
household operation. This same issue Paul and other writers treat elsewhere,
encouraging people in congregations to <i>“obey”</i> the ones that have the <i>“rule”</i>
over them in the church (Hebrews<b> 13</b>:17, <i>etc</i>.).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>From the standpoint of vocabulary, <i>“deference” </i>could
have two formats, one format in which (a) two persons do the same things to the
other and another format in which (b) people in a group defer in one direction to
certain others in the group. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            Interpreters have been trying to handle
Ephesians <b>5</b>:21 in the first format ever since the women’s rights issue
became part of American cultural scene. In so doing, their idea has been to
remove any essential distinctiveness of the husband-to-wife <i>vs</i>. the
wife-to-husband relationship. We sympathize with the desire not to create more differences
between the general culture (“the world”) and Christianity (“the church”) than
is necessary, to avoid creating unnecessary hurdles for accepting the Christian
faith and the truthfulness of the Bible. We can also sympathize with the
concern to find a way of counteracting the abuse of male domination, chauvinism,
and a condescending attitude toward wives and women. In fact, Paul seems to
have that very concern in this section of Ephesians, because he gives commands
to husbands and wives respectively that address the faults that each most often
have toward the other. He commands husbands to <u>love</u> their wives, because
husbands frequently sin by “running over” their wives. He commands wives to <u>respect</u>
their husbands (but not <i>vice versa</i> here or elsewhere) because wives
frequently sin by competing with their husbands.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            The first understanding of the reciprocal
pronoun ends up meaning a purely interpersonal manner of operation between (a)
husbands and wives, (b) parents and children, and (c) masters and slaves. There
is a sense in which parents can “submit” to their children—by not discouraging
them (Colossians<b> 3</b>:21). Masters could “submit” to slaves in not being
hard on them (Ephesians <b>6</b>:9). Husbands can “submit” to their wives in
giving themselves for their spouses (Ephesians <b>5</b>:25; cp. 1 Corinthians<b>
7</b>:1ff.). Christ “submitted” to the church in giving himself for it
(Ephesians <b>5</b>:25). In all four cases, we could say that attitudinally <u>both</u>
sides of the relationship submit to each another as in, <i>“Let each person
consider the other better than himself.”</i> While this last idea is true and,
in fact, important to stress, it should be derived from Philippians <b>2</b>:3-4
or perhaps Galatians <b>6</b>:1-5 in connection with their subjects, not from
Ephesians <b>5</b>:21. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><b><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            </span></b><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>The
first format is appropriate in any circumstance as regards attitude, but in
this context a person would not expect Paul to be referring only to attitude. In
fact, he seems to do the opposite. He wants to prevent the disorder that comes
when no one receives responsibility and others do not cooperate with assigned
responsibility. (a) If <b>5</b>:21 introduces the succeeding context, it should
be understood in a way that applies to the whole section on household teaching.
If so, the second arrangement would have parents submitting to their children <b>(6</b>:1-4),
masters submitting to their slaves (<b>6</b>:5-9), and Christ submitting to his
church (<b>5</b>:25-32) in the same sense that children, slaves, and Christians
are to submit. While it is possible in an interpersonal sense for masters,
husbands, and Christ to submit, they do not do so in the same sense that
slaves, wives, and children submit.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            Furthermore, (b) the mutual submission
mentality does not negate one-directional deference in order. One deals with
attitude while the other deals with responsibility. Nothing about authority
means proper attitude is forfeit. Authority derives from responsibility, which
in turn derives from purpose. In the final analysis, the point of authority
here is the very opposite of what people fear in it. The text assigns responsibility
for the home, putting the onus on the husband rather than letting him do and
act how he pleases without recourse from the other. As Paul tells masters to
remember that they have a Master in heaven (Ephesians <b>6</b>:9; Colossians <b>4</b>:1),
so also husbands are under the headship of Christ, whom they are to respect (Ephesians<b>
5</b>:21; 1 Corinthians <b>11</b>:3). </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in'><span style='font-size:8.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            (c) There are other texts where </span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ἀλλήλων</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:
12.0pt'> </span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>(<i>allēlōn</i>) does not mean
the kind of reciprocity involved in format #1 above. Revelation <b>6</b>:4 is perhaps
the clearest: <i>“. . . that they should kill one another.”</i> Devouring one
another appears to be one-directional within a group (Galatians<b> 6</b>:15). Luke
<b>12</b>:1 speaks of the multitudes treading on one another. They probably did
not take turns stepping on one another’s feet in a strictly reciprocal way. Children
in the marketplace call to one another, <i>“We piped to you and you did not
dance; we wailed and you did not weep”</i> (Luke <b>7</b>:32). Matthew<b> 24</b>:10
speaks of <i>“delivering one another up,”</i> which would seem to be
one-directional between individuals within people in a group. On several
occasions, like in Luke<b> 8</b>:25, people in a crowd ask one another, <i>“Who
then is this, that commands even the wind and water and they obey him?” </i>(=
Mark <b>4</b>:41; <b>8</b>:16; <b>15</b>:31; Luke <b>2</b>:15; <b>4</b>:36; <b>6</b>:11;
<b>8</b>:15; <b>20</b>:14; <b>24</b>:32; John <b>4</b>:33; <b>5</b>:44;<b> 6</b>:52;
<b>11</b>:56; <b>16</b>:17; <b>19</b>:24; Acts<b> 4</b>:15-16; <b>26</b>:31; <b>28</b>:14).
Acts <b>7</b>:26 may be another example, depending on the particulars in the
event: <i>“Men, you are brothers. Why do you do wrong to one another?” </i>(Two
other reciprocal expressions in Greek are </span><span lang=EL
style='font-size:12.0pt'>ἑαυτοῦ</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>[<i>heautou</i>] and </span><span lang=EL
style='font-size:12.0pt'>εἷς</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>τὸν</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>
</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ἕνα</span><span style='font-size:
12.0pt'> [<i>h</i><i>eis ton hena</i>].)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            In cases of one-directional submitting
within a group, an alternate translation may help express the concept better: <i>“Submit
yourselves ‘one to another.’”</i> </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            James <b>5</b>:16 may be another example
of “one-directional” use of </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ἀλλήλων</span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'> [<i>allēlōn</i>], because the text has been talking
about elders who have been called to heal someone. The healed man’s confession
rather than the elders’ confession also may be the intended point, although in
parallel to confession is prayer for one another. Of course, the prayer for one
another may be the prayer of the elders for the sick man as the sick man’s
confession is to the elders.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            Whether to take the first or second format
of reciprocation depends on the kind of situation is at hand. In the case of
domestic relations, order is not set aside. <i>“Be servants to one another,”</i>
(Galatians <b>5</b>:13) is an interesting case that could go either way.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>middle voice                                                                       Ephesians<b>
5</b>:21</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>“Submit” [</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ὑποτασσόμενοι</span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>, <i>hypotassomenoi</i>] is middle voice; therefore,
this is not male domination. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><b><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            </span></b></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><b><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            </span></b><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>Such
a comment misses the point of the middle voice. Obviously male “domination” is
not acceptable so as to be given an alternative here. By the nature of the
case, deferring is something that is done by the person of lower “rank.” The
significant thing is that Paul addresses the deferring one, not that, having
decided to address the deferring one, he uses the middle voice. The verb is
deponent in the New Testament; it is not used in the active voice so as to
contrast with active voice. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><i><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>“So husbands ought to love their wives</span></i><span

lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>τὰ</span><span

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            (1) <i>“their wives as (they love) their
own bodies.”</i></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            (2) <i>“their wives as-much-as (they love)
their own bodies.”</i></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            (3) <i>“their wives in-the-same-way-as
