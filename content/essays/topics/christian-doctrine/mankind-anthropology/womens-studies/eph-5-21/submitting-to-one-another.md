<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;

<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;
margin-bottom:0in;margin-left:22.5pt;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:center'><b><span
style='font-size:14.0pt'>Ephesians 5:21-6:9</span></b></p>

<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;
margin-bottom:0in;margin-left:22.5pt;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:center'><b><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></b></p>

<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;
margin-bottom:0in;margin-left:22.5pt;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:center'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>Virgil Warren, PhD</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:22.5pt;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:22.5pt;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:22.5pt;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>         This portion of Ephesians deals with “household
teaching” on the relationship of husbands and wives, parents and children,
masters and slaves. Other comparable texts are Colossians <b>3</b>:18-4:1 and 1
Peter <b>2</b>:(13-)18-<b>3</b>:7.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:22.5pt;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:22.5pt;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>         1. In regard to each relationship, Paul
appeals to interpersonal principles to set the tone for it. In other places, he
does the same thing to solve problems that impact practical relationships in
the body (languages, 1 Corinthians <b>12-14</b>; leadership, 2 Corinthians <b>10-13</b>;
<i>etc</i>.). He can do that because personal relationship is always the
context in which further considerations exist and must exist in a way that does
not interfere with that context. Paul implies that interpersonalism should
carry the process as much as possible. Only when <u>influence</u> for practical
reasons is inadequate is there a need to invoke the principle of <u>authority</u>
(much less <u>force</u>).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:22.5pt;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>         2. <u>Unity,</u> not <u>equality</u>, is the
ultimate concept in any group. That concept allows for the practical factors in
the situation, particularly (a) the diversity between people and (b) the need
for organization to gain efficiency and harness power in a division of labor. The
ideal is not a <u>sameness</u> that leads to absolute interchangeability or <u>equal
status</u>. Such ideals are unreal to the nature of people operating in group
process.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:22.5pt;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>         3. Paul brings each human relationship into
the context of vertical relationship with Christ:</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:22.5pt;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:22.5pt;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>             wives:      be in subjection to your own
husbands <u>as to the Lord</u> (<b>5</b>:22, 24, cp. 23);</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:22.5pt;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              husbands: love your
wives <u>as Christ</u> loved the church and gave himself </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:22.5pt;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              for it (<b>5:</b>25, 29)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:22.5pt;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:22.5pt;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>             children:  obey your parents <u>in the
Lord</u> (<b>6</b>:1)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:22.5pt;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>             parents:    nurture them in the
discipline and admonition <u>of the Lord</u> (<b>6</b>:4)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:22.5pt;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:112.5pt;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify;text-indent:-1.25in'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>             servants:  be obedient . . . <u>as to
Christ</u> (<b>6</b>:5) . . . as servants <u>of Christ</u> . . . doing the will
<u>of God</u> (<b>6</b>:6) . . . as <u>to the Lord</u> (<b>6</b>:7)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:112.5pt;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify;text-indent:-1.25in'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>             masters:   do the same to them . . . knowing
that he who is both their Master and yours in is heaven (<b>6</b>:9)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:22.5pt;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:22.5pt;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>         4. Paul addresses the “submitters” first in
each case: wives, children, slaves. The same pattern appears in Colossians <b>3</b>:18-<b>4</b>:1
and 1 Peter <b>2</b>:13-<b>3</b>:7. There may be no special reason for this
pattern—except perhaps custom, but the reason may be that subordination applies
to more people. It may also be the harder thing for people to do. Christianity’s
emphasis on the worth of the individual only compounds the problem if the worth
of the individual is allowed to feed individualism and competition.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:22.5pt;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>         5. Paul gives directives to <u>both</u> sides
of the relationship, not just to those who should submit. Headship and
submission have to occur simultaneously. Problems in that situation cannot be
solved from just one side of it. The positions are correlative; neither side is
absolute.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:22.5pt;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>         6. In the directives Paul gives to each side,
he addresses the weakness that most typically characterizes that side:</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:22.5pt;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:22.5pt;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>             wives:      defer to your husbands (<b>5</b>:22,
24); respect your husband (<b>5</b>:33)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:22.5pt;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>             husbands: love your wives (<b>5</b>:25-29,
33</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:22.5pt;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:22.5pt;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:22.5pt;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>             parents:    do not frustrate your
children; nurture, discipline, and admonish them </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:22.5pt;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              (<b>6</b>:4)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:22.5pt;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>             children:  obey your parents (<b>6</b>:1);
honor them (<b>6</b>:2)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:22.5pt;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:22.5pt;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>             slaves:      obey your masters (<b>6</b>:5)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:22.5pt;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>             masters:   forbear threatening (<b>6</b>:9)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:22.5pt;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:22.5pt;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>         7. The relationships Paul treats in this
household material are not completely alike, or homogeneous. Wife-husband is
not the same as child-parent, and neither is like slave-master. Other examples
of lists that are not homogeneous include Acts <b>15</b>:20, 29; <i>etc.</i> The
relationships in <b>5</b>:21-<b>6</b>:9, however, do have important elements in
common that allow Paul to treat them as a unit:       </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:22.5pt;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:22.5pt;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>             a. they all involve interpersonal
relationships;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:22.5pt;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>             b. they all involve interpersonal
relationships in the household setting</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:76.5pt;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify;text-indent:-.75in'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>             c.  they all involve mixing the dynamics
of headship-deference with those of interpersonalism in the home.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:22.5pt;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>             </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:22.5pt;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;
margin-bottom:0in;margin-left:22.5pt;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:center'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>Comments on the Concept of “Mutual Submission” for <b>5</b>:21</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:22.5pt;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:22.5pt;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>         Despite the popularity of the idea, for
reasons given below <i>“submit to one another”</i> (</span><span lang=EL
style='font-size:12.0pt'>ὑποτάσσεσθε</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>
</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ἀλλήλοις</span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>, <i>hupotassesthe allēlois</i>) does not mean <u>mutual</u>
submission. That is in fact almost the opposite of Paul’s meaning. The problem
he deals with is not the mistaken practice of having domestic order, but the
failure to observe it properly because interpersonal qualities are not
impacting both sides of the relationships.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:22.5pt;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:22.5pt;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>         a.  The meaning of <i>“submit one to one another”</i>
in <b>5</b>:21 must fit <u>the whole set of household relationships addressed
in <b>5</b>:22-<b>6</b>:9</u>. If <b>5</b>:21 is the heading of anything, it is
the heading of everything that deals with submitting, not just with the first third
of it. The sense in which we understand submission in the case of
husbands-wives needs to fit with the cases of parents-children and
slaves-masters as well. Mutual submission would mean that children no more obey
parents than parents obey children and that slaves no more need to submit to
their masters than masters submit to their slaves. (Note also 1 Peter <b>2</b>:13-17
regarding submission to earthly rulers.) An argument that proves too much does
not prove what it seems to prove; consequently,<b> 5</b>:21 should not be used
to argue for mutual submission.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:22.5pt;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>         b. The sense in which we understand<b> 5</b>:21
must fit with <u>the specific statements inside the husband-wife material
itself</u>. Those specific statements include comparisons between husband-wife
and Christ-church relationships. The Christ-church relationship is not mutual
submission; so neither is the husband-wife relationship. The sense in which the
husband is “head of” the wife and the wife “defers” to her husband is the sense
in which Christ is head of the church and the church submits to him. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:22.5pt;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>             In <b>5</b>:22-<b>6</b>:9 Paul does not
say here or anywhere else that husbands, parents, or masters should submit, but
he does say that wives, children, and slaves should. So, he does not mean
reciprocal submission.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:22.5pt;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>         d. The meaning of <i>“submit to one another”</i>
must fit with <u>what the New Testament teaches elsewhere</u>. Other comparable
texts do not prefix an element like <i>“submit to one another.”</i> They begin
directly with statements about wives deferring to husbands: Colossians <b>3</b>:18;
1 Peter <b>3</b>:1; Titus <b>2</b>:5; cp. 1 Corinthians<b> 11</b>:3-10; <b>14</b>:14-35.
None of these examples have any contextual aspect to change their obvious
meaning in the direction of what is often proposed for Ephesians <b>5</b>:21. So,
we should take <i>“submit to one another”</i> in Ephesians <b>5</b>:21 in a way
that fits with free-standing usages of “submit” rather than <i>vice versa</i>. Scripture
never tells a husband to submit to his wife as he does in a straightforward way
tells wives to “defer” to their husbands as he does in Titus <b>2</b>:5; so
interpreters should not try to take advantage of the wording in Ephesians <b>5</b>:21
to make it say something never said anywhere else.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:22.5pt;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>             Peter’s material on submission begins
with an even wider application than household submission. It starts with
submission to temporal authority (1 Peter<b> 2</b>:13-17), then proceeds to
slaves (<b>2</b>:18-25) and then wives (<b>3</b>:1-6). This fact adds <u>temporal
authority</u> to <u>parent</u> and <u>masters</u> as three cases that are not
reciprocal out of the four cases of submission.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:22.5pt;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>         e.  We should choose the meaning for <i>“one
another</i> [</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ἀλλήλων</span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>, <i>allēlōn</i>]<i>”</i> that fits with <u>the kind
of the subject addressed here</u>. <i>“One another” </i>has two meanings, not
just one. In group settings where the nature of the case allows, “one another”
means A to B, X to Y, <i>etc.</i> (one-directional between various sets of
individuals win the group); it does not have to mean A to B to A
(two-directional between the same two individuals). </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:22.5pt;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>             The <u>latter meaning</u> shows up in
statements like <i>“love one another.”</i> Person A loves person B,, and person
B loves him back. Loving (1 Thessalonians <b>4</b>:9), forgiving (Ephesians <b>4</b>:32),
greeting (2 Corinthians <b>13</b>:12) can be done back and forth between the
same two individuals. The <u>former meaning</u> shows up in Mark <b>4</b>:41;<b>
8</b>:16; <b>9:</b>34; Luke <b>2</b>:15; <b>4</b>:36; <b><u>7</u></b><u>:32</u>;
<b>8</b>:25; <b>24</b>:22; John <b>4</b>:33; <b>6</b>:52; <b>11</b>:56; <b>16</b>:17;
<b>19</b>:24; Acts <b>2</b>:7;<b> 4</b>:15; <b>26</b>:31; 2<b>8</b>:4; <b>11</b>:33;
<u>Revelation <b>6</b>:4</u> (<i>“kill one another”</i>), and perhaps other
cases (Matthew <b>24</b>:10 <i>“betray one another”</i>; Luke <b>12</b>:1; John
<b>5</b>:44; <b>13</b>:14; Acts <b>7</b>:26; <b>19</b>:38; 1 Corinthians <b>7</b>:5;
James <b>5</b>:16 <i>“confess your sins to one another”</i>). Ephesians <b>5</b>:21-<b>6</b>:9
is a group setting comprised of the total family, not just a statement about
two individuals—a man and a woman.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:22.5pt;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>         f.  By the nature of what submission is, <u>mutual
submission would be self-contradictory</u>, something like a male mare. “Submit”
normally means deferring to authority. By the nature of what authority is, “mutual”
submission is not possible. If we suppose a figurative application of the term,
the concept is still not very clear. Two people cannot submit to each other in
the same sense at the same time and it still be submitting. Person A could
submit to person B in one respect while person B would submit to person A in
some other respect. But that meaning of the phrase would not eliminate
hierarchical (<i>vs</i>. egalitarian) arrangement in marriage and the home,
which is the goal in wanting to take <i>“submit to one another”</i> as mutual
submission. Dividing up individual items so that husbands submit to wives on
some matters while wives submit to husbands on others is apparently eliminated
by the expression submitting to husbands <i>“in everything”</i> <b>(5</b>:24).
The apostles evidently means <i>“as the overall structure of things”</i> in a
marriage. Submitting back and forth in the same sense could occur in acts of
submission occurring at different times; in that case <i>“submit to one another”</i>
becomes a loose expression for interpersonalism comparable to the situation in
1 Corinthians <b>7</b>:4, but where we do find such a meaning associated with </span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ὑποτάσσομαι</span><span lang=EL
style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>(<i>hypotassomai</i>)?
Mutual submission as an English expression may not seem self-contradictory, but
that does not make it the intended meaning of the Greek term in Ephesians <b>5</b>:21.
An interpretation is not true because it cannot be disproved. <i>“The burden of
proof rests on the affirmative,” </i>we say. An interpretation can be
considered true when adequate evidence can be given <u>for</u> it; that is not
something accomplished by appealing to an—at most—ambiguous passage.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:22.5pt;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>         g. In all the history of biblical
interpretation, no one ever supposed such a meaning as “mutual submission” for
Ephesians <b>5</b>:21 until the secular women’s liberation movement started
influencing the church and consequently biblical interpretation.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:22.5pt;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:22.5pt;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>         Paul writes this section of Ephesians to
discourage “bucking the system” out of self-interest. Interpreters confuse his
point about order with the more basic interpersonal setting in which order does
take place and which should serve as the corrective for abuses in headship-deference.
Paul’s appeals to interpersonal items like love and mutual respect are not
calculated to <u>remove</u> headship-submission, but to <u>qualify</u> the
manner in which it takes place. A skewed concept of headship creates a skewed
concept of deference. Headship is not domination, but responsibility; so,
submission is not self-negation, but respect for the other’s responsibility.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:22.5pt;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>         At best, <i>“submit yourselves to one another”</i>
is an ambiguous expression. But proper interpretation calls for taking the
ambiguous in light of the clear. One clear way of interpreting this expression
equals the uniform household teaching everywhere else in the New Testament. That
is how we should take it here: people should be deferring to the ones they
ought to defer to. Christianity’s emphasis on interpersonalism does not give us
an excuse to rebel against authority for self-interest. Rather, it establishes
the manner in which we cooperate with those in positions of responsibility and
the manner in which responsible people use their authority with people they
have responsibility for. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:22.5pt;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;
margin-bottom:0in;margin-left:22.5pt;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:center'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>One Final Note</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:22.5pt;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:22.5pt;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>         In the work of biblical interpretation and
personal application, distinct issues should be kept distinct. The first issue
here is (1) what Paul means by the expression <i>“submit yourselves to one another.”</i>
That is a separate matter from (2) whether we even believe what he said, or (3)
whether Paul’s commands apply to cultures beyond his own. That last item is
where the question about present-day marriage and home relationships belongs. But
whatever we decide on that point has nothing to do with what Paul meant when he
wrote to the Ephesians in the first century.</span></p>
