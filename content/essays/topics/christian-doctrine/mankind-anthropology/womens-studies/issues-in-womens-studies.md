<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                 Below are six items for thought and
research on women’s studies. Some points may be referred to in <u>Hermeneutics:
Getting the Point</u>; check the scripture index for locations. As much as
possible, consciously integrate into your write-up the principles studied in
the course. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                 Some items involve work with the
original languages and may not seem fully understandable to those who have not
studied Greek. There is no expertise in biblical languages required, however,
since the exercises are vocabulary items that can be looked at through <i>Englishman’s
Greek Concordance to the New Testament</i>. On some questions students may want
to consult Dr. Warren’s “Preliminary Essays on Women’s Studies.” </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                 The project should equal ten to
twelve typed pages. These exercises are intended to be interpretation exercises
rather than research projects. Nevertheless, if you felt you had to use some
resource to “get you started,” after the question indicate any secondary
source(s) you used to prepare your answer.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                 The biblical texts most applicable to
women’s studies are <u>Genesis <b>2-3</b></u>; Exodus<b> 15</b>:20-21; <u>Acts <b>2</b>:18
(&lt; Joel <b>2</b>:28</u>; cp. Judges <b>4</b>:1-<b>5</b>:31; 2 Kings <b>22</b>:14;
2 Chronicles <b>34</b>:22; Nehemiah <b>6</b>:14; Isaiah <b>8</b>:3; Luke<b> 2</b>:9;
Acts <b>21</b>:36-38; <b>2</b>:41-45 + 46-55); <b>18</b>:24-26; Romans <b>7</b>:1-3;
<b><u>16</u></b><u>:1-2</u>, 3-5a, 6, 7, 12, 15); 1 Corinthians <i>7</i>; <b><u>11</u></b><u>:2-16</u>;
<b><u>14</u></b><u>:33b-36</u>; <u>Galatians <b>3</b>:28</u>; <u>Ephesians <b>5</b>:21-<b>6</b>:9</u>
[= <u>Colossians <b>3</b>:18-<b>4</b>:1</u> = <u>1 Peter <b>2</b>:13-<b>3</b>:7</u>];
Philippians <b>4</b>:3; <u>1 Timothy <b>2</b>:8-15</u>; <b><u>5</u></b><u>:1-16</u>;
2 Timothy<b> 3</b>:14-15 + <b>5</b>:1; Titus <b>2</b>:1-3:15.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                 A. Make a charted comparison of
elements between the “household teaching” texts: Ephesians <b>5</b>:21-6:9;
Colossians <b>3</b>:18-4:1; 1 Peter<b> 2</b>:13-3:7 (1 Timothy <b>2</b>:8-15). From
this layout, note similarities and differences that seem appropriate to
questions about marriage and the home, particularly as to whether marriage is a
purely egalitarian/equalitarian/ interpersonal arrangement or a hierarchical
one. Are the issues entirely homogeneous that are mentioned in these blocks? (Note
the work on “Comparative Method” in the Interpretation Section of this <i>Christian
Internet Resources</i>.)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                 B. Look at all the passages where “one
another” (<i>allēlōn</i>) occurs in the New Testament. If you have not studied
Greek, use <i>The Englishman’s Greek Concordance of the New Testament</i>,
under <i>allēlōn</i>, pp. 29-30. Do not use a concordance for an English
translation—<i>Strong’s,</i> <i>Young’s</i>, or <i>Cruden’s</i>; they do not
give you all the passages that apply here, because the Greek term is translated
in more than one way (“each other,” “one another,” “one to another,”<i> etc</i>.).
If you have taken Greek, you can use Moulton and Geden’s <i>Concordance to the
Greek New Testament</i>, pp. 43-44.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                             The task is to identify
all uses of the word as to whether it is reciprocal between individuals (A to B
to A) or can also be one-directional within a group (A to B, X to Y, Q to R). In
the first format, one person does to the second what the second person does
back to the first: “one another.” The second format pictures a group within
which one individual acts in a certain way toward a second, a third person acts
that way toward a fourth, and so forth. Unlike the first pattern, it does not
picture the second person of each pair as turning around and doing the same
thing back to the first person; in English we could reserve the expression “one
to another” for this format.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                             The point of this
exercise is to evaluate the notion of “mutual submission” in marriage as based
on Ephesians <b>5</b>:21, <i>“Submit yourselves to <u>one another</u>.”</i> Besides
the information gathered in the concordance exercise above, (a) what
considerations affect which meaning that “one another” has in this statement? (b)
Does scripture ever tell husbands to be subject to their wives?</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                 C. Is it a fallen condition described
in Genesis <b>3</b> when God tells Eve that her desire will be toward her
husband, and he will “rule” [<i>mashal</i> = Masoretic Text; kyrieuō =
Septuagint] over her? Does Genesis <b>3</b>:16 describe a fallen condition that
Galatians <b>3</b>:28 reverses? In this respect, was the Adam-Eve relationship
before the Fall different from this hierarchical relationship after the Fall? How
is the Genesis text used in the New Testament to answer this question? (Note
Romans <b>7</b>:1-3?; 1 Corinthians <b>11</b>:2-16; <b>14</b>:34; 1 Timothy <b>2</b>:8-15.)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                 D. Is Galatians <b>3</b>:28 an
absolute statement when it says that in Christ there is neither male nor
female? (a) Does immediate context, the New Testament generally, or the nature
of the case limit the application of the principle? (b) Are there any senses in
which there <u>is</u> a difference between male and female? (c) What is Paul’s
subject, or “frame of reference,” in the context of Galatians <b>3</b>:28? (d)
What thing(s) is/are common to the three sets; that is, to what extent are
male-female, bond-free, circumcised-uncircumcised alike? If the positive
comment addresses one item common to three classes that differ otherwise,
further considerations must determine whether those other distinctions may
exist if they do not interfere with the reason Paul mentioned them together.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                 E. (a) How can we reconcile the
implications of Paul’s directives to the Corinthians about women prophesying in
1 Corinthians <b>11</b>:2-16 (note also other places where they prophesy) and
his prohibition against women “speaking” (laleō) in 1 Corinthians <b>14</b>:33b-36?
(b) What does context include in “not speaking” in 1 Corinthians <b>14</b>:34? (c)
Is it likely that “speak” could mean <i>“rattle on and on,” </i>or <i>“prate”</i>?
(d) What variable might explain the difference between 1 Corinthians <b>11</b>
and <b>14</b>? Consider the following possibilities: </span></p>
