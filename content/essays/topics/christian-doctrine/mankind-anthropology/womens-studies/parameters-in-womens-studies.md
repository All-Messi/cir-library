style='font-size:12.0pt'>            A.  The order of the home and the church should
be compatible.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  The husband is responsible for the
family. The wife’s private or public behavior toward him should reflect
appropriately on that responsibility. If the husband has final responsible for
the home, it seems unnatural for the wife to have final responsibility in the
church. The church should not involve a reversal of responsibility structure
from what it is in the home. As the home is a social unit that involves all
aspects of life, so also is the church.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  1.   Therefore, we would not expect
the <u>ultimate leadership</u> in the church to be the wives of the men who
have final responsibility at home, because the men’s final responsibility in
the home involves many of the same religious matters of living covered by the
church.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  2.   However, would it seem
inappropriate to have the wife above her husband anywhere up and down the
authority levels of the church on individual matters? That would have to do
with <u>secondary levels of leadership</u>.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  3.   It would also seem that the
wife of one man should not have responsibility/authority over some other
husband <u>in the levels of leadership</u>. If such an arrangement were to
happen, the woman would certainly have to conduct herself in ways that would be
“deferential” toward men.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  4.   That might imply that unmarried
women might have a different posture among church members than married ones
have. In a campus ministry situation, for example, there might need to be little
difference between a group of unmarried young people.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;

expedient.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  Does “teacher” connote authority in
Paul in the public setting? Prophesying, however, is a function, not an office.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            P.   The reason for deference and authority</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify;text-indent:-58.5pt'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                      The parity of reasoning
on women and slaves: Miles. <i>Husband-Wife Equality</i>, pp. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify;text-indent:-58.5pt'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                      176-77.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify;text-indent:-58.5pt'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  But Paul does not root the slavery
issue in the kinds of reasons he gives for husband-wife relationship. The
master-slave relationship is never grounded <u>theologically</u>, that is, in
the nature of things or as per divine intent. Slavery is not tied to the
God-Christ-believer continuum as it is in 1 Corinthians <b>11</b>:3: God-Christ-man-woman.
The husband “is” the head of the wife (cp. “is” in Hebrews’ appeal to Psalm <b>110</b>:4:
<i>“You <u>are</u> [appointment] priest . . .”</i>). The God-Christ-believer
pattern sits inside the husband-wife section of Ephesians <b>5</b>:22-23, so it
should not be applied to master-slave relations in<b> 6</b>:5-9. The
relationship of husband and wife is grounded in God’s nature, but master-slave
relationship is not said to be grounded that way.  </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  The master-slave issue is never
grounded <u>historically</u>. Paul does not give historical reasons associated
with creation or past divine acts as he does for the man-woman relationship,
where he cites the sequence of creation and the fall. The husband-wife
relationship is a matter of <u>nature</u> as reflected historically in the
order of creation, whereas the slave-master relationship is a matter of
function not based in creation.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  Paul never encourages a woman to
seek “freedom” from her husband as he acknowledges that slaves may do to obtain
their freedom if they can (1 Corinthians <b>7</b>:21).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  A slave is to submit to the master
for expediency’s sake; the wife is to submit to the husband for nature’s sake.
The first affects that submission psychologically by <u>comparing it</u> to the
Lord. The second affects that naturally by teaching that both men and women reflect
the divine nature.</span></p>
