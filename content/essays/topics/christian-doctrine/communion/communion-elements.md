<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-right:-27.0pt;text-align:center'><b><span
style='font-size:14.0pt'>COMMUNION ELEMENTS </span></b></p>

<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-right:-27.0pt;text-align:center'><b><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></b></p>

<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-right:-27.0pt;text-align:center'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>Virgil Warren, PhD</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-27.0pt;text-align:justify'><b><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></b></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-27.0pt;text-align:justify'><b><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></b></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-27.0pt;text-align:justify'><b><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>     </span></b><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>I. Unleavened
Elements</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-27.0pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-27.0pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            In regard to the nature of the elements of
the Lord’s Supper, the basic fact is that Jesus instituted it during a Passover
meal. Consequently, he used elements available at that feast.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-27.0pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-27.0pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            A.  The <u>bread</u></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-27.0pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-27.0pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  Only unleavened bread was to be
eaten in the Passover meal itself as well as during the preceding week (Exodus <b>12</b>:8,
15-20; <b>13</b>:6-7; Leviticus<b> 23</b>:4-8, 15; Deuteronomy <b>16</b>:3-4,
8). This was such a prominent feature of the Passover that it was even called <i>“the
feast of unleavened bread” </i>(Deuteronomy <b>16</b>:16, <i>etc</i>.). Anyone
disobeying this rule was <i>“cut off from the congregation of Israel, whether a
sojourner or one born in the land&quot;</i> (Exodus <b>12</b>:15, 19).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-27.0pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-27.0pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            B.  The <u>cup</u></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-27.0pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-27.0pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  1.   Observations suggesting that
unfermented wine would have been used at the </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-27.0pt;text-align:justify'><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        </span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>Passover </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-27.0pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-27.0pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        a.   Passover texts about
leaven apparently do not refer just to unleavened bread. No leaven itself was
to be <i>“in all their borders,”</i> in their houses (Exodus <b>12</b>:15), and
no one was to eat anything leavened (Exodus<b> 12</b>:19-20). These provisions
may indicate that no spoiled food was to be kept in the home. Since
fermentation is accomplished by the same process that leavens bread, if nothing
leavened was to be eaten, it would seem to be in the spirit of the feast not to
drink leavened fruit of the vine as part of the meal.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-27.0pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        b.   The liquid element of the
Lord’s Supper is never called “wine” (</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:
12.0pt'>οἶνος</span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>, [<i>oinos</i>]) in the New
Testament. It is always designated <i>“the cup”</i> (Matthew <b>26</b>:27 =
Mark <b>14</b>:23 = Luke<b> 22</b>:20 = 1 Corinthians <b>11</b>:25-28; 1
Corinthians<b> 10</b>:16, 21) or <i>“the fruit</i> [</span><span lang=EL
style='font-size:12.0pt'>γέννημα</span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>] <i>of
the vine”</i> (Matthew <b>26</b>:29 = Mark <b>14</b>:25 = Luke <b>22</b>:18). The
second expression is not apt to be a euphemism, as is occasionally suggested,
because “wine” is hardly a term that calls for a euphemism. Although it is a
technical point, even if </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>οἶνος</span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'> [<i>oinos</i>] were used of the Lord’s Supper, it
could mean “must,” or unfermented wine (Matthew <b>9</b>:17).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-27.0pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        c.   In connection with the
Lord’s Supper some have noted that the New Testament uses “leaven” to symbolize
evil influence (Matthew <b>16</b>:5-12 = Mark <b>8</b>:14-21; cp. Luke <b>12</b>:1-3;
1 Corinthians <b>5</b>:1-8; Galatians <b>5</b>:9), but it does not always mean <u>evil</u>
influence (Matthew <b>13</b>:33 = Luke<b> 13</b>:21) and therefore that
leavened bread or grape juice would be inappropriate in divine observances. No
point about leaven as symbolism for evil, however, is ever made in connection
with the Passover bread or the communion bread. Instead, the lack of leaven is
originally connected with the Israelites’ hurried departure from Egypt (Exodus <b>12</b>:11);
they did not have time to knead the dough, put leaven (yeast) in it, and let it
rise. This not waiting would not apply to grape juice with leaven in it, that
is, fermenting juice. People would not be waiting to let the grape juice become
what the fermentation would produce.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-27.0pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        d.   Probably the bottom line
is that there is no reason to use wine, especially in our circumstance, where
grape juice is so readily available. Furthermore, it must be remembered that
natural fermentation process, the only one available in antiquity, creates less
than 3% alcohol content. Even then, the Greeks usually mixed water with their
wine, a practice adopted among the people of Palestine by the first century A.D.
The situation today is obviously quite different.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-27.0pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-27.0pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  2.   Counter considerations</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-27.0pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-27.0pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        a.   The question about the
elements needs to be conditioned by the fact that the Lord’s Supper was instituted
at a Passover meal. Had it been instituted at another time, would Jesus have
worried with getting <u>un</u>leavened bread to represent his body? The lack of
leaven is not a stated part of the symbolism in the supper, but it did bear a
significance to the Passover. The base question on the elements, then, is
whether the precise form of them is a function of the Passover meal, and we
should not make its peculiarities determinative for the Lord’s Supper.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-27.0pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        b.   Today Jewish people use
regular wine in the Passover feast without supposing that they are breaking the
ancient Passover regulations. This may not have been the original practice,
however, since <i>“the earliest evidence for the use of wine at Passover comes
from the Hellenistic period”</i> (Judith <b>49</b>:6; New <i>International
Standard Bible Encyclopedia</i>, IV:1070).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-27.0pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        c.   The vintage season for
grapes came in the fall—around September—while the Passover feast was in the
spring—March/April. Grape juice from even the most recent harvest would still
have been potentially intoxicating at the Passover and Pentecost feasts (“new
wine,” cp. Acts<b> 2</b>:13, 15). After six months significant fermentation
would have taken place. Although the Israelites—at least by the first century—had
pneumatic sealing, it is doubtful that under normal conditions they would have
used it for grape juice.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-27.0pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        d.   In his comments to the
Corinthian church, Paul notes that during the Lord’s Supper some people were “drunken,”
which could imply fermented wine (1 Corinthians <b>11</b>:21). However, Paul is
describing an agape feast of which the Lord’s Supper was a part. In so doing,
he contrasts “drunken” (</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>μεθύω</span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'> [<i>methyō</i>]) with hungry (</span><span lang=EL
style='font-size:12.0pt'>πεινάω</span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>, <i>peianō</i>
<b>11</b>:21) and connects it with both drinking <u>and</u> eating (<b>11</b>:22),
facts which would suggest that he means “satiated” or “stuffed,” rather than
intoxicated (note John <b>2</b>:10).    </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-27.0pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-27.0pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  About the only positive comment we
could give for fermented wine is that one-cup communion would be less liable to
spread disease if “real” wine is used.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-27.0pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-27.0pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>II.  <i>“This is my body”</i>; “this is my blood”
(Matthew <b>26</b>:26 + 28 = Mark <b>14</b>:22 + 24 = Luke </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-27.0pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            <b>22</b>:19; 1 Corinthians <b>11</b>:24;
cp. <b>11</b>:27, 29).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-27.0pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-27.0pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            A.  “Is” means “represents.” In the <u>mind</u>
of the participant the elements of the Lord’s Supper are “transformed” into the
body and blood of Jesus; that is, the participant views the emblems as having
that meaning.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-27.0pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-27.0pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  1.   In the context of the
institution, Luke <b>22</b>:20 and 1 Corinthians <b>11</b>:25 have Jesus say of
the liquid element, “This is the new covenant in my blood” instead of saying, “This
is my blood.” Presumably the two statements are regarded as equivalent with the
implication that “is” means “represents.” A “covenant” is not something
substantive. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-27.0pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  2.   A figurative significance is
found in similar texts both on the lips of Jesus and in the mouth of Paul (cp.
Matthew<b> 13</b>:37-39; Luke <b>11</b>:8; <b>15</b>:26; John <b>10</b>:6; <b>12</b>:48;
Galatians <b>4</b>:24-25; cp. John<b> 6</b>:51- 58).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-27.0pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  3.   Jesus himself—hence, all of his
body and blood—is present when he tells the disciples that the bread and cup
are his body and blood. Therefore, when he refers to something besides his body
as his body, he means something other than body and blood.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-27.0pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-27.0pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            B.  The doctrine of “real presence” says
that at the prayer of consecration by the administrator of the sacraments, the
bread and grape juice are (ontologically, hypostatically) transformed into the
real body and blood of the Lord (<u>transubstantiation</u>), or the actual body
and blood of Jesus are mystically intermingled with the bread and grape juice (<u>consubstantiation</u>).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-27.0pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-27.0pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  1.   As a matter of plain fact, in
the process of observing the Lord’s Supper, the elements do not change any
characteristics perceivable to the senses. They look, taste, and feel like
bread and grape juice.    </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-27.0pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        Despite the lack of
perceivable change in the elements, the claim is that though the “accidents”
admittedly do not change, the “essence” changes at the prayer of consecration. This
essence-accidents distinction comes from Greek philosophy, which distinguished
between real and ideal; the accidents were the sum total of the material
characteristics, and the essence was the ideal nature.    </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-27.0pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        We simply reject the supposed
distinction by saying that the real is all that ever really exists; the real is
the sum total of all characteristics and associations. The supposed ideal is
merely the formal, mental concept a person has in his mind about the real; the “ideal”
has no existence apart from the mind. The real-ideal distinction involves a
subjective-objective confusion. Under that confusion Jesus would have been
eating himself. The job here is not disproving the essence-accidence
distinction, but what is the basis for it.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-27.0pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  2.   The liquid element is called “fruit
of the vine” after he prays, after he enjoins his disciples to partake of it,
and after he says that he will not drink of it until he does so with them in
his Father’s kingdom (Matthew<b> 26</b>:27). Consequently, even today what he
drinks with us <u>when we drink it</u> is still “fruit of the vine.”</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-27.0pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  3.   In the course of the
institution, the same item is called in the one case “the cup,” <i>“the fruit
of the vine,”</i> and <i>“my blood”</i>; and in the other case “the loaf” and “my
body.” Even on an assumption about essence-accident distinction, the same thing
could not be all three things—blood, a cup, fruit of the vine—at the same time
in the same sense. It is therefore arbitrary to pick blood as what it
essentially is (<i><u>ad hominem</u></i><u> argument from consistency</u>).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-27.0pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  4.   Christ no longer has a
flesh-and-blood body because he has been glorified, and henceforth we know him
no longer after the flesh (2 Corinthians <b>5</b>:16). It is not until our
glorified condition comes that we will be like him and therefore able to see
him as he is (1 John <b>3</b>:2).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-27.0pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  5.   The observance is to be in
remembrance of Jesus. If the bread and cup are actually his body and blood, his
actual essence would be present in a substantial sense. We wonder whether it
would be likely to call the observance a “memorial” in such a circumstance.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-27.0pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  6.   By way of comparison, the
water-become-wine at the marriage feast in Cana is called wine after Jesus
miraculously changed it from being water. It looked and tasted like wine (John<b>
2</b>:1-10). A similar observation could be made about the water Moses
miraculously changed to blood in Egypt (Exodus <b>4</b>:9); it looked like
(Exodus <b>7</b>:20) and tasted like (Exodus <b>7</b>:24) blood. Similarly, if
the fruit of the vine were miraculously changed to real blood, it would have
been perceived as such; from control cases there are no analogies for anything
else (<u>argument from analogy</u>).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-27.0pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  7.   The hypostatic reading of the
statement does not bear its positive <u>burden of proof</u>, whereas the
emblematic reading can do so. No positive basis can be given for a hypostatic
reading of Christ’s words over against a figurative reading. Surely a
figurative significance (metaphor) to his words gives a sufficient relevant
meaning, as when we say of a picture on the desk, “This is my wife.” In other
words, “is” means “means” or “represents.” (Note the hermeneutical problem
called “reification”; <i>Interpretation: Getting the Point</i>, p. 52).    </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-27.0pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  8.   Christianity concerns itself
with interpersonal matters rather than ontological ones. The kind of cause must
conform to the kind of result (the “law of the harvest”). Consequently, we have
no positive basis for supposing that ontological considerations belong to an
act that has spiritual significance as per the purpose of the faith in general.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-27.0pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  A similar confusion ontologizes behavioral/relational/personal
as demonstrated in the ancient pagan practice of eating the heart of a brave
man slain in battle as a way of becoming brave and strong. A characteristic of
behavior learned by experience is conceived of as transmitted by matter. Our
eating the body and blood of Christ is not something that has value in the
ontological realm or in an ontological sense; we internalize his values and
purposes symbolically by “ingesting” what represents what he as a
flesh-and-blood man of God stood for while he was among us.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-27.0pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-27.0pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>III. Substituting Other Elements for Unleavened Bread
and the Fruit of the Vine</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-27.0pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-27.0pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            In some parts of the world, grapes are not
readily available. Other fruit drinks have been substituted rather than
importing grapes and cultivating them for communion purposes. Less
understandable, however, is the idea of replacing the traditional elements with
hamburgers and coke just to become “culturally relevant” or to do something
different.</span></p>
