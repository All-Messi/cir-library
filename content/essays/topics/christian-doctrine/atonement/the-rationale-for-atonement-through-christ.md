<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='text-align:center'><span

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:
115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>Summary statement: <i>“Christ alone
has achieved perfect goodness as a man in the flesh by obeying the Father in
everything even to the point of yielding to an agonizing death. When we commit
ourselves to Christ as Lord together with the values and purposes he shares
with the Father, God considers us righteous like him, forgiving our sin,
reckoning aspiration for achievement, reconciling us to himself.”</i></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><b><span style='font-size:12.0pt;
line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>            Salvation as
reconciliation to God </span></b><span style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:
115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>(2 Corinthians<b> 5</b>:18-21).<b> </b>Salvation
through Christ taps into our understanding from experience how restoring broken
friendships works and why it behooves us to avail ourselves of its benefits
with God and other people (Matthew <b>5</b>:23-24). Reconciliation between us and
God operates the same way it does between us. God is a person, the One who gave
us the interpersonal capacity by putting his image in us (Genesis <b>1</b>:26-27).
Salvation means reconciliation between us and him. The remaining point is to honor
Christ for his atoning role in that framework. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>            <b>Salvation
in three parts</b></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><u><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>What
the Son did</span></u></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt;
line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>            The Son entered
the world by birth as a man (incarnation), <u>sent by the Father</u> to live <u>sinlessly
</u>in the flesh to the point of violent death, thereby establishing himself as
the One the Father could appoint as the basis for reconciliation.           
THE RIGHTEOUSNESS OF THE SON</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><u><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>What
we do</span></u></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt;
line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>            As the Father’s
condition for reconciliation to himself, we identify with the Son by committing
totally, permanently, and exclusively to his lordship, purposes, and values he
shares with the Father.                                                              OUR
IDENTIFICATION WITH THE SON</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><u><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>What
the Father will do</span></u></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>            The
Father will consider us righteous <u>like</u> the Son we commit ourselves to
(forgiveness), reconciling us to himself (gift of the Spirit), and counting us
among all others so reconciled (church membership).                                     
                                RECONCILIATION BY THE FATHER</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:0in;margin-bottom:0in;

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><b><span

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><b><span

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:0in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;text-align:justify'><b><span style='font-size:12.0pt;
line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>The difference is that
the Son established his righteousness in the flesh, which makes him all the
more the One we have been appropriately called on to commit ourselves to.</span></b></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:0in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;text-align:justify'><b><span style='font-size:12.0pt;

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:0in;margin-bottom:0in;

style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>
He <i>“emptied himself”</i> (<i>kenosis</i>) of his rights and privileges as

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:0in;margin-bottom:0in;

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:0in;margin-bottom:0in;

style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'> Since

feelings of our infirmities (Hebrews <b>2</b>:17-18; <b>4</b>:15; <b>5</b>:2). </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><b><span

 Our Identification with the Son</span></b></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:0in;margin-bottom:0in;

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:0in;margin-bottom:0in;

with the Son as committing to him, his values, and purposes amounts to
identifying with the Father because they have the same values and purposes. The
difference between them is that the Son maintained his values and purposes in
the flesh against ultimate physical agony as temptation for him to depart from
them.</i> </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:0in;margin-bottom:0in;

because God so appointed that <u>any</u> of our responses have <u>any</u> value

available to Israel (Acts 5:31) and to Gentiles (Acts 11:18). The things God
appoints as conditions are appropriate to the forgiveness he offers.</b></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><b><span
style='font-size:14.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>II.
The Reconciliation Process: Repentance Plus Forgiveness</span></b></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><b><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>            </span></b><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>Salvation
is reconciliation as these texts show: Romans <b>5</b>:10-11; 2 Corinthians <b>5</b>:18-20
(<i>“ministry of reconciliation”</i>); Ephesians <b>2</b>:14-18; Colossians <b>1</b>:20-22;
Hebrews <b>2</b>:17 as well as the references about <i>“friendship with God”</i>:
2 Chronicles <b>20</b>:7; Isaiah <b>41</b>:8; (John <b>15</b>:15); James,<b> 2</b>:23.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><b><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>            </span></b><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>Reconciliation
requires regaining goodness in the eyes of God by getting rid of the guilt from
behaviors that bring alienation.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>            Reconciliation
involves repentance by the offender plus forgiveness by the offended. The
offender’s repentance separates him from the kind of person he was when he
offended; the offended person’s forgiveness separates the offender from the
kind of person he was when he offended. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>            The
reconciliation occurs in the <u>minds</u> of the persons reconciling because
the past sin itself cannot be changed, but that is not the issue. The issue is
how the persons view each other and relate in the present.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>            Repentance
by the offender and forgiveness by the offended applies in all cases of
reconciliation between people or between people and God. The two acts separate
the present person from the past sin that caused the separation.</span></p>

line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>      </span></b><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>THE
MOSAIC SACRIFICIAL SYSTEM</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:center'><b><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>&nbsp;</span></b></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><b><span
style='font-size:14.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>IV.
Aligning the Terminology with the Reality</span></b></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><b><span
style='font-size:14.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>&nbsp;</span></b></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><b><span
style='font-size:14.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>         </span></b><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>The
layout above analyzes the reality that makes <u>reconciliation</u> clear and
sensible.</span><span style='font-size:14.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:
"Times New Roman",serif'> </span><span style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:
115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>The comments below make <u>statements
about reconciliation</u> straightforward. Scripture often expresses these
matters in shorthand expressions and figures of speech—metaphors, analogies, oxymorons,
paradoxes, phenomenological statements (the way it looks), idioms, poetic expressions,
symbolism. Without the reality alongside, these ways of speaking may not
communicate to those who do not already understand, especially in modern
western culture that tends to speak in more straightforward ways. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>            Figurative
presentation lends color, interest, and affective nuances and creates a “sense”
of the meaning without necessarily providing a clear picture of it. Poetic
speech can be more efficient; speaking literally can take longer to express the
point—as a reader can verify when explaining the complex ideas literally. (See sample
texts explained below.) Speaking more literally may lose that engaging coloration,
but it aids understanding. It can help avoid <i>“getting lost in the words” </i>by
using “reality thinking.” Such thought concentrates on actions and involves the
interpersonal character of the Christian worldview and purposes.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>            (1)
Aspects and terms (“metaphors of salvation”) about the full reality </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                        Absolution,
adoption, atonement, cleansing, conquest, conversion, creation, discipleship, election,
eternal life, expiation, fellowship, forgiveness, illumination, imputation, indwelling,
justification, obedience, predestination/foreordination, propitiation, ransom, reconciliation,
redemption, regeneration, remission, sacrifice, salvation, sanctification, submission</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                        </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>            (2)
Atonement elements involved in laying the foundation for reconciliation: blood,
death, crucifixion, sacrifice</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                        <u>Death</u>
means that Christ did not just live as a man among people for a while and then
return to the Father after, say, his teaching ministry. Rather, in his <i>righteousness</i>
he endured ultimate human suffering in <i>obedience</i> to the Father
(interpersonal cause). Note Romans <b>5</b>:8, 10; <b>14</b>:9; 1 Corinthians <b>8</b>:11;
<b>15</b>:3; 2 Corinthians<b> 5</b>:14; <b>6</b>:15</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                        <u>Blood</u>
connects with atonement, not as its agent (impersonal cause), but as an indication
that Christ did not die from old age, sickness, poison, accident, or disease,
but by execution by people that hated him. Passages include Matthew <b>26</b>:28
(cp. Mark <b>14</b>:24; John <b>22</b>:20; 1 Corinthians <b>10</b>:16; 11:25,
27); Acts <b>20</b>:28; Romans<b> 3</b>:25; <b>5</b>:9-11; Galatians <b>1</b>:14,
20; Ephesians<b> 1</b>:7; <b>2</b>:13; Colossians <b>1</b>:14, 20; Hebrews <b>9</b>:12-14,
20-25; <b>10</b>:19, 29; <b>12</b>:24; <b>13</b>:12, 20, 21; 1 Peter <b>1</b>:2,
19; 1 John <b>1</b>:7; <b>5</b>:8; Revelation <b>1</b>:5; <b>5</b>:9; <b>7</b>:14;
<b>12</b>:11.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                        <u>Crucifixion</u>
was the way as an innocent man he voluntarily (Matthew <b>26</b>:51-53; John<b>
10</b>:18; <b>15</b>:13; <b>18</b>:5-11) submitted to an agonizing death used
for <i>punishment </i>of criminals, something even more than dying on the
battlefield (Romans <b>5</b>:8; Hebrews <b>9</b>:12). </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                        The
Mosaic blood <u>sacrifices</u> foreshadowed Messiah’s dying <u>on account of</u>
the sins of others and f<u>or</u> the sins of others. The paschal lamb most explicitly
looked forward to the Messiah in the stipulation that not to break any of its
bones at Passover, the event at which Jesus was “sacrificed” (Exodus <b>12</b>:46;
Numbers<b> 9</b>:12; Psalm<b> 22</b>:17; <b>34</b>:20 &lt; John <b>19</b>:36;
cp. John <b>1</b>:29; 1 John <b>3</b>:5).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>            (3)
Imagery for the atonement includes cleansing, washing, healing, curing, as well
as saving (Romans <b>8</b>:24; 1 Corinthians <b>1</b>:30)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'> </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>            Approximate
pictures for Christ’s work in reconciliation</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                        Conquest/Victor
over sin and death: Romans <b>4</b>:22-25</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                        Ransom:
Matthew <b>20</b>:28; Mark<b> 10</b>:45; 1 Timothy <b>2</b>:6</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                        Purchase/redemption:
1 Corinthians <b>6:</b>20; <b>7</b>:23; Ephesians<b> 1</b>:7, 14; Colossians <b>1</b>:14;
</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                              Hebrews<b>
9</b>:12, 15; 2 Peter <b>2</b>:1; Revelation <b>5</b>:9; <b>14</b>:3-4; </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                        Substitution/sacrifice:
Isaiah <b>53</b></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                        **A
common element makes these pictures appropriate: a result freely provided by another
for someone in need who cannot contribute to that result in a causal sense.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>            (4)
Reality thinking applied to a sampling of atonement passages</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                        <i>“The
Lamb of God that takes away the sin of the world”</i> (John <b>1</b>:29, 36;
Acts <b>8</b>:32; 1 Peter <b>1</b>:19; Revelation <b>5</b>:6, 8, 12-13; <b>6</b>:1
&lt; Isaiah <b>53</b>:32). Christ carried out ultimate righteousness by submitting
to death on a cross. God counts us righteous if we commit ourselves to Christ, his
lordship, values, and purposes. Considering us righteous is the positive side
of forgiving our sins. Christ takes away sin by making righteousness through
forgiveness available to those who identify with him.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                        <i>“He
that believes on the Son has eternal life”</i> (John <b>3</b>:36). In itself,
believing on the crucified Christ no more makes people righteous than looking
at a brazen serpent cures snakebite. In both cases, the actions are not causes
but appropriate conditions God specified for <u>his</u> forgiving sin or curing
snakebite. The text presents them as parallel. The point is conditionality, not
causality. God inserts his forgiveness between our trust in Christ and our receiving
eternal life from God. (Conditions can be called “occasional causes.”)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                        <i>“You
have been bought with a price”</i> (1 Corinthians <b>6</b>:20; cp. <b>7</b>:23;
2 Peter <b>2</b>:1) as if buying the freedom of a slave. That image is an
approximate comparison because there is no one to pay the price to.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                        <i>“He
who knew no sin [God] made to be sin on our behalf”</i> (2 Corinthians <b>5</b>:21).
Note the expression <i>“make God a liar”</i> (1 John <b>1</b>:10; <b>5</b>:10; Matthew
<b>5</b>:32; <b>17</b>:12). The Father allowed Christ to be <i>“treated like a
sinner”</i> because in so doing Christ became sinless in the highest
degree—voluntary suffering to the point of violent death in obedience to the
Father. Having endured ultimate suffering in the flesh, he established ultimate
righteousness in our fleshly realm. He became the righteous One in the flesh
whom the Father could appoint for us to commit ourselves to in order for the
Father to consider us righteous like him.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                        <i>“Christ
became a curse for us”</i> (Galatians <b>3</b>:13). <i>“The curse of the Law”</i>
arises from the comment that <i>“everyone is accursed that dies on a tree”</i>
(Deuteronomy <b>21</b>:23). Christ died on a “tree”/cross so we can avoid dying
as a curse for our sins. The dying was not a <i>punishment</i> on <u>him</u>
for <u>our</u> sin; it was a <i>suffering</i> <u>he</u> underwent to establish
the perfect righteousness <u>we</u> could commit ourselves to and thereby “receive”
forgiveness by being viewed as righteous <u>like</u> him. It is a righteousness
God considers us as having because we commit ourselves to him who has that
righteousness.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                        <i>“Christ
Jesus came into the world to save sinners”</i> (1 Timothy <b>1</b>:15). He came
into the world because those who need salvation are flesh and blood people. It
was appropriate that Christ live out his righteousness in the weaknesses and
temptations present in the realm where we people fail to live it out. He could
then be the One we could most appropriately commit ourselves to for the
Father’s considering us righteous like the One we indicate we want to be like
and considered as like.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                        <i>“Christ
bore our sins on the cross”</i> (Hebrews<b> 9</b>:28; 1 Peter<b> 2</b>:24). By
obeying the Father to the point of that agonizing death, Christ established
himself in the flesh as the One we can commit to, so that the Father views us
as righteous <u>like</u> him as the Father promised. Coming to view us as
righteous (positive) amounts to the same thing as forgiving our sins (negative)
in terms of Christ’s righteousness that we commit to. He was treated like the
criminal he was not so we could be treated like innocent people we are not. If
we had not sinned, there would have been no need for him to endure the cross to
establish an example of real righteousness in the flesh for us to identify
with. He bore our sins in the sense that he died because we had sinned, not as
a substitute criminal, but as the righteous object we commit ourselves to for
reconciliation to God. Sinfulness and righteousness are moral qualities that
cannot by nature be transferred from one person to another: <i>“The
righteousness of the righteous will be on himself, and the wickedness of the
wicked will be on himself”</i> (Ezekiel <b>18</b>:20).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                        <i>“They
had washed their robes and made them white in the blood of the Lamb”</i>
(Revelation <b>7</b>:14). Robes represent what covers the saints, what characterizes
them; and the symbolism unfolds from there. “Washing” does not clean off sins;
washing robes in blood would not make them white anyway; white represents
purity. That comes about really by purification from sin by the Father’s forgiveness
conditioned on our commitment to the sinless One who shed his blood in
maintaining his sinlessness.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                        Isaiah
<b>53</b> is the beautiful <i>Suffering Servant Poem</i>, which incorporates several
expressions that New Testament writers pick up on in referring to Messiah’s atonement.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                                    <b>53</b>:4-5,
11: To observers it looked like God had struck him for <u>his</u> iniquities
(phenomenology), but he was struck for <u>ours</u>. But more than that, he was
not smitten in <u>punishment</u> for ours, but in <u>completing his obedience</u>
for us to commit to. When we commit ourselves to the One who perfectly obeyed the
Father, God considers us like him in intent and forgives our sin accordingly.
Committing to Christ and <u>his righteousness</u> is the same thing as
repentance from <u>our unrighteousness</u>. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                                    <b>53</b>:5:
<i>“With his stripes we are healed.”</i> The statement is an oxymoron because
stripes do not heal; if anything, they make wounds worse. He experienced
stripes as an innocent man in establishing the highest degree of innocence
possible. On the condition of our identifying with the Servant, God forgives
our sins, which Isaiah calls “healing” us from them. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                                    <b>53</b>:6:
<i>“God laid on him the iniquities of us all.”</i> The sentence traces back to the
practice of laying the hands of the sinner on the head of the sacrificial
animal (Leviticus <b>16</b>:21-22; Numbers <b>8</b>:12. It was a symbolic
gesture that pictured transferring the worshiper’s sins onto the animal
sacrificed in his place. But that sacrificial background is <u>symbolic</u> for
ridding from sin, not real, because in the first place (1) <i>“the blood of
bulls and goats cannot take away sin”</i> (Psalm <b>40</b>:6 &lt; Hebrews <b>10</b>:1,
4, 11; cp.<b> 9</b>:9; Romans <b>8</b>:3). Furthermore, (2) neither guilt nor
punishment for it can be transferred from one person to another (Deuteronomy <b>24</b>:16;
2 Kings <b>14</b>:6; 2 Chronicles <b>25</b>:4; Jeremiah<b> 31</b>:29-30;
Ezekiel<b> 18</b>:4, 20; Micah <b>6</b>:6-8). Guilt is as personal as the act
that produces it. So transfer is symbolic. The reality behind this symbolism is
that the ultimate righteousness in the flesh was established in the Servant’s
obedience to the point of agonized suffering unto the death that Isaiah
describes. God has willed for “us all” to commit ourselves to the Righteous
One, his lordship, values, and purposes as God’s condition considering us
righteous like the Suffering Servant.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                                    <b>53</b>:7:
<i>“The Lamb did not open its mouth”</i> is a picture of innocence, a lamb
unaware of what’s to happen, not objecting to it or trying to get away.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>            </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><b><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>V.
Postscript on Alternative Explanations</span></b></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><b><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>            </span></b><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>Christ’s
atonement does not come through magic or miracle (impersonal mechanisms),
transfer of guilt or goodness (legal transaction), influence or example (human achievement),
or legal pronouncement (by third parties). Commitment to the Son, his lordship,
values, and purposes equals commitment to the Father (Matthew <b>28</b>:19) because
they share the same purposes and values, and because the Son came to do the
will of the Father who sent him to establish perfect righteousness in human
realm. <i>“God loved the world so much that he gave his Son so people who put
their trust in him can have eternal fellowship with God”</i> (John <b>3</b>:16;
Romans <b>5</b>:8-11).</span></p>
