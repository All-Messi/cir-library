<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>I. Common Ground</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            We all believe that <b>(1)</b> salvation
means being brought back into personal relationship with God (<u>reconciliation</u>).
In this respect, 2 Corinthians<b> 5</b>:17-21 stands out by using <u>reconciliation</u>
repeatedly to describe the nature and purpose of Christian mission: <i>“ . . .
in Christ God was reconciling the world to himself . . . having given us the
ministry of reconciliation”</i> (<b>5</b>:19).<span style='position:relative;
top:-3.0pt'>1</span> </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            We all confess that <b>(2) </b><u>Jesus
Christ is the only savior</u>, the only One in terms of whom God figures the
salvation of any person that is saved in any dispensation or circumstance. We
put the point this way because Jesus Christ is more than savior of those who
lived after him. Hebrews <b>9</b>:15 says that God figures the salvation of all
Jewish saints in terms of Christ: </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><i><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>“He
is the mediator of a new covenant that, a death having taken place for the
redemption of the transgressions that were under the first covenant, they who
have been called may receive the promise of the eternal inheritance.”</span></i><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>1 Peter <b>3</b>:19-<b>4</b>:6 speaks similarly about
saved people from the patriarchal age: <i>“. . . the gospel was preached even
to the dead that they might be judged indeed according to people in the flesh,
but live according to God in the spirit.” </i></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            We all believe that <b>(3) </b><u>sin is
what separates</u> us from God, that <u>we have all sinned</u> and are all
thereby separated from him, and that <u>not everyone will be saved</u>.<span
style='position:relative;top:-3.0pt'> </span>In Isaiah <b>59</b>:2<span
style='position:relative;top:-3.0pt'> </span>God declares<i>, “Your sins have
separated you from me.”</i> Romans <b>3</b>:23 serves as a familiar reference
for the conclusion that <i>“all have sinned and fall short of the glory of God.”</i>
Matthew <b>25</b>:46 joins many texts in saying, <i>“These will go away into
eternal punishment, but the righteous into eternal life.”</i></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            We all know that <b>(4)</b> <u>we cannot
save ourselves</u>, but that <u>God saves us on the basis of our identity with
Jesus Christ</u> (Ephesians <b>2</b>:4-10, <i>etc</i>.).<span style='position:
relative;top:-3.0pt'> </span>In addition to the Great Commission itself, a host
of statements about Christian baptism express the idea that in it we identity
with Christ: <i>“As many of you as have been baptized <u>into</u> Christ have
put on Christ” </i>(Galatians <b>3</b>:27). <u>In</u> Christ we receive grace
and glory (Ephesians<b> 1</b>:3-14, <i>etc</i>.) and have fellowship with the
Father (1 John<b> 1</b>:3).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            Furthermore, we agree that <b>(5)</b> as a
matter of practical appropriateness, God identified with mankind through
incarnation in the person of the Son, who carried his obedience to the Father
to the point of shedding his blood in violent death on a cross, thereby
establishing his own obedience to the same degree that he calls on his own
disciples to do for him as their savior (Matthew<b> 10</b>:38; <b>16</b>:24;
Mark <b>8</b>:34; <b>10</b>:31; Luke [<b>8</b>:23];<b> 14</b>:27). </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            <b>(6)</b> Identification with Christ
means deliberate, total, permanent, exclusive commitment to him in contrast to
all parallel possibilities. It means commitment to carry our righteousness to
the same level as he carried his in our physical arena. It means commitment to
him as a person, to his values for our life, to his purpose for our future, to
his leadership and lordship in everything. It means our sense of being on his
side as well as our sense of who we are. We agree on the facts up through this
point.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>II. Statement of the Distinctive Issue</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            The difference in our view comes in the
next step. What is <u>the manner in which Christ saves us</u>? What does
identification with Christ give us access to? Does it give us access to an
interpersonal act (forgiveness as in <u>identification theory</u>), a legal
mechanism (penal substitution as in <u>John Calvin’s atonement theory</u>), a
supernatural power (conquest of Satan, sin, and death as in <u>Christus Victor
theory</u>), or a <u>ransom</u> of one person by another? Identification theory
means that personally identifying with Christ is the condition God established
for viewing us as righteous and for giving us everything implied by that. On
the basis of our identity with him who <u>is</u> righteous, God is willing to <u>view</u>
us as righteous (forgiveness), regard us as in fellowship with him (gift of the
Holy Spirit), and add us to the body of Christ (church membership). Salvation
occurs in God’s mind. The death that we are in (alienation) and would continue
to be in (hell) is replaced by reconciliation, a reconciliation based on being
viewed as righteous, not because we are but because we have identified with him
who is. The correctness and adequacy of this last step is the point at issue
and no other.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            In other words, the issue lies in “the
theory of the atonement.” Any concern over the theory offered here implies one
of two things: <b>(1)</b> this final step contradicts one or more of the
previous confessions or cannot provide the basis for them; <b>(2)</b> this
final step cannot account for actual statements of scripture. On the first
point, we have not seen any such implication. On the second, we have not found
any texts that are incompatible with identification theory. In fact, our search
for an alternative to John Calvin’s popular penal substitution theory arose
because of texts and concepts that did not fit with substitution language taken
as straightforward explanation. </span></p>
