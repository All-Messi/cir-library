<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.25in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'> I. Methodology</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.25in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>            A. 
 Investigate the activities of the Father, Son, and Holy Spirit (as well as
statements about them) in passages where they are mentioned together, thus
observing interaction in real circumstances.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>            B.  Examples
of relational <u>activities</u> are clearer than abstract <u>statements</u>
about relationship. That procedure minimizes problems created by figurative
language, the various meanings that words like “one” can have, and the
possibility of accommodation to our mode of thinking and being.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>            C.
 Start with passages that talk about the three and then study the texts that
use the word <i>one</i>. The word <i>three</i> is clearer than <i>one</i>
because <i>one </i>can be understood in more than one way.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>            D.
 Make sure to read the material before and after verses that might seem
confusing. Doing so establishes the sense in which unclear statements are
meant.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>      </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'> VI.
Suggested models (<u>objective trinity</u>)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>         
*A.  <u>Ideal marriage</u>: Genesis<b> 1</b>:26-27 + <b>2</b>:24</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify;text-indent:-1.0in'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                          1.   Proposal:
the twoness-in-oneness of marriage in humankind reflects the </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify;text-indent:-1.0in'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        threeness-in-oneness of the
trinity in deity—the image of God in its broad sense.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  2.   Observations
on parallelism in Genesis<b> 1</b>:26-27</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  3.   Effects
of the proposal</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        a.   Captures
both the distinctness and the oneness within the Godhead</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        b.   Maintains
the personal element in deity</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        c.   Maintains

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        d.
  Complementary roles</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        e.   Different
order/rank/levels of “hierarchy”</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        f.    Compenetration</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        g.   Full

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        h.   Common

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        i.    Simultaneous
threeness-in-oneness</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  4.   Caution:
the distinctive fleshly nature people have does not pertain to the parallels because
God as spirit has no consort as often is the case in pagan religions.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>            B.  <u>Ideal
church</u>: John <b>17</b>:11, 20-23 (note<b> 15</b>:10; Ephesians <b>5</b>:22-33)
and the body imagery for the church (Romans <b>12</b>:4-5; 1 Corinthians <b>10</b>:16-17;
<b>12</b>:12-30; Ephesians <b>3</b>:6; <b>4</b>:4, 16-17)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>            C.  Compare
1 John <b>5</b>:8: <i>“There are three that bear witness, the Spirit and the
water and the blood, and the three are one [in agreement].”</i></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>            D.  The
trinity should be understood in terms of persons in relationship: interpersonalism.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  1.
  Interpersonalism captures both distinctness and oneness at the same time, the
three in one total picture.    </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  2.
  Interpersonal trinity shows how both difference and oneness relate to each;
it shows the <u>sense</u> in which they are three and one. The distinctness and
relationship between the Father, Son, and Spirit is an interpersonal one: the
Son <u>prays</u> to the Father and the Spirit is <u>another</u> comforter (John
<b>14</b>:16).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>      </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>            The
trinity is not just any kind of threeness-in-oneness; it is <u>interpersonal</u>
threeness-in-oneness and <u>contemporary</u> threeness-in-oneness. That point
is important because it can account for the somewhat overlapping character of
even personal identity. Who we “are”—in a psychological sense at least—is the
sum of all our aspects of being, action, and <u>relationship</u>. Interpersonal
threeness-in-oneness can also take into account the <i>plural</i>, <i>reciprocal</i>,
<i>transactional</i>,<i> complementary</i>, and <i>compenetrating </i>features
of personal relationship.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>            Other
three-in-one situations are significantly inadequate, because they lack this
interpersonal element even though they do have contemporary
threeness-in-oneness. That is the fundamental problem with emanationism,
tritheism, manifestative/economic trinity. They do not involve the kind or
degree of distinction, on the one hand, or oneness, on the other, that
characterizes interpersonal oneness.  </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>            Speaking
of interpersonal threeness-in-oneness avoids the verbal entanglements
associated with saying “the Father, Son, and Spirit are <u>separate</u>,” “they
are <u>together</u>,” “they are <u>one</u>,” and so on. Such statements have at
least one term that can carry more than one meaning; consequently, people spend
a lot of time sorting through those options to clarify which they mean. The
result is a cacophony of confusion filled with subtle distinctions that leave
the hearer cold. “Trinity” ends up dying a death of a thousand qualifications. Non-interpersonal
models do not get at interpersonal gestalt, and they do not naturally
incorporate complementariness.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>            Texts
like those above in II and III show that <u>during</u> history Father, Son, and
Spirit have interpersonal threeness-in-oneness. <u>Before</u> and <u>after</u>
history they have interpersonal threeness-in-oneness. Strictly speaking, we
cannot show that they have <u>eternal</u> threeness-in-oneness even though that
makes the best sense given what we know about personal and interpersonal
identity. Of course, it cannot be shown either that somehow they become one
person at some time in the future.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>VII.
General approaches</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>      </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>            A. 
Three permanently distinct beings (trinity and tritheism)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>            B. 
Three temporarily distinct beings. The idea that part of God was drawn aside
and incarnated and will ultimately return to the one: 1 Corinthians <b>15</b>:28
is sometimes appealed to (note the <i>all-in-all</i> expression; note also the <i>a-in-b-in-a</i>
idea).           </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>            C. 
Three emanations (Origen)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>            D. 
Three beings with first creating the second as functional deity and creating the
</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  third
as divine energy</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>            E.   Three
manifestations of the same individual</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>VIII.
Alternatives set aside</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>            A.  Monarchianism
(God as a single individual): Isaiah <b>43</b>:11; Deuteronomy <b>6</b>:4</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  1. 
 Modalistic (dynamic; <u>economic trinity</u>)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  2.   Adoptionistic:
Jesus is God’s adopted son.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>            B.  Emanationism:
Origen’s doctrine of the eternal generation of the Son (like a </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  ray
of light continuously generated by the sun)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>            C.  Tritheism
(polytheism) <i>vs</i>. trinity contrasts in the way a man and a woman </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  contrasts
with a husband-and-wife relationship and operation.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>            D.  <u>Manifestative
trinity</u>: the idea is that Father, Son, and Spirit are three roles or</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  manifestations
in which God presents himself. (But see John <b>1</b>:2; <b>17</b>:5.)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                   
<img width=245 height=157 src="/cir-library/images/essays/the-divine-trinity/image002.png"></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:8.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>            E.   Other
models</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  1.   Three
cookies from the same dough</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  2.   Three
points of one iceberg</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  3.   Three
corners of one triangle</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  4.   Three
states of one chemical: gas, solid, liquid</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  5.   Three
members of one committee</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  6.
  Three functions of one mind: memory, reason, will</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  7.   Three
relationships by one person: one person who is a son, a father, and a </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        brother</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  8.   The
white, yolk, and shell of one egg (cp. cherry pie: crust, cherries, and </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        thickening)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-left:.25in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
-.25in'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>            F.   The supposition that
deity does not conform to the categories of finite intelligence (<i>e.g</i>.,
the idea that deity is such that “opposites” may both be true)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-left:.25in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            The trinity is not just any kind of
threeness-in-oneness; it is <u>interpersonal</u> threeness-in-oneness and <u>contemporary</u>
threeness-in-oneness. That point is important because it can account for the
somewhat overlapping character even of separate personal identities. In a
psychological sense at least, who we “are” is the sum of all our aspects of
being, action, and <u>relationship</u>. Interpersonal threeness-in-oneness can
also account for the <i>plural</i>, <i>reciprocal</i>, <i>transactional</i>,<i>
complementary</i>, and <i>compenetrating </i>features of personal relationship.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-left:.25in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            Other three-in-one situations are
significantly inadequate, because they lack that interpersonal element even
though they do have contemporary threeness-in-oneness. That is the fundamental
problem with emanationism, tritheism, manifestative/economic trinity. They do
not involve the kind or degree of distinction, on the one hand, or oneness, on
the other, that characterizes interpersonal oneness.  </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>IX.
Passages raised as difficulties for the above view</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>            The
Gospel of John has the most Christological material of any gospel. Revelation,
Hebrews, and Philippians also have “high Christology.”</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>            The
texts that have caused the most misunderstanding are in John’s gospel. Interpreters
often take them as disallowing distinctness of persons between Father and Son;
yet <b>14</b>:16 is the clearest text in the whole Bible on the distinctness
between Father, Son, and Spirit.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>            A.  John
<b>10</b>:30: <i>“I and the Father are at one”</i></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  The
statement means “same” in kind and purpose; we are <i>“at one”</i> (note: <b>10</b>:31-39).
The Son equals the father in that he does the Father’s works, is in the Father
and <i>vice versa</i>; and the Father sanctified and sent him (cp. 1 John <b>5</b>:8).
The following verse brings up the Son of God, which involves distinction from
the Father.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>            B.  John<b>
14</b>:7-10;<b> 15</b>:24: <i>“Seeing <u>me</u> is seeing the <u>Father</u>.”</i></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  The
preceding and succeeding contexts mention all three (<b>14</b>:7, 9). <i>“The
one that has seen me has seen the One who sent me.”</i> There must be
distinction between sender and sent, yet he says seeing the sent is seeing the
sender. So, the expression is a way of saying, <i>“. . . is as good as.”</i> Sending
the Son was for revealing/declaring the Father (cp. John <b>1</b>:18). In
seeing what the Son is like, we see what the Father is like inasfar as deity
can be manifested in the material realm. Perhaps the passage is best read with
an emphasis on <u>me</u> rather than on <u>seen</u>: <i>“Have I been so long
time with you, and you have not seen <u>me</u>? If you have seen <u>me,</u> you
have seen the <u>Father</u>”</i>;<i> </i>that is,<i> “If you have seen <u>me</u>,
you have seen [what the Father is like].”</i> The reader should also note that
in <b>15</b>:23 Jesus says, <i>“Hating me is hating the Father <u>too</u>,”</i>
which implies distinction between them.  </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  Should
we make any connection between “seeing God” in this context and Jesus’ comment
in Matthew <b>5</b>:8 that the pure in heart will see God? John <b>12</b>:44-45
is interesting in connection with the present topic: <i>“He that believes on me
<u>believes</u> not [only] on me but on him that sent me. And he that beholds
me beholds him that sent me.”</i></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  When
you (or Moses) ask to see the Father, you really want to know what the Father
looks like. If you have seen Christ, you have seen what he looks like. They
look just alike<i>. “Like Father; like Son.” </i>The Son “looks just like” the
Father. Compare also the English expression, <i>“If you have seen one, you have
seen them all.”</i> What Christ is like is the closest thing people can have of
seeing the Father, because in his own nature no one has seen or “can see” God (1
Timothy<b> 6</b>:16).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>            C.  John
<b>14</b>:12 + 18; <b>14</b>:28; <i>“I’m going away . . . and I’m coming to
you,”</i> taken to mean the coming of the Spirit is the next form (spiritual)
of the Son (physical).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>       
    Compare <b>14</b>:3 with <b>14</b>:18:   a.   =  the Son’s second coming,
not the Spirit’s </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                                                   
coming.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                               
 or</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                                       
b.  =  the Spirit’s representative coming for the Son</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                               
 or</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                                     
 *c. = the Spirit’s representative coming is the proleptic presence (earnest)
till the Son’s second coming. He comes representatively through the Spirit (on
the day of Pentecost) until he comes personally in the end (parousia).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  The
Father will send the Spirit in Christ’s name (<b>14</b>:26).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>            D.  John<b>
14</b>:10, 11, 20: <i>“The Father is in the Son, and the Son is in the Father.”</i></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  Note
<b>10</b>:28; <b>14</b>:20; <b>17</b>:21-24.  </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  A-in-b-in-a
means close identification (via love?), not identicalness. There is mutuality
and reciprocity. In a rougher analogy, interpersonal influence and love are
like interpenetrating gases blown into the same container.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>            E.   Deuteronomy
<b>6</b>:4: <i>“The Lord our God is one Lord.”</i></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  1.   It
must mean “solicity” (uniqueness), not singleness. Singleness would not afford
a reason to love him; a person may love God whether God is single or triune. “God
is not a unit but a union/unity.”</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  2.   “One”:
see Ezekiel<b> 7</b>:5; Job <b>23</b>:13; cp.  </span><span lang=HE dir=RTL
style='font-size:14.0pt'>בֶּן יָחִיד</span><span dir=LTR></span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'><span dir=LTR></span>; (“only son”; Genesis </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        <b>22</b>:16)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>            F.   Isaiah
<b>43</b>:11: <i>“I, even I, am Yahveh; and there is no savior besides me.”</i></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>            It
is easier to understand passages about oneness in light of passages about prayer,
sending, intercession, and so on, than it is somehow to understand these last activities
as instances of singularity.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'> X. General
Observations</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>            A.  The
trinity is not a mathematical absurdity; <i>they</i> are not three in the same
sense as <i>they</i> are one. The threeness and the oneness are not at the same
level; the threeness is within the oneness.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  1.   <u>Three</u>
as to individual personalities</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  2.   <u>One</u></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><u><span style='font-size:12.0pt'><span
 style='text-decoration:none'>&nbsp;</span></span></u></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        a.   Uniqueness:
from all others proposed as gods (<u>kind</u> in contrast within </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              a
category)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        b.   Commonality:
of the same nature and purpose (kind in comparison within a category). In
Galatians <b>3</b>:28 Paul says there is no bond or free, male or female; but
all are one in Christ<i>. One</i> here does not mean “united” or “the same one,”
but everyone is viewed as the <u>same</u> (kind) in Christ.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        c.   “Loneness”:
only Yahveh is in the category of deity: he is the “only </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              one.”</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        d.   “Corporateness”:
a whole is bigger than the sum of its parts (unity within the category). His
prayer in John<b> 17</b> could include oneness in a corporate sense, or  </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        e.   “Unicity”:
complementariness; they complement each other’s work; or</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        f.    “Unity”:
Jesus’ prayer in John <b>17</b> records Jesus’ prayer that his disciples would
be one as he and the Father are one. In such a context, “one” can mean “united.” 
They are “at one.”</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>            B.  Most
models neglect the unity at the expense of the distinctness and <i>vice versa</i>.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>            C.  Understanding
of the trinity is through divinely chosen models.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  The
reason for sticking with divinely chosen models is that if we do not do so, we
may construct something that does not parallel the reality at crucial points. The
idea of a son who is later a father who is later a grandfather is a model that
gets three and one, but it fails to have them at the <u>same</u> time, the mark
of Father-Son-Spirit in John <b>14</b>:16 and other places.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>            D.  Knowledge
of the trinity is partial.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  Within
the trinity there is distinction during time and before time. The oneness of a
bucket of water (before time) from which a cup of water is later taken (during
time) does not explain John <b>10</b>:30, where Jesus says that he and the
Father are one and says it during the incarnation, when supposedly a
monarchianist must admit there was separateness. That same thing is said of
their relationship before time. Consequently, the question becomes one of where
we get the idea that relationship was any different <u>before</u> time from
what it was <u>in</u> time. Likewise, we have to ask why there is some problem with
their being distinct <u>before</u> as they were <u>in</u> time.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'> XI.
Reasons for Interest in the Trinity Discussion </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>            A.  Desire
to understand what God has revealed to us about himself</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>            B.  Missions
to Jews and Muslims: We must understand the issue sufficiently to articulate it
to non-Christians and remove unnecessary stumbling blocks to their accepting the
Christian message.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>            C. 
 Christian unity: some groups within Christendom as well as individuals in more
“mainline” movements object to the traditional formulation of trinitarianism. We
have to decide whether the alternatives they adopt are sufficiently
objectionable to warrant separation, division, and withdrawal of fellowship. We
may find it possible to maintain fellowship to the extent of agreement while
not putting them in leadership positions</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='text-align:center'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>Concluding Summaries</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='text-align:center'><b><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></b></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>            The
trinity involves an <u>interpersonal</u> threeness-in-oneness. The distinctness
between the Father, Son, and Holy Spirit takes place within their larger
oneness. That threeness-in-oneness is at the same time rather than at different
times.</span></p>
