<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>            Treating other people the
way we want to be treated implies the fundamental continuity of humankind. Variations
between people do exist, but they do not normally deal with matters that
impinge on ethical considerations. Supposing the continuity of human nature as
the background for the Golden Rule is preferable to supposing that the Golden
Rule describes a non-content procedure, the approach of R. M. McIver, “The Deep
Beauty of the Golden Rule” in <i>Philosophy and Contemporary Issues</i>, pp.
233-39.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>            The principle touches on the
reason for doing good in return for evil because that circumstance necessarily
arises if we are ever to break the downward spiral of deteriorating
relationships. It also touches on the reason they are often bad: they do not
know experientially a more excellent way. They will not experience a preferable
way of acting unless we do not return their evil in kind. Living ethically calls
for experiencing a better behavior to know that it is good because it brings a more
fulfilling life.</span></p>
