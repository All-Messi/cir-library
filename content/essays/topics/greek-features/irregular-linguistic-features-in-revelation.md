<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:center'><b><span
style='font-size:14.0pt'>IRREGULAR LINGUISTIC FEATURES IN REVELATION:</span></b></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><b><span
style='font-size:14.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></b></p>

<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:center'><b><span
style='font-size:14.0pt'>INTERACTING WITH R. H. CHARLES</span></b></p>

<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;
margin-bottom:0in;margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:center'><b><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></b></p>

<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:center'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>Virgil Warren, PhD</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
.5in'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>Three possibilities can explain
irregularities: they were intentional, they were due to ignorance, or they were
unintentional mistakes. R. H. Charles wrote the two-volume commentary on
Revelation in the <i>International Critical Commentary</i>. It presented an
extended investigation of linguistic peculiarities in the Book of Revelation. He
concluded that the writer was a Jew thinking in Hebrew and writing in Greek,
which he did not know very well. Other writers, particularly Henry Cadbury,
have challenged that conclusion, saying that the linguistic irregularities are
deliberate. The issue is of some importance because it relates to the
authorship issue for the Apocalypse. If the anomalies were deliberate, it is
not as necessary to posit a different author for Revelation <i>vs</i>. the Gospel
of John and the three Epistles of John, which do not have such grammatical
features. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
.5in'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>In responding to Charles' contention,
several authors have argued that all the peculiarities he notes appear in
non-literary papyri and the like. They infer that it is not necessary to appeal
to Hebrew grammatical habits to explain the phenomena in Revelation. The amount
of these peculiarities and the nature of some of them, however, do look away
from an explanation that depends on analogies from Greek backgrounds. Furthermore,
some phenomena are not objectionable Greek <i>per se</i>, but they are minority
features that predominate in this book. The issue is not whether the style is
Semitic, because it is, not only in its abnormalities but in other ways. The
issue is <u>why</u> it has this Semitic coloring. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;
margin-bottom:0in;margin-left:4.5pt;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:center'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>A Summary of Linguistic Peculiarities According to R.
H. Charles</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:4.5pt;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:4.5pt;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify;text-indent:31.5pt'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>1. Occasionally words are in a different <u>gender</u>
from the words they modify. For example, </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:4.5pt;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify;text-indent:31.5pt'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>2. Some Greek words in Revelation are assigned <u>secondary

word: </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>οἱ</span><span lang=EL
style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>πόδες</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>αὐτοῦ</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ὡς</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>στῦλοι</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>πυρός</span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'> (<b>10</b>:1). Here Charles thinks that </span><span

means “legs” because </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>στῦλοι</span><span

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:4.5pt;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify;text-indent:31.5pt'><span

lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ὢν</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>καὶ</span><span

lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ὁ</span><span

lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>παντοκράτωρ</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>is a

style='font-size:14.0pt'>יהוה</span><span lang=HE dir=RTL style='font-size:

lang=HE dir=RTL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=HE dir=RTL

style='font-size:12.0pt'>, </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ὁ</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ὢν</span><span

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
.5in'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>4. In its context </span><span lang=EL
style='font-size:12.0pt'>βάλλω</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>εἰς</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
.5in'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>5. Grammatical constructions are sometimes

lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>καὶ</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>
</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ποιήσῇ</span><span

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:4.5pt;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify;text-indent:31.5pt'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>6. Instances of <u>tense</u> usage represent the

lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>δώσουσιν</span><span

style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>(<b>4</b>:9-10).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:4.5pt;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify;text-indent:31.5pt'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>7. The <u>pleonastic use of the pronoun</u> occurs

Nominativus pendens is also used in Revelation: </span><span lang=EL
style='font-size:12.0pt'>ὁ</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>νικῶν</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:4.5pt;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify;text-indent:31.5pt'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>8. A noun or participial phrase dependent on a

lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ἀπὸ</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>

lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>μάρτυς</span><span

<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:center'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>Gender</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
.5in'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>There are nine words that sometimes
occasion a lack of concord in gender: </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:

style='font-size:12.0pt'>θηρίον</span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>, </span><span

lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ἀρνίον</span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>, </span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>κτίσμα</span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>, </span><span

</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
.5in'><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>Φωνή</span><span lang=EL
style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>(“voice, sound”)

In <b>5</b>:11-12 the text says, </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>καὶ</span><span

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;text-indent:

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;

lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>στῦλοι</span><span

lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>σοι</span><span

lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>αὐτούς</span><span

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:4.5pt;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify;text-indent:31.5pt'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>Charles comments on the possibility of borrowing the
wording from the LXX:</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-left:.5in;text-align:justify;text-indent:-.5in'><span

dependent upon it”</i> (p. 76)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:4.5pt;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:4.5pt;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>If he did not know Greek very well, he depended to an

notes that the root of </span><span lang=HE dir=RTL style='font-size:14.0pt'>תרעם</span><span
dir=LTR></span><span lang=HE style='font-size:12.0pt'><span dir=LTR></span> </span><span

lang=HE style='font-size:12.0pt'><span dir=LTR></span> </span><span

lang=HE dir=RTL style='font-size:14.0pt'>תִּרְעֵם</span><span dir=LTR></span><span

as does Symmachus and pointed it </span><span lang=HE dir=RTL style='font-size:

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:4.5pt;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify;text-indent:31.5pt'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>The author's independent translation of the Hebrew
need not be an independent translation. It could be a paraphrase of one already
existing. We cannot tell the difference. Whether he depended on the LXX is
incidental. If he was familiar with the Septuagint, he may have used it. Theoretically,
he could have used it even to the point of doing injustice to the Hebrew; after
all, according to Charles he was “unfamiliar with the fine shades of meaning in
the Greek words.” </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:4.5pt;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify;text-indent:31.5pt'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>Charles lists some examples where </span><span
lang=HE dir=RTL style='font-size:14.0pt'>רעה</span><span dir=LTR></span><span

lang=HE dir=RTL style='font-size:14.0pt'>תרעם</span><span dir=LTR></span><span

of the Apocalypse could have used the verse mainly as a note to his
Christianized, Hellenistic readers to recall Psalm<b> 2</b>:8-9, the meaning of
which had been previously qualified. Yet perhaps in all this, the apparent
parallelism is not even intended.</span></p>

12.0pt'><span dir=LTR></span>—a</span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>t least in
a sense that implies illiteracy in the Greek language. It may be noted that in

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;

the case of </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ποιμαίνειν</span><span

<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:center'><span

<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:center'><span

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
.5in'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>The oft-recurring phrase </span><span
lang=HE dir=RTL style='font-size:14.0pt'>יהוה האלהי צבאות</span><span dir=LTR></span><span
lang=HE style='font-size:12.0pt'><span dir=LTR></span> </span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>is frequently translated by the LXX as </span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>κύριος</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:
12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ὁ</span><span lang=EL
style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>θεὸς</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>παντοκράτωρ</span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>. Revelation <b>1</b>:8 uses the phrase </span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>κύριος</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:
12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ὁ</span><span lang=EL
style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>θεὸς</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ὁ</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ὢν</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>καὶ</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ὁ</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ἦν</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>καὶ</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ὁ</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ἐρχόμενος</span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>, </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ὁ</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>πρωτότοκος</span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>. Charles objects to the presence of </span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ὁ</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>
</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ὢν</span><span lang=EL
style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>καἰ</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ὁ</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ἦν</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>καἰ</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ὁ</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ἐρχόμενος</span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'> between </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ὁ</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>θεός</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>and </span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ὁ</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>
</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>παντοκράτωρ</span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'> because </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ὁ</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>κύριος</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ὁ</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>θεός</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ὁ</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>παντοκράτωρ</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>is a
set phrase in the LXX for translating </span><span lang=HE dir=RTL
style='font-size:14.0pt'>יהוה האלהי צבאות</span><span dir=LTR></span><span
lang=HE style='font-size:12.0pt'><span dir=LTR></span> </span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>(cp. <b>19</b>:6).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'> Hebrew has very few adjectives. To express the
adjectival concept another noun is frequently used with the one being modified.
“Godly men” is expressed in Hebrew as “men of God” (</span><span lang=HE
dir=RTL style='font-size:14.0pt'>אנשי</span><span lang=HE dir=RTL
style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=HE dir=RTL style='font-size:14.0pt'>אלהים</span><span
dir=LTR></span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'><span dir=LTR></span>). The
adjective “godly” is replaced by its cognate noun while “men” is put in the “construct
state,” a state of relationship with God. This changes “men” to “men of&quot; (</span><span
lang=HE dir=RTL style='font-size:14.0pt'>אֲנְשִׁים</span><span dir=LTR></span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'><span dir=LTR></span>) to (</span><span lang=HE
dir=RTL style='font-size:14.0pt'>אַנְשֵׁי</span><span dir=LTR></span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'><span dir=LTR></span>). Thus “men of” cannot stand
alone but must appear with another noun to express a complete thought. In </span><span
lang=HE dir=RTL style='font-size:14.0pt'>יהוה צבאות</span><span dir=LTR></span><span
lang=HE style='font-size:12.0pt'><span dir=LTR></span> </span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>(“Lord-of hosts”), </span><span lang=HE dir=RTL
style='font-size:14.0pt'>יהוה</span><span dir=LTR></span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'><span dir=LTR></span> is in the construct state
awaiting to complete its meaning; so it cannot stand alone. Since </span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ὁ</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>
</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>κύριος</span><span lang=EL
style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ὁ</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>παντοκράτωρ</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>is used
to translate </span><span lang=HE dir=RTL style='font-size:14.0pt'>יהוה צבאות</span><span
dir=LTR></span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'><span dir=LTR></span>, </span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ὁ</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>
</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>κύριος</span><span lang=EL
style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>represents the
construct state of the tetragrammaton, which must immediately lean on </span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>παντοκράτωρ</span><span lang=EL
style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>to complete its
meaning. Therefore, Charles declares that an ignorant scribe, not realizing
this, interpolated </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ὁ</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ὢν</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>καὶ</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ὁ</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ἦν</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>καί</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ὁ</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ἐρχόμενος</span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'> between the two nouns. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
.5in'><span lang=HE dir=RTL style='font-size:14.0pt'>צבאות</span><span dir=LTR></span><span
lang=HE style='font-size:12.0pt'><span dir=LTR></span> </span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>is the plural of </span><span lang=HE dir=RTL
style='font-size:14.0pt'>צבא</span><span dir=LTR></span><span style='font-size:

lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>Παντοκράτωρ</span><span lang=EL
style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>is a compound

lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>παντοκράτωρ</span><span lang=EL

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
.5in'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>To consider </span><span lang=EL
style='font-size:12.0pt'>ὁ</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>κύριος</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:

style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>παντοκράτωρ</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>a

dir=LTR></span> makes </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>παντοκράτωρ</span><span

But it is declined each time with the same case as </span><span lang=EL
style='font-size:12.0pt'>κύριος</span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'> (1 Kings <b>19</b>:10,
14). If it were a noun, Greek would put it in the genitive case, because “Lord-of

second noun to a genitive. Since </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>παντοκράτωρ</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>is an
adjective, there is no reason why John could not break precedent, use it in its

phrase stressing a third aspect of God's person—his eternality. The two
concepts together present the most important theme of the apocalypse: God, not
Satan, will be in control of the future—</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:
12.0pt'>παντοκράτωρ</span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>, and it will always
be so— </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ὁ</span><span lang=EL
style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ὢν</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>καἰ</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ὁ</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ἦν</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>καἰ</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ὁ</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ἐρχόμενος</span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                         </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
.5in'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>A final consideration applies to this
example. The text history of this passage in Revelation shows no sign of
scribal tampering in the way Charles supposes. The fact, then, that the “inserted”
element appears as it does can argue that the writer was not closely tied to
the Hebrew equivalent. He was at least facile enough in Greek that he was not
woodenly substituting Greek words in Hebrew syntax.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
.5in'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>As part of his effort to show that the
writer of Revelation was thinking in Hebrew while writing in Greek, Charles
gives a list of Hebrew idioms literally translated into Greek. He contends that
their proper meaning is apparent only when translated back into Hebrew.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
.5in'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>Revelation<b> 1</b>:16 says, </span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>καὶ</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>
</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ἡ</span><span lang=EL
style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ὄψις</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>αὐτοῦ</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ὡς</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ὁ</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ἥλιος</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>φαὶνει</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ἐν</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>τῇ</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>δυνάμει</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>αὐτοῦ</span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>. The Hebrew would read, </span><span lang=HE dir=RTL
style='font-size:14.0pt'>ופניוכשמש יאחר בגדורתן</span><span dir=LTR></span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'><span dir=LTR></span>, meaning <i>“and his face (was)
like the sun shining in its strength.”</i> The Hebrew would typically use a
finite verb to express the last verbal idea that sequences with the participles
earlier in the verse. Greek idiom would equal the English: </span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>καὶ</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>
</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ἡ</span><span lang=EL

<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;

style='font-size:12.0pt'>Closing Observations</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;
margin-bottom:0in;margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:center'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            Charles’ approach does not allow
sufficiently for originality or digression from precedence without being
considered ignorant. His overall criticism assumes too much. That is especially
true in exegesis; in his interpretation of the book, he first seems to decide
on a meaning and then align the text and writer to it.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            His first proposition that the grammar of
Revelation is unique calls for a few observations. Two things in its uniqueness
are the <i>“unheard of expressions”</i> and the solecisms. Other writers have noted
that some solecisms are not necessarily solecisms, and that the unique
expressions were intentional.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            Some of his irregularities are warnings
against literalism or to illustrate the figurative nature of the imagery. What
is said to be Hebrew appeared in Greek writings even other than the papyri.
That is true of the LXX in the exact places where the writer is supposed to be
quoting. At least one of Charles’ interpretations is unwarranted—the
interpretation of </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>πρωτότοκος</span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            With the foundation of his observations
made questionable, the second preposition, which rests on the first, becomes
questionable: the writer thinks in Hebrew while writing in Greek or is ignorant
of certain sections of the Greek language. In fact, at least one major
classification of irregularities cannot be accounted for by Hebrew: unusual
uses of gender. The overwhelming proper usages demonstrate that in every case
discussed above, the author knew the “proper” construction. In all the mentions
of the LXX, there is not to be the other extreme: that the author did not know
Hebrew, because he evidently uses it as long as it does no injustice to the
Hebrew because of the <u>readers’</u> familiarity with that translation. So the
peculiarities, as far as they have been studied here, are accounted for by
intention plus the use of the Septuagint.</span></p>
