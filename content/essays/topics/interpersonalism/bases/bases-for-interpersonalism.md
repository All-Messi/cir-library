<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:center'><b><span
style='font-size:14.0pt'>BASES FOR INTERPERSONALISM</span></b></p>

<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:center;
text-indent:4.5pt'><b><span style='font-size:10.0pt;font-family:"Arial",sans-serif'>&nbsp;</span></b></p>

<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:center'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>Virgil Warren, PhD</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:center;
text-indent:4.5pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:center;
text-indent:4.5pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'> I. What Is Most Original (the [inter]personal
character of ultimate reality)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
4.5pt;line-height:18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  A.  God created the
universe out of nothing.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        God’s
creatorship means that the material, inorganic, impersonal universe is
secondary to the (inter)personal creator. God <u>spoke</u> the universe into
existence (Genesis <b>1</b>:3, 6, 9, 11, 14, 20, 24, 26, 29; Hebrews <b>11</b>:3;
2 Peter <b>3</b>:5). Its origin came from an act of the <u>will</u> (Acts <b>17</b>:22-31),
not from determinism through physical cause-effect. It was created (John <b>1</b>:1-3;
Colossians <b>1</b>:14-20), not out of God, but by him from nothing else.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  B.  The Creator
possesses interpersonal characteristics.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        Trinitarianism
places interpersonalism in the ultimate frame of reference and before the
material universe existed. John <b>14</b>:16 shows that Son and Father differ
sufficiently for the Son to pray to the Father. The Father and the Spirit
differ sufficiently for the Father to send the Spirit. The Spirit and the Son
differ sufficiently for the Spirit to be “another.” God is interpersonal, not
just in bent, but in objective nature. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>II. What Is Most Central (<i>vs</i>. what
is secondary, foreign, and contradictory to Christian understanding)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
4.5pt;line-height:18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  A.  The First and
Second Great Commandments are about love. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        Making love both
the First and Second Great Commandments accentuates the interpersonal center of
biblical religion. Love is central, not just to Christianity, but also to
Mosaism because the first two commandments—vertical and horizontal love—provide
the context that the rest of the Mosaic Law stands within. The <u>First and
Second Great Commandments</u> and the <u>Golden Rule</u> (which restates the
Second Great Commandment) fulfill the Law and the prophets (Matthew<b> 22</b>:34-40;
Romans <b>13</b>:8-10; Galatians <b>5</b>:13-15; Matthew <b>7</b>:11). James
connects the <u>Royal Law</u> with the same context where he says, <i>“Pure and
undefiled religion . . . is to visit the fatherless and widows in their
affliction and to keep oneself unspotted from the world”</i> (<b>2</b>:8-9 + <b>1</b>:27;
<b>2</b>:14-16). Paul says, <i>“The end of the charge is <u>love</u> out of a
pure heart and a good <u>conscience</u> with <u>faith</u> unfeigned”</i> (1
Timothy<b> 1</b>:5).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  B.  Matters less
central than interpersonal concerns</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:0in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify;text-indent:67.5pt;
line-height:18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>1.<span style='font:7.0pt "Times New Roman"'>&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;
</span></span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>Love is more central than <u>supernatural
spiritual gift</u>s (1 Corinthians <b>13</b>:13; <b>14</b>:5; Luke <b>10</b>:17).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              Paul used
love as the middle chapter in his three-chapter corrective on misusing spiritual
gifts. By going back to basics, he put the charismata in interpersonal
perspective. Operating through love, relationships set the manner and purpose
of using the gifts (1 Corinthians <b>14</b>:5). By contextualizing them in
love, the apostle sought to eradicate excesses that had arisen in Corinth. The
practitioners were using gifts in self-centered ways to gain attention. They
needed to become outward-directed, as love requires people to be.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        2. Salvation is
more important than the <u>ability to perform exorcisms</u> (Luke <b>10</b>:17).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              Jesus told
those returning from the Mission of the Seventy to rejoice, not so much that
demons were subject to them, but that their names were written in heaven. <i>“Written
in heaven”</i> means saved, which refers to being in fellowship with God.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        3.  Proclamation
of the message is more important than <u>who performs baptisms</u> (1 Corinthians<b>
1</b>:17).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              Another
interpretation of Paul’s point might be the relative centrality of the message
over formal observances involved in it. Either way, interpersonal proclamation
is more basic or more central.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
.25in;line-height:18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  4.   What
we eat does not commend us to God (1 Corinthians<b> 8</b>:8; Mark <b>7</b>:19;
Romans<b> 2</b>:4, 6; <b>14</b>:17; Colossians <b>2</b>:16-17; Hebrews <b>9</b>:8-10).
The whole category of ceremonial uncleanness/taboo/defilement has become
immaterial (Romans<b> 14</b>:14, 20). As respects conscience, they cannot give a
sense of moral purity (Hebrews<b> 9</b>:8-10). Life is more than food and
clothes (Matthew <b>6</b>:25; Luke <b>12</b>:23).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
.25in;line-height:18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  5.   Christianity
set aside the Jewish <u>festival cycle</u> (Colossians <b>2</b>:16-17).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-indent:.25in;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  6.   The New Testament
contains many statements about the gospel and the Christian experience in
personalized terms. It speaks of </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              a.   <u>preaching
Christ</u>: Acts <b>8</b>:35; <b>11</b>:20; <b>17</b>:18; Romans <b>16</b>:25; 1
Corinthians <b>1</b>:23; 2 Corinthians <b>1</b>:19; <b>4</b>:5; Philippians<b>
1</b>:15-18 (cp. Acts <b>5</b>:42); yet note “preaching Moses” in Acts <b>15</b>:2;
hence, some of this may reflect Hebraism.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              b.   <u>receiving
Christ</u>: Colossians <b>2</b>:6</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
-.25in;line-height:18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                    c.
 <u>earning Christ</u>: Ephesians <b>4</b>:20</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
-.25in;line-height:18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                    d.
 <u>putting on Christ</u>: Romans<b> 13</b>:12-14 </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
-.25in;line-height:18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                    e.
 <u>knowing nothing but Christ</u>: 1 Corinthians<b> 2</b>:2 </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
-.25in;line-height:18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                    f.
 <u>Christ as the foundation</u>: 1 Corinthians<b> 3</b>:10-15</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
-.25in;line-height:18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                    g.
 <u>Christ as our peace</u>: Ephesians <b>2</b>:14</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
-.25in;line-height:18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                    h.
 <u>presenting Christ</u>: Galatians <b>3</b>:1</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
-.25in;line-height:18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                    i.
 <u>Christ as the wisdom, righteousness, sanctification, and redemption of </u></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'> <u>God</u>: 1 Corinthians <b>1</b>:30</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              j. <u>in/into
the name of Christ</u>: Acts <b>10</b>:43; Colossians<b> 1</b>:27 (<i>“Christ
in you the hope of glory”</i>); Matthew<b> 28</b>:20; and so on (being <i>“baptized
into Christ”</i>)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              k. <u>the
indwelling of Christ</u>: Colossians<b> 1</b>:27</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              l. <u>judgment
in terms of Christ</u>: Acts <b>17</b>:30-31 (<i>“God has appointed a day when
he will judge the world in righteousness <u>in</u></i> [</span><span lang=EL
style='font-size:12.0pt'>ἐν</span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>] <i>that man
whom he has appointed.”</i>)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              m. <u>the
church is called <i>“Christ”</i></u>: 1 Corinthians<b> 12</b>:12 (<i>“As the
body is one with many members, so is Christ.”</i>)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
-.25in;line-height:18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                    n<i>.
“<u>For me to live is Christ</u>”</i>: Philippians <b>1</b>:21 </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
-.25in;line-height:18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                    o.
<u>the “I am” statements of Jesus</u>: <i>“<u>I</u> am the way, the truth, and
the life”</i> (John <b>14</b>:6; cp. Romans <b>5</b>:2); <i>“<u>I</u> am the
light of the world” </i>(John<b> 8</b>:12; <b>9</b>:5; <b>12</b>:46); <i>“<u>I</u>
am the door”</i> (John <b>10</b>:7, 9); <i>“<u>I</u> am the good shepherd”</i>
(John <b>10</b>:11); <i>“<u>I</u> am the bread of life”</i> (John <b>6</b>:35).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
-.25in;line-height:18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        In keeping with
that emphasis, it has been customary to say things like “no creed but Christ.” Christianity
is not a set of doctrines to be believed but a relationship of persons to be
lived. It is not a creed, but a <i>“being in Christ.”</i> The name for the
believers is not derived from a form of government or the form of an ordinance,
but from the person of Jesus the Messiah—Christians. The idea is to put Christ
back into <u>Christ</u>ian.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        Other terms like
“life,” “truth,” “knowledge” often have an interpersonal thrust, as in the
statement <i>“You will <u>know</u> the <u>truth</u> and the truth will make you
<u>free</u>.”</i> Knowledge is experiential, truth is personal, and freedom is
relational; that all makes especially fine sense in an interpersonal setting.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  C.  Matters considered
non-germane to Christian concerns </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        1.   Dietary
regulations</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              In several
passages, Paul sets aside vegetarianism <i>vs.</i> eating meat: Romans <b>14</b>:2-3,
6b, 17, 20-21, 23; 1 Corinthians <b>8</b>:8. The guiding principle is that <i>“.
. . the kingdom of God is not eating and drinking, but righteousness and peace
and joy in the Holy Spirit”</i> (Romans <b>14</b>:17). In 1 Corinthians <b>8</b>:8
Paul comments, <i>“Food will not commend us to God: neither if we do not eat
are we the worse, nor if we eat are we the better.”</i> Our hearts are established
by grace (interpersonal) rather than meats (<u>dietary</u> <u>regulations</u>;
Hebrews <b>13</b>:9). Eating one thing rather than another has no relevance to
relationships. The only time food becomes a concern is when it might cause a
weaker brother to stumble, that is, when it might affect relationships. Misbehavior
affects relationships (a) by destroying a person’s ability to operate
interpersonally (as in substance abuse), (b) by causing a weaker brother to
stumble because of one’s misleading example, and (c) by acting in ways
unbefitting the relationship, including amoral actions not acceptable to the
person’s preferences or culture. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
-.25in;line-height:18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              2.
Physical appearance</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
-.25in;line-height:18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                    In
choosing a king to replace the Saulite dynasty, God told Samuel not to look at
countenance or stature because <i>“Yahveh does not see like people see; people
look at the outward appearance, but Yahveh looks at the heart”</i> (1 Samuel <b>16</b>:7),
a particularly interesting comment since Saul was head and shoulders taller
than the other people (1 Samuel<b> 9</b>:2).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        3.   Material
wealth</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
-.25in;line-height:18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                    When
John the Baptist sent messengers to ask Jesus whether he was the Messiah, the
Lord marked his Messiahship by saying that even the poor have the gospel
preached to them (Matthew <b>11</b>:5). </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        4.   Special
days (Romans <b>14</b>:5-6a; Colossians <b>2</b>:16-17)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        5.   Marital
status</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
-.25in;line-height:18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                    Whether
a person is married is immaterial to the Christian calling (1 Corinthians<b> 7</b>:17,
20, 24, 26-40). Marriage has interpersonal character, in fact, the most intense
such relationship; but it is not a required state because other states of
personal relationship exist. There is neither male nor female in Christ
(Galatians <b>3</b>:28).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
-.25in;line-height:18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              6.
  Societal status </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              Whether
bond or free is immaterial to a Christian’s worth (1 Corinthians <b>7</b>:17,
20-24). There is neither bond nor free in Christ (Galatians <b>3</b>:28). Christianity
is not an elitist religion.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
-.25in;line-height:18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              7.
  National identity (Galatians <b>3</b>:28; Matthew<b> 28</b>:19-20)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              There is
neither Jew nor Gentile, barbarian nor Scythian in Christ. Neither circumcision
nor uncircumcision means anything, but keeping God’s commandments (1
Corinthians <b>7</b>:17, 19-20). In contrast to faith, love, and a good
conscience, some heretics concerned themselves with myths and genealogies (1
Timothy <b>1</b>:35). </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
-.25in;line-height:18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              8.
  It is not what we know or say (1 Corinthians <b>4</b>:20) or believe (James <b>2</b>),
but what we do and the power we have in our lives. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
-.25in;line-height:18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  The antidote to all
these irrelevant distinctions is that we are all one in Christ.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
-.25in;line-height:18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  D.  Observations on
the patriarchal and Mosaic periods</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        Not all things
equally true are equally important. The Law was true down to the last jot and
tittle (Matthew <b>5</b>:17-20); yet there were weightier matters among features
of the Law (Matthew <b>23</b>:23-24).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
-.25in;line-height:18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        1.   <u>Animal
sacrifices</u> were not so important as obedience, kindness, and humility, which
are interpersonal traits (Micah <b>6</b>:6-8; Hosea <b>6</b>:6; Deuteronomy<b>
10</b>:12-13; 1 Samuel <b>15</b>:22; Jeremiah <b>7</b>:22-23; cp. Psalm <b>40</b>:6-9).
Performing ritual sacrifice of animals, libations of oil, or the sacrifice of
children has no value in correcting personal relations: <i>“Will I give the
fruit of my body for the sin of my soul?”</i> (Micah <b>6</b>:1). Animal
sacrifice is artificial to interpersonal problems—except as expressions of the
heart and attitude of the worshiper. The provision does not correspond to the
need. Micah<b> 6</b>:8 shows that interpersonal problems call for interpersonal
solutions: doing justly, loving kindness, and walking humbly with God. That
formula held sway even during the Mosaic Law because the interpersonal precedes
the legal both in logic and time (patriarchal period). </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                               Other passages
indicate that animal sacrifice, though divinely instituted, was secondary and
ineffective: Hosea <b>6</b>:6 cited by Jesus in Matthew<b> 9</b>:13;<b> 12</b>:7;
compare Mark <b>12</b>:32-33. Jesus said to leave the gift beside the altar and
go work out differences with other people before offering gifts (Matthew<b> 6</b>:23-24).
The admonition implies the greater urgency of personal relationship over
religious observances. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
-.25in;line-height:18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              2.
Jesus considered justice, mercy, and faith weightier matters of the Law than <u>tithing
regulations</u> (Matthew <b>23</b>:23-24). <i>“You should have done these and
not to let the other undone”</i> is Jesus’ way of describing relative importance.
The whole Law was true, but interpersonal matters were weightier truths.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              The
Pharisees had lost perspective by becoming legalistic. The legal mentality
absolutizes everything that is true so everything in the Law is equal in its
being there. Unqualified legal thinking does not distinguish between <u>equally
true</u> and equally important. Legalistic thought does not allow practical
considerations to dictate the lawful use of law. The Pharisees, especially, had
not prioritized the value and needs of persons as qualifying factors on the
application of legal provisions. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
-.25in;line-height:18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              3.
The welfare of people pre-empted <u>tabernacle regulations</u> when David and
his men were famished and ate the showbread lawful for only priests to eat
(Matthew <b>12</b>:1-8; Mark <b>2</b>:23-28; Luke <b>6</b>:1-5; 1 Samuel <b>21</b>:1-6).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
-.25in;line-height:18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              4.
Hezekiah prayed for some pilgrims who had not had opportunity to meet properly
the <u>ceremonial cleansing</u> requirements for observing the Passover, and
God hearkened to the king’s request to let them observe it anyway (2 Chronicles
<b>30</b>:13-22). Jesus often disregarded the traditions of the elders on these
ceremonial cleansings (Matthew <b>15</b>:1-20 = Mark<b> 7</b>:1-23; Luke <b>11</b>:37-41).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
-.25in;line-height:18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>      5. Jesus
promised the thief on the cross a place in Paradise with Jesus even </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>though he could not fulfill any relevant
ritual for his sin (Luke <b>23</b>:39-43; there really were no Mosaic
sacrifices for capital crimes anyway even if the insurrectionist on the cross
could have come down and offered one.)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        6.   Jesus’
treatment of the woman caught in adultery shows the priority of repentance over
the <u>legal requirement for capital punishment for mortal sin</u> (John <b>7</b>:53-<b>8</b>:11).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        7.   The gospel
accounts show the priority of personal needs over <u>Sabbath regulations</u>,
especially as interpreted and practiced by the Pharisees via the tradition of
the elders. <i>“The Sabbath was made for people,”</i> not <i>vice versa</i>
(Mark <b>2</b>:27; cp. Exodus <b>23</b>:12d; Deuteronomy <b>5</b>:14c). The
worth of persons takes precedence over the forms of religion. Personal concerns
precede ritual observances and the positive commandments that establish them. Person
precedes law; personal transcends legal. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        8.   Even while <u>Mosaic
legislation</u> was in effect, faith was the real basis for anyone’s relationship
with God (Habakkuk <b>2</b>:3-4 &lt; Galatians <b>3</b>:8-14; Romans<b> 1</b>:17-8:39).
</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        9.   Abraham’s
friendship with God rested on faith before <u>circumcision</u> had even been
instituted as the national Jewish covenant sign (Romans <b>4</b>:6-15).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              Paul
comments quite boldly about righteous Gentiles by saying, <i>“If the Uncircumcision
keeps the ordinance of the Law, their uncircumcision will be regarded as
circumcision, won’t it? And won’t the Uncircumcision, which is natural, if it
fulfills the Law, judge you who with the letter and circumcision transgress the
Law?”</i> (Romans<b> 2</b>:26-27).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        10. Jesus told
the woman of Sychar that although salvation was from the Jews, <u>where people
worshiped</u> would soon no be longer relevant because worship must be in
spirit and truth (John <b>4</b>:19-26). </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              The
divorce law of Deuteronomy <b>24</b>:1-4 recognized divorce for any cause,
whereas Genesis<b> 2</b> implies that marriage is permanent. The harmonization
comes from recognizing that Deuteronomy <b>24</b> was civil legislation whereas
the implications of Genesis <b>2</b> were natural and moral considerations. The
interpersonal identity in marriage transcends the lower legal standard that
sought to alleviate some of the yet more extreme abuses of women in ancient
society. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                   11.  Interpersonalism
harmonizes certain <u>antinomies</u> in the Old Testament Law. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              Habakkuk<b>
2</b>:4 declares that God’s righteous one will have life on the basis of faith.
Faith, however, is not what operates in law. Perfection is the requirement and
faith in another is not involved in becoming righteous after sinning (Deuteronomy
<b>27</b>:26; Leviticus <b>18</b>:5; Galatians<b> 3</b>:10-14). The
harmonization comes from recognizing that the Law was not intended to be a <u>basis</u>
for righteousness and relationship to God, but a <u>definition</u> of what God wanted
in the relationship. The basis for human-divine relationship has always been
faith––interpersonal process. Antinomy comes from reading the Law as final
rather than provisional and secondary to interpersonal process.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
-.25in;line-height:18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                         12.
Jesus continually conflicted with the religious leaders over the tradition of
the elders, particularly over <u>Sabbath regulations</u>. Interpersonal
expression makes us wonder what Jesus meant in Luke <b>6</b>:5 (= Matthew <b>12</b>:8;
Mark <b>2</b>:28), <i>“The Son/son of Man/man is Lord/lord even/also of the Sabbath.”
</i>Did he mean that <u>he</u> was above Sabbath regulations or had the right
to change them, or did he mean that the needs of any <u>human person</u> trumps
Sabbath regulations? Person precedes law and form. The two ideas may connect: Jesus
highlighted himself as God’s ideal man. Other examples of setting aside traditional
Sabbath regulations appear in Matthew <b>12</b>:1-4 (= Mark <b>2</b>:23-<b>3</b>:6;
Luke <b>6</b>:1-11); Luke <b>13</b>:10-17; John<b> 5</b>:9-18; <b>7:</b>19-24;<b>
9</b>:1-41.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
-.25in;line-height:18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                         13.
Lineage had no value for anything but temporal blessings during the Mosaic
dispensation. John the Baptist told his contemporaries not to say to
themselves, <i>“‘Abraham is our father.’ I tell you, God can raise up children
to Abraham from these stones.”</i></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              Rather
than concentrate on person-to-person behavior, we find ourselves concentrating
on formal and procedural matters because they require less effort. Personal
relationship is the most difficult activity because it is the most complex; we look
for what we can do more easily (knowing things, <i>e.g</i>.) or be proud of
without effort (genealogy, IQ, athletic ability, good looks). We prefer to do
anything rather than change: lie, blame someone else, use authority to shut people
up who point out our weaknesses. The rich look down on the poor while they
themselves are rich only by inheritance. Nobility looks down on commoners when
they themselves were only born into status. The intelligent look down on the
slow-of-mind though they themselves have been given their good minds. The
educated ridicule the ignorant who lack opportunity for education. Secondary
true things become false when made primary.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  E.   All the great
summaries of the Judaeo-Christian faith are interpersonal. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        1.   The First
Great Commandment: <i>“Love the LORD your God.”</i></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        2. The Second
Great commandment: <i>“Love your neighbor as yourself.”</i></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        3. The Golden
Rule: <i>“Treat others the way you would have others treat you.”</i></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        4.   James<b> 1</b>:27:
<i>“Pure and undefiled religion before our God and Father is this: to visit the
fatherless and widows in their affliction and to keep oneself unspotted from
the world.”</i></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        5.   Habakkuk <b>2</b>:4:
<i>“The just will live by faith.”</i></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
-1.0in;line-height:18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                                6.   Micah
<b>6</b>:8: <i>“What does the Lord require of you but to do justly, love
kindness, and walk humbly with your God?”</i></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
-.25in;line-height:18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                    Likewise,
Deuteronomy <b>10</b>:12-13 said earlier: <i>“What does Yahveh your God require
of you but to fear him, to walk in all his ways, and to love him and to serve him
with all your heart and with all your soul, to keep his commandments and his
statutes, which I command you today for your good?”</i></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:1.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify;text-indent:-1.5in;
line-height:18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              7.   Hosea
<b>4</b>:1: <i>“There should be truth, goodness, and knowledge of God in the
land.”</i></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        8.   Matthew<b>
25</b>:31-46: The judgment at the end of time rests on how people treated other
people in this life.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>III. What Is Most Enduring</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  Paul stresses love as
greatest (1 Corinthians <b>13</b>:13) because love applies to the <i>“now”</i>
and the <i>“then</i>,<i>”</i> <i>“seeing through a glass darkly”</i> and <i>“seeing
face to face.”</i> Since love applies to personal relationships wherever and
whenever, it is more eternal than material-political systems of this world. Love
relates to the next life as well as this one.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'> IV. What Is Ultimate</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  A.  Love surpasses <u>knowledge</u>.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        Love carries out
the interpersonal pattern of association. Paul declares, <i>“That you might <u>know</u>
the <u>love</u> of Christ that surpasses <u>knowledge</u>” </i>(Ephesians <b>3</b>:19).
Knowledge is not the ultimate consideration, not meaning that it is separate,
but that love surpasses it. Not only is knowledge not enough, but love—the
higher plane of reference—can overlook ignorance and error if love (attitude)
is present. Paul declares that he received forgiveness because he persecuted
the church in ignorance and disbelief (1 Timothy <b>1</b>:13; cp. Acts<b> 3</b>:17;
Luke <b>23</b>:34; Acts <b>17</b>:30 + 23; see also Acts <b>13</b>:27; <b>26</b>:9;
Ephesians <b>4</b>:17?; 1 Corinthians <b>2</b>:8; Hebrews<b> 5</b>:2;<b> 9</b>:7
as well as Numbers <b>15</b>:24-29 = Leviticus <b>4</b>:2, 22, 27; <b>5</b>:15,
18; 1 Peter <b>1</b>:14; James<b> 4</b>:17). <i>“Knowledge puffs up, but love
builds up”</i> (1 Corinthians<b> 8</b>:1).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        Love is not (1) <u>contrary
to</u> knowledge; so education does not eliminate spirituality. It is not
simply (2) <u>more than</u> knowledge. It (3) <u>makes up for</u> lack in
knowledge. It always (4) <u>transforms</u> knowledge by putting it in the
larger interpersonal context and putting it into motion.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        The goal of
knowledge is appropriate behavior summarized in love. Paul encouraged Titus to
speak things fitting to sound doctrine (knowledge) that <i>“older men may be .
. . sound in . . . love . . . that older women . . . may teach the younger
women to love their husbands, to love their children.” </i>Departing from that
format leads to unfitting discussions about <i>“foolish questionings,
genealogies . . . and wrangling about the Law”</i> (<b>3</b>:9; cp. 1 Timothy <b>1</b>:35).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        Romans <b>2</b>:12
offers a lengthy statement that it is not knowledge (<i>i.e</i>., having the Law
through which the knowledge of God and his will comes), but behavior that has
to do with not perishing.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  B.  Faith takes
precedence over <u>knowledge</u> (2 Corinthians<b> 5</b>:7). </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        It is not so
much what we know but who we trust that makes us able to conduct life as we
ought. If we can trust God, we do not have to know so much. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  C.  Personal behavior ranks
higher than <u>purely doctrinal matters</u>.                                                                      Titus
<b>2</b>:1ff shows that the purpose of sound doctrine is proper behavior toward
other persons. Doctrine is fruitless if it does not produce behavioral
correlates; doctrine is fruitless if it does not bear fruit. Jesus would not
allow the woman of Sychar to shift attention from her personal life and
redirect it to the doctrinal issue of where people should worship. Not everyone
that <u>says</u>, <i>“Lord, Lord,”</i> but the one that <u>does</u> the will of
the Father (Matthew <b>7</b>:21) will enter the eternal kingdom (James <b>2</b>).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  D.  There is a peace
that <i>“passes understanding”</i> (Philippians <b>4</b>:7).                                                                  The
statement could mean a peace we cannot explain to non-Christians, a peace
others cannot see the reason for, or a peace we feel but cannot ourselves understand.
It may a peace that does not come from knowledge, but from trust, an
interpersonal circumstance. It is a peace we have, not because we <u>understand</u>
what is happening and why, but because we <u>trust</u> that God controls what
is happening and why, even though the situation looks chaotic and destructive.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
-.25in;line-height:18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        E.
Love surpasses behavior (James <b>5</b>:20; 1 Peter<b> 4</b>:8; Luke <b>7</b>:36-<u>47</u>-50;
Proverbs <b>10</b>:12; <b>17</b>:9; cp. 1 Corinthians <b>13</b>:4-7). Attitude
is more important than acts.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        Love covers a
multitude of sins in that other people are more willing to overlook and forgive
the sins of loving people. Love covers the sins of the loving person (Luke <b>7</b>:47).
</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        Another understanding
says that loving people are more prone to overlook other people’s faults. Love
causes us to overlook faults in others because we want the best for them and
want to accentuate the positive in them. We focus on their strengths because that
is what we can work with to help them get better yet. Love does not criticize
because it does not focus on faults. Love covers the sins of the loved person. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        Yet another understanding
says that love covers sins by loving people keeping their own sins out of the
picture in the first place. Love for other people overrides self-centered
impulses. People who out of love want to convert other people watch their own
behavior closely so it does not thwart their evangelistic efforts. The sins
covered are by the loving persons who themselves prevent their own sins.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  F.   The New Testament
tends to speak of good and bad in terms of their ultimate inspiring source,
their final frame of reference, which are personal references/.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        Paul’s “thorn in
the flesh” he calls “a messenger of Satan” (2 Corinthians <b>12</b>:7). “Satan
thwarted”  Paul’s desire to see the Thessalonians (1 Thessalonians<b> 2</b>:18).
Disciplining the sinful man in Corinth Paul speak of as “delivering to Satan”
(1 Corinthians <b>5</b>:5; 1 Timothy <b>1</b>:20).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        On the positive
side, Paul talks about doing everything “in the name of the Lord Jesus”
(Colossians <b>3</b>:17) and obeying masters as “respecting the Lord”
(Colossians <b>3</b>:22).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
4.5pt;line-height:18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'> V. The Way Christianity Is Defended</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  Christian apologetics
in the New Testament draws heavily on personal and interpersonal matters (cp.
Acts <b>5</b>:33-42). (For details, see the interpersonalism document on “Apologetics.”)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
.5in;line-height:18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>A. Regarding Jesus’
resurrection, Gamaliel in effect argues from what it means to be Messiah––an
eternal king of a person-centered system.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        His argument for
the resurrection turns on Christianity as a person–centered system. When the
person is removed from the center of such a system, the system collapses. That is
what happened in the cases of Theudas and Judas, who were evidently Messianic
claimants as well. When they were killed their movements disintegrated because,
as John’s gospel says, <i>“Messiah abides forever”</i> (John <b>12</b>:34). If
Messiah leads a universal, eternal kingdom, there can be only one Messiah, and
he cannot be overthrown.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        Such an approach
to Messiah and his kingdom contrasts with an ideology–centered system. Ideas
and viewpoints may pass from person to person; the death of a founder does not
destroy the movement. A prophet testifies about some message he claims to have
received from God. The Messiah did not just prophesy; he personally led his
people.  </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  B. Paul’s argument for
Jesus’ resurrection turns on a presupposition about human </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        nature.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        Paul’s argument
for Christ’s resurrection comes down to a behavioral observation: we will not
give our lives for what we know is false. If Paul and his companions had been
false witnesses, while knowingly claiming that Christ resurrected, they would
have had no reason to endure deprivation, prison, torture, and the prospect of
death for what they had preached.         </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  C. The power of
presenting the Christian message is greater when presented by persons who care about
the ones they evangelize.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  D. Much of the power
in the Christian message itself derives from its desire to </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>answer the needs people the most: meaningfulness,
innocence, security, and love. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  A.  Love             </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  B.  Other
fruit-of-the-spirit terms like <i>“joy</i>,<i>” “peace</i>,<i>” “patience”</i>
(Galatians  <b>5</b>:22-</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        23)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  C.  Faith/Trust</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        All
interpersonal processes require faith/trust for them even to occur. Natural,
legal, and logical processes are one-directional; they normally have the
pattern of a straight-line causal series. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        In its salvation
application, “faith” does not mean mere belief of information. We do not have
salvation because we <u>believe</u> Christ, but because we <u>believe in</u>
Christ. Believing deals with knowledge; believing-in deals with trusting in
someone.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        All the major “faith”
treatments in the New Testament come from Habakkuk <b>2</b>:4 (Romans<b> 1</b>:16-<b>5</b>:21;
Galatians <b>3</b>; Hebrews <b>10</b>:37-<b>12</b>:2) or Genesis <b>15</b>:6
(Romans <b>4</b>), the call and direction of Abraham.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  M. Christ</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        In some New
Testament texts, <i>“Christ”</i> seems to be a metonymy a system parallel to
the legal, formal system that operated during the Mosaic period. At least those
special usages of <i>“Christ”</i> seem removed from the individual person Jesus
the Christ. We can regard these usages as a term for the interpersonal system
of things and the interpersonal approach to life.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        1.   <i>“‘. . .
and to your seed,’ which is Christ”</i> (Galatians <b>3</b>:16)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              Paul
observes that the Genesis text says <i>“seed</i>,<i>”</i> not <i>“seeds</i>,<i>”</i>
in the promise that Abraham’s seed would bless all the families of earth; the
seed is singular: <i>“not as of many, but as of one.”</i> He does not mean many
individuals in contrast to One individual—Jesus of Nazareth. Paul means, not <i>versus</i>
many in <u>number</u> but <i>versus </i>one as of <u>kind</u>, not Jesus
Christ, but the Christ kind, the faith kind who are God’s friends and children
by faith. <i>“If you are ‘of Christ’</i> [</span><u><span lang=EL
style='font-size:12.0pt'>Χριστοῦ</span></u><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>]<i>”</i>
means descriptive genitive rather than possessive genitive. The faith kind
focalizes in Jesus the Messiah. It is not just epitomized by him; it is
identified with him. Christ was in full degree what Abraham, Israel, Moses, and
so on, were relatively; and we can become that through him. We identify with
Jesus Christ that we may become sons of Abraham and sons of God by faith. In
identifying with Christ, we identify ourselves with the faith system he
embodies, epitomizes, represents.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              The
expression <i>“Abraham’s seed”</i> likewise does not speak about the <u>identity</u>
of his descendants, but a <u>kind</u> of “descendants”: trusting people like <i>“faithful
Abraham” </i>(Galatians <b>3</b>:8-9). His physical descendants <u>may not be</u>
his seed as John the Baptist observed (Matthew <b>3</b>:9 = Luke <b>3</b>:8;
cp. John <b>8</b>:31-44); those who did not descend from him <u>may be</u> his
seed (Romans <b>4</b>:10-17). <i>“They are not all Israel who are of Israel”</i>
(Romans <b>9</b>:6). Relationship with God does not rest on physical descent
but on personal character. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        2.   <i>“That
rock was Christ”</i> (1 Corinthians <b>10</b>:4).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        3.   <i>“The
spirit of Christ that was in them”</i> (1 Peter <b>1</b>:11).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        4.   <i>“Considering
the reproach of Christ greater riches than the treasures of </i></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><i><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              Egypt”</span></i><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'> (Hebrews<b> 11</b>:26) </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
-.25in;line-height:18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              5.   Previously
<i>“Gentiles were separated from Christ”</i> (Ephesians<b> 2</b>:12).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              In
Ephesians <b>2</b>:11-13 <i>“Christ”</i> is contemporaneous with not
participating in the commonwealth of Israel with its covenants and promises. Both
of these describe a time during the Mosaic dispensation, a time before Christ
came as a historical person to embody the <i>“Christ nature.”</i></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        6. <i>“Abraham
rejoiced to see my day; he saw it and was glad”</i> (John <b>8</b>:56-58).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              Does Jesus
imply that he had appeared to Abraham as perhaps the <i>“angel of the Lord,”</i>
or does he mean that the faith approach to divine relationship is what Abraham
experienced?</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        7. The <i>“gospel”</i>
was preached to Abraham (Galatians<b> 3</b>:8).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        8. <i>“The ‘law’
of Christ” </i>(1 Corinthians <b>9</b>:21; cp. Romans <b>8</b>:2)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              Does <i>“the
‘law’ of Christ”</i> amount to the same thing as the “interpersonal principle”?
<i>“The ‘law’ of Christ” </i>could even be translated <i>“the Christ principle.”</i></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        9. Apollos
preached accurately the things about Jesus, knowing only John’s baptism (Acts <b>18</b>:24-28).
</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              Acts <b>18</b>:27-28
notes his effectiveness. Perhaps he was responsible for converting <i>“the
twelve disciples of John”</i> in Acts <b>19</b>:1-7. If he knew only John’s
baptism, how could he be preaching accurately Jesus’ things? Does it mean his
awareness of what had transpired in Palestine had somehow gone no further than
what the situation was during Jesus’ ministry even though Acts <b>18-19</b>
deals with a time nearly twenty-five years after the resurrection and after
Pentecost?</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              We can
make sense of <i>“accurately about Jesus”</i> plus “<i>only the baptism of John”
</i>by supposing that Apollos correctly understood the non-political,
pan-ethnic nature of the Messianic kingdom. That becomes another way of saying
interpersonal.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        10. <i>“Christ”</i>
is put for the body of those who make “him” up. As one body has many members, that
is the way Christ is (1 Corinthians <b>12</b>:12). Similarly, the church is
often called the <i>“body of Christ.”</i></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        11. John’s use
of the term <i>antichrist</i> may relate to this idea as well (1 John <b>2</b>:18,
22; <b>4</b>:3, 7). Every perversion of Christianity has been some sort of departure
from its interpersonal character. The Gnosticism of John’s day had the spirit
of antichrist because it denied that Jesus had come in the flesh. Politicizing
the church into the Roman empire later affords another example.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                         12. Christ is
put for the content of the message: <i>“preach Christ”</i> (Galatians </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              <b>1</b>:16).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              We could
be dealing with a cultural way of using language more than with the nature of
Christianity. Acts <b>15</b>:21 says that <i>“Moses is preached.”</i> The
expression refers to a legal system, yet that way of speaking describes its
content. In 1 Corinthians <b>10</b>:1 Paul says the <i>Israelites “were
baptized to Moses in the cloud and sea.”</i> Jesus speaks of the abode of the
saved as <i>“the arms of Abraham”</i> (Luke <b>16</b>:19-31). In that same
episode, Abraham tells the rich man, <i>“They have Moses and the prophets,”</i>
meaning the writings and revelation that came through them. Paul uses <i>Adam</i>
and <i>Christ</i> as personal names for categories of people (Romans<b> 5</b>:12-21;
1 Corinthians <b>15</b>:21-22). Patriarchal names like <i>“Israel</i>,<i>” “Jacob,”
“Esau” </i>name the political entities (patronymics, Hosea <b>11</b>:1). Such
illustrations may indicate a Jewish way of speaking that concretizes the
intangible by personalizing it in terms of a significant personage or inspiring
source.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              Although
the speech habit could explain the phenomenon, the phenomenon is at least
personalization rather than personification, metonymy, or synecdoche. Personification
puts personal characteristics on a thing; personalization relates a thing to a
person. Jesus is a person with the ideas associated with him. <i>“Christ”</i>
is not used to <i>“stand for”</i> the subject matter. Even if Paul were using <i>“Christ”</i>
as a metaphor for what he taught, he would still be personalizing the content
of that teaching, because it had mainly to do with him. The fact that he so
characteristically puts <i>“Christ”</i> for the gospel and the church shows how
natural it was for him to do so. In a sense, interpersonalism pervades the
spiritual aspect of Mosaism as well since law was an added element beyond
purely dynamic factors. The expression <i>“Moses is preached”</i> does not
prove that Mosaism is interpersonal in the sense that we are using similar
expressions about <i>“Christ”</i> to argue the interpersonal character of
Christianity. Legal process does not pre-empt the interpersonal in
Christianity, nor does it get added theologically to the essence of the faith. Christianity
is a matter of being <i>“<u>in</u> Christ,” </i>a matter of entering <i>“<u>into</u>
Christ,”</i> of wearing the <i>“<u>name</u> of Christ” </i>(see “Q” below).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  N.  Spirit</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        Spirit
terminology appears to correspond with personal and interpersonal matters. The common
element in the word <i>“spirit”</i> is invisible reality. The (a) <u>wind</u>
blows, but we can see only its effects, not the thing itself (John <b>3</b>:8).
A person can have a certain mentality, or (b) <u>attitude</u>, as in the
statement<i>, “You do not know what spirit you are of”</i> (Luke <b>9</b>:55
marginal text). The term refers to (c) <u>demons</u>. In contrast to location, <i>“spirit”
</i>refers to (d) <u>mental presence</u>, as when Paul in Ephesus speaks of
being present with the Corinthians in spirit though not in body (1 Corinthians <b>5</b>:3).
1 Corinthians <b>5</b>:4 may refer to Paul’s (e) <u>approval</u> or
encouragement in exercising church discipline on the incestuous man. In
contrast to flesh, the (f) <u>spirit of a person</u> refers to the transcendent
nature that survives death or at least to the non-material aspect of a person. <i>“Spirit”</i>
refers to the (g) <u>transcendent lifestyle</u> that persons can live. In
distinction to Father and Son, there is the (h) <u>Holy Spirit</u>. Especially
in the expression <i>“filled with the Spirit,”</i> the term can mean (i) a <u>surge
of boldness</u> or concern. In contrast to the letter of the Law, <i>“spirit”</i>
in Paul seems to mean the (j) <u>intent</u>, or purpose, of law. In this usage
the interpersonal integrates with the usage of the word <i>“spirit.”</i> </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        1.   Romans <b>2</b>:28-29
deals with spirit in contrast to physical circumcision, outward man, letter, praise
of other people, the Law (<b>2</b>:26). Spirit correlates with circumcision of
the heart, inner self, praise of God, the gospel.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        2.   Romans <b>7</b>:6
speaks of being discharged through death from the bondage of the Law so people
can live by the newness of the spirit <i>vs.</i> the oldness of the letter.                                  3.         The
context of 2 Corinthians <b>3</b>: contrasts law and grace, works and faith,
Moses and Christ, the old covenant and the new, the same contrast found in
Romans and Colossians where the spirit-letter contrast appears (cp. Galatians <b>3-4</b>;
John <b>1</b>:17). The spirit gives life whereas the letter kills. <i>“Kills”</i>
could refer to condemnation, because law operates in terms of works (a person’s
own doing) under a perfection requirement. Since no one lives up perfectly to a
standard of rules and regulations, everyone under law stands condemned by law
to the death penalty. Spirit <i>vs.</i> letter involves spirit as personal,
because the “intent” and purpose of the Law override the exact verbal
expression used by the Person making the Law.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        4.   The
indwelling of the Holy Spirit</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              We can
speak of “spirit” as referring to the “transmaterial” in contrast to physical,
the “transcultural” in contrast to political, the “translocational” in contrast
to spatial limitation, and “trans-informational” in going beyond knowledge.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  O.  Fellowship   </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  P.   Blessing</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  Q.  Name </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        What defines a person
is who they belong to, whose name they wear, what people call them. <i>“Naming
the name of Christ”</i> (2 Timothy <b>2</b>:19) means confessing whose side people
are on, who has their allegiance, whose values they adopt, whose purposes they
serve, who their primary Significant Other is. We are baptized into the “name”
of the Father, Son, and Holy Spirit. In that frame, <i>“in the name of” </i>is
not so much <i>“by the authority of”</i> as <i>“in identity with.”</i> <i>“In
the name of”</i> deals with <u>identity</u> (interpersonal), not <u>authority</u>
(legal).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>VII. Jeremiah <b>31</b>:31-34 and the
New “Covenant” </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  Jeremiah prophesied
that God would make a new covenant with Israel, different in identity and kind.
In that new covenant, everyone would <i>“know” </i>God from least to greatest. It
would not be like the covenant Yahveh made with the Israelites after the
exodus; it would not be a political, national, or legal covenant. (See
introductory comments in the essay “Understanding Christian Ordinances.”)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>VIII. Galatians <b>3</b>:15-29 (as noted
earlier)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  The interpersonal reality
pre-dated the Mosaic Law. When the Law came, it did not annul the prior, more
basic truths implied by <i>“promise</i>,<i>”</i> an interpersonal term. The Law
was a temporary overlay for practical purposes until Christ would personally
come into the human scene and really represent the <i>“seed” </i>of Abraham. Salvation
cannot even come by law, that is, by legal principles; if it could, salvation could
have come by the Mosaic Law (Galatians<b> 3</b>:21).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;text-indent:

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  A.  The priority of
interpersonal over <u>knowledge</u></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><u><span style='font-size:12.0pt'><span style='text-decoration:none'>&nbsp;</span></span></u></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        1. Peace that

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        2. <i>“I am the
way, the <u>truth</u>, and the life”</i> (truth is personal). Note also, <i>“You
will know the truth, and the truth will make you free” </i>(John <b>8</b>:32).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        3.   Knowledge
puffs up; love builds up. It builds up by stressing relationships, whereas
knowledge stresses facts.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        4.   If anyone
thinks he knows anything, he does not yet know what he should know; but if
anyone loves God, the same is known by him (1 Corinthians <b>8</b>:2-3; cp. <b>3</b>:18).</span></p>

fulfill the Law, judge you who with the letter and circumcision transgress the Law?”</i>
(Romans <b>2</b>:25-26).</span></p>
