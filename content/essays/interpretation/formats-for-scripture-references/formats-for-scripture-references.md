<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;

<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;
margin-bottom:0in;margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:center'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>Special Notations</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>    1.    useful abbreviations in scripture citation     </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>           MT = Masoretic Text, the standard form of
the Hebrew Old Testament</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>           LXX = Septuagint, the Greek translation of
the Old Testament that was most widely used by Greek-speaking Jews, or
Hellenists. It is so-called because tradition has it that seventy men were
involved in making the translation; <i>septuaginta </i>is the Latin word for “seventy.”</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>           TR = Textus Receptus (Received Text), the
“received text” that lay behind the Authorized Version of 1611; its Greek text
is sometimes called “the majority text” because the greatest number of extant
New Testament manuscripts represent this text type.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>    2.    Hebrew <i>vs</i>. English references in the
Psalms </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>           If you are citing the Hebrew text of the
Psalms, you must remember that different versification is used in many Psalms.
Those without introductory notations have the same numbering as the English
(Psalm<b> 1</b>,<b> 2</b>, <i>etc.</i>). Others that have brief notations
incorporate the introductory comment in the first verse (Psalm <b>11</b>, <b>14</b>,
<i>etc</i>.). Many, however, number the introduction as verse 1, which means
that the numbering is one off compared to standard English numbering (Psalm <b>3</b>,
<b>4</b>, <i>etc</i>.). In a few cases the introduction is assigned two verses
in the Hebrew; so the subsequent numbering is different by two (Psalm <b>51</b>,
<b>52</b>, <b>54</b>). The Hebrew sometimes makes other minor adjustments
relative to the English. In Psalm <b>18</b>, for example, “and he said” is part
of the introduction in English Bibles, but the Hebrew text makes it part of the
next verse. A format for clarifying the text intended is Psalm <b>3</b>:3 MT
and in parenthesis (Psalm <b>3</b>:3 MT); if you are indicating both, from the
English viewpoint it would be Psalm <b>11</b>:2 (= <b>3</b>:3 MT) and in
parenthesis (Psalm<b> 11</b>:2 [= <b>3</b>:3 MT])</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>    3.    Septuagint <i>vs</i>. English references in
the Psalms </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>           The Septuagint follows the Hebrew numbering
by including introductory comments in verse 1 (the equivalent of Psalm <b>13</b>,
<b>14</b>, <i>etc</i>., in the Hebrew) or assigning them a separate verse
(Psalm <b>3</b>, <b>4</b>,<i> etc</i>.) or two (the equivalent of Psalm <b>51</b>,
<b>52</b>, <b>54</b> in the Hebrew). In addition, however, the numbering of the
Psalms themselves is off by one beginning with Psalm <b>9</b>. Psalms <b>9</b>
and <b>10</b> are combined so that every subsequent Psalm number is off by one.
The Septuagint divides Psalm <b>116</b> and Psalm <b>147</b>, which throws the
numbering off again in the other direction. There is also an added Psalm at the
end, Psalm <b>151</b>, which does not appear in the standard Hebrew text. In
many cases, then, both the chapter and verse designations are different from
the corresponding English referencing system: <i>“Have mercy on me, O God,
according to your lovingkindness”</i> (Psalm<b> 51</b>:1 = <b>50</b>:3 LXX = <b>51</b>:3
MT). </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>    4.    Septuagint<i> vs</i>. English references in
Jeremiah </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>           The Book of Jeremiah in the Septuagint is
significantly different from the Hebrew Masoretic text both in extent and
order. Citations made from it need to be correlated with English format, which
is based on the Masoretic text.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>    5.    In the Septuagint (LXX) 1 &amp; 2 Samuel as
well as 1 &amp; 2 Kings are called 1, 2, 3 &amp; 4 Kings [or Bas., for <i>Basileus</i>,
</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>Βασιλεύς</span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>]. 1 &amp; 2 Chronicles are called 1 &amp; 2 <u>Par.</u>,
an abbreviation for <i>Paralipomenon</i>. Song of Solomon is called <u>Canticles</u>
(<i>Canticum</i>, abbreviated <i>Cant.</i> A number of other differences in
titles and spellings need to be observed when making references from this
version of the Old Testament.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>      </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify;text-indent:13.5pt'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>6.<span style='font:7.0pt "Times New Roman"'>&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;
</span></span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>1 John<b> 5</b>:7 is now generally
set aside as not being part of the original letter of 1 John. In subsequent
translations verse 6 is divided in half to make the same number of verses in
the chapter. As a result, if you cite 1 John <b>5</b>:7 from the King James
Version, you need to indicate that the quotation is from the King James Version;
otherwise, the reader will be confused if he reads the verse from a
twentieth-century translation.</span></p>
