<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:center'><b><span
style='font-size:14.0pt'>HATE, ADULTERY, AND DIVORCE:</span></b></p>

<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:center'><b><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>Matthew 5:21-32</span></b></p>

<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:center'><b><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></b></p>

<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:center'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>Virgil Warren, PhD</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;line-height:18.0pt'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>Introduction  </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  Matthew <b>5</b>:21-6:18
may be regarded as an outgrowth of the comment in <b>5</b>:20 that people’s
righteousness should exceed the righteousness of the scribes and Pharisees.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  Murder, adultery,
divorce, and the lesser sins of anger (<b>5</b>:22a), hate, name calling (<b>5</b>:22b),
revenge (<b>5</b>:38-42), and capricious oath taking (<b>5</b>:33-37) are all
of the same sort: they depersonalize other people. Depersonalizing
relationships can be done by </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  (a)  legalizing them.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  (b) externalizing
them.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  (c)  competing with
them.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>The competitive mentality that sets
others at naught needs to be replaced by the Golden Rule that restates the
Second Great Commandment. Self-centeredness and false individualism need to
yield to interpersonalism as befits social relationship with God and other
people. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>            The three forms of
depersonalization are negative or at best neutral; the positive thrust comes
from love.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span lang=FR style='font-size:12.0pt'>    I. Murder: lessons from <b>5</b>:21-26
(Exodus <b>20</b>:13; Deuteronomy <b>5</b>:17)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span lang=FR style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span lang=FR style='font-size:12.0pt'>            </span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>      A.  Not just restraint from the overt act, but
from the inner attitudes (21-22a)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        Jesus gets rid
of unintended <u>legalism</u> in the interpretation of Old Testament texts. He
reasons that the things which tend toward what is prohibited and things that
are like what is prohibited are themselves also prohibited.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  B.  Not just restraint
from the ultimate crime, but from similar lesser acts (22b)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        Anger, hate, and
verbal abuse like name calling have lesser degrees of the same nature as murder
and may lead to it in the end.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  C.  Not just restraint
from negative behavior, but positive effort at reconciliation (23-</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        26)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        Jesus advocates
replacing the <u>adversarial</u> stance with a <u>conciliatory</u> one. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  D.  Vertical
relationship cannot be divorced from horizontal relationship (23-24).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        The principle
here is illustrated elsewhere in the Parable of the Ungrateful Servant and in
Paul’s treatment of the Lord’s Supper in 1 Corinthians <b>11</b>.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  E.   Religious
observance cannot be divorced from everyday relationships (23-26).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        The vertical and
the horizontal are governed by the same basic principles because they are of
the same basic sort: interpersonal. Therefore, they must be tied together in
practical righteousness for people who serve as a light to the rest of the
world (<b>5</b>:14-15; cp. the Parable of the Unforgiving Servant).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>   II. Adultery:<b> 5</b>:27-30</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  A.  Adultery is backed
up to <u>lust</u>.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        Jesus means
looking <u>to</u> lust. In a sense, people are not able to avoid bodily drives,
and there is nothing wrong with them as such. What he refers to, however, is
deliberate attempts to create sexual arousal such as seeking out occasions and
literature and pictures that can be directed toward the sex drive.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  B.  Adultery grows out
of internal lust. Therefore, it must be dealt with by dealing with what leads
to it: (a) eye control, (b) cloth control, (c) taking care of drives within their
appropriate context (1 Corinthians<b> 7</b>:1-3).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>  III. Divorce: <b>5</b>:31-32 (Deuteronomy
<b>24</b>:1-4)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  A.  Structure of the
text: two approaches to <b>5</b>:27-32</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        1.   Within the
adultery framework: <b>5</b>:31-32 as a sub-set of <b>5</b>:27-32</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              In favor
of this arrangement is the mention of adultery in <b>5</b>:32, although the
adultery is something the husband somehow “causes” his former wife to do.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              a.   The “legal
adultery” approach</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                    In
this view Jesus objects to using divorce to accomplish what would otherwise
have been adultery. Instead of going ahead and committing adultery with
someone, the man divorces his wife and marries the other woman. The motivations
behind such a procedure amount to the same thing as adultery; consequently,
Jesus’ statements here about divorce fall within the category of adultery. He
is not dealing with divorce as such, but divorce as driven by the same
motivation that results in adultery. Divorce for avoiding an undesirable
marriage situation without any predetermined prospect for remarriage falls
outside the scope of Jesus’ teaching in this text. The contrast with
Deuteronomy <b>24</b> (<i>“but I tell you”</i>) would consist of limiting what
that legislation did not clearly forbid.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              b.   The
betrothal approach</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                    Jesus’
comments are for getting the Pharisees to see that general divorce is not meant
by Deuteronomy<b> 24</b>; consequently, Moses did not mean divorce for any
cause, but for cases where the man wanted to end betrothal or where
unfaithfulness in the bride was discovered after the marriage was consummated. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              c.   The
incestuous-marriage approach</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                    Jesus’
comments again are for getting the Pharisees to see that Moses did not mean
general divorce, but for a special situation—that of incestuous marriage.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              These
three viewpoints assume too limited a context for Deuteronomy <b>24</b>, which
Jesus would presumably be correcting. There are no contextual cues in
Deuteronomy <b>24</b> to restrict Moses’ words to a more limited framework than
giving a writing of divorcement in any situation where the husband is repulsed by
or dissatisfied with something in the wife. Likewise there are no contextual
cues in Matthew <b>5</b> to restrict Jesus’ words to what would stand in
contrast to such specific situations. Besides, a parallel discussion in Matthew
<b>19</b> records Jesus as saying that the Deuteronomy legislation was enacted
because of the hardness of men’s hearts. It would not necessarily seem hardhearted
to divorce a woman who had misrepresented her virtue during betrothal.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        2.   Parallel to
the adultery category: <b>5</b>:31-32 as subsequent to<b> 5</b>:27-30</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              In favor
of this arrangement is the recurrence of the formula <i>“It was said . . . but
I say.”</i> Besides the parallel structure, the proximity of these comments on
adultery and divorce are sufficiently accounted for by the fact that both
practices represent something less than the ideal relationship God intends for
men and women, especially since the motivation behind the latter can be the
same as that behind the former—as noted above under “legal adultery.”</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  B.  Setting of the
discussion: the assumed dispensational connections</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        1.   <i>Within
Mosaism</i>: the contrast within Mosaism is between <u>civil code</u> and <u>moral
issue</u>—between the <u>civil divorce</u> law found in Deuteronomy <b>24</b>
and the <u>real nature of marriage</u> indicated by Genesis <b>1-3</b> and
Malachi <b>2</b>:14-16. God hates divorce and considers the husband as dealing
treacherously with his wife.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        2.   <i>Between
Mosaism and the millennium</i>: the true Messianic kingdom will not be present
until the millennium because the Jews rejected Jesus when he came the first
time and so the church was parenthetically instituted meantime. Perfection is required
at that time, the time to which the Sermon on the Mount applies. Meantime,
during the parenthetical church age the perfect standard does not fully apply,
and there is greater freedom from the perfection principle.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        3.   <i>Between
Mosaism and Christianity</i>: Moses allowed divorce for any cause, but Christ
allows divorce for adultery.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              The
dispensational setting seems immaterial, because the principles that govern
marriage arise from the nature and purpose of marriage as a reflection of the
divine image (Genesis<b> 1</b>:26-27). Dispensational distinctions do not apply
to behavior that arises from the nature of the case; morality in general is
non-dispensational. Even in the Mosaic age a distinction should have been made
between Moses’ civil code and God’s moral intent; consequently, even if there
will be a future political phase of the Messianic Kingdom on earth, the present
church age already participates in the ideal of being perfect as God in heaven
is perfect. People are to be like the God in whose image they are created male
and female (Genesis <b>1</b>:27).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  C.  Content</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        1.   How “make
her an <u>adulteress</u>”?</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              Calling
the divorced woman an adulteress casts blame on her.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        2.   How “<u>make</u>
her an adulteress”?</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              Saying
that the husband <u>makes</u> her an adulteress casts blame on him.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>            </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              a.   <i>By
the way “he used” her</i>: interestingly, the Greek infinitive about the woman’s
adultery is passive: “. . . </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ποιεῖ</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>αὐτὴν</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>μοιχευθῆναι</span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>.” The clause appears to be translatable as <i>“he
causes her to be adulterated.”</i> Such a thing could be caused by putting her
in a situation where she has to remarry (see below) or by speaking in terms of
effect: he has lived with her and now sets her aside as a single woman who has
nevertheless lived with a man.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              b.   <i>By
treating her like an adulteress</i>: relative to God’s real intention for
marriage, there would be no divorce except for sexual impurity (<b>5</b>:32b). On
this background, if a man does put away his wife, he implies that she has been
unfaithful to him. The Greek verb </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ποιέω</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>might
carry the force here of <i>treat</i> (her like an adulteress) rather than <i>cause</i>
or <i>make </i>(her an adulteress). </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              Both <i>using
her</i> and <i>treating her as an adulteress</i> look backward for an explanation
of why Jesus calls her an “adulteress.” The following two approaches look
forward for an explanation of his terminology.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              c.   By
exposing her to prostitution as her only means of support</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                    Missionaries
to polygamous cultures have sometimes attempted to eradicate the polygamy by
having the converted husband discard all his wives except the first one. They
discovered that their policy created a worse situation than the polygamy itself,
because these women often had no means of support except through prostitution. A
person wonders whether a divorced wife in Judaism, however, would have
necessarily been as exposed to such a condition; she could in all likelihood
return to her father’s house.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              d.   By
her new marriage</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                    The
former husband causes her to commit adultery by putting her in a situation
where she will, or will have to, remarry as a means of support. In support of
this interpretation is (1) the statement that whoever marries the divorced
woman commits adultery (<b>5</b>:32c; cp. <b>19</b>:9c). Moreover, (2) in
Matthew <b>19</b>:9a the divorcing husband is said to commit adultery against
his former wife when he remarries.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              <i>Makes</i>
her an <i>adulteress</i> seems to affirm two things in tension with each other,
because it simultaneously implies blame on his part (<i>makes</i>) and hers as
well (<i>adulteress</i>). In a situation like this the interpreter suspects
that something is going on here beyond the first impression given of the words.
For the present, however, the following observations seem clear.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  D.  The main point</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        In the teaching
of Jesus <u>divorce cannot occur without sin being assigned somewhere</u>,
either on the part of the divorced or the divorcing spouse. Herein lies the
difference between Christianity and the civil marriage code of Mosaism. Legal
guilt was not assigned to divorce, and no legal stigma accompanied it. Moses
allowed divorce for any cause short of sexual immorality, which was a capital
offense punishable by stoning. Theoretically under Jewish law there would have
been no divorce for sexual unfaithfulness. A complicating practical factor in
the first century was that the Romans did not allow the Jews to carry out
capital punishment, but in Matthew <b>5</b> (and <b>19</b>) Jesus makes his
presentation in terms of Mosaic legislation. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;line-height:
18.0pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        Presumably Jesus’
presentation in Matthew <b>5</b> assumes the same guidelines he enunciated in
Matthew<b> 19</b>, where he goes back before the law to created origins. He
argues that the manner of creating man and woman, the situation God himself
originally established and the teaching of Genesis <b>2</b>:24, show God’s
intention for marriage. In fact, all New Testament teaching on men-women
relationships—whether directed against fornication, temple prostitution,
adultery, divorce, or role-relations in church processes—are referenced to this
same creation account. The character of marriage lies in the nature of things,
not in the law of things. It is not simply a matter of legally joining together
a man and a woman; it is a matter of naturally doing so. To put it another way,
ultimately <u>God</u> joins partners in marriage, not <u>law</u>. Human
operation under law—including the Mosaic Law—should not contravene divine
intent for the reality itself. <i>“What God has joined together</i> [naturally]
<i>let not people put asunder </i>[legally].<i>”</i></span></p>
