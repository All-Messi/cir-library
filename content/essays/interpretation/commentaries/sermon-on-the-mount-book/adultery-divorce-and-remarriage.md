<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.1in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>I.          Biblical considerations regarding the
possibility of remarriage</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.1in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.1in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            A.  1 Corinthians<b> 7</b>:27-28 is said
to validate remarriage because it says, <i>“If you [who are </i></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.1in;text-align:justify'><i><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  loosed from a wife] remarry, you
have not sinned,”</span></i><span style='font-size:12.0pt'> but</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.1in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.1in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  1.   “Loosed” could mean by the <u>death</u>
of the spouse rather than by the <u>divorce</u> of a </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.1in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        spouse.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.1in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  2.   In the immediate context Paul
is talking about virgins (<b>7</b>:25), not divorcees.                                               widow(er)s.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.1in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                *3.   <b>7</b>:28a contrasts with
female virgins (<b>7</b>:28b); hence, the preceding context (<b>7</b>:25-28a)
deals with male virgins; hence, “virgin” means virgin woman in contrast to
virgin man, not virgin in contrast to divorcee. “Loosed” can be taken as a
stative passive, rather than a simple passive (perfect passive, </span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>λέλυσαι</span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>)
that refers to <u>un</u>-married (never married [32-38] or widowed [39-40]) rather
than <u>dis</u>-married.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.1in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.1in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            B.  The human need for intimacy and sexual
satisfaction</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.1in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.1in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  1.   <i>“It is not good that man
should be alone”</i> (Genesis <b>2</b>:18).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.1in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  2.   <i>“It is better to marry than
to burn [with passion]”</i> (1 Corinthians <b>7</b>:9).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.1in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  3.   <i>“On account of fornication
let each man have his own wife and let each woman </i></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.1in;text-align:justify'><i><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        have her own husband”</span></i><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'> (1 Corinthians<b> 7</b>:2-5).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.1in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  4.   Not everyone can abide the
single state (Matthew<b> 19:</b>10-12; 1 Corinthians <b>7</b>:7).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.1in;text-align:justify'><b><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></b></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.1in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        Obviously, speaking of the
“need” for intimacy and sexual release does not mean speaking of a life need;
consequently, “need” amounts to saying “strong preference” or “natural
inclination.”</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.1in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        It is recognized that Jesus is
speaking here of the single state, but not abiding in the single state would
seem to be even harder after a person was married and experienced sexual
involvement, especially for the woman.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.1in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.1in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            C.  Remarriage of the “innocent party” is
apparently allowed (Matthew<b> 19</b>:9).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.1in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.1in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  Inasmuch as complete innocence
seldom, if ever, exists in divorce, “innocent party” means innocent of
unchastity (</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>πορνεία</span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>). Certain defects in the innocent spouse may
contribute to the other spouse’s unchastity without justifying it.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.1in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.1in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            D.  Legal separation was not an
ancient-world marital status.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.1in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.1in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  Without a specification like 1
Corinthians <b>7</b>:11 (<i>“If she is divorced, let her remain unmarried or be
reconciled to her husband”</i>), first-century Jewish and Gentile hearers would
not have assumed a point was being made about permanent separation for life;
there was no such institution in these cultures. The single state is not
desired in itself, although it might be more desirable than an unbearable marital
condition. Divorce does remove a person from an unacceptable identity, but with
no remarriage option divorce appears to “punish” the righteous with the wicked
by consigning them to being permanently single. Consequently, it is laid down
as a basic principle that what is grounds for divorce is, so to speak, grounds
for remarriage.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.1in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.1in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            E.   The exception clause of Matthew <b>5</b>:32
and <b>19</b>:9 is not necessarily exhaustive.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.1in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.1in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  1.   If only Mark <b>10</b>:11-12
and Luke <b>16</b>:18 existed, interpreters could assume Jesus had <u>no</u>
exception in mind. By parity of reason, the exception mentioned in Matthew <b>5</b>:32
and <b>19</b>:9 does not have to be any more exhaustive than the “unnamed
exception” in Mark and Luke. A stated item does not have to be restrictive in
intent or exhaustive in scope.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.1in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  2.   <i>“Not being under bondage”</i>
if the unbelieving spouse deserts (1 Corinthians <b>7</b>:15) evidently means
not being married any longer, that is, not feeling bound to honor a covenant
broken by the other spouse.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.1in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  3.   The one spouse (a) may not have
wanted the divorce, or (b) may not alone be able to provide for self and
children on a permanent basis—particularly if she is a woman with few
marketable skills outside the home. The children (c) may need a father figure,
not just for disciplinary reasons but for healthy, well-rounded psychological
development. (d) Abuse (physical, sexual, verbal, and otherwise both of the
spouse and the children), drunkenness and other chemical and psychological
addictions and dependencies, lack of physical and social provision, desertion,
homosexual bent, corrupting influences on the children, and the like on the
part of a spouse are as unbearable in a practical sense as having to live with
a sexually unfaithful partner would be; yet these matters are presumably not
addressed while only unchastity legitimizes divorce. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.1in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.1in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            F.   What is not addressed should not be
forbidden.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.1in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.1in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  A supposition for remarriage is set
by the previous considerations that remarriage is everywhere assumed, that
ancient culture knew nothing of permanent separation as a marital status, and
that human need normally requires sexual satisfaction. Unless there is clear
scriptural prohibition, remarriage in general should not be prohibited. Aside
from particular situations scripture may specify for not remarrying,
interpreters should be silent where scripture is silent. Not remarrying in 1
Corinthians <b>7</b>:11 should be limited to the circumstance it addresses.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.1in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.1in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>II.        “Let her remain unmarried or else be
reconciled” (1 Corinthians<b> 7</b>:11).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.1in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.1in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            A.  Circumstance</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.1in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.1in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  1.   Both spouses are Christian.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.1in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
-.75in'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              2.   The
statement is spoken from the viewpoint of the one initiating the divorce
(contrast <b>7</b>:15).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.1in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  3.   The directive deals with
divorce for reasons other than unchastity.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.1in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.1in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        a.   Remarriage for
unchastity, rather than remaining unmarried, is allowed in </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.1in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              Matthew <b>19</b>:9;
whereas Paul speaks here of remaining single.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.1in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
-1.0in'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                          b.   Presumably
then the divorce is for other than unchastity, perhaps for abuse, drunkenness,
or negative consequences of one spouse’s conversion to Christ.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.1in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.1in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            B.  The reason for Paul’s command to
remain single</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.1in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.1in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  1.   Because the previous marriage
was not dissolved in God’s eyes (?)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.1in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.1in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        a.   She is explicitly said to
be “unmarried.” “Still married in God’s eyes” confuses a specific case with God’s
general ideal. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.1in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        b.   The scripture says, <i>“What
God has joined together <u>let not</u> man put asunder”</i>; it does not say, <i>“What
God has joined together man <u>cannot</u> put asunder.”</i> If divorce could
not be done, a person wonders whether there would be any point in prohibiting
divorce.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.1in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        c.   Is not permanent
separation implicitly forbidden by 1 Corinthians <b>7</b>:5?</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.1in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        d.   Other reasons can be
given for remaining single (see next entries).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.1in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.1in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  2.   Because of the present distress
(?)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.1in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.1in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        Later in <b>7</b>:28b-38 (cp. <b>7</b>:8-9)
Paul does bring in his concern about the present distress as well as the
greater freedom in serving the Lord without distraction when unmarried. In that
section of the chapter, however, he is dealing <u>virgins and widows</u>—evidently
meaning older widows since in 1 Timothy <b>5</b>:11-15 he commands widows (at
least those under sixty—<b>5</b>:9) to remarry. For widows his reason for not
remarrying is that he thought they would be happier (1 Corinthians <b>7</b>:40).
</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.1in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.1in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  3.   Because of concern for
reconciliation</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.1in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.1in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        a.   To give opportunity for
adjustments by the divorcee</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.1in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        b.   To give opportunity for adjustments
by the divorcer—adjustments and</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.1in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              improvements in matters
that may, in fact, have aggravated the first marriage</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.1in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              Those who specialize in
dealing with marriage problems say that a person needs two or three years
before getting involved in another relationship. There is a general problem or
rushing into a new marriage on the rebound or out of an unconscious dependency.
</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.1in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.1in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        c.   If the other person
remarries, begins practicing fornication, or it becomes generally obvious that
nothing can be done to reconcile, then the possibility of remarriage becomes a
different matter.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.1in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.1in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  4.   To make clear that the divorce
was not <u>in order to</u> remarry</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.1in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.1in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        Paul pictures the one leaving
as initiating the divorce—in contrast to <b>7</b>:15, where </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.1in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        the divorce has been initiated
by the other person.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.1in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.1in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  5.   To allow the person to
re-establish himself instead of rushing into another </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.1in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        marriage.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.1in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.1in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        It takes a while for a person
to re-establish himself as an autonomous individual, </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.1in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        not referenced to a spouse.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.1in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.1in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>III.       The “innocent party” limitation (Matthew <b>19</b>:9)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.1in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.1in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            With the exception of innocent parties,
all remarried persons are called “adulterers”: (1) a spouse who divorces and
remarries when the former spouse was not guilty of </span><span lang=EL
style='font-size:12.0pt'>πορνεία</span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>; (2) the
person who marries a divorced person that divorced a former spouse that was not
guilty of </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>πορνεία</span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.1in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.1in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            A.  Except for 1 Corinthians <b>7</b>:11,
it is everywhere as much assumed that the divorced person will remarry as that
single widowed people will marry. Note II, E, 3 above and II, B below.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.1in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.1in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            B.  <i>“<u>Causes</u> her to commit <u>adultery</u>”</i>
(Matthew <b>5</b>:32) is said in respect to an innocent party, because the
exception clause accompanies the statement: except for unchastity if a man
divorces his wife and marries someone else, he <i>“causes her to commit
adultery.” </i>Not only does the verse assume remarriage by the wife who has
been wronged (“causes”), it labels her an “adulteress” when she does so. Jesus
evidently means, then, that in the theory of things remarriage under any
circumstance during the lifetime of a former mate is tantamount to adultery;
that is, it falls short of God’s ideal of one man for one woman for life. So to
speak, Jesus is using the word <i>adultery</i> with quotation marks around it;
he says it aside from dealing with all the practical issues involved in
divorce-remarriage that may make remaining single worse than the remarriage
that goes against the ideal nature and purpose for marriage.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.1in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  The only other possibility would be
to suppose that Jesus means by “causes” that, right or wrong,
characteristically she will in fact remarry, as if to say, <i>“She will die in
her sins, but her blood I will require at your hand.”</i></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.1in;text-align:justify'><i><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></i></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.1in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>IV. Practical prerequisites of remarriage</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.1in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.1in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            A.  <u>Repent</u> for your part in the
previous marriage failure.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.1in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.1in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  1.   Again, “innocent party” means
innocent of unchastity, but the innocent party can contribute to the other
spouse’s unfaithfulness by exhibiting a critical spirit, by insensitivity to
the partner’s sexual desires, by excessive absence from the family, by
unnecessary interference with disciple of the children, by lack of sufficient
attention to personal hygiene, by disinterest in the other’s pursuits, by
unwillingness to listen, by insistence on having one’s own way, and so on. These
admonitions apply regardless of whether a person was the innocent party or not.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.1in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.1in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  2.   Forgiveness that makes possible
a new beginning always assumes repentance.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.1in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.1in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        A note of warning is in order
for someone who contemplates divorcing a spouse. He should not count on being
able later to repent for having divorced the spouse or for “the roving eye”
that may be urging a divorce that would not otherwise be all that necessary. When
a person has later “just what he always wanted,” it is behaviorally impossible
to repent of having it or of doing what made having it possible.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.1in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.1in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            B.  <u>Commit</u> yourself to change
weaknesses.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.1in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.1in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  The divorce rate among the remarried
is much higher than in first marriages. The weaknesses that caused the first
divorce are obviously being carried right on into the next one—with the same
results.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.1in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.1in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            C.  <u>Marry</u> in the Lord (1
Corinthians <b>7</b>:39).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.1in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.1in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  Divorced people often remarry the
very type of person they had to divorce the first time. When they remarry heavy
drinkers, hot-tempered individuals, verbally abusive persons, they are going to
have the same problems all over again. When the newness of the relationship
wears off, the spouse will almost invariably return to behavior evidenced
before infatuation began.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.1in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  We take it that marrying in the Lord
is advice rather than moral commandment; that is, marrying a non-Christian in
not immoral in itself; otherwise, Paul could presumably not have encouraged
Christians to remain married to their pagan spouses. Nevertheless, to say the
least, it is not wise to marry someone who does not share a person’s values,
purposes, and moral commitments. A Christian who divorces a non-Christian
spouse because of character defects or immoral behavior should not expect
things to be any different the next time around with the same situation.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.1in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.1in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>Conclusion</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.1in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.1in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  Jesus’ teaching in the Sermon on the
Mount and in Matthew <b>19</b> have to do with the <u>nature</u> and <u>purpose</u>
of marriage. They deal theoretically with God’s intentional will for marriage. The
redemptive principle is brought to bear on the practical situation in the
context of God’s conditional will for marriage as Paul appears to do in 1
Corinthians <b>7</b> in regard to the complexities of human existence that
involve matters over which a person has no control.</span></p>

style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>
