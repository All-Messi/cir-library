<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='text-align:center'><b><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>Explanatory
Comments</span></b></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt;
line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>Categories put for <u>degrees</u></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt;
line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>            Languages tend
to word matters in two-term sets even though the referents are not exact
binaries and the speakers realize it. In an example like “it’s a tree or a
rock,” the two terms apply to <u>unrelated</u> categories. But people say
things like “good or bad” even though they know both are degree matters whose
labels come from the two ends of a continuum. The form of statement is
categorical even though the reality is continual.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt;
line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>            Hebrew has an
even greater tendency to use the binary format than English does, and does it
with a greater range of topics. One prime example is these love-hate expressions.
Here, category (form) is put for degree (referent). In Luke<b> 14</b>:26, Jesus
talks about loving him and hating family members and oneself. In this case, a
similar sentiment appears in Matthew <b>10</b>:37, <i>“He who loves father or
mother more than me is not worthy of Me; and he who loves son or daughter more
than Me is not worthy of Me”</i> (NASB). The passages laid beside each other
show that Jesus is talking about different degrees of love, but Luke’s account
words the point in categorical form under the paired words <i>love</i> and <i>hate</i>.
The Hebrew idiom would have been “to love x ‘from’ y.”</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>            A
similar approach applies to Jesus’ comment about serving two masters (Matthew<b>
6</b>:24; Luke <b>16</b>:13). Loving one does not in itself mean hating the
other. The more likely point is that an employee will prefer one boss over the
other. It is all the more so, of course, with a slave and two masters; a slave
situation would call for total and involvement with two masters, which is not
possible. The slave will do the will of one and “set aside” the will of the
other. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>            John
<b>12</b>:25 follows this format as well. Transcendent, eternal “life” takes
precedence over physical life (“in this world”). Category is put for degree.
After all, Paul says with approval, <i>“Nobody hates his own body, but feeds
and takes care of it”</i> (Ephesians <b>5</b>:29).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt;
line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>Opposites put for <u>alternatives</u></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify;text-indent:.5in'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>In
the Jacob-Esau instance, a similar element comes into play. In choosing one
rather than the other, something is said about what God does with the other. God
loved Jacob and “hated” Esau (Malachi<b> 1</b>:2-3). Likewise, is God’s
choosing David as Saul’s successor (1 Samuel <b>16</b>:1-13). The LORD
“refused” Eliab, Jesse’s oldest son (KJV). The parallels with the other
brothers say “not chosen.” In both instances, God’s choosing the one (David or Jacob)
necessarily means passing over, not choosing, the others (David’s brothers or Esau).
“Refuse/reject” sounds like a deliberate, active negativity toward, when the
real situation is more that David was a better choice. Opposites are put for
alternatives.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify;text-indent:.5in'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>Maybe
this same construct could apply to Ecclesiastes <b>3</b>:8, a time to love and
a time where love needs to be kept out of the situation, <i>i.e</i>., a time to
love and a time not to love. But the wording is handled as paired opposites
rather than alternatives: <i>“a time to love and a time to hate.” </i>Another
approach could adjust the meaning of “hate” from negative to neutral. Doing so would
call for adjusting the second category. The other entries in Ecclesiastes<b> 3</b>:1-8
do fit as paired opposites.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt;
line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt;
line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt;
line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>Binary terminology put for
<u>threes </u></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify;text-indent:.5in'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>Positive<sup>a</sup>-</span><sup><span
style='font-size:18.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>neutral</span></sup><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>-negative<sup>b
</sup>applies to situations with three possibilities, which can get handled verbally
as two. Moral-immoral may be the way people talk—and even think—about matters
that really require a three-part format as in moral-amoral-immoral. In addition
to the moral continuum of one extreme to another, another category parallel to that
continuum occurs in the real situation. What is amoral can carelessly get dumped
in with immoral and be considered equally objectionable. This last example
could apply to belief-unbelief-disbelief while scripture can say “not believe”
to both the second and third idea.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt;
line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>Supplementary notations</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>            Ancient
Hebrew had a <u>less replete vocabulary</u> than modern English. Cases put as
opposites (“not A but B”) may mean “not so much A as B.” Listeners are expected
to supply the distinction.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>            Applied
to non-personal matters (ideas and behaviors), <i>love</i> and <i>hate</i> can
carry their unqualified values: </span><sup><span style='font-size:14.0pt;
line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>“</span></sup><i><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>You’ve
loved goodness and hated lawlessness; so God, your God, has anointed you with
greater joy than your companions” </span></i><span style='font-size:12.0pt;
line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>(Hebrews <b>1</b>:9).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>            </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>            Love-hate
usage is part of a larger characteristic of Semitic usage: a tendency toward <u>binary
expression</u>. It shows up in the habit of (a) making an affirmation plus a
denial of its opposite: <i>“She will do him good and not evil all the days of
her life”</i> (Proverbs <b>31</b>:12); and (b) using parallelism as the primary
feature of poetry as well as in narrative and didactive material.        </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>            Binary
expression, in turn, is part of an even larger tendency toward <u>replete
expression</u> as seen in (a) specificity (“Hear <s>the sound of</s> his voice”
<i>vs</i>. “hear his voice” or even “hear him”) and (b) anaphoric use of
adjective, article, prepositions, possessives, and conjunctions.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:6.0pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>            All
of that in turn is part of Hebrew’s <u>pleonastic expression</u>, which to the
English ear is unnecessarily “wordy.”</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:6.0pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>            As
a couple final notes, (1) judgments on the examples above derive more from
reality than from wording about reality, which is a feature of language in
general. Sometimes as well, (2) statements occurring in <u>other</u> texts
serve as guides for adjusting first impressions on <u>this</u> text for
harmonization purposes. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
.5in'><span style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>Proper
interpretation comes from (1) <u>proper thinking</u> (rationality), which is
based on realities more than language: reality thinking. That is because language
is a loose fit for its referents and derives from them. Experience coupled with
revelation elsewhere in biblical interpretation supplies a basis for correct understanding.
Proper thinking comes from (2) having a grasp of <u>all relevant information</u>
and from (3) understanding <u>how language works</u> (linguistics). Those three
factors—especially the third—inform the above comments about <i>love</i> and <i>hate</i>
in Hebrew parlance.</span></p>
