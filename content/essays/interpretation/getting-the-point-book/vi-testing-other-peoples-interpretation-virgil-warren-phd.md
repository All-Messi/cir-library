<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><b><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'> VI. TESTING OTHER PEOPLE’S INTERPRETATION                   </span></b><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>Virgil Warren, PhD</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            <i>Interpretation is a community effort</i>.
In practice, Christians do not just individually interact with the scripture;
they interact with each other as well. For biblical interpretation purposes, we
need to understand how language works both in studying scripture and in
teaching and hearing the way of the Lord more perfectly. Theology as a
community effort includes studying the history of Christian thought; it
provides another resource for biblical understanding. It identifies the appropriate
role of tradition as teacher rather than authority. Christians are church
theologians, never professional theologians; they do something with what they
learn (education for use), and they do it in community with other people inside
and outside the body of Christ.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            Understanding the truth itself is not the
hardest part. The difficulty lies in (a) getting to that truth and in (b)
keeping truth distinguished from similar ideas. The sheer number of views gets
bewildering at times. For these reasons reading the Bible needs to happen with
other Christians who can make us aware of viewpoints and ways of thinking that
are crucial to proper understanding.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            When people discuss their variant
understandings of a text, they can be like two universes passing through each
other or two ships passing in the night. There is no meeting of minds because
neither person understands the other on their own terms, but hears them in
terms of their own categories, presuppositions, understanding of words, and
patterns of thought. When pride gets added in, interaction proves fruitless. On
the other hand, people can be too easily swayed by another person’s viewpoint
and arguments for it. As a result, the honest interpreter must listen both
critically and openly. The tests below strike a medium between being gullible
and being unchangeable.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify;text-indent:-.5in'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            [Note: An asterisk before capital letters
means something unique in testing other people’s interpretation.]</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify;text-indent:-.5in'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            A. The burden of proof rests on the
affirmative. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  The first point about evaluating any
viewpoint is keeping the burden of proof for it on the affirmative. Other
observations below take place within this principle. An interpretation is not true
because it cannot be disproved. It is not legitimate (1) to make a claim and
challenge other people to show what is wrong with it. It is also not legitimate
(2) to appeal to silence as a way of escaping the positive burden of proof or
relieving contradictions created by one’s scheme of interpretation. (cp. <i>What
the Bible Says About Salvation</i>, pp. 142-43.) The Great Dilemma is often
handled this way when another interpreter tries to appeal to God’s sovereignty
to escape having to explain the implications of 2 Peter <b>3</b>:9: God’s
desire for all to come to repentance plus God’s decision to enable only some to
come. It is not appropriate to say that scripture does not tell us how this
dilemma is resolved (= appeal to silence), to appeal to divine sovereignty, to
chastise someone for wanting revelation to conform to human pride (supposedly
indicated by the desire for biblical harmony). People must use sanctified
imagination at least to <u>suggest</u> ways for relieving difficulties created
by their own interpretation.  </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  Within this framework, the pattern
of working through an issue is as follows: (a) a <u>claim</u> made, (b) <u>evidence</u>
given for it, (c) <u>testing</u> whether the evidence requires that
interpretation.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  Accepting the positive burden of
proof contrasts with:</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify;text-indent:58.5pt'><span
style='font-size:10.0pt'>(a) <span style='font:7.0pt "Times New Roman"'>&nbsp;&nbsp;
</span></span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>making a claim that is considered
established unless someone can <u>disprove it</u>. People are doing this when
they say, <i>“Why not?”</i> This problem comes up, for example, in applying the
doctrinal test to people who claim to be prophets.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify;text-indent:58.5pt'><span
style='font-size:10.0pt'>(b) <span style='font:7.0pt "Times New Roman"'>&nbsp; </span></span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>making a claim that is considered established unless
someone can <u>propose an alternative explanation</u>. People do this when they
say, <i>“What else could it be?”</i></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify;text-indent:58.5pt'><span
style='font-size:10.0pt'>(c) <span style='font:7.0pt "Times New Roman"'>&nbsp;&nbsp;
</span></span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>making a claim that is considered
established because people have <u>provided evidence that fits with that
explanation</u>. People do this when they say, <i>“Doesn’t this <u>support</u>
it?”</i></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  We should not feel pressured to
disprove other people’s doctrinal claims. At most our task is to show that the
basis is inadequate. Then we can propose our own claim with adequate evidence
for it. Seldom can an interpretation be disproved satisfactorily. Showing that
one position is true eliminates all other positions including the one
originally presented. Sometimes, of course, one person’s understanding amounts
to an aspect of another’s view or <i>vice versa</i>; so they are not mutually
exclusive. Sometimes each idea comprises a different part of the same complete
picture; so the difference is resolved, not by absolutizing one or the other,
but by making them complementary. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  Below are three techniques for
dealing with evidence from passages misused to get mistaken positions:</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify;text-indent:-.5in'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                    (a)  Show that <u>another
way</u> exists for understanding the passage. To eliminate any concept, a
person needs only to provide another reasonable alternative. If a passage fits
more than one viewpoint, it does not establish either of them. It becomes
secondary, or confirmatory, evidence (see below). One reason for seeing whether
the data can be accounted for by more than one reconstruction is that if it
could either way, it eliminates treating it as primary evidence for either
position. It becomes as a matter of opinion, or another starting point for the
view must be found. If no other starting point can be found, the matter stands
unresolved. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        (b) Show that the idea is <u>foreign</u>
to the flow of thought. An idea relevant to the context is more likely correct.
In offering alternatives and noting an idea’s irrelevance, an interpreter is
showing that there is no basis for <u>introducing</u> the proposal into the
picture.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        (c)  Show that the
interpretation is <u>contradictory</u> to the near or distant context or to the
nature of the case. Here an interpreter is disproving the idea by shouldering
the negative burden of proof and can thus show reason for <u>removing</u> the
proposal. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              Different interpretations
usually cannot be proved or disproved from individual passages, because that is
not where they came from. They came from general viewpoints based elsewhere.
From a practical standpoint, the task may be first to remove each piece of evidence
by providing another legitimate interpretation. Next comes the call for
justifying the general perspective from which inference was made about this
passage. To be effective and efficient though, interpreters do better to state
evidence for the underlying perspective, because no resolution of specific
interpretations can occur without doing that. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  The burden of proof always rests on
the affirmative because the test of an interpretation is the test of
consistency, which is another expression for the “law of harmony”; positions
not capable of positive demonstration become opinion. Because more than one
interpretation might satisfy the consistency test, interpreters must shoulder their
burden of proof when they choose one affirmation over another.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  <u>Minimum affirmation</u>
corresponds with positive burden of proof. We should not claim more than we can
substantiate. The positive burden of proof relates to the law of parsimony. If
six factors can seemingly account for a process, that does not disprove there
are more than six; it means only that a seventh factor need not be introduced.
If, for example, laws of nature can account for the present world, God is <u>not
necessary</u> even though his existence is <u>not disproved</u>.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  From available information, we may
not be able to disprove there are men on Mars, but that does not mean we has to
believe somebody’s claim to that effect without adequate evidence. A wife might
accuse her husband of being unfaithful. He is not apt to succeed at disproving
her claims and quieting her fears. In general, accusations of moral turpitude
are especially insidious because they involve private sins where there are no witnesses.
So, jurisprudence operates on the principle that a person is presumed innocent
until proven guilty.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  Some issues associated with Holy
Spirit matters put us in this position. Someone who claims to speak with
tongues may try to get us to try it, saying that it will be a self-authenticating
experience. One person cannot deny another person’s experience, but the second
person is in as good a position as the first when it comes to equating that
experience with what the New Testament means by tongue speaking or the “baptism
in the Spirit.” There is often the claim that all Christians truly converted
will speak in tongues as a sign of God’s accepting them. All we can do in such
cases is ask for the biblical evidence; ambiguous experiences do not qualify as
evidence.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  Other examples that cannot individually
bear the positive burden of direct proof include the following:</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        the belief that <u>feet washing</u>
is an ordinance of the church. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        that <u>sex is for procreation
always</u>, never for marital pleasure alone.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify;text-indent:-76.5pt'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                                  that
after divorce <u>God still views the former spouse as the divorcee’s spouse</u>
</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify;text-indent:-76.5pt'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                                        even
after one or both remarry. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        that a person’s day of death
is prescribed by God (note Job <b>14</b>:5). </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        that each person has a
specific task God wants that person to do in the </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              kingdom.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        that fasting can be only for
religious purposes. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        that Jesus was a created being.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        the perpetual virginity of
Mary (Matthew <b>1</b>:25 “till” does not work well; cp. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              <b>28</b>:20).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        the official administration of
baptism.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        a second type of tongue
speaking.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>These items and most others that seem never to get
resolved are really implications of general ideas that are not recognized, and
there is no hope of solving the specifics without dealing with the general
ideas.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  For an illustration of how bearing
the positive burden of proof applies to the <u>form of Christian baptism</u>,
see <i>What the Bible Says About Salvation</i>, pp. 322-39.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  Maintaining the positive burden of
proof is especially important in dealing with <u>positive commandment</u>
because a person cannot check his work against the nature of the case. Matters
of positive commandment do not arise from the nature of the case. (Note <i>What
the Bible Says About Salvation</i>, p. 396.)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  Maintaining the positive burden of
proof is also important in evaluating views of <u>predictive prophecy</u>. That
is the case because the hearer cannot verify the view by experience. A major
problem with systems of eschatology is that they are not falsifiable because
they deal with the future so they must be held as opinions. More of the total
system comes from the interpreters than from the biblical materials they cite.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  The point regarding <u>evolution <i>vs</i>.
creation</u> is not whether a person can believe in evolution despite the
fossil evidence. The question is whether there is adequate evidence for any
position that can then be defended against “holes” in the evidence or against “apparently
conflicting information.”</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  The <u>moralizing view of history</u>
is hard to disprove from life experience itself.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  In the area of <u>divine guidance</u>,
it is hard to disprove from experience or the Bible that God has a specific
will for each person at each point along the way.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  There is the idea that religious <u>fasting</u>
is not a deliberate act but a natural response only.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  The six-day, 24-hour understanding
of Genesis <b>1</b> is a case where the burden of proof can get shifted from
the positive to the negative. People who are not convinced that is the point of
Genesis <b>1</b> are called on to <u>disprove</u> the 24-hour reading. Along
with this pattern of thought goes the questionable principle of taking literal
over figurative unless the literal is not possible—another example of calling
for the negative burden of proof. The real primary interpretation principle is
authorial intent.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  The only way to deal with such
issues is to let their advocates attempt to sustain their position and then
show that each point of their evidence can consistently be understood differently.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  The positive burden of proof must be
borne by <i>primary proof vs.</i> <i>confirmatory evidence</i>. Primary proof
means what <u>must</u> be the conclusion; confirmatory evidence means what <u>may</u>
be the conclusion. In popular parlance, confirmatory evidence is often expressed
by saying that something “supports” or “is in keeping with” a viewpoint.
Conclusions built on primary evidence are matters of faith and fact;
conclusions built on confirmatory evidence are matters of opinion. Any
ambiguous situation can serve to illustrate secondary evidence</span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:windowtext'>.</span><span style='font-size:12.0pt;
color:red'> </span><span style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>For example, a
neighbor once carried our garbage cans up from the curb and put them a few feet
from our house. The cans had been by the curb for two days. Was he being
helpful, or was he expressing disapproval of our leaving the trash cans out
there too long? The situation was unclear, so we did not make inferences.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  Primary proof and confirmatory
evidence correlate with <i>faith </i>and<i> opinion</i>. <i>Opinion</i> refers
to what cannot be shown for certain. In the interests of Christian unity, it
may be well to add at least another category and call it “fact” so that the
total set becomes <i>faith, fact, </i>and <i>opinion</i>. Fact can refer to
matters that are clear but are not such that would affect salvation, fellowship,
or ministry. One obvious justification for saying that things equally true are
not equally important comes from Jesus’ own teaching in Matthew <b>5</b>:19: <i>“Whoever
breaks one of these least commandments . . . will be called least in the
kingdom of heaven.”</i> The same implication applies to the terminology “First”
and “Second” Great Commandment (Matthew <b>22</b>:34-40). Confirmatory evidence
correlates with silence and opinion.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  As matters that are <u>hermeneutically
certain</u> include faith and fact, so also matters that are <u>hermeneutically
uncertain</u> (opinion) include personal preference and cultural pattern.                                                                                                    </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  One complicating factor here is the <i>Semitic
tendency not to</i> <i>distinguish primary proof and confirmatory evidence</i>.
Readers must determine which type of evidence a statement is</span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>. For example, Jesus said that a true
prophet does not make merchandise of people. Possibly a false prophet might
have a selfless demeanor even though his teaching is not true. It is one thing
to say that a true prophet has this characteristic; it is quite another to say
that one who has this characteristic is a true prophet. The qualification seems
to be, not a final test, but a negative test to be laid alongside others. Other
possibilities are that Jesus was stating a general observation rather than an
absolute rule or that he was talking about right attitude rather than right
content. </span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>As a technique for determining
whether evidence is primary or confirmatory, see whether <u>the point appealed
to can fit with another viewpoint</u>.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  A person can use the <i>argument of
silence</i> legitimately against a failure to present primary evidence. In such
a case, argument from silence observes that there is no primary proof in the
data. Silence can point out the lack of primary proof. Silence can eliminate
proposals that violate the nature of the case. In organizational theory, for
example, silence about offices other than the ones specified eliminates them <i>de
facto</i> if the body of literature provides a complete picture and if New
Testament church organization, for example, was intended to set precedent.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  When we speak of the positive burden
of proof, <i>we are speaking of the position taken</i> <i>rather than the form
of the statement made in taking it.</i> A proposition may be either positive or
negative as far as verbiage is concerned. But when someone verbalizes it, the
one who makes a claim bears the responsibility for showing it is true. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  <i>Some things may not be directly
falsifiable</i>. In bearing the positive burden of proof, we must often
establish the basis for it by proving the logically prior point(s) on which it
rests; so, the person must at least be able (a) to assimilate more of the data
into his system harmoniously than other options can do, (b) to point out the
inadequacies of alternative views, or (c) to show that the data for alternative
positions are capable of other interpretations.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  Opinion and confirmatory evidence
bring up the point that the theological task has two aspects: (1) determining
positively what we ought to believe and (2) establishing the boundaries of
variation in what we should believe. That is so because the Bible student
cannot always decide what the truth is on some questions. There is no primary
proof in some cases; they are matters of likelihood. In such cases, the
theological task is not over with the verdict that no conclusion can be reached.
The task remains of calculating the possibilities that do not infringe on
things that are certain. One boundary is pragmatic: when a view implies
behavior contrary to God’s express command, it has pushed past the acceptable
boundary of variation. To whatever level of exactness scripture speaks,
theology is free to go; at whatever level of exactness scripture speaks, theology
is responsible for establishing the range of variation.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            *B. Illustrations do not prove.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  The fact that an illustration can be
thought of does not mean it proves anything. A distinction between <i>illustration</i>
and <i>example</i> applies here. An example is a case that serves as a
specimen. An illustration is something of a different kind that shares certain
characteristics with the matter of interest. <i>“He is as strong as an ox”</i>
illustrates how strong someone is; it does not say anything about his
intelligence. Finding some parallels between two things does not mean the
second is an example of the first.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  </span><span style='font-size:12.0pt;
color:#7030A0'>**The trinity is like a triangle or an iceberg with three parts
sticking out of the </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                       water.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                  **The New Testament canon
is like a walled city. But someone may say, <i>“But cities have suburbs,”</i> with
the implication that there are degrees of canonicity. The first person could
respond<i>, “This is an eastern city: outside its walls there is nothing but
desert.”</i> That kind of banter settles nothing about the canon. It may show
what each person thinks, but it does not show that either idea is right.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                  **Jesus made a
statement about not taking food for the children and giving it to the family’s
dog. The Syro-Phoenician woman responded by saying that the dogs can at least
have the crumbs. Her comment carried forward the legitimacy of her request
because Jesus’ response did not prove he should not help her.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  Changing the “terms” of another
person’s illustration does not discredit his point any more than the use of the
illustration proves it. Illustrations create an affective impression, convey a
cognitive point about the unknown, or make something concrete. Illustrations do
not prove; they illustrate. Illustrations show what the presenter means, not
whether it is true. For example, someone might illustrate the notion of moral
imperative by saying that a craftsman making bookshelves ought to use nails
instead of glue. The listener has not disproved the moral imperative by saying,
<i>“But he could have used screws.”</i> The listener has simply changed the
”terms” of the illustration, not disproving the truth of the illustrate.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  So much arguing about scripture amounts
to a battle of wits over illustrations.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            *C.  Check to see whether the right model
is being used.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  Comparison between two different
things mean there are some likenesses and some differences. One common thinking
problem comes from transferring from the model an element that does not
correspond with an element in the thing modeled. An apparent argument rests on
an aspect that is unlike the thing pictured.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  Effective presentation appeals to an
example in people’s experience (a real thing) rather than appealing to an
illustration of one kind of thing by another (an ideal thing).</span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                                                          </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                  **Human beings can
take only so much; when they break, they are irreparable. They are like a tree
uprooted by a strong wind and cannot be set upright again.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                  </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            *D. Do not decide the correctness of an
interpretation by whether it fits with what you already believe.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  Getting into that habit conditions us
to agree with what we already believe. If we are wrong, we multiply our errors
and get accustomed to sloppy thinking. Other people will notice this tendency
and be more cautious about listening to us.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  </span><span style='font-size:12.0pt;
color:#7030A0'>**Someone that believes in single-action immersion should not, for
example, use the expression “one baptism” in Ephesians <b>4</b>:5 to argue
against trine baptism or multiple modes of baptism. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                  **To avoid belief in
modern-day tongue speaking, many people have accepted the view that “the
perfect” in 1 Corinthians <b>13</b>:10 refers to the completed canon, an idea
foreign to Paul’s line of thought in the text. That same motive has caused them
to use Acts <b>8</b>:18-24 to argue that only the apostles could pass on the
ability to impart supernatural gifts.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            E. Check to see about the intended
reference.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  Frame of reference can be a problem
of <u>part-to-whole confusion</u>. An example is Paul’s use of the Old
Testament in connection with (a) veil-wearing or (b) the female-deference
principle. Frame of reference can also be a <u>problem of two categories
terminologically connected</u>. Baptism can be looked on as a legal-category
matter or an interpersonal-category one. Reading texts about baptism does not
automatically clarify whether the act is in one category or the other. We must
decide that from considerations elsewhere.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;

someone’s explanation because you cannot </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            G. Your inability to explain a difficulty
between scriptures does not mean they</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  contradict. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  On the assumption that scripture
does not err, reaching a conclusion that creates contradiction serves warning
that something has gone wrong in the interpretation process. The situation
calls for tracing back through our work to discover the fallacy. Contradictions
can represent lack of internal consistency or lack of correspondence with
external facts. These problems can be “corrected” by assigning them to
transmissional error or hermeneutical mistake.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  Imagination, creativity, speculation,
and such serve a positive role by removing hindrances to believing and obeying.
(a) Speculation does not establish the truth itself but makes it believable to
honest hearts. (b) Speculation does not have to find <u>the</u> answer to
apparent discrepancies; it needs only to propose a credible solution that
restores confidence in the biblical record and the Christian worldview. (c) Speculation
does not establish tests of fellowship, because the postulations of sanctified
imagination are extra-biblical content and therefore matters of opinion. Speculations
are developed to relieve one’s own mental conflict and to help others who
accept them for their own confidence about the Christian faith. Sanctified
imagination justifies God to his servants.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  1. Contradictions are resolved by <u>appealing
to first principles</u> (nature of the case). The reality itself (referent)
provides the criteria for sorting through models and word meanings. In our
efforts at carrying out systematic theology, we have relied many times on the
characteristics of interpersonal relationship.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        a. <i>Words have multiple
meanings</i>. Interpreters create contradictions by equivocating terms that mean
different things in different contexts. We can resolve apparent discrepancies
by checking context to make sure that the same subject is in view, that the
same time period is referred to, that the same people are addressed.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              </span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>**Luke <b>12</b>:51: <i>“Do not think
that I came to give peace in the earth.”</i> +</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                              John <b>14</b>:27:
<i>“My peace I give you.”</i></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                              **Galatians
<b>6</b>:2: <i>“Bear one another’s burdens.”</i> +</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                              Galatians <b>6</b>:5:
<i>“Each person will bear his own burden.”</i></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:1.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify;text-indent:-.25in'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>b. <span style='font:7.0pt "Times New Roman"'>&nbsp;&nbsp;
</span></span><i><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>Words may be used figuratively</span></i><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify;text-indent:58.5pt'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>          There are two levels of usage with a word: (a)
it may refer <u>to one idea in contrast to another</u> (Does “for” [</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ὑπέρ</span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>, <i>hyper</i><u>]</u>
in connection with the atonement, <i>e.g</i>., mean “as a substitute for” or
“in order to help”?); it may refer to the thing itself or to something analogous.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              </span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>**Revelation <b>14</b>:10 says the
wicked will be tortured in the presence of the angels and the Lamb; yet God
will wipe away all tears from the eyes of the righteous (Revelation <b>7</b>:17;
<b>21</b>:4). The passage brings up incompatible imageries. How could the
righteous saved have no tears at seeing evil loved ones tortured? This case may
be an example of imprecatory apocalyptic (cp. the imprecatory Psalms). The idea
involves exaggerated imagery to stress the negativeness of the situation.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                              **Hell contains
fire and brimstone (Revelation <b>14</b>:10; <b>19</b>:20; <b>20</b>:10; <b>21</b>:8);
yet hell elsewhere is comparable to darkness outside (Matthew <b>8</b>:12; <b>22</b>:13;
<b>25</b>:30). Besides, the resurrected body is a spiritual body. Incompatible
figures can refer to compatible realities.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                              **Relative
to atonement theory <i>What the Bible Says About Salvation</i>, pp. 68-69,
shows how incompatible metaphors can refer to compatible realities.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                              **See <i>What
the Bible Says About Salvation</i>, p. 26, for an application of figurative
interpretation as a way of avoiding contradiction created by the concept of
inherited guilt.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  2. Contradictions can be resolved by
putting disparate items in a <i>larger frame of reference</i>.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        The apparent contradiction can
come from taking something as an absolutely that was meant relatively—within a
limited framework. The larger frame of reference sufficiently qualifies the
individual statements that they become compatible.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  3. Contradictions are resolved by <u>using
sanctified imagination</u>.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        A person postulates ways to
resolve seeming contradictions but does not make such postulations binding on
other people.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        </span><span style='font-size:
12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>**God had promised Abraham that in Isaac his seed would
be called (Genesis <b>21</b>:12). Later, he commanded Abraham to sacrifice him
on an altar atop Mount Moriah (Genesis<b> 22</b>:1-19). Abraham used sanctified
imagination to justify God for issuing a command that contradicted a promise. He
supposed that after he sacrificed Isaac, God would resurrect him from the dead
(Hebrews <b>11</b>:17-19).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                        **The problem of
free will <i>vs</i>. foreknowledge and predestination (cp. <i>What the Bible
Says About Salvation</i>, p. 181.)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  4.   “contradiction” between
scripture and experience</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        Somewhat akin to
contradictions are cases of apparent conflict between the </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        Bible and experienced reality.
</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        </span><span style='font-size:
12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>**<i>“Pray without ceasing” </i>(1 Thessalonians <b>5</b>:17)
is not possible. In this example we could <u>redefine the word</u> <i>pray</i>
to <i>“being in an attitude of prayer,”</i> where supplicants could enter into
prayer at any moment without finding it incongruous with what is happening in
their lives. It would amount to doing everything with a vertical implication so
that prayer becomes the whole of life. Better, the reader could <u>play on the
aspect of action</u> in the verb; it would mean iterative action—a Christian
should maintain a regular practice of prayer <i>vs.</i> continuing prayer. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                        **The lack of
supernatural experience—resurrection—does not invalidate the possibility of it
if there is sufficient causation beyond the natural realm (note larger frame of
reference). Since experience is not universal, sometimes we can reread
experience to make universal knowledge compatible with that experience
(omniscience).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                  </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        In summary, apparent
contradictions may be resolved by checking to see </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        whether</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              a. the <u>word</u> has
shifted meaning.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              b. one statement is
figurative and the other literal.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              c. the statements
address different people.     </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              d. the comments appear
in different dispensations.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              e. one statement is
practical <u>advice</u> and the other theological </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                       <u>commandment</u>.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              f. both statements are
in contrast to the same thing.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              g. one statement is a <u>generalization</u>,
the other an <u>absolute uniformity</u>; one may be a degree matter that is
worded in absolute terms.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              h. there is a manuscript
problem.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              i. there is a difference
between English and biblical idiom at this point.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            *H. Understanding another person does not
mean agreeing with the person.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  What is understandable is not
necessarily true. There is a natural series comprised of knowing about, understanding,
believing, and doing. Being understandable requires internal consistency, but it
does not require conformity to reality. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  Willingness to listen is
prerequisite to being able to correct someone (or to be corrected), because
listening is prerequisite to understanding. People tend to accept what they
understand. Knowing this, they are afraid to listen for fear that if they
understand, they will accept what they do not believe now and end up being led
astray.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  For practical purposes, there may be
a difference between the pattern of presenting an idea to someone else and the
pattern of getting to that idea. We do not want to adopt for our own <u>exegetical
pattern</u> the <u>presentation pattern</u> used in teaching. A presenter often
states the conclusion and spends time describing it <u>before</u> offering the
reasons for it. As hearers, we need to distinguish these two patterns.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            I.  Do not try to refute <u>while</u> you
are trying to understand (cp. John <b>8</b>:37).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>          *J. Evaluating an interpretation is largely
the same process as coming to our </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        own conclusion.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        1. The basic question is
whether a viewpoint represents a <u>valid</u> derivation </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              from this passage.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        2. The second question is
whether a viewpoint is <u>consistent</u> within itself </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              and with other sure
facts.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:1.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify;text-indent:-1.25in'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        3. The third question is
whether it is <u>practical</u>, that is, whether it fits with experience,
whether it will work.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            K. Distinguish between truth and getting
it from <u>this</u> passage.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  We tend to accept an interpretation
if the idea makes sense. We need to distinguish between what is true and what
is meant in this passage. We should not agree with an argument for a conclusion
because the conclusion is true; such thinking fosters imprecise thinking, and
that will “catch up with us” later because we will not be corrected if we are
wrong. So much “interpretation” is merely attaching ideas people think are
right to passages that have nothing to do with them. This can happen when
people try to “pad their proof.” It does not help a viewpoint to multiply
proofs by adding numbers of irrelevant passages. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  Getting in the habit of associating
true ideas with irrelevant passages allows us to get sloppy in his thinking. The
time will come when an idea read into a text is not in fact true, but it is a
comfortable idea that sounds like a biblical one; and so it is acted on and
passed on to other people. It is like the notion that a belief or practice is
true and good if “it intends to honor Jesus.”</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  </span><span style='font-size:12.0pt;
color:#7030A0'>**“Mutual submission” may express an important attitudinal
principle in interpersonal relationships, but it is more than doubtful that
Ephesians <b>5</b>:21 has this in view. In fact, it is likely the opposite of
Paul’s point. Mutual submission would better come from Philippians <b>2</b>:3, <i>“.
. . in lowliness of mind, each counting the other better than himself.”</i></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                  **We might argue that
Acts <b>1</b>:6 is an imperatival future, <i>“And you will be my witnesses. . .
.”</i> Jesus does command his apostles to preach to the uttermost parts of the
earth; but it is more likely that this text simply makes a prediction, because
the statement combines with a prediction about receiving the Holy Spirit.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            L. Distinguish between what makes sense
and what scripture teaches. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  Interpretation gets a message from
the text and deals with how we do it. People simply describe what they believe
rather than show why a text means that. Description does not prove. The catch
is that what a person thinks is true may not be true. It may make sense to say
that water applied one way rather than another has no ability to wash away sin,
but that is not the issue. What God intended the action of baptism to be is the
issue. Baptism is a positive commandment; consequently, the reason for applying
water one way rather than another cannot necessarily be given. Interpretation
does not deal with the power of an idea or even its accuracy, but with the
intended meaning of a text. The issue is not how <u>reasonable</u> something
seems, but why it is <u>true</u>. Apologetics should not be confused with
hermeneutics.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  Akin to confusing interpretation and
truth is confusing what God <u>can</u> do and what he <u>does</u> do. What he <u>does</u>
do relates to the interpretation enterprise because interpretation deals with
statements like promises. People learn what God promises by interpreting his
word aright. For example, the question is not whether God can heal all
Christians from their diseases, but whether he has promised to do so.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            M. Do not adjust your own interpretation
of a passage so it will eliminate someone else’s unacceptable view on something
else.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  Adjustment can come in the forms of
overreaction, overkill, and “eisegesis,” which means reading into a passage
something the interpreter needs to find somewhere. <b>Extremism is one big
plague in biblical interpretation</b>. Ulterior motives create the pendulum
effect; and the results do not increase understanding but confusion. Forced
interpretation is like having shoes on the wrong feet.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  Reactionary exegesis illustrates the
pendulum effect. It is not to be confused with the principle that truth tends
to lie between the extremes. We should not let ourselves get into “grasping for
straws,” “reaching,” and “overdoing it.”</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  </span><span style='font-size:12.0pt;
color:#7030A0'>**To avoid the use of 1 Corinthians <b>14</b> to advocate modern
tongue-speaking, some have suggested that the gifts exercised in the Corinthian
church were not legitimate manifestations of the Spirit. Instead of coming
right out and calling them illegitimate activities, Paul supposedly dealt with
them pastorally—a form of accommodation. He laid down such guidelines for their
use that these pseudo “gifts” could not be continued.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                  **Some especially
extreme views have been proposed for “baptism for the dead” in 1 Corinthians <b>15</b>
to avoid the admitted misuse of that text to baptize living people for dead
ancestors. Salvation is personal.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                  **To avoid the
conclusion that baptism is associated with salvation (rather than, say, church
membership or testimony to others), exegetes have put forward several curious
interpretations for the expression “born of water” in John <b>3</b>:5. “Water” supposedly
refers to amniotic acid that surrounds the unborn baby, to male semen, to a
symbolic representation of the Hold Spirit.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:10.0pt'>                  </span><span style='font-size:12.0pt;
color:#7030A0'>**The idea is proposed that the “cross” was a stake, so as to
avoid using it as an object of adoration. The idea may have come from the
ancient derogatory description of Jesus as “the son of the stake.”</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                  **Originating the idea
that <i>“husband of one wife”</i> meant that elders and deacons had to be
married came up in Alexander Campbell’s debate with a celibate Roman Catholic bishop
in Cincinnati.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                  **<i>“Baptism in the
Spirit” </i>is said by some to have occurred only at Pentecost and at the
household of Cornelius, which avoids certain evangelical and Pentecostal views
associated with the term.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                  **Non-instrumentalists
have developed the concept that silence in matters of worship equals
prohibition primarily, it would seem, to eliminate what they consider excesses.
During the Reformation, Calvin did something of the same thing in contrast to
Luther.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                  **ERA advocates have
developed in response to discrimination in the job market and have in the
process created more confusion than the good they could otherwise have done.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                  **Acts <b>8</b>:18
evidently provides an illustration of overreaction. Those looking for arguments
for the cessation of miracles at the end of the apostolic age read this verse
as a generality statement: the transmission of miraculous gifts comes only through
the imposition of apostolic hands. However, Simon the sorcerer saw only this
one occasion; so it is not clear that Luke is reporting Simon’s observation as
a generality. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:10.0pt'>                  </span><span style='font-size:12.0pt;
color:#7030A0'>**Reaction against child abuse has brought into vogue objecting
to corporal punishment in any form or degree. Christians who have attempted to
assimilate this idea into their child-rearing philosophy are faced with the
clear proverb usually paraphrased, <i>“Spare the rod and spoil the child” </i>(Proverbs
<b>13</b>:24; cp. <b>22</b>:15; <b>23</b>:13-14; <b>29</b>:15). One attempt to
get around this has been to say that the rod was used by shepherds to guide the
sheep along as they drove them from place to place; so the passage, taken
figuratively, says that a child needs to be guided as he grows up; and if he
is, he will not depart from that guidance later (Proverbs <b>13</b>:24; cp.<b>
22</b>:15; <b>23</b>:13-14;<b> 29</b>:15). </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                        In the first
place, this interpretation fails to distinguish the rod from the shepherd’s
staff (<i>“Thy rod and thy staff comfort me”</i>). The crook on the end of the <u>staff</u>
was what the shepherd would use to corral errant lambs. The rod would be used
to beat off predators. But more importantly, the interpretation assumes the
uniform meaning of words in different specialized settings. In a statement
about child-rearing, “rod” does not mean the same thing as it does in a
statement about shepherding. The only rod involved in child-rearing is the rod
used for corporal discipline. The only rod involved in shepherding is the rod used
for protecting the sheep. Neither specialized context indicates any intention
of connecting one setting with the other. People who start from a non-biblical
frame of reference often stand distant from the biblical material they try to use
to reinforce their presentations.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                  **Another example of
overreaction is the denial of individual predestination as a way of reacting
against Calvinistic predestination.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                  **1 Corinthians <b>13</b>:8-10
has been overused by both sides of the Pentecostal controversy. The “perfect”
has been defined as the completed New Testament canon by opponents of tongue
speaking and as the second coming of Christ by those who practice glossolalia.
Apparently “the perfect” refers to features of the next life <i>vs</i>. the
features of the present one, features that begin at an individual’s death.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:10.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  To eliminate a false view of an
ambiguous passage, we do not have to present an alternative. All that is
necessary is to propose a view equally fitting the words without necessarily advocating
it. To avoid a false viewpoint derived from an ambiguous text, we need only to
show that the position is <u>unnecessary</u>, not that it is <u>false</u>. The
temptation is to make the meaning more different from our own belief than the
passage allows. “Polemic expediency” means creating arguments against a
position by applying irrelevant observations as if they belonged to the
discussion. Extremes beget extremes.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  </span><span style='font-size:12.0pt;
color:windowtext'>Keeping the burden of proof on the affirmative avoids the
overreactions in M.</span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'> Overreaction <i>gets
the dogmatic question ahead of the exegetical one. </i>A person puts a
conclusion out ahead of the reasoning pattern and then works toward it.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            N. Do not <i>get the practical problem
ahead of the exegetical one</i>.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify;text-indent:22.5pt'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>          The exegetical question comes first because
exegesis does not deal with truth itself but with the meaning of communication.
In our zeal to solve a practical problem, we may misread texts in the direction
of our need.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            O.  Do not accept a view because of the
people that hold it—people with education, a friend, people who has admirable
Christian life quality, people that are famous, people that are eloquent (1
Corinthians<b> 1</b>:18-31), people that can get their material into print.
Likewise, do not adopt an idea because it is a majority viewpoint or a popular belief.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify;text-indent:-.75in'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              P. Make a certain amount
of allowance for people mis-expressing themselves. What they say may be false
understood as their words should be taken, but experience shows that people do
not necessarily mean what their words say.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            Q. Be willing to listen to people who have
previously been wrong on other points.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  Being wrong about one thing does not
mean being wrong about everything. </span><span style='font-size:12.0pt;
color:#7030A0'>In Acts <b>18</b>:24-28, Apollos needed to know the way of the
Lord “more perfectly.”</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            R. Do not demand a specific verse for
every item of Christian understanding.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  Viewpoints often come from
combinations of passages or from necessary inferences from other factors. Demanding
chapter and verse makes an inappropriate demand for proof. The Bible is not a
reference book—dictionary, encyclopedia, catechism, or fix-it manual, which does
not depend on context for meaning. So, a person’s view can come from a stream of
thought not expressed “in a nutshell.”</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            S. Distinguish between describing an idea
and proving it.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  Describing an idea is different from
showing that it is true. Giving information that fits with an idea is different
from getting to the idea in the first place. Showing the practical value of an
idea is different from showing that the Bible teaches it.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  For practical purposes, there may be
a difference between the pattern of presenting an idea to someone else and the
pattern of getting to that idea. We do not want to adopt for our own <u>exegetical
pattern</u> the <u>presentation pattern</u> often used in teaching. For clarity
purposes, a presenter may state the conclusion and spend time describing it <u>before</u>
he offers the reasons for it. As listeners, we need to distinguish these two
patterns so we can listen accordingly.           </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><b><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></b></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            A general difficulty in evaluating the
ideas of other people is keeping a proper connection between the idea and the
source of that idea. On the one hand, we do well to be cautious about ideas
brought forward by people whose positions in general are suspect; their
inaccuracies in other matters may provide the foundation for this one. Examples
include interpretation by theological liberals, by quasi-Christian groups, by
unbelievers, or by people from widely different theological persuasions from
our own. On the other hand, we should not overlook other people’s insights just
because they are not formally educated in biblical studies or are not especially
gifted.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            In general, we cannot let people <u>scare us</u>
into ideas by their authority or their threats. We must not let them <u>pressure
us</u> into doing and believing things by haranguing or putting undue urgency
on the matter. We must avoid letting others <u>exhort us</u> into ideas by
their winsome personality or emotional appeal. We dare not let them exhort us
before they convince us.</span></p>
