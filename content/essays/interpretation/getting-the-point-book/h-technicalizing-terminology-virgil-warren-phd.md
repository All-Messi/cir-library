<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            H. Technicalizing terminology                                                     
Virgil Warren, PhD</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  Assuming technical usage in the
scripture is a way of trying to get something more specific out of a text than
the author meant. Technicalizing is a kind of restrictive reading.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  Technical contrasts with positive, general,
or descriptive usage. Technicalizing shows up in taking words as appellative
names rather than as descriptive ones: church of Christ, church of God, disciples
become Church of Christ, Church of God, Disciples. Technicalizing leads to expectancy
for uniform usage. It specifies a smaller group within those who could be described
by the word picture. In church offices, <i>elder</i> means more than “older”;
it </span><span lang=HE style='font-size:12.0pt'>‎</span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>means a(n older) man who oversees a group of believers
as when qualifications are listed and a process of selection takes place.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  In biblical study, interpreters
should not expect as much technical usage because (a) the books are not tightly
worded philosophical treatises; (b) they were sent to the general membership
comprised of the general populace; (c) they were written by people whose
background was Semitic, and Hebrew tends to exhibit a more loosely worded
language convention. (d) The writings were delivered by bilingual authors communicating
cross-culturally. (e) The New Testament writings are from the infancy of the
church, when formal terminology is less likely to have developed. Cross-cultural
communication needs to be more fluid, more generalized, dealing more with
central issues, relying less on terminology and more on intuitive perception
via the nature of the case. A particular turn to a word may exist in a context,
not because it is a specialized usage, but because it is in that specialized
context.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  Saying that biblical books were
written in the “language of the people” means (1) they have lass technical
terminology, (2) are not highly abstract, (3) are not tightly structured, (4)
have practical application in view, (5) are not burdened down with rare
expressions, (6) are written with a lower degree of efficiency of expression.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  The technicalizing tendency in
biblical interpretation comes from people poring over the biblical text for
centuries. They also bring to it questions that the ancient authors were not
discussing; consequently, to have the text discuss such issues, readers resort
to supposing technical expression. The best we can do with unaddressed matters
is to extract principles that clearly lie behind the directives biblical
writers <u>do</u> give and apply <u>those</u> to today’s issues. In the end,
some of our questions may need to remain matters of opinion.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  1. Technicalizing terms in biblical
interpretation and English usage</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        </span><span style='font-size:
12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>**church of God, church of Christ</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                        **</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>προστάτις</span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'> (<i>prostatis</i>), “helper,” in Romans
<b>16</b>:2</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                        **</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>διάκονος</span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'> (<i>diakonos</i>), “deacon,” can mean a
helper, server, minister, or a special office in contrast to other offices</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                        **</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>ἀπόστολος</span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'> (<i>apostolos</i>), “apostle,” can (1)
mean anyone sent. At a more specialized level it (2) refers to an itinerant or general
functionary who serves as an extension of (3) the apostolic office in a
technical sense. (See elsewhere “Paul as Primary Apostle.”)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                        **</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>μαθητής</span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'> (<i>mathētēs</i>), “disciple”; a
disciple can be a learner, or follower, of another person. In the New Testament
it describes all of Jesus’ followers, but in many references, it indicates the
twelve in contrast to the rest.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                        **<i>Baptism in
the Holy Spirit</i> has become for some a technical expression for the
outpouring of the Spirit on the church collect <i>vs</i>. upon the individual
person within it. The expression is probably as broad, however, as the flexible
phrase <i>gift of the Holy Spirit</i> since it originated with John the Baptist
as he addressed his general audiences.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                        **Note liquid <i>vs</i>.
fluid. The former technically applies to what forms its own boundaries (water,
oil, juice) while the other describes what does not form its own boundaries
(gases). Everyday people discard the difference.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                        **<i>neighbor</i>;
<i>faith</i> and <i>works</i> in Paul, <i>break bread</i>, <i>ordain</i></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                        **<i>Basketball</i>
is usually a positive term that means the game as normally played. It can be
made technical by saying basketball in contrast to <i>“make-it-take-it,”</i>
where the team that scores gets to keep position.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                        **James D. J.
Dunn, <i>Jesus and the Spirit</i>, pp. 98ff.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                        **A “cup” refers
loosely to however much a vessel shaped like a cup will typically hold—a cup
rather than a gallon, a cup of cold water (Matthew <b>10</b>:42; Mark <b>9</b>:41).
Nevertheless, “cup” as a measurement in recipes is a definite amount equal to a
half pint, that is, 8 ounces.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                        **<i>The twelve</i>
refers to the primary set of apostles even at a time when Judas was not part of
the group: 1 Corinthians <b>15</b>:5 (<i>“</i>[Jesus] <i>appeared to Cephas,
then to the twelve.”</i>)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                        **In Luke <b>6</b>:15,
for example, is <i>Zealot</i> a technical term meaning (1) a member of the sect
of the Zealots—Simon the Zealot—or does it mean (2) an energetic, a high-energy
person?</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:10.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  2. Technicalizing terms in
historical theology</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        </span><span style='font-size:
12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>**justification <i>vs</i>. sanctification. <i>Sanctification</i>
has come to mean growth in Christian life quality, whereas in the Bible itself
it means the more general idea of being set aside to God.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                        **<i>Saint</i>
in scripture designates Christians because they have been made “holy” in
Christ. In Christian tradition, however, it refers to Christians who have been
formally recognized as especially holy for outstanding things they have done—like
miracles—or experiences they have had.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                        **<i>Discipling</i>
has come to mean a one-on-one pattern of accountability in carrying through on
Bible study and memorization, prayer, and other spiritual exercises designed to
promote spiritual maturation. In the Bible, it is used more broadly for the
whole process of gathering disciples of Jesus, converting them, and helping
them grow in their relationship to him.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                        **<i>Shepherding</i>
today sometimes refers to the practice of dividing up the congregation and
assigning them to the different elders of the church, who are supposed to keep
close to their groups to know their needs and assist whenever possible. Shepherding
in the New Testament refers to the whole range of the elder’s responsibilities
in a congregation.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                        **“Persecution
for the name” is an expression <i>we</i> use for a particular kind of
persecution of Christians that developed in the latter first century and early
second century. That does not become a reason for dating 1 Peter later than
Peter’s lifetime, because he says, <i>“If you are reproached for the name of
Christ, you are blessed.”</i></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                        **Pro-life, Pro-choice,
ordain</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        The dictums <i>“use biblical
terms in biblical ways”</i> and <i>“calling Bible things by Bible names”</i>
originated in contrast to technicalizing biblical terms to carry more precise
meanings than the Bible uses.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:1.0in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify;text-indent:0in'><span
style='font-size:10.0pt;font-family:"Arial",sans-serif'>3. <span
style='font:7.0pt "Times New Roman"'>&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; </span></span><i><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>Pesher</span></i><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>
method of interpretation</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        <i>Pesher</i> refers to a
specific application of a general principle. The idea differs somewhat from
technical expressions or specific applications. Technical expression means the <u>author</u>
intended the term in a technical (<i>vs</i>. general) sense. Specific
application is the work of the <u>interpreter</u>, not what the author meant
directly. Accuracy lies with the skill of the applier, not the author; the
applier may correctly apply a general point to a specific case <u>he</u>
believes is appropriate. Pesher, however, may assume that the <u>author</u>
intended that meaning at two levels. One intended meaning is the general
principle or prediction itself, and the other meaning is the specific case to
which the interpreter applies it—presumably with authorial approval.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        Cases like Acts <b>6</b>:20
(&lt; Psalm <b>69</b>:25 + <b>109</b>:8) are <u>applications</u> of Old
Testament principles, not instances that imply <u>interpretation</u> of
authorial intent as if meant in reference to this particular New Testament event.</span></p>
