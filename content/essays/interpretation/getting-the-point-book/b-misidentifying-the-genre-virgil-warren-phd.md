<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-40.5pt;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            B. Misidentifying the genre                                                         
     Virgil Warren, PhD</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-40.5pt;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-40.5pt;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  The genre issue comes into the
reading of Genesis <b>1-11</b>, the Book of Job, the Book of Jonah, and
apocalyptic works like Revelation, Ezekiel, and Daniel. History is more replete
with names, places, sequences of time; but myth, saga, novelle, and the like,
may have these elements as well. Especially in myth, the place where an event
happens may not be of prime importance because myth uses story form to teach
timeless, universal ideas.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-40.5pt;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  The difference between history and
non-historical genres is not always easy to recognize from the piece itself. External
information is usually not available for ancient literature. Saga and some
other genres are somewhat historical. They take a core of historical events and
embellish them for didactic and affective purposes. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-40.5pt;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-40.5pt;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  1. Jonah (For a more complete
coverage of the specifics for introductory elements to this book, see elsewhere
“Introductory Considerations on Jonah.”)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-40.5pt;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-40.5pt;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        Two views have been taken of the
Book of Jonah: (1) the historical and (2) the literary. Under the former, Jonah
chronicles actual events. In the latter, the work is a short story that expresses
general beliefs about God’s attitude toward people outside Israel. The
significance of the matter along with the procedure for determining the genre is
dealt with below.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-40.5pt;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-40.5pt;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            Literary genre determines <u>hermeneutical
method</u> and <u>truth base</u>. (1) As to method, putting Jonah in the
literary genre affects what and how much readers are to get from it. They take <u>less</u>
since place, time, and specific actions are vehicles for what the story teaches;
hence, they are not actual, but typical. Other times, places, and acts could as
well have been chosen. The truth intended did not happen in history; it is
affirmed by the author in historical form. Characteristic truths rather than
discrete events are the writer’s intent.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-40.5pt;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            (2) The danger exists of getting <u>too
much</u> out of the book. Planted events purportedly bear the author’s message.
That leads to attaching significance to more features of the writing. Selectivity
in historical writing is the closest thing to creativity that history involves.
History restricts what a historian can do with it. As a result, the reader supposes
significance for elements in a made-up story because they have been
manufactured for a purpose.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-40.5pt;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            (3) A final hermeneutical consequence is
that <u>wrong</u> ideas may come from misclassification. If a historical piece
is purely literary, a historical setting that called it forth has to be
discovered, which affects its interpretation. In the case of Jonah, scholars
sometimes propose that the book came from the period of reconstruction in
reaction to a hyper-nationalism in Ezra and Nehemiah. Thus construed, Jonah
advocates a position opposed to theirs. One of these prophets’ two positions
must then be questionable.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-40.5pt;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            Putting a historical book into a literary
category also alters <u>the truth base for its contents</u>. In history the
fact of occurrence guarantees the truth; the only issue is giving it a proper reading.
In literature the correctness of the author’s perception must guarantee the truth.
If the events described in Jonah actually happened, their implications are
true: God does control the affairs of non-Jews; he does care about the heathen;
the principle that human repentance leads to divine forgiveness operates among
Gentiles as well (note Acts <b>12</b>:18). If the events in Jonah did not
happen, the correctness of its theological import rests on the writer’s source
of insight. Inspiration rather than experience could provide that insight; short
story as much as parable can legitimately communicate a divine message. But the
truth base being altered, error is more possible, interpretation more uncertain,
and faith less grounded.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-40.5pt;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-40.5pt;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  2. Job</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-40.5pt;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><b><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></b></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-40.5pt;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        Stylistic repetition appears
in Job <b>1</b>:15, 16, 17, 19: <i>“. . . and only I escaped to tell you.”</i>
In <b>1</b>:7 and <b>2</b>:2 stylistic repetition occurs in Satan’s response to
God’s question about where he had been: <i>“From going to and fro and up and
down in the earth.” </i>Satan tells God twice that if he does such and such,
Job will renounce him to his face (<b>1</b>:11; <b>2</b>:5). In <b>1</b>:8 and <b>2</b>:3
God’s description of Job is given in the same way. Job <b>1</b>:3 and <b>42</b>:12
record the numbers of his sheep, camels, oxen, and donkeys with exactly twice
the number in the latter as in the former. His original seven sons and three
daughters (<b>1</b>:2) are replaced exactly (<b>42</b>:13). Coincidence strains
to explain so many elements and the highly patterned series of speeches in the
book; so Job is more than straightforward history; it appears to be a drama composed
around a historical person.</span></p>

an antisupernatural bias that wants to remove divine intervention from the
record because such things do not happen in scientism based on naturalism.</span></p>
