<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            F. Confusing language and reality                                                   
Virgil Warren, PhD</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  Since language is based on
perception, it expresses a person’s subjective viewpoint. Consequently, (a)
statements can be made from the way things look more than from the way they
really are (phenomenological language). In a related matter, (b) language does
not reproduce the whole of the reality it refers to, but only enough to let the
hearer know what the speaker is talking about. From previous experience or
explanation, a hearer fills in the rest of the picture. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  Because of the distinction between
words and their referents, biblical interpreters need to use caution lest their
discussions with other interpreters end up being nothing but arguments over
words (2 Timothy <b>2</b>:14; 1 Corinthians <b>2</b>:1-4).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  </span><span style='font-size:12.0pt;
color:#7030A0'>The ecumenical movement involves the error of confusing reality
and word. A contentless unity exists largely around a symbol, not a fact, an
element of a medium, not around a message. If <i>Jesus is Lord</i> is a form to
which “Christians” subscribe, they have no unity; Gnostics could have said
that. Unity is a reality, not a symbol; so it must be based in reality, not in
symbol.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  1. <u>Inherent importance does not
necessarily correlate with grammatical independence</u>. At best, grammatical
dependence-independence reflects the author’s emphasis in this instance. We can
take two actions and make either one the dependent or independent element</span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>: <i>“I went downtown to buy groceries” vs</i>.
<i>“When I went downtown, I bought groceries.” </i>The choice of what is
prioritized grammatically may be due to the (a) <u>emphasis</u> a writer makes,
(b) how best to <u>connect</u> this sentence with the preceding ones, (c) what
else the writer may want to include, or (d) what the <u>conventions</u> of the
language allow.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                        **The fact that
“make disciples” is the only finite verb in the Great Commission does not
automatically make it the most important thing in the commission (Matthew <b>28</b>:18-20).
Judgment about the most important—or most general—element in the commission comes
from the nature of the elements listed.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                        **One argument
against using Acts <b>2</b>:38 for baptism as connected to the salvation
process is based on this discontinuity between grammar and reality (<i>What the
Bible Says About Salvation</i>, pp. 254-56).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  2.   <u>The aspect of the verb does
not indicate the aspect of the action</u>. Greek uses imperfect and aorist
verbs respectively for linear <i>vs</i>. punctiliar (factitive) action. The
same difference occurs between present and aorist participles and infinitives,
and between the “present” and aorist forms of the optative, subjunctive, and
imperative moods. The distinction lies in the ways the speaker looks at the
actions, not in the nature of the actions themselves. Linear action does not
“take longer” than punctiliar action; any act can be referred to by either
verbal “aspect.” </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                        **The point can
be illustrated by comparing the English simple past and progressive past<i>. “Yesterday
I <u>washed</u> the car.” “Yesterday, while I <u>was washing</u> the car, I got
a sunburn.”</i> Both statements refer to the same washing event.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                        **The fact that
“baptizing” is a present rather than aorist participle in the Great Commission
does not mean baptizing is itself a process with sub-units, hence, triune
baptism <i>vs</i>. single-action baptism. That matter needs to be resolved some
other way or left as a matter of opinion.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                        **John <b>11</b>:5,
<i>“Jesus loved/was-loving</i> [imperfect/progressive past] <i>Mary and Martha”</i>
does not mean his loving them went on longer than if John had said “loved”
[aorist/punctiliar].</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:10.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  3.   <u>The amount of attention
given to a subject does not necessarily correspond with its inherent importance
or theoretical centrality</u>. How much attention an item receives comes from
the practical situation as well as from theoretical matters.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  4.   <u>The same word does not
always have the same meaning</u> every time it is used.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        One difficulty in overly
appealing to “logic” in biblical interpretation stems from the difference
between a series of biblical statements and a set of premises in a syllogism. In
a syllogism the interpreter assumes that recurring terms carry the same
meaning. Most of the difficulty in understanding word communication, however,
comes from determining the meaning of terms, which has to occur before any “reasoning
in the scripture.” </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        </span><span style='font-size:
12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>**<i>Faith</i> and <i>works</i> as used in James and Paul
do not have the same force, because they appear in different contexts relative
to different concerns. Conclusions based on the assumption that they mean the
same thing are artificial conclusions. Making them the same in meaning is an
example of “equivocating,” putting the same word on two different ideas (maybe
even with the intent to deceive).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                        **The word <i>judge</i>
means “evaluate” as well as “condemn.” In relation to whether Christians go
through the judgment, the answer is “yes” in the sense of evaluation but “no”
in the sense of condemnation (Romans <b>14</b>:10; 2 Corinthians <b>5</b>:10).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  5.   <u>Different words referring to
the same thing do not necessarily have exactly the same import</u>. Using different
words for the same thing does not make them interchangeable as if they were
synonyms. They may be different word pictures for the same referent,
highlighting different facets of it or presenting it under different analogies
for affective purposes.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        </span><span style='font-size:
12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>**In different accounts of Jesus’ baptism </span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>ἀπό</span><i><span lang=EL
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'> </span></i><span style='font-size:12.0pt;
color:#7030A0'>(<i>apo</i>, away from) and </span><span lang=EL
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>ἐκ</span><span style='font-size:12.0pt;
color:#7030A0'> (<i>ek</i>, out of) may retain their distinctive meanings even
though they substitute for each other in the synoptic accounts of Jesus’
baptism. Either one would serve to translate the Hebrew word </span><span
lang=HE dir=RTL style='font-size:14.0pt;color:#7030A0'>מִן</span><span dir=LTR></span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'><span dir=LTR></span> that these
prepositions may be representing</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                        **The variation
between </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>γνῶσις</span><i><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'> </span></i><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>(<i>gnōsis</i>, knowledge) and </span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>ἐπί</span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'> + </span><span lang=EL
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>γνῶσις</span><i><span lang=EL
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'> </span></i><span style='font-size:12.0pt;
color:#7030A0'>(<i>epignōsis</i>, knowledge) in 1 Corinthians <b>13</b>:12 does
not necessarily indicate knowledge <i>vs</i>. clear knowledge, another kind of
greater knowledge. It can mean knowledge generally and direct personal
knowledge.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                        **Systematic
theology uses traditional terminology in non-traditional ways. Such a practice
does not necessarily deceive. Retaining terminology while switching the meaning
correlates aspects of the previous understanding with the equivalent aspects of
the new understanding. In historical theology, for example, “depravity” refers
to a biologically inheritable fallen ontic nature, which renders a person
incapable of responding to spiritual matters. It can be used appropriately to
mark man’s typical sinfulness. In modern theology the expression is often
employed to mean man’s lower, animal nature. In evolutionary thinking, humankind
evolved from that previous, purely animal level. Being depraved, then, refers
to refusing to live by the higher nature that has evolved. This kind of
depravity replaces the fall with a failure to rise above the
“un-advanced-ness.”</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                        **Baptism in the
name of Jesus does not mean something different from baptism in the name of the
Father, Son, and Spirit. Indeed, it could not be incompatible with the other
expression without accusing scripture of inconsistency and contradiction.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  6.   <u>Different words do not
necessarily have different referents</u> (<i>What the Bible Says About
Salvation</i>, p. 342). </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        </span><i><u><span lang=FR
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>Justification</span></u></i><u><span
lang=FR style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>, <i>sanctification</i>, <i>reconciliation</i>,
<i>regeneration</i>, <i>conversion, salvation</i></span></u><span lang=FR
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>. </span><span style='font-size:12.0pt;
color:#7030A0'>Normally in theological usage “justification” refers to the
initial divine act of salvation when God changes the person from one state to
another. “Sanctification” refers to growth in the Christian life. The first is
categorical (either-or); the second is continuous (a matter of degree). The
first is a change of state while the second is an improved condition within the
new state. Justification is quantitative; sanctification is qualitative. In scripture,
however, these terms usually represent two word-pictures for the same event or two
aspects of the same event. Justification views salvation in legal imagery as a
pronouncement of acquittal by a judge; sanctification views the event from a
relational perspective as a setting aside to a special use or being made holy
in a stative sense, in this case an interpersonal relational sense. Taken as a
group, do these terms refer to different things or to different facets of the
same thing, or are they different models of the same thing?</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                        There is a set
of power terms that are not always finely differentiated: </span><span lang=EL
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>κράτος</span><span style='font-size:
12.0pt;color:#7030A0'> (<i>kratos</i>), </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:
12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>ἰσχύς</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt;
color:#7030A0'> </span><span style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>(<i>ischys</i>),
</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>δύναμις</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'> </span><span style='font-size:
12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>(<i>dynamis</i>), </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:
12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>ἐξουςία</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt;
color:#7030A0'> </span><span style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>(<i>exousia</i>).
While the last in the list tends to mean “right” <i>vs</i>. physical strength
or psychological stamina, the distinctions cannot always be pressed (note John <b>10</b>:18).
“Power” is an example of taking a word already in the language and “bending” it
in the direction of the new subject in Christianity. Normally, authority (</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>ἐξουσία</span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>), force (</span><span lang=EL
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>δύναμις</span><span style='font-size:
12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>), and might (</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt;
color:#7030A0'>κράτος</span><span style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>)
speak of impersonal power, but in the Christian setting they can refer to
interpersonal power.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                        Kingdom of
heaven <i>vs</i>. kingdom of God are different phrases for the same thing as
shown by parallels between Matthew (kingdom of heaven) and the other synoptics
(kingdom of God).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                        House of Judah <i>vs</i>.
house of Israel</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:12.0pt;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                        There is a whole
set of gift terms that that scripture uses somewhat interchangeably: </span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>δωρεά</span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'> (<i>dōrea</i>), </span><span lang=EL
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>δώρημα</span><span style='font-size:
12.0pt;color:#7030A0'> (<i>dōrēma</i>), </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:
12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>χάρισμα</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt;
color:#7030A0'> </span><span style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>(<i>charisma</i>),
</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>δῶρον</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'> </span><span style='font-size:
12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>(dōron), </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt;
color:#7030A0'>δόμα</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>
</span><span style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>(<i>doma</i>), </span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>χάρις</span><span lang=EL
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'> </span><span style='font-size:12.0pt;
color:#7030A0'>(<i>charis</i>), </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt;
color:#7030A0'>μερισμός</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>
</span><span style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>(<i>merismos</i>), as well
as the verbs </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>δίδωμι</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'> </span><span style='font-size:
12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>(<i>didōmi</i>), </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:
12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>ἀποδίδωμι</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt;

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:12.0pt;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:

style='font-size:4.0pt;color:#7030A0'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        </span><i><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>Gift of the Holy Spirit</span></i><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'> and <i>baptism in the Holy Spirit</i>
are both used for the Pentecost event and what happened at the house of
Cornelius (Acts<b> 1</b>:5; <b>11</b>:16-17). “Baptize in the Spirit”
originated with John the Baptist as a terminology adapted from his ministry. Jesus
uses that terminology (Acts <b>1</b>:5) to connect that prediction with his own
promise to the apostles regarding Pentecost (Acts <b>2</b>). Peter uses it to
connect Cornelius’ household (Gentiles) with Pentecost (Jews). The alternative
would be to make baptism in the Holy Spirit more restrictive than gift, but
Paul does use it for the Corinthians generally (1 Corinthians <b>12</b>:13).
Likewise, in John’s original promise, he is speaking to a general audience.
Consequently, baptism in the Holy Spirit does not apply just to the apostles.
It does not apply only to a gift of the Spirit that involves supernatural
gifting unless an interpreter is prepared to say that every Christian has a
supernatural gift.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        </span><i><span

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                        The “poor and
needy” (</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>πρωχὸς</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'> </span><span lang=EL

style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:
12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>πένης</span><span style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>,
<i>ptōchos</i> <i>kai</i> <i>penēs</i>) are paired fourteen times in the
Septuagint, as pleonastic references to the same ones (hendiadys).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  7.   <u>Lists</u> need to be handled
carefully in relation to their referents.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        a.   Lists are not necessarily
<u>parallel</u>. The number of words does not necessarily equal the number of
realities—reification. They may overlap; they may represent a series of
different word pictures for the same </span><span style='font-size:12.0pt;
color:windowtext'>thing </span><span style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>(cp.
“good morning, <u>friends and neighbors</u>”; </span><span lang=EL
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>στέγω</span><span style='font-size:12.0pt;
color:#7030A0'> (<i>stegō</i>) and </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt;
color:#7030A0'>ὑπομένω</span><span style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'> (<i>hypomenō</i><u>)</u>
in 1 Corinthians<b> 13</b>:7; cp. Paul as a herald, apostle, and teacher in 2
Timothy <b>1</b>:11).</span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'> Since reality
itself has a hierarchical nature, language also is hierarchical; so lists may
have some terms that fit with another. Besides, language sometimes employs
pleonasm for emphasis </span><span style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>(various
and sundry).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;

example of the old fallacy <i>post hoc ergo propter hoc</i> (<i>“after this;

list does not have to correlate with some kind of sequence. Lists may be
partially sequential, as with the ten commandments, where the first item seems
prior to the rest without the rest being in some order. The same may apply to
spiritual fruits in Galatians <b>6</b>:22-23, where love appears first.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;

style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  8.   The <u>form of a statement</u>
must be distinguished from the <u>reality of the referent</u>.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        </span><span style='font-size:
12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>**Leviticus <b>10</b>:1 says that Nadab and Abihu
offended God by offering strange fire that he had not commanded them to offer. The
statement is in the form of silence, but the real situation was one of
prohibition because Exodus <b>30</b>:9 expressly prohibited what in Leviticus
is worded as a silence statement. Non-instrumentalists use the Nadab and Abihu
incident to illustrate the principle that silence in matters of worship equals
prohibition, but that usage plays on the form of the statement rather than on
the reality of the situation and the nature of the case. This illustration also
shows the importance of not trying to use narrative material for a doctrinal
point since the logical connectors and motivations involved are not always
evident from an account.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                        **The objection
is raised by some that people should not say, <i>“I <u>feel</u> such and such,”</i>
but <i>“I <u>think</u> such and such.”</i> “Feel” is taken to mean an emotional
impression rather than a thought-out conclusion. The reason for using the word <u>feel</u>,
however, does not reflect an affective orientation instead of a cognitive one; <u>feel</u>
softens the statement so as not to “fly in the face” of someone while
discussing a sensitive point. It is comparable to saying <i>“I think such and such
is true”</i> instead of saying <i>“This is true.”</i></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                        **It has been
argued that the term </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>διαθήκη</span><i><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'> </span></i><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>(<i>diathēkē</i>) means “covenant,” not “testament,”
because a covenant involves the agreement of both parties. It may be true that
in salvation both parties are involved, but a model does not have to correspond
to everything in the reality. “Testament” gets at the idea of one person’s
willing to another what the other has not earned; it is clearly so used in
Hebrews <b>9</b>:15-22. Whether the other person has a part in willingness to
receive or in meeting conditions to receive lies beyond the figure’s capacity
to communicate.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                        **Some preachers
have commented on John <b>11</b>:43 by saying, <i>“If Jesus had not said
‘Lazarus,’ in the command ‘Lazarus, come forth,’ all the tombs would have been
emptied.”</i> Such a notion confuses language and reality. Jesus would have <u>meant</u>
Lazarus, regardless of whether he verbalized the point or not. (Besides, the
imperative form in the Greek is singular! Preachers should not try to be
spectacular.)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:10.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  9.   Several problems come from making
propositions ultimate, that is, from not making the language-referent
distinction.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        a.   It <u>depersonalizes God</u>
in the eyes of his people. That, in turn, creates inadequate attitudes toward
God. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        b.   <u>Propositions are
inflexible</u>. They do not take attitude into consideration and foster the
impulse to disallow new considerations beyond their purview.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        c.   <u>Propositions are
partial</u>. Since they do not reproduce all the reality they point to, they do
not tell the reader many things he might like to know.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        d.   <u>Propositions may be
misunderstood</u> even when people hear the revelation.</span></p>
