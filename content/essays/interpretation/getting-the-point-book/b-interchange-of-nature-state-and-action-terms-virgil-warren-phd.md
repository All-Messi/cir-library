<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  B. Interchange of Nature, State, and
Action Terms                Virgil Warren, PhD</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>   </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        A series of examples may be
found in Virgil Warren’s doctoral dissertation <i>The Implication of Divine
Self-Consistency for the Doctrine of Natural Depravity</i>, pp. 157-66. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>        <img width=326 height=206
src="/cir-library/images/essays/b-interchange-of-nature-state-and-action-terms-virgil-warren-phd/image001.png"></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            As to words, people shift categories
without shifting them as to concepts, because the categories are interconnected
in actual experience. Being, action, and relationship are equally present in
any real event, but with the mind we can differentiate between them. So
language, which reflects the subjective activity of sensory perception and
mental conception, differentiates between the aspects of an event even though
they are objectively interrelated. If we do not distinguish language and
reality, we take a point stated about one aspect and slide it onto another. Furthermore,
language itself may use a nature term to refer to an action or to state
reality. If readers do not keep in mind the nature of the issue, they can make
all kinds of false inferences.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            Over the centuries a recurring general
problem for interpretation has been the ontologizing character of Greek
philosophy. It has shown itself in the doctrine of real presence (communion),
natural depravity (anthropology), and miracle for influence (causality), to
indicate a few. This kind of “reification” confuses the medium with the
message.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  1. Nature-state/circumstance substitution</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        </span><span style='font-size:
12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>**<u>the doctrine of real presence</u> in the Lord’s
Supper (impanation, transubstantiation, consubstantiation) </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                        **The doctrine
of <u>natural depravity</u> comes mainly from reading statements about stative
and functional reality as if they were talking about the ontic nature of man. In
this place belongs a whole series of “cannot” statements. See examples under “Mechanics:
Nature of the Case—Inability.”</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                        **Scripture uses
“death” as a state-of-being term for broken relationship, because when people
die, they no longer exist to their spouses and others. Ephesians <b>2</b>:3
speaks of “deadness” as the kind of thing that comes from sinning (cp. Luke <b>15</b>:32).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                        **Romans<b> 11</b>:24
says that the Gentiles were by <u>nature</u> a wild olive tree. The verse is
not talking about their created nature, but about their relational circumstance.
They were apart from special revelation, which led to their acting in wrong ways.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  2. State-action substitution</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        Language makes process static
and actions substantive. It fosters the notion of static being, because static
being is the closest analogy to language. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  3. Nature-action substitution
(concretized speech)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        Biblical writers frequently
use substantive imagery for spiritual realities. Over the centuries
interpreters have misread Semitic idiom to create their substantive and legal
theological systems. Semitic idiom, however, is more characterized by graphic <u>pictures</u>,
which lends itself to being read hypostatically. The body-parts expressions are
typical: “by the hand of,” said even of God.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        </span><span style='font-size:
12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>**The “image of God” in Old Testament used by Paul for behavioral
quality and functional pattern (Romans <b>8</b>:28-<u>29</u>-39).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                        **Even we speak
of <i>“halfhearted attempt,” “backbiting,” “I can’t stomach the idea,” “shoulder
the responsibility”</i>; cp. biblical expressions like “heart was hardened,” “bowels
of compassion,” and other body-part language. Such speech assigns psychological
values to substantive words.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                        **Note the
ambiguity even in the term <i>god</i> as to whether it is functional or ontological
deity. Psalm <b>82</b>:6 calls “gods” those who served as representatives of
God, or as extensions of God’s activity. An age-old controversy continues over this
distinction in the use of “God” as a reference to Jesus of Nazareth. Was he
ontological or functional deity? Was he homoiousia (similar being) or homoousia
(same being)? What of Hebrews <b>1</b>:3 (the <i>“very image of his substance”</i>)?</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                        **Philippians<b>
2</b>:7 says, <i>“. . .</i> [Christ Jesus] <i>emptied himself, taking the form
of a servant. . . .” </i>We say that Jesus gave up the free exercise of his
rights as deity, not his status or deity or abilities. Nature terms are used
here for functional reality (“slave”), and action terms are used figuratively
(“emptied”).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                        **1 Timothy <b>4</b>:2
speaks of a “conscience seared as by a hot iron.”</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                        **2 Peter <b>1</b>:4
speaks about partaking of the “divine <u>nature</u>,” not meaning ontological
deification, but coming to approximate the character of God. Peter is talking
about characteristic action. In their book <i>The Seduction of Christianity</i>,
Hunt and McMahon referred to groups that want to take passages like this one to
promise the ontological deification of people through the supposed Christian
message (p. 219). When he used the term <i>nature</i>, however, Peter was
talking about behavioral likeness (action) rather than substantive being or
divine status (relationship), as is clear from the succeeding context, which talks
about developing various virtues. The English term <i>nature</i> is unclear
because it can refer to substance or behavioral pattern, as in the statement, <i>“It’s
just his nature to be grumpy,” </i>or <i>‘“She is good natured.”</i> Grumpiness
and being good natured, however, are patterns of behavior, not types of
substance. Ephesians <b>2</b>:3 says that “by <u>nature</u>” Paul’s readers
were previously children of wrath. They were dead through their trespasses and
sins, not through birth. See also above under “Nature-State Substitution.”</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                        **The
expressions “born in” (something) and “from the womb” mean to be totally
characterized by something, as when we say, <i>“He is a born leader, a born
liar, a born loser.” </i>He is an “inveterate” sinner. Many characteristics and
activities involved would not be present in the beginning months and years. (a)
People are said to be <u>wicked from the womb</u>. Psalm<b> 58</b>:3 says, <i>“The
wicked are estranged from the womb; they go astray as soon as they are born,
speaking lies.”</i> In light of that clear case of exaggeration for emphasis,
we should read Psalm <b>51</b>:5, <i>“I was brought forth in iniquity, and in
sin my mother conceived”</i> (cp. Job <b>11</b>:11-12; <b>15</b>:10, 14-16; <b>25</b>:4).
Because of his birth defect, the Pharisees said that the man in John <b>9</b>:34
was born in sin (<b>9</b>:2). This terminology provides the background for
Isaiah’s saying of Israel that they were called transgressors from the womb
(Isaiah <b>48</b>:8). God said of the antediluvian people that the imagination
of their heart was evil from their youth (Genesis<b> 8</b>:21). </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                        It should be
clear that born in wickedness does not mean biologically inherited racial
depravity. There are (b) statements about <u>goodness from the womb</u>. John
the Baptist was filled with the Holy Spirit from his mother’s womb (Luke <b>1</b>:15;
cp. Nazirite statements below). Job says that he guided the widow from his
mother’s womb (<b>31</b>:18). A child was a Nazirite from birth (Judges <b>13</b>:5,
7; <b>16</b>:17; cp. Numbers <b>6</b>:1-21). None of the Nazirite regulations
would be relevant in the earliest years—shaving, drinking liquor, or eating
grapes. Likewise, David says in Psalm<b> 22</b>:9-10 that God made him trust
him when he was on his mother’s breasts, that he was cast on the Lord from the
womb, and that Yahveh was his God from the time that his mother bore him. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                        (c) People are
said to be separated and <u>called to divine tasks from the mother’s womb</u>. Jeremiah
was sanctified before he came from the womb (<b>1</b>:5; cp. Galatians <b>1</b>:15).
</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                        (d) Timothy <u>knew
the sacred writings</u> from the time he was a baby (2 Timothy<b> 3</b>:15, </span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>βρέφος</span><span lang=EL
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'> </span><span style='font-size:12.0pt;
color:#7030A0'>[<i>brephos</i><u>]</u>). </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                        (e) The
foreigners in Acts <b>2</b>:8 were <u>born in the language they spoke</u>. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                              Other,
non-controversial usages of these expressions occur in Job <b>10</b>:18-19; <b>31</b>:18;
Isaiah <b>46</b>:3-4; 49:1, Matthew <b>19</b>:12; Acts<b> 3</b>:2; <b>14</b>:8;
Romans <b>9</b>:11 (&lt; Genesis <b>25</b>:23; Malachi<b> 1</b>:2-3). God forms
people and nations in the womb (Job <b>31</b>:15) and from the womb (Isaiah<b>
44</b>:2; <b>44</b>:24; <b>49</b>:5). </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                        **The word <i>flesh</i>
indicates ethical behavior based on the drives of the flesh. It serves as a
word for self-centered living. Translating </span><span lang=EL
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>σάρξ</span><span style='font-size:12.0pt;
color:#7030A0'> (<i>sarx</i>) as “sinful nature” is a misleading rendering in
the New International Version in its earlier edition, because it can set up a
foreign set of contrasts.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                        **<i>Leaven</i>
serves as a symbol for influence.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                        **In scripture, <i>sin</i>
is used for action and state but not for being (although it is used in this
latter sense in historical theology). In historical theology, “original sin” does
not mean the original act of sin, but the supposed inheritable fallen nature
incurred by our first parents when they sinned.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                        **“If you don’t
quit that, I’m going to beat it out of you.”</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:10.0pt'>                  </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  Among other things the issue here is
the intended meanings of the <u>linking verb</u>: “is, are, was, were.” <i>Is</i>
can mean           </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              (1)  Present <u>time</u>
(the English progressive present); “is” of </span><span style='font-size:12.0pt;
color:windowtext'>time </span><span style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>(“He
is here.”) Is Jesus assuming present-time “is” rather than “is” of timeless
identification in the Exodus<b> 3</b>:6 text he quotes to the effect that at
the time of Moses God says, <i>“I am the God of Abraham” </i>(Matthew <b>22</b>:32
= Mark <b>12</b>:26-27 = Luke<b> 20</b>:37-38)? </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              (2) Timeless <u>description</u>
(the English simple present); ‘“is” of predication </span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>(1 Timothy <b>6</b>:10, <i>“The love of
money <u>is</u> the root of all evil.”</i>)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              (3) <u>Identification</u>
</span><span style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>(<i>“He is John.”</i>)</span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>; “Is” of co-extensiveness.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              (4) <u>Appointment</u> </span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>(Psalm <b>110</b>:4, <i>“You <u>are</u>
a priest forever after the </i></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><i><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                       </span></i><i><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>order of Melchizedek.”</span></i><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                    (5) <u>Correlation</u>;
“is” of analogy (When things correlate with </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                             something,
people sometimes say they <u>are</u> that thing.) </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                       </span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>**Matthew <b>26</b>:26, 28, and the like,
say, <i>“This <u>is</u> my body/blood,” </i>meaning that the bread and grape
juice <u>represent</u> his body and blood. (cp. <i>“That is my grandfather,”</i>
said of a portrait.)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                                       **Matthew
<b>13</b>:37-39 interprets the Parable of the Tares by saying that the field <u>is</u>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;

style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                                       **In

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              (6) <u>Means</u></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                                       **John
<b>10</b>:6: <i>“They did not understand what-the-things <u>were</u>”</i> (</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>ῥήματα</span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>, <i><u>rhēmata</u></i> implied);
another translation might be “that he was saying to-them”).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                                       **Luke
<b>1</b>:29: <i>“She cast in her mind what manner of salutation this </i></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><i><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                                          
 might be.”</span></i></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                                       **Luke
<b>8</b>:12: <i>“This is the parable.”</i></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                                       **Luke
<b>15</b>:26: <i>“He asked what these things would be.”</i></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                                       **Acts
<b>10</b>:17: <i>“Peter was perplexed what the dream would be.”</i></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:22.5pt;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                            (7) <u>Exists</u></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:22.5pt;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:10.0pt'>                                       </span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>Hebrews <b>11</b>:6: <i>“. . . must
believe that he <u>is</u>.”</i></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:12.0pt;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:0in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                    (8) <u>Phenomenological
existence </u></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:12.0pt;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:0in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><u><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'><span style='text-decoration:none'>&nbsp;</span></span></u></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:10.0pt'>                                       </span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>Rachel would not be comforted because
the children <u>were</u> not. The passage does not teach that children do not
exist in heaven. Since they were no longer visible, they did not exist in her
mind; they did not <u>seem</u> to be there. They did not exist for practical
purposes of relationship.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              (9) <u>Amounts to</u>,<u>
is tantamount to</u></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:10.0pt'>                                       </span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>All the “I am” statements in John. <i>“I
<u>am</u> the resurrection”</i> (John <b>11</b>:25). He amounts to the
resurrection because it is only through him that resurrection comes.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  A related problem is <u>reification</u>,
assuming that a label means there is a thing it refers to (“the word guarantees
the thing”)—there is a thing because there is a noun. Often, however, a noun
does not refer just to a person, place, or thing, but also to an abstraction (<u>idea</u>)
or to a pattern of <u>action</u>. Reification occurs in situations where
phenomenological language is used.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  </span><span style='font-size:12.0pt;
color:#7030A0'>Reification becomes an important issue in discussing the <u>nature
of man</u>, for example. Does each person have a soul in distinction from a
spirit, a will, a mind? This approach postulates a faculty psychology, which
means that there is a corresponding component in a person for each functional
phenomenon experienced—which is then labeled by a word. These terms, however,
may simply represent phenomena and say little or nothing “scientific” about the
substantive makeup of the human person or the relationship of mind and brain. There
is a current expression, <i>“A part of me”</i> (wants to do one thing) but <i>“another
part of me”</i> (wants to do something else). People really mean that they have
opposing inclinations, but they picture these inclinations as different <u>parts</u>
of themselves.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                  The <u>nature of God</u>
can too easily be modeled after the nature of a statement or the statement of a
law. The unchangeableness of the will of God, for example, may be modeled after
the unchangeableness of statements about the will of God. The kind of
unchangeableness that a statement has once it is made is transferred to the
idea the statement is made about. So to speak, the legalistic view of God and
history may end up having the characteristics of the medium used to communicate
it. There is a connection here with one idea that the Bible is the word of God.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                  All the attempts to
equate the number of concepts with the number of words run the risk of another
form of reification. See the treatment of lists later in these notes.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  Because of reification, people can
get into the idea that you cannot think without words. Educationally, they may
suppose that if they can name it, they know it. It relates to the belief that
if you can say it, you can control it, as well as the assumption that if you
can say it, it can be done. <i>“Honesty compels you to do this”</i> may
represent reification. It is interesting that aphasic people cannot say something
they cannot do. The loss of language functioning in the brain can come from
some kind of cerebro-vascular accident.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            C.  Implicit limitation (general rule and
absolute uniformity)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  Implied limitation to instances like
the following: </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  These cases show that an expression in
their place does not mean everything it could be used to mean, but what the
author intends it to mean. There may be an <u>implicit limitation</u> that is
not given in the statement itself. Jeremiah <b>18</b>:7-8: <i>“If at any time I
announce that a nation or kingdom is to be uprooted, torn down, and destroyed,
and if that nation I warned repents of its evil, then I will relent and not
inflict on it the disaster I had planned.” </i>The Book of Jonah is another
example of thi point. This concept can be called “frame of reference.” There
may be universal and absolute words in the statement, but they are intended
within an implied area of concern, within a limited category, like the topic at
hand, the time period in view, the circumstance envisioned. Generality <i>vs</i>.
absolute is another form of this same phenomenon. The meaning is iterative <i>vs.</i>
continuous or characteristic <i>vs.</i> uniform.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  The issue here is the difference
between the absolute form of a statement and the general nature of the
observation it makes. Statements of general principle can be made without going
ahead to specify <u>exceptions</u> or <u>additional </u>considerations in the
practical situation. Actual situations bring together factors that qualify each
other; so no one factor in a set can be treated as an absolute if it can be
impacted by the other factors in the set.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  The issue here is also the intended
meaning of <u>universal and absolute words</u>: all, every, each, no, none, as
many as, whatever, whoever, entirely/wholly (that is, quantity and number
words); never, always (that is, frequency words), forever (that is, extent-of-time
words), greatest/highest/easiest, perfect (that is, degree words). Superlative
forms are not normally to be understood absolutely; they often have an elative
force: very great/high/easy. </span><span style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>A
Christian praise song says in the first verse, <i>‘“The greatest thing in all
my life is knowing you.’” </i>But the next verse says, <i>“The greatest thing
in all my life is loving/praising you.”</i></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                  </span><span lang=HE
dir=RTL style='font-size:14.0pt;color:windowtext'>כָּל</span><span dir=LTR></span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:windowtext'><span dir=LTR></span> in Hebrew,
hence, </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt;color:windowtext'>πᾶς</span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:windowtext'> among Hebrew writers in Greek, is a
reference to the category. It therefore approaches the value of the Greek
(definite) article, which thinks of the noun as an identity (as when set in
contrast to a parallel alternative) <i>vs.</i> quality. Often this amounts to
potential “all,” characteristic “all,” phenomenological “all.”</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  In a way, we should distinguish
between universal and absolute words. Absolute words refer to matters of
degree, while universal words refer to matters of number. <i>Perfect</i>, <i>holy</i>,
<i>good</i> are examples of the former; <i>all</i>, <i>never</i>, <i>forever</i>
are examples of the latter. Universal and absolute words, however, are alike in
that they are often meant in a relative sense.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  Universal words may be</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        (a) <u>actually all</u>, that
is, an absolute statement.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              <u>actually all within
an implied limitation</u> that can be discovered from the context or by the
nature of the case.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              <i>Forever</i> is used
within the implied limitation of the Mosaic dispensation.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        (b) <u>generally all</u> <i>vs</i>.
absolutely, characteristically <i>vs</i>. uniformly. <u>All</u> may simply
observe a significant generalization or even a noticeable pattern, one that is
important to the subject at hand. Note the rhetorical device called “glittering
generalities.” Note in this connection Paul’s statement in 1 Timothy <b>6</b>:10,
<i>“The love of money is the root of all </i>[kinds of] <i>evil.”</i></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:1.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify;text-indent:-1.25in'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        (c)  <u>potentially all</u><i>
vs</i>. actually all </span><span style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>(John <b>1</b>:9;
see <i>What the Bible Says About Salvation</i>, p. 120).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        (d)  <u>phenomenologically all</u>,
that is, that is the way it looks </span><span style='font-size:12.0pt;
color:#7030A0'>(Romans <b>7</b>:15).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:1.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify;text-indent:0in'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>(e) <span style='font:7.0pt "Times New Roman"'> </span></span><u><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>psychologically all</span></u><span style='font-size:
12.0pt'>; that is the way it feels.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        (f)  <u>all as to kind</u> </span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>(Perhaps this is the point of passages
like Mark <b>6</b>:23, which promise that Christians will receive whatever they
ask in Christ’s name believing. See below under “limited universals”). See <i>What
the Bible Says About Salvation</i>, pp. 141-46, for the way some use these
options to deal with the biblical promise about the availability of salvation
to every human person.</span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        (g)  <u>emphatically all</u>.
Universal, or absolute, words also appear in statements that exaggerate for
emphasis. (See also below under “Category <i>vs</i>. Continuum.”) Universal
words are often used for emphasis: <i>“We have tried <u>everything</u>.”</i></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        (h) <u>All</u> may mean final
or ultimate. When Jesus says that all authority has been given to him (Matthew <b>28</b>:28),
it does not mean that no one else has any. It means that he has ultimate authority
in heaven and earth. Having authority over all means that whatever other
authority there is falls under his.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  1. Limited universals</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        </span><span style='font-size:
12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>**Genesis <b>3</b>:20 says that Eve was the mother of all
living, yet she was not </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                                the
mother of Adam (or the mother of all the animals that lived).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                        **Genesis <b>7</b>:7-8
tells of God’s promise to Abraham that the land of Canaan would be an everlasting
possession. The whole dispensational theology is based on reading <i>everlasting</i>
as meaning as long as the earth will last.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                        **Exodus<b> 9</b>:6
says that all the livestock of Egypt died, but later there is </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                             mention of
livestock.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;

the seeds” (Matthew <b>13</b>:32) may be a generalized reference, especially if

large results. (See also Matthew<b> 17</b>:20; Luke <b>17</b>:6; <b>21</b>:21 =

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;

style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                        **Universal
words like “all” and “whatever” may be understood as universals of frequency or
of kind. Matthew <b>21</b>:22 says, <i>“Whatever you ask in faith believing you

(in <u>number</u>) they ask for, or that they can get all <u>kinds</u> of
things through prayer though not every one of them every time.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                        **Luke <b>3</b>:21

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                        **Luke <b>21</b>:32:
Jesus told his disciples that all things written in the prophets would be
fulfilled in this last trip to Jerusalem. He obviously meant all prophecies
pertaining to that period in his ministry; everything that was supposed to be
fulfilled would be fulfilled, but there was still the Pentecost application of
Joel <b>2</b>. Much of what the prophets predicted had already been fulfilled.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                        **John<b> 1</b>:11
+ 12 says, <i>“His own (people) did not receive him, but as many as did receive
him . . .”</i> (In John <b>3</b>:32 + 33-34 the same thing happens.)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;

style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                        **John <b>8</b>:35:

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                        **In Luke <b>2</b>:30-31

which you have prepared before the face of all peoples.”</i></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                        **Luke <b>18</b>:31
says, <i>“We are going up to Jerusalem and everything written by the prophets
will be accomplished to the Son of man.”</i> Does this mean that the Old
Testament does not contain any predictions that look forward to a time after
the Messiah’s first advent or anything prior to the final week?</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                        **Acts <b>1</b>:1
says of the Gospel of Luke that it gave an account of all that Jesus began to
do and to teach (cp. Luke <b>1</b>:3), but John<b> 20</b>:30-31;<b> 21</b>:24-25
says that the world could not hold all the books if everything Jesus did and
taught were written down.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                        **Acts <b>2</b>:47
says that all the people held the Christians in favor, yet the religious
leaders did not (<b>4</b>:1 ff.).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                        **In Acts <b>4</b>:32-34
(cp. <b>2</b>:44-45) Luke says that as many as owned possessions, land, or

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;

style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                        **After the
persecution surrounding Stephen, Acts <b>8</b>:1 says, <i>“[The Christians] were
all scattered abroad.”</i></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                        **Romans <b>3</b>:23
says that all have sinned, but Jesus did not sin.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                        **Romans <b>11</b>:26
says <u>all</u> Israel will be saved, yet those that reject Messiah are not
saved. “Israel” probably means spiritual Israel here.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                        **1 Corinthians <b>1</b>:4:
<i>“I thank my God always for you”</i> (cp. 1 Thessalonians <b>5</b>:17; characteristic
behavior).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                        **Is 1
Corinthians <b>2</b>:14 a general rule or an absolute uniformity when it says, <i>“A
natural person does not receive the things of the Spirit of God . . .”</i> (cp.
<b>1</b>:26-27; <i>What the Bible Says About Salvation</i>, pp. 51-52)?</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                        **1 Corinthians <b>6</b>:12
says, <i>“All things are lawful</i>,” perhaps meaning all <u>things</u> may be
used, rather than that all <u>acts</u> may be done. The statement applies to dietary
regulations and matters like meat offered to idols. All such things are good in
principle, but using them is not expedient if they are detrimental to the
kingdom. Paul refused to let desire for such things override more important
considerations.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:10.0pt'>                        </span><span style='font-size:
12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>**1 Corinthians <b>15</b>:27 observes regarding Psalm <b>8</b>:6
(“He put all things in subjection under his feet”), <i>“He is excepted who put
all things in subjection to him.”    </i>                                                               **Ephesians
<b>5</b>:20 says, <i>“. . . giving thanks always <u>for</u> all things.”</i>
This passage goes beyond 1 Thessalonians <b>5</b>:18, which says, <i>“Give
thanks <u>in</u> everything.”</i></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                        **Ephesians <b>5</b>:29
claims, <i>“No one ever hated their own flesh.”</i> (The reference could be for
all “people” [</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>ὑπὲρ</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'> </span><span lang=EL
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>πάντων</span><span style='font-size:
12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>, <i>hyper pantōn</i>].)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                        **Paul, the
prisoner in Rome, wrote to the Philippians that he “had all things” (<b>4</b>:18),
meaning that he had received from Epaphroditus the supplies they had sent him
and that he had everything he felt he needed.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                        **Colossians <b>1</b>:16
does not mean “the firstborn of all creation” created God and the Holy Spirit
just because the text says he created all things.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                        **Colossians <b>3</b>:20,
22 says for children and servants to obey their parents and masters in all
things. Are there not some things wherein it would be appropriate to disobey?</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                        **Paul tells

goes on to say that the house of Onesiphorus stood by him (<b>1</b>:16-18).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                        **Hebrews <b>9</b>:27
claims that it is appointed to man to die once, but Enoch and Elijah did not
die; neither will they die who are alive at Christ’s coming.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                        **1 Peter <b>2</b>:13
tells people to submit to every ordinance of man, but Peter himself told the
authorities in Jerusalem that he had to obey God rather than people.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                        **<i>Forever</i>
often stands within an implied limitation, particularly an implied
dispensational limitation. For a list of examples, see <i>What the Bible Says
About Salvation</i>, pp. 125-28; add Leviticus <b>23</b>:21 regarding the
observance of Pentecost; Psalm<b> 111</b>:7-8; <b>119</b>:152, 160, though the
law has been nailed to the cross (Colossians <b>2</b>:14); Luke<b> 16</b>:9;
and John <b>14</b>:16. This word gets very much involved in the issue of
whether the damned have eternal conscious existence. 2 Chronicles <b>7</b>:16
says that God was to reside in his temple “forever.”</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:10.0pt'>                        </span><span style='font-size:
12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>**The scope of the atonement—both as to universalism and
particularism—involves the force of universal terms that appear in passages
addressing this issue (<i>What the Bible Says About Salvation</i>, pp. 119-23).
Regarding the scope of the atonement, universalists take the universal terms as
actual, conditionalists take them as potential, and particularists take them
within implied limitations.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                  Universal words are
often associated with prayers: Matthew <b>7</b>:7 (= Luke <b>11</b>:9); <b>17</b>:19-20;
<b>18</b>:19; <b>21</b>:21-22; Mark <b>11</b>:24; John <b>14</b>:13; (<b>15</b>:7);
<b>15</b>:16b; <b>16</b>:23-24; 1 John <b>3</b>:22; <b>5</b>:14.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                  From another angle, we
wonder whether the universal expressions that speak of the lostness of the
unevangelized may compare with the universal expressions that speak of the
lostness of Israel. <i>“He came to his own but his own did not receive him”</i>
is meant as a generality rather than an absolute, as can be told from the
succeeding statement, <i>“but to as many as <u>did</u> receive him he gave the
right to become sons of God”</i> (John <b>1</b>:11 + 12). Similarly, Paul
speaks of Israel: <i>“What Israel is seeking he did not obtain, but election
obtained it”</i> (Romans <b>11</b>:7). In the surrounding context, the apostle
speaks of the remnant doctrine in that it is a hardening “in part” (<b>11</b>:25,
<i>etc</i>.). By and large, Israel was lost; by and large the unevangelized are
lost—an even higher proportion presumably.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:10.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  2. Limited negative (See examples
under “Law of Contrast.”)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  3. Characteristic action (<i>vs</i>.
continuous)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        One recurring translation
problem comes from the fact that Greek uses the same form for characteristic
and iterative action as it does for progressive action in the present,
imperfect, and future tenses.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        </span><span style='font-size:
12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>**Acts <b>8</b>: <i>“When Simon saw that the Holy Spirit
was-given/was-being-given.”</i> Either translation is possible, but the ideas
are quite different. Does the text mean that the Holy Spirit was
characteristically given this way? More likely it means the Spirit was being
given this way in this case; Simon would not likely have been in a position to
conclude such was the characteristic pattern (unless the characteristic aspect
came more from Luke’s knowledge of the process than from the viewpoint of
Simon).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                        **Romans <b>11</b>:29
says, <i>“The gifts and the calling of God are not repented of,”</i> making it
a generality statement. The verse could be translated, <i>“The gifts and the
calling of God are not being repented of,”</i> a progressive present applicable
to the specific issue Paul is discussing regarding the relation of God to
Israel. The gifts and calling of Israel as not being repented of in opening up
the gospel to Gentiles as Gentiles. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                        There are cases
where gifts and calling <u>were</u> withdrawn: Saul’s dynasty was removed from
being the royal house in Israel, Eli’s lineage was removed from serving as the
priests, Judas was removed from being an apostle, Samson’s supernatural
strength was taken away; note Luke<b> 20</b>:16, <i>“He will come and destroy
these husbandmen and will give the vineyard to others” </i>(cp. Matthew<b> 21</b>:41);
and especially Matthew<b> 21</b>:43 in relation to Romans <b>11</b>:29, <i>“The
kingdom of God will be taken away from you and given to a nation bringing forth
the fruits thereof.”</i> </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                        **Hebrews <b>7</b>:7
makes the observation, <i>“Without any dispute, the less is blessed by the
greater.”</i> Or should the verse be translated, <i>“The less is being blessed
by the greater</i> [in this instance]” since people are said to bless God (Luke
<b>2</b>:28 + 34;<b> 24</b>:53? James <b>3</b>:9)? The claim that Melchizedek
was greater than Abraham (as indicated by his blessing the latter) comes from the
nature of the situation recounted in Genesis.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  4. Generality statements (principles
<i>vs.</i> rules/laws/regulations)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        Statements are often cast in
the form of absolutes when they are meant as generalities. They assume all
things being equal. This kind of expression is particularly prominent in wisdom
literature like proverbs, because such literature deals with generalities and
notable tendencies.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        </span><span style='font-size:
12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>**Proverbs <b>22</b>:7 says that if you train up children
in the way they should go, they will not depart from it when they are grown up.
Solomon is stating a general principle, not making a promise.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                        **The
generalized anti-divorce statement in Mark <b>10</b>:2-12 does not contradict
Matthew <b>5</b>:31-32; <b>19</b>:3-12 (= Mark <b>10</b>:2-12), which contain an
exception clause about adultery, or 1 Corinthians <b>7</b>, which brings up
desertion. Perhaps it is legitimate to envision other circumstances like the
one Jesus specifies. The Lord, for example, evidently infers from the
prohibition against murder that it is wrong also to hate. What the commandment
prohibited also prohibits what leads to it or has the same character.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                        **John <b>1</b>:11-12:
<i>“His own people did not receive him, but as many as did </i></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><i><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                             receive him
. . .”</span></i></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                        **Acts and the

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;

in categorical form (note “relative negation” above) and <i>vice versa</i>. Matters
of process are expressed as matters of state; degree terminology substitutes
for categorical difference. </span></p>

there are an infinite number of possibilities between crosswise and head-on.</span></p>

style='font-size:12.0pt'>            E. Semitic Tendency to Use Concrete for
the Non-Concrete (abstract and spiritual)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  Hebrew thought not only
personalizes, historicizes, and ritualizes; it also concretizes abstract
reference. Planting becomes the picture for getting something started; growing
is scripture’s image for increase by degrees; fruit and harvest are its images
for cause and result. One function of figurative speech is to make abstract
things tangible and concrete; consequently, this Semitic characteristic further
illustrates the tendency to speak figuratively rather than literally or
scientifically.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        </span><span style='font-size:
12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>**We can illustrate the difference between concrete and
abstract this way. If parents owe a daughter three allowances of $5 each and
she owes them $11.95 for phone bills and they owe her $14 for housecleaning and
she owes them $7.50 that she had borrowed, they could abstractly figure out on
paper the final result ($9.55), or they could concretely exchange money back
and forth till they had paid each other everything they owed.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                        **Scripture uses
body-part word pictures with God (anthropomorphisms).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                        **The “presence”
of the Lord with someone uses a locational figure for a non-locational (interpersonal,
“spiritual”) reality. Presence is put for relationship (cp. the indwelling idea).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            F. Amount and Number for Degree</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        </span><span style='font-size:
12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>**The statement <i>“I learned a lot”</i> means <i>“I
learned something important,”</i> because there have not been a large number of
things taught.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                        **Compare the
Hebrew idiom called <u>majestic plural</u>, where plural number is put for high
degree.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            G. Effect for Process (purpose-result)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        </span><span style='font-size:
12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>**English has the expressions “amounts to,” “in effect,” “is
tantamount to,’” and “is as good as (done)” (<i>“He is as good as elected”</i>;
<i>“She is as good as dead”</i> [1 Timothy <b>5</b>:6; Romans <b>4</b>:19;
Hebrews <b>11</b>:12]). It is a “done deal.” Hebrew has a comparable idiom
called “<u>prophetic perfect</u>,” where the perfect tense—completed action—refers
to something yet future that is sure to happen.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                        **<u>Effective
proleptic usage</u> may explain the variance in the Lamb’s-Book-of-life imagery
in Revelation (see <i>What the Bible Says About Salvation</i>, pp. 456-60).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                        **Psalm <b>16</b>:10:
<i>“You will not allow your Holy One to see corruption.”</i></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                        **In Matthew <b>5</b>:32
Jesus says that when a man divorces his wife, he causes her to commit adultery,
but he does not explain how this is the case. One explanation has been that
divorcing her in effect puts her back in a condition of singleness despite her
having lived with a man. Being divorced is, in effect, like not having meantime
been married. Another effective proleptic approach has been to say that
divorcing her puts her in a practical situation where she about has to remarry
to live, which in terms of God’s ideals is not what he wants, which amounts to “adultery.”
A better understanding is that, by divorcing her, the husband makes her out to
be an adulteress—since adultery is the reason Jesus just gave for divorce; he
ruins her reputation. An even more likely meaning is that he treats her like an
adulteress (cp. <i>“you make God a liar”</i>; 1 John <b>1</b>:10; <b>5</b>:10).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                        **<i>“I never
knew you” </i>in Matthew <b>7</b>:22-23 may illustrate this use of speech.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                        **In John <b>11</b>:4
Jesus says that Lazarus’ sickness is not unto death but for the glory of God,
even though Lazarus later dies, and Jesus knows he will (11-14). In the final
effect, Lazarus did not end up dead but was resurrected by Jesus to the glory
of the Father.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                        **John <b>17</b>:4:
<i>“I have finished the work you gave me to do.”</i> But he had not yet died;
he had not yet said, <i>“It is finished”</i> (John <b>19</b>:30).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                        **The curious
wording in Hebrews <b>3</b>:14 may be effective use of terms: <i>‘“We <u>have
become</u> partakers if we hold fast our original confidence firm <u>to the end</u>.’”</i></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                        **Perhaps this
is the point behind Jesus’ statement about Jairus’ daughter that had died: <i>“She
is not dead; she is sleeping”</i> (Matthew<b> 9</b>:2 = Mark <b>5</b>:39 = Luke
<b>8</b>:52). Inasmuch as Jesus was going to resurrect her from the dead, her
temporary death “amounted to” being asleep—was like being asleep. </span></p>
