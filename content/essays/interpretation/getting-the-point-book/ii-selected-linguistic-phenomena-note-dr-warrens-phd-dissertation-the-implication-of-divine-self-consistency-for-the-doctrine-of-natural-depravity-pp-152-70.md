<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-27.0pt;margin-bottom:

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  Streamlining causal distinctions is a
shorthand way of speaking about causal relationships. It occurs especially when
the exact manner of connection is unimportant for present purposes. It can
create ambiguity because the (1) <u>mode of causality</u> and (2) <u>the
directness of causation</u> get blurred. All languages streamline causality and
interchange modality, but Hebrew does so more than English and other modern
languages do in cultures impacted by scientific precision. Much of the “God
talk” in scripture comes to mean something different when English speakers use
it; they bring the verbiage into their language without adjusting its import. By
allowing variation within the mode, Semitic thought tends to be general and
holistic in the following ways:  </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-27.0pt;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span

0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        a.   It conflates <u>permission
and prescription</u>.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-27.0pt;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span

effect</u> (purpose and result).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-27.0pt;margin-bottom:

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-27.0pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        1. Prescriptive-Permissive </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-27.0pt;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        What God allows he is said to
do. Semitic idiom credits God with doing what he allows, commands, and
motivates. Prescriptive means that God initiates the activity; permissive means
that he allows what secondary agencies initiate, whether natural or personal.
In any case, God supplies the decisive factor as to whether something happens,
because he is creator, omnipotent, and omniscient. Since God originated <u>natural
law</u> to operate blindly, any occurrence he does not prevent represents his
permissive will. Since he does not intervene between a <u>personal choice</u>
and its active fulfillment, the occurrence is his permissive will. Inbuilt laws
of nature and supernatural intervention correlate with permissive and
prescriptive.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        The difference between
required and allowed is comparable to the different meanings for the English
expression <i>“it is biblical.”</i> It can mean <u>fits with</u> the Bible
(although it goes beyond it) or <u>is required by</u> the Bible. The difference
between immediate and ultimate frames of reference appears sparingly in
English, but occasionally expressions are used like “act of God” in reference
to hurricanes or floods. The point is not that God directly, purposefully
caused them, but that such catastrophes lie beyond anyone’s control; so they
are under <u>God’s</u> control only. Similarly, the Bible uses expressions like
<i>“Wait on the Lord,” “Turn it over to God,”</i> meaning something as loose as
<i>“Don’t force things.”</i></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>**Genesis <b>42</b>:28: <i>“What is this that <u>God</u>
has done to us?”</i> The previous narrative explains that <u>Joseph</u> had the
money planted in his brothers’ sacks. Joseph told his brothers later that God
had put their treasure in their sacks even though <i>“I had your money”</i> (Genesis
<b>43</b>:23).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;

12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>**<u>the problem of evil (theodicy</u>): Hebrews <b>12</b>:5-6

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                        **<u>The Hebrew
word for <i>give</i> also means <i>allow</i></u> (</span><span lang=HE dir=RTL
style='font-size:14.0pt;color:#7030A0'>נָתַן</span><span dir=LTR></span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'><span dir=LTR></span>, <i>natan</i>). It
is like the English word <i>can</i>, idiomatically used to cover both personal
ability and circumstantial ability (“<u>allow</u>,” “may”). Jesus tells Pilate
that he would have no power over Jesus if it were not given him from above (John
<b>19</b>:11). <i>“The God of heaven”</i> gave Nebuchadnezzar his kingdom
(Daniel <b>2</b>:37-38). Giving does not eliminate accepting what is given,
even as we recognize despite using the question, <i>“Who gives this woman to be
married to this man?”</i> Jesus says that he had not lost any of the ones the
Father had given him except Judas—who later apostatized (John <b>17</b>:12).
Children are conceived of as given by the Lord: <i>“You </i>[Lord] <i>have
given me no seed”</i> (Abraham; Genesis <b>15</b>:3; cp. Genesis <b>30</b>:2,
18, 20, 23).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                        **Calvinism,
Augustinianism, Pentecostalism, fiat creationism are especially prone to read
this Semitic phenomenon like a straightforward Western expression. The resulting
interpretations are difficult to counter because we could almost always imagine
some kind of miraculous intervention as a mechanism that justifies unlikely
sounding inferences. In literalism generally, supernatural causation could
override any objection to a literal take on virtually any figurative or
parabolic presentation: a talking snake in the Garden, a tree that makes a
person wise, a fruit that can cause eternal life, a woman constructed out of a
man’s rib that is not missing later; the man in the Parable of the Rich Man and
Lazarus who talks from hell across a great gulf to Abraham and asks for drops
of water to cool his tongue in a fire, and so on and so on. Mistaking this
linguistic feature in interpretation leads to overly supernaturalizing other
biblical processes as well, not the least of which are salvation matters. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;

style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                        **Man’s turning

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  2. Direct-indirect</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        What people do through others they
do themselves. What God commands he is said to do. In direct-for-indirect
fashion, a third party would treat the one you are identified with the same way
he is treating you. The third party assumes you are alike if you identify with
each other. Streamlining causal distinctions happens more prominently in
personal series and interpersonal associations than in impersonal ones.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        </span><span style='font-size:
12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>**Using an amanuensis does not keep the men of the Bible
from being the authors of their works: Jeremiah <b>36</b>:1-3 + 4; Romans <b>16</b>:22
+ <b>15</b>:15; 1 Corinthians<b> 16</b>:21 + <b>14</b>:37.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                        **Exodus <b>4</b>:11
records the conversation between Moses and God in which God credits himself
with giving dumbness, deafness, blindness.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                        **God spoke to
Moses on Mount Sinai (Exodus <b>20</b>), but it was more directly an angel who
spoke to him (Acts <b>7</b>:38, 53; Hebrews<b> 2</b>:2)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                        **God hardened
Pharaoh’s heart (Exodus<b> 10</b>:1; cp.<b> 4</b>:21; <b>8</b>:19; <b>9</b>:35;
<b>10</b>:20; <b>11</b>:20; <b>14</b>:4, 8, 17; Joshua <b>11</b>:20; Romans<b>
9</b>:18), yet Pharaoh hardened his own heart (Exodus <b>8</b>:15; cp. <b>8</b>:32;
<b>9</b>:34; 1 Samuel <b>6</b>:6; Proverbs <b>21</b>:29; note as well Exodus<b>
7</b>:22, 14; <b>9</b>:7).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                        **In the course
of Exodus <b>23</b>:28-31, God not only says <u>he</u> will drive out the
Canaanites, but that he will deliver them into the hands of the <u>Israelites</u>,
who will drive them out. The point is that the Israelites need not fear going
into battle, because he will guarantee victory even if he has to add to their
efforts.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                        **In Moses’ Song
of Triumph, Miriam and the young women say that God cast the horse and his
rider into the [Red] sea (Exodus <b>15</b>:1) when, as a matter of fact, the
Egyptian soldiers drove into the sea of their own accord.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                        **<i>“I will
visit the iniquity of the fathers upon the children of the third and fourth
generations” </i>(Numbers <b>14</b>:13; cp. Exodus <b>20</b>:2-5). One
translation says in the Exodus passage that God “punishes” the children unto the
third and fourth generations, thus introducing the implication of guilt. Natural
social process provides the first level of investigation here. Natural social
process is indirect, but God is said to do it. Evidently, the text is talking
about natural social consequences (<u>suffering</u>) rather than <u>guilt,</u>
because Ezekiel <b>18</b>:20 says exactly the opposite: <i>“The son shall not
be put to death for the father’s sin”</i>; <i>“the unrighteousness of the unrighteous
shall be upon himself.”</i> At a second level, the Exodus text anticipates the
prospects of idolatry when the Israelites get drawn to the gods of the
Canaanites if they do not eradicate these native peoples. When God delivered
the Israelites to the Babylonians or Assyrians, three to four generations passed
by before deliverance from those oppressions took place.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        Direct-indirect includes
actions assigned to <u>their ultimate inspiring source</u>. In Semitic idiom
evil and unfortunate things are simply assigned to Satan and good things are
assigned to God. (a) The same activity is sometimes assigned to both God and
Satan. (b) Man’s activity is assigned to Satan (it is also assigned to God; see
the previous listing). (c) Man’s turning away is God’s giving up. (d) God and
Satan are said to undo each other’s activities. (See also above under
“permissive-prescriptive,” where God does evil and Satan does good.) Semitic
idiom (e) assigns to God causes that are unclear or invisible.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                         <i>“What people do through
others they do themselves”</i> suggests a way those who deny the deity of Jesus
can understand the use of <i>God</i> in reference to him. He is functional
deity; that is, he is deity in the sense that he operates on behalf of deity. This
is the reason it is clearer to look at what happens (forgiving sin, creating) rather

style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  4. Purpose-result conflation</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        Some results are not purposed.
A colleague walked into my office one day and said he could not drink coffee
because he had a test to write, which reminded me that I had one to write too. He
did not intend (purpose) to remind me, but that was the result. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        In biblical interpretation one
complicating factor comes from (1) t<u>he nature of Semitic expression</u>, as often
noted already. Besides the general tendency not to distinguish modes of
operation and causality, the idiom tends not to differentiate between purpose
and result. Another complicating factor for the purpose-result distinction
comes from (2) <u>the nature of God</u>. Omniscience-plus-omnipotence blurs the
purpose-result distinction. A course of action may originate elsewhere in the
free will of secondary agencies, yet since God knows about it ahead of time
(omniscience) and can intervene to stop it (omnipotence), he has a kind of
purpose in mind by letting it happen. That purpose, however, is permissive rather
than prescriptive in such cases. God knows that such and such will not
frustrate his purposes.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        We say that “in effect” this
or that is what someone is doing. Examples of this force of comments include
Isaiah <b>6</b>:9-10; Deuteronomy<b> 29</b>:4; Psalm <b>69</b>:23, passages quoted
in Romans <b>11</b>:8-10 (cp. Matthew <b>13</b>:14-15 = Mark <b>4</b>:12 = Luke
<b>18</b>:10; John <b>12</b>:40; Acts<b> 28</b>:26-27). God was not trying to
close their eyes so the Israelites could not see, and the like, but that was
the effect of what he did when he spoke to them for quite opposite reasons. The
difference between intent and effect is caused by the hearer’s character and
responses. The “in effect” idea applies also to passages about hardening Pharaoh’s
heart (see above).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                        **John <b>18</b>:32:

style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  5. Logical-chronological order</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        Logical and chronological
order are not necessarily the same. Two things may be chronologically
simultaneous while logically sequential or in opposite order. (Note the
objection to the mental slip, <i>post hoc, ergo propter hoc</i>: “after this,
therefore because of this”) The logical-chronological distinction can be let
slide as immaterial as long as it does not affect practical action, but failing
to make creates in practice a plethora of difficulties.</span></p>

margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                  **a <u>prayer</u>
offered after the time when God sets things in motion to answer </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;

style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                        **Romans<b> 5</b>:12:

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                        **<u>predestination</u>
of individuals to eternal life or death before the foundation of the world.
There is a distinction between <i>“God wrote a person’s name in the book <u>before</u>
the person had faith”</i> (Matthew <b>25</b>:35; Revelation<b> 13</b>:8; <b>17</b>:8)
and <i>“God wrote his name in the book <u>because</u> he had faith”</i>
(Revelation <b>20</b>:12; <b>21</b>:27). If the apostles were included in the
mission of the seventy, then Judas’ name was in the Book of Life at one time
(Luke <b>10</b>:20: <i>“Rejoice that your names are written in heaven”</i>);
yet he <i>“went to his own place”</i> (Acts<b> 1</b>:25). Judas’ name would
have been “blotted out of” the Book as per Revelation <b>3</b>:5; <b>22</b>:19b
&lt; Psalm <b>69</b>:28. Other passages speak simply of being or not being
written in heaven or the Book: Daniel <b>12</b>:1 &lt; Philippians<b> 4</b>:3;
Hebrews <b>12</b>:23; Revelation <b>20</b>:15.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                        The mixture of (a)
already being or not being <u>in</u> the book before the foundation of the
world plus (b) being removed <u>from</u> that book illustrates the variance of
usage we must accommodate in biblical interpretation regarding modes of
causality on a whole range of other subjects as well. The idiom relates to the
doctrines of predestination and perseverance of the saints (<i>vs</i>. apostasy).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        As noted before, much of theological
controversy on primary issues results from not seeing that Jewish expression
tends to omit intermediate causes, to blur purpose-result distinctions, to
overlook the permissive-prescriptive variable, and to correlate logical-chronological
sequence. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        One reason it is better to
develop theology out of didactic material than out of historical events is that
in the record of events, logical connectors are even less clear than in
descriptive statements. For this reason, the interpreter does well, for
example, not to try to develop from accounts in Acts an understanding of the
relationship between baptism and receiving the Spirit. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#7030A0'>                        Peter appeals to
the conversion of Cornelius’ household as a basis for saying that Gentiles as
Gentiles can be identified with the Messiah; they do not have to become part of
national Israel in the process (Act<b>s 15</b>:7-12;<b> 10</b>:34-35). It would
not be legitimate to take every feature of that conversion account and use it
as a model for conversions as 1 Corinthians <b>12</b>:30 shows in that not
everyone speaks in tongues.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        What is beyond a person’s
control is said to be God’s act, even as we speak of tornados and floods as “acts
of God.” In other words, what people often call “luck” scripture verbalizes as
divine activity.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        In summary, God is said to do
(1) what he <u>does directly</u>; (2) what he does through another—<u>does
indirectly</u>, including what he commands another to do, what he is the
ultimate inspiring source of in someone else’s doing, and what is done by those
identified with him; (3) what he <u>allows others to do</u>—to originate and
carry out, (4) what he <u>makes a decisive contribution to doing</u>. All four
of these variables are significant because (a) they impact our understanding of
the <u>nature of language</u>—Semitic language especially—and the conclusions
we draw from its statements; and (b) because they relate to the <u>nature of
God</u> as omniscient and omnipotent.</span></p>
