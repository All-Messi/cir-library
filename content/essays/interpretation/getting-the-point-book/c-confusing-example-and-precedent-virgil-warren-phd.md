<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            C. Confusing example and precedent                                           Virgil
Warren, PhD</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  Interpretation deals with what
scripture teaches about doctrinal matters to be <u>believed</u>. It deals with
what scripture teaches about practices to be <u>done</u>. Within “praxis” lies
a further distinction between example and precedent—between what was done and
what was meant to be done by other people thereafter. Establishing precedent has
two steps: (1) showing that such and such was, in fact, a New Testament pattern;
and (2) showing that the pattern was <u>meant</u> to apply universally and
permanently.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  1. Distinguishing the types of things</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        a. Precedent <i>vs</i>. example</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              “Precedent” has more
than one meaning. Saying there is precedent for handling a matter in some way
could mean that it <u>may</u> still be handled that way (as one possibility). In
this study, however, precedent means the way a matter <u>was intended to be </u>handled.
Precedent in this sense becomes case law (as opposed to statutory law) like the
tradition of the elders in Judaism (as opposed to the Mosaic Law itself).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              Hermeneutics deals with <u>doctrinal
affirmation</u> of scripture about the nature of things, the purpose of God,
the pattern of authentic human life. Precedent adds the task of attaching
significance to <u>apostolic practice</u> as recorded in the New Testament. Commandment
and precedent both represent God’s intentions for the body of Christ. “Precedent”
combines with “example” as two ways of viewing today’s meaning for the
apostles’ ways of doing things. The former term takes their actions simply as
examples of how they operated in the first-century world; the latter adds the
conviction that those procedures established norms for subsequent Christian
practice. Adherents of the faith need principles for distinguishing one from
the other.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              At issue here is a significant
concern for the modern church because it affects the status of culture and the use
of functional equivalents. It likewise bears on Christian unity because
Christians may vary in how they perceive the significance of New Testament
practice. There is significant correlation between the precedent-example and
gospel-culture distinctions.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              The precedent-example
issue impacts cross-cultural witness. What are Christians free to do with New
Testament practices that are counter-cultural today? What of one-cup communion
in a society that is hypersensitive to germs or feetwashing in a culture that
ridicules “smelly feet”? What about immersion in places with little water, or
communion where grapes do not grow? Do missionaries substitute functional
equivalents, or do they suspend action till it can be done “the way God wants
it done”? If they do not clearly understand what constitutes Christianity, they
may find themselves culturalizing rather than Christianizing, or syncretizing
rather than Christianizing by overdoing functional equivalency. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              The issue is primarily a
matter of hermeneutics because it is primarily a matter of authority. Precedent
for subsequent generations amounts to the same thing as the cross-cultural
transmission of gospel forms, because many questions about precedent have to do
with formal, ritual, symbolic acts. An evangelist needs to transmit a form to
the receiving culture—especially if difficulties are involved—rests on the <u>intent</u>
behind the New Testament examples of form and more basically <u>who</u> the
intending persons were. God’s spokesmen cannot override divine authority for
anything doctrinal or behavioral; so they must decide what to do.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              Because of unity
concerns, Christians raise warnings about t<u>rying to build doctrines on narratives</u>.
Without accompanying explanations, the interpreter cannot always tell from what
happened what the reasons were for what happened. For these reasons,
explanatory materials need to take precedence over unexplained narrative in
building any viewpoint from scripture (See <i>What the Bible Says About
Salvation</i>, p. 302.)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              Primitivism—or restorationism,
as it is also called—has drawn heavily on the concept of apostolic precedent. Some
have taken it to the point of becoming formulaic and patternistic, as if
everything can and must follow those exact patterns or desired results are not
forthcoming. Primitivism may get rid of some excesses of tradition by using precedent
as a restrictive tool; but it does not provide a trustworthy guide because it
can bring in an apparently irrelevant restriction. The positive approach calls
for knowing where to stop in deciding what New Testament examples would have
been meant as precedents. Otherwise, to do anything as a church would require
justification for doing it. The presence of example does not necessarily
require (precedent), and the absence of example does not necessarily prohibit. There
has to be more to the solution of the problem; so the following considerations
come into the process.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        b. Apostolic precedent <i>vs</i>.
church tradition</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              The discussion about
precedent deals with “approved precedent” in contrast to church tradition. The
conviction in primitivism has been that only biblical teaching and apostolic
precedent can have any <u>authority</u> in the church. Even in Protestantism,
however, there has been the belief that tradition has value for <u>interpretation</u>.
Primitivism has not granted <u>priority</u> to ancient interpretation despite
the common idea that temporal proximity to the apostles gave the early fathers
extra-biblical access to their teaching and practice. Justified skepticism here
arises from the fact that even before the apostles had passed off the scene—indeed
from the very beginning of the church, foreign teaching and practices were
being promoted by Jewish and pagan converts as well as by outsiders who sought
to syncretize the Christian movement with Greek philosophy. The legal bent in
Jewish backgrounds and the hypostatic presuppositions in Greek philosophy
combined early on to pervert the interpersonal message of Christianity with
wooden ideas issuing from tasteless interpretation and clumsy theologizing—and
that from among the most prominent persons in the early church.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;

style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  3. Principles for distinguishing
precedent and example</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify;text-indent:-1.25in'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                                      a.   <u>Sufficient
examples</u> to establish a pattern and show some geographical and cross-cultural
distribution</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        b.   <u>Uniform examples</u></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        c.   <u>Presence of
accompanying propositions</u> that explain the reasons for the practice. Is the
example limited or universalized by anything in the context, as for example the
matter of not marrying or remarrying <i>“on account of the present distress”</i>
(1 Corinthians<b> 7</b>:26)? The precedent issue can be mixed with teaching
materials.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        d.   <u>Consistency in the
argument pattern</u>; by parity of reason can the argument pattern be used on
other issues as well? If the same pattern of reasoning can yield obviously
false results on other topics, there is something wrong with it on this topic. What
“proves too much” does not prove what it seems to prove (<i>What the Bible Says
About Salvation</i>, p. 401, ¶ 1-2).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        e.   <u>Nature of the case</u>;
can the principle involved be expressed in other ways, or is it tied to the
form in a one-for-one fashion either by uniformity of example or clear positive
commandment?</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        f.    <u>Persons giving the
examples</u>; is this practice also carried out by non-religious people outside
the church? If so, it is a cultural thing: <u>the holy kiss</u> (1 Corinthians<b>
16</b>:20; 2 Corinthians <b>13</b>:12), <u>footwashing</u> (Genesis <b>18</b>:4;
<b>19</b>:2; <b>24</b>:32; <b>43</b>:24; Judges <b>19</b>:21; 1 Samuel <b>25</b>:41;
2 Samuel <b>11</b>:8; John <b>13</b>:5-12; ?1 Timothy<b> 5</b>:10 + Hebrews <b>13</b>:2),
<u>anointing with oil</u> (Ruth <b>3</b>:3; Luke <b>4</b>:3-50; cp. Psalm <b>23</b>:5;
Matthew<b> 6</b>:17; cp. 2 Samuel <b>12</b>:20;<b> 14</b>:2). Approved
precedent is apostolic precedent.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        g.   <u>Dispensational
distinctions</u></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        h.   <u>Cultural practice
outside the church</u></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        i.    <u>Motives for seeking
an alternative to the New Testament practice</u></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  4. Application of principles to
selected issues</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        a. The instrument question</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              Non-instrument
hermeneutics regarding behavior/practice derives from commandment, precedent, necessary
inference. Certain issues should be considered in each case. In <u>commandment</u>,
readers must discern (1) whether the imperatives of the text are addressed to them
(dispensational distinctions, special cases,<i> etc</i>.) and (2) whether they
are cases of commandment or advice. In <u>precedent</u>, the issues are (1)
whether the action was <u>intended</u> to set a precedent or was simply an
example and (2) whether it has the marks that a precedent needs to have. In <u>necessary
inference</u>, the questions are (1) whether people are properly inferring and
(2) whether the accompanying presuppositions they bring to the issue are
correct.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              (1) <u>Uniformity,
distribution, and amount of example</u> do not apply, because there is no
example one way or the other. Old Testament examples are disallowed because of
the distinction between covenants. References in Revelation are dismissed
because they are in figurative eschatological contexts. Examples in non-church
settings are set aside because they do not relate to worship.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              (2) <u>The nature of the
case</u> does not help, because musical instruments are not evil in themselves,
or they could not have been allowed in Old Testament worship. The idea that
silence equals prohibition could not be applied to life as a whole; hence, by
some principle it must be limited demonstrably to worship so that the principle
becomes “silence in matters of worship equals prohibition.” A distinction applies
here between <i>acts of worship</i> and <i>aids to worship</i>. Accompanying
singing with an instrument or setting the mood of a service with instrumental
music is not the same thing as worshiping God with an instrument.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              (3) <u>Accompanying
propositions</u> could conceivably prove useful, because God might decide for
reasons adequate to himself that he prefers a particular set of ways for being
worshiped. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                       (a) Hebrews <b>7</b>:14
has been used for this purpose. Silence in the law regarding anyone from Judah
serving as priest prohibited anyone from Judah serving in this role in Mosaism.
The problem, however, is that who can legitimately serve as priest is an
organizational matter, not a matter of worship; it is a qualification for an
office, not a description of worship. Priests led in worship, but who could be
priest is a different question from what priests should do. It is agreed that
under law, silence is prohibition relative to organizational structure; it is not
agreed that silence on non-legal things in general is prohibition.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                       (b) Arguments
from analogy are used, including the fact that Cain's sacrifice was not
acceptable whereas Abel’s was. The supposition is that Cain offered from the produce
of the land rather than from the offspring of the herd, and God had not
commanded produce to be used as an offering. The Old Testament does not say,
however, that such was the reason God did not accept it. Hebrews <b>11</b>:4
simply puts the reason in the lack of Cain’s faith.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              (4) <u>Consistency of
the argument pattern</u> becomes difficult since silence as prohibition cannot
apply to the whole of life and no passage establishes it within the limited
area of worship. It may be observed, however, that in the Old Testament the Law
of Moses did not provide for instrumental music; yet David brought it into the
temple worship if in fact it did not appear with the tabernacle worship before.
If silence prohibits in matters of worship, then he would have sinned in
introducing it.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                       Much of the
impetus for the non-instrument worship came originally in part of the
Reformation as a reaction to perceived excesses in medieval worship,
particularly from John Calvin. That raises the question of how such practices can
be eliminated if not because silence as prohibition. The answer comes is that
certain promises are associated with some of those practices that are
unacceptable; God has not promised the blessings the clergy promises in
connection with them. But more to the point is that the church is an
interpersonal system, and its worship is interpersonal; so formalism and ritual
are not germane to it or its operations and detract from the purposes for which
people assemble before God.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        b. Church organization</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              (1) <u>The amount,
consistency, and distribution of the examples</u> are rather clear, especially
in regard to eldership: </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                       (a)   Jerusalem:
Acts <b>11</b>:30; <b>15</b>:5</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                       (b)   Philippi:
Philippians <b>1</b>:1</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                       (c)   Galatia
and Syria: Acts <b>14</b>:23</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                       (d)   Ephesus: Acts
<b>20</b>:17, 28; Ephesians <b>4</b>:11; 1 Peter<b> 5</b>:2-5; </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                               1
Timothy <b>3</b>:1ff., and so on</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                       (e)   Crete: Titus
<b>1</b>:5ff.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                       (f)    Northern
Asia Minor: 1 Peter <b>5</b>:1ff. (James <b>5</b>:14?)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              (2) <u>The nature of the
case</u> is that a specified organization is necessarily exclusive of all other
parallel possibilities. In organizational matters, injunction is restriction. Any
additions at the top or in the middle would reshuffle the responsibilities of
the offices provided for, from lordship all the way down through the diaconate.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              (3) <u>Accompanying
propositions</u> show that apostles, prophets, pastor-teacher-elder-bishops are
of <i>divine origin</i> (1 Corinthians <b>12</b>:28; Ephesians <b>4</b>:11; cp.
Acts <b>20</b>:17, 28; <b>14</b>:23). Christ received these gifts and gave them
to men; the Holy Spirit made the Ephesian elders responsible for their charges;
Christ personally chose the apostles. The <i>intended permanence </i>of the
offices can be seen individually in reference to the Lord (1 Corinthians <b>15</b>:24ff.),
the apostles (Luke <b>22</b>:30; Revelation <b>21</b>:14), and elders (Acts <b>20</b>:28-30;
1 Timothy <b>3-4</b>; Titus<b> 1</b>:9-<b>2</b>:13). Furthermore, there is no
amendment clause.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                       These
observations, however, still leave unaddressed whether the pattern we might
discern was intended to be that way in an exclusive sense. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        c. Anointing with oil for
physical healing</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              Some religious groups
practice what they call faith healing and include a rather formulaic approach
to what makes the healing “work.” The procedure must be followed precisely, or
the promise is not necessarily effective. Since anointing with oil does not
have a place in Western non-religious culture, Western Christians do not even
think about it when they approach the Lord for healing. Faith healers, then,
characteristically tie the lack of healing to not following the correct pattern
of calling the <u>elders</u> (plural), <u>laying on hands</u>, <u>praying</u>,
and this matter of <u>anointing with olive oil</u>. James <b>5</b>:14-15
provides the central text for this view of healing.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              (1) Uses of oil in the
New Testament</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                       (a)   As an
indication of the end of mourning: Matthew <b>6</b>:17 (cp.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                               2
Samuel <b>12</b>:20; <b>14</b>:2)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                       (b)   In
preparations for burial: Mark<b> 16</b>:1; compare<b> 14</b>:8 = John </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                               <b>12</b>:3-8
+ <b>11</b>:2?</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                       (c)   As a cosmetic:
Luke <b>4</b>:3-50 (cp. Psalm <b>23</b>:5); Ruth <b>3</b>:3; Luke <b>7</b>:38 +
4b; compare Mark <b>14</b>:8 = John<b> 12</b>:3-8 +<b> 11</b>:2?</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                       (d)   As a sign
of divine appointment: Luke <b>4</b>:18; Acts <b>4</b>:27; <b>10</b>:38; 2
Corinthians<b> 1</b>:21; Hebrews <b>1</b>:9</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                       (e) In
connection with healing: Mark <b>6</b>:13 (miraculous); James </span><span
lang=FR style='font-size:12.0pt'>5:14 (non-miraculous); compare Revelation <b>3</b>:18;
Luke <b>10</b>:34</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span lang=FR
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span lang=FR
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              </span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>(2) Observations on James <b>5</b>:14</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                       (a)   James <b>5</b>:14
evidently does not refer to miraculous healing: <i>“the prayer of faith will
save the sick”</i> (<b>5</b>:15). It appears in a context about the
effectiveness of prayer.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                       (b)   This
situation presupposes all the conditions normally attending prayer: that it is
in accordance with God’s will, that the request is made in faith, that God does
not consider his grace already sufficient for the person in need (2 Corinthians
<b>12</b>:9). In conclusion, the particulars of the James <b>5</b> text are no
stronger than the general promise that whatever believers ask in Christ’s name
believing they will receive (Matthew <b>21</b>:22,<i> etc</i>.).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              (3) Observations on faith
healing</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                       Only two cases appear
where anointing with oil accompanies healing—Mark<b> 6</b>:13 and James<b> 5</b>:14.
There is lack of uniformity in the examples: in all the other cases of
miraculous or answered-prayer healing, no mention is made of anointing with
olive oil. The kind of thing it is can vary without contradiction. A
non-germane cultural pattern does not have to be maintained by the church in
other cultures; olive trees do not even grow in some parts of the world. Other
explanations could be given for the use of oil in the text of James <b>5</b>:
it may be a way of showing the expectancy of answered prayer by preparing the
person to “meet the public” or resume daily tasks (Matthew <b>6</b>:17); it
could be a non-essential symbol of the Holy Spirit (1 John <b>2</b>:20, 27).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        d. The use of church buildings</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              There is a lack of
uniformity in the use of meeting facilities by the early New Testament church. They
met in the temple (Acts <b>2</b>:46), in the school of Tyrannus (Acts <b>19</b>:9-10),
and in homes. There are no examples of church buildings. There are no
accompanying prohibitions; the nature of the case is such that variation can
occur. As a result, the matter falls under expediency and preference. The only
question may be the advisability of wrapping up so many resources in property
and buildings.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        e. Private property</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              There is clearly a lack
of uniformity in the examples. While many Jerusalem Christians sold their
possessions and brought the money to the apostles, not everyone did. Acts <b>12</b>:12
mentions the house of Mary the mother of John Mark, which served as a meeting place
for early Christians. The same applies to Lydia (Acts <b>16</b>:15) and all the
cases of house churches. The houses belonged to someone who could speak for
their use.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              The right to private
property goes all the way back to the situation assumed in the eighth commandment
<i>“Do not steal.”</i></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>
