<p>The author of the Fourth Gospel termed himself “the disciple Jesus loved.” Presumably he wanted to keep his involvement from being too prominent and perhaps distracting while still emphasizing his personal witness of what he records. Choosing this self-designation later in life when he wrote may reflect Jesus’ influence on him from early being one of the “sons of thunder” (Mark <strong>3</strong>:17). Instead of a contrast, however, “the disciple Jesus loved” may be an understatement that any disciple might adopt.</p>
<p>The description occurs first at the Passover meal (<strong>13</strong>:23), where Jesus instituted what we are observing here. He became the real replacement for the lamb in the Passover meal. Our Lord’s Supper commemorates the crucifixion that says of us all that we are disciples Jesus loves; “he loves even me.” Instead of leaning back on Jesus’ flesh and blood at the original supper, we taste the bread and wine that represents the flesh and blood this disciple felt who considered himself most significantly “a disciples Jesus loved.”</p>
