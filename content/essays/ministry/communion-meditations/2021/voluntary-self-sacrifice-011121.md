<p>One notable feature of Christ’s crucifixion is that it was voluntary. Jesus himself did not try to fight against the arresting mob in Gethsemane. He did not use supernatural to drive the temple guard away-even though he evidently could have: the guard falling backward when he said, <em>“I’m the one you are looking for”; let these others go”</em> (John <strong>18</strong>:10-11), He did not let his disciples—like Peter—fight in the Garden to defend him (John <strong>18</strong>:10-11). Jesus pointed out to Pilate. “My kingdom is not of this world, or my servants would be fighting to keep me from being delivered to the Jews” (John<strong> 18</strong>:36). He did not call on the twelve legions of angels at his disposal to protect himself at his arrest (Matthew <strong>26</strong>:53).</p>
<p>That his death for us was voluntary emphasizes his concern for us. He volunteered to do that even before we knew about him and his love for us—which makes it even more astounding.</p>
<p>Our natural response is to have that same mentality and act accordingly-to lay aside personal welfare for the welfare of other people. Taking the bread and wine makes a claim to that virtue.</p>
