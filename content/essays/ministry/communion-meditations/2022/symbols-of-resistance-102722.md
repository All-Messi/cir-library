<p>Resisting temptation is as much a part of life as <em>“doing the right thing.”</em> Jesus did not call the more than 60,000 angels available to him to disperse the mob in Gethsemane (Matthew <strong>26</strong>:23). He could <em>“even now”</em> ask the Father to send them, having already prayed earnestly three times to the Father not to let him get crucified.</p>
<p>He did not use any personal powers to drive his attackers away. John <strong>18</strong>:6 does indicate that <em>“they drew back and fell to the ground”</em> when he confirmed that he was the One they were looking for. Interesting!</p>
<p>Though repeatedly taunted by religious leaders, by the two insurrectionists crucified beside him, by passers-by (Matthew <strong>27</strong>:38-44), and the soldiers that had driven the spikes (Luke <strong>23</strong>:36), he did not yield to the temptation to come down from the cross to spectacularly demonstrate his claims. Saving himself would have relieved the unbelievable pain—but lost the cause.</p>
<p>Like a lamb that doesn’t bleat before its shearers (Isaiah <strong>53</strong>:7), Jesus didn’t rail out condemnations against those falsely accusing him as someone worthy of such a way to die.</p>
<p>Today and in the days ahead, we will cope with temptations to <em>“do the wrong thing.”</em> Here’s a way to look at it: we’re taking in these representations of his body and blood, so we have in us Jesus’ appointed emblems of resistance to temptation.</p>
