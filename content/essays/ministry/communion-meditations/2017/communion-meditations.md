<p class=MsoNormal>Sources of communion meditation</p>

<p class=MsoNormal>            </p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'>            Recorded events that accompanied
his death-resurrection.</p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'>&nbsp;</p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'>            Deliberately working
backwards from the sermon. There are so many appropriate facets of this event. Finding
natural connections with the subject of the day’s sermon triggers some thoughts
that might not come to mind otherwise.</p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'>&nbsp;</p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'>            Festival cycle: The
religious year provides associations with communion observance. Later, the
meaning of the Lord’s Supper may come back to mind by what people encounter after
gathered worship.</p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'>            </p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'>            Current events. What
is happening locally or in the experiences of those gathered can relate
naturally to Christ and aspects of his culminating ministry in Jerusalem. Included
here are social, political, world events, but also the vivid, trying, </p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'>&nbsp;</p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'>            Items associated with
identification with Christ as implied by this memorial observance. Commitment
to Christ to the extent he carried out his commitment to the Father. Internalizing
the values and love for Christ as pictured by consuming the elements.</p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'>&nbsp;</p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'>            In short, many sources
for sermon material serve also as sources of communion meditations.</p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'>&nbsp;</p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'>&nbsp;</p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'>Scriptures especially appropriate
for communion observance</p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'>&nbsp;</p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'>            Isaiah <b>53</b></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'>            Matthew <b>26</b>:26-29
and parallels</p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'>            1 Corinthians <b>11</b>:23-26</p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'>            The Book of Hebrews
with all its priestly references </p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'>&nbsp;</p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'>&nbsp;</p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'>Recommendations</p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'>&nbsp;</p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'>            Length: Communion
meditations are best kept short—maybe two minutes at most. One strong idea is
about all an observant can concentrate on in any one observance. Second and
third ideas can weaken the primary thought. Longer presentations and larger
sets of related ideas can be developed in the sermon. The meditations ought not
compete with the sermon time in the service.</p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'>&nbsp;</p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'>            Relevance. Whenever
natural, it is best to connect the memory of the Messiah with the experiences
and needs of the communicants. The sheer memory of him itself suggests implications
for those who remember him.</p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'>&nbsp;</p>

<p class=MsoNormal>These meditations are not meant to be read to a
congregation. That would detract from the genuineness of the speaker’s
comments.</p>
