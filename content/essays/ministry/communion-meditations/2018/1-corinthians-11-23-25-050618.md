<p>Observing the Lord’s Supper supplies a corrective on church life. In one of the most familiar passages on this matter, Paul says,</p>
<p><em>“</em><em>I received from the Lord what I passed on to you. The night he was betrayed he took a loaf, gave thanks, broke it, and said, </em><em>‘</em><em>This is my body, which is for you. Do this in memory of me.</em><em>’</em><em> After the meal he took the cup, </em><em>‘</em><em>This cup is the New Covenant in my blood. As often as you drink it, do it in memory of me</em><em>.</em><em>’</em><em>”</em></p>
<p>The body and blood that he gave is a correction on the thoughtlessness demonstrated in their eating to the full in front of people who did not have anything to eat (<strong>11</strong>:21). The self-centeredness that lay behind their divisions (<strong>11</strong>:18-19) contrasted with the self-giving of Christ’s very life.</p>
<p>The observance that the early Christians shared called for a corrective in how they observed it. By extension it was to pull them away from other manifestations of these same faults in their broader church life.</p>
<p>Like Christians of old, we are prone to the common faults and weaknesses that people have. Like them, we remember the given body and shed blood as a corrective on our church life.</p>
