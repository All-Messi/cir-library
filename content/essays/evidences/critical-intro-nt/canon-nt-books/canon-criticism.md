<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>    I. Definition of Canonicity</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  Canonicity constitutes <u>normative</u>
literature. It sets the doctrinal “norms” by which we consider issues and measure
viewpoints. In Christianity, canonicity correlates with <u>inspiration</u>. The
canon consists of writings that came from “inspired” men, men whose writings
were as <u>correct</u> and <u>authoritative</u> as they would have been if God
himself had <i>“exhaled them”</i> (2 Timothy<b> 3</b>:16).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>   II. Principles for Canonicity (See separate
document “The Determining Principles for </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>               Canonicity.”)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  The identity and relative importance
of the principles of canonicity—historical, doctrinal, and subjective—form the
framework for determining and evaluating (a) <u>canon</u>icity, (b) continued <u>revelation</u>,
and (c) non-revelational special divine <u>guidance</u>. (See “Testing Current
Manifestation” in notes associated with “Gifts of the Spirit.”)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>  III. Characteristics of Canonicity</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  A.  Apostolicity</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        Those New Testament writings
are canonical that have come from apostles in the primary sense and from those
who served as extensions of the apostolic office, one broad use of the term “apostle.”
The apostle <u>Matthew</u> covers his own gospel; <u>John</u> covers the Gospel
of John, 1 John, 2 John, 3 John, and Revelation; <u>Peter</u> covers 1 Peter
and 2 Peter as well as Mark (because Mark was known as the interpreter for
Peter) and James and Jude (because Peter was the apostle to the circumcision). <u>Paul</u>
covers Romans through Philemon as direct author as well as Hebrews either
because he was the author or because the book came from the Pauline circle as
from Apollos. Paul also covers Luke-Acts because the Luke also served in the
Pauline circle. So Mark, Luke-Acts, James, and Jude are apostolic because they came
from extensions of the apostolic office. They worked, so to speak, under the
supervision of apostles.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        Apostolicity does not mean
what comes from the apostolic age. There were Judaizing and Gnostic teachers infiltrating
the churches during the apostolic age itself.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  B.  Catholicity </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        What was accepted as canonical
by everyone everywhere in the apostolic age is received as canonical by the
church later. Catholicity is a practical aid that helps determine what books came
from apostolic hands. It offsets special interest groups and localized viewpoints
in the early church. (Note separate document “Orthodox Christianity.”)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'> IV. Canonicity and Tradition</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  A.  Tradition as an authoritative norm</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        The <u>Roman Catholic</u> view
of the church gives the church logical priority over both scripture and
tradition. Tradition has authority over the consciences of Christ’s people because
it supposedly represents the Spirit-led understanding the church has had throughout
the ages since the apostolic period. Within tradition stands the role of the
ancient councils in formulating creeds and orthodox stances on profound
questions like Christology, trinity, incarnation, and canonicity itself. The
view includes belief in apostolic succession and the concept that grace flows
through the church by authority rather than directly from God to his people
interpersonally.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  B.  Tradition as an interpretative tool</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        <u>Protestantism/reformation</u>
has regarded early tradition as an interpretative tool. The assumption has been
that those who knew the apostles or were close to the apostolic age could have
had access to unrecorded information that would aid in understanding the
Christian message represented in the written word. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        <u>Restoration/primitivism</u>
has put an even more reduced emphasis on the value of tradition. The presence
of so much heretical teaching even during the time of the apostles themselves
suggests a high degree of caution about the doctrinal views of those after the
time when apostles were no longer present to correct false teaching. Under the
last approach, tradition may have a confirmatory role but no more so than other
non-canonical thought.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>   V. Canonicity and Guidance</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  A.  Revelational guidance</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        Overt manifestation of God’s
will for specific situations comes in a way that the receivers can distinguish their
own thoughts and feelings from the revelational experience; it is <i>“face to
face”</i> as with Moses, audible as in <i>“Samuel, Samuel”</i> in the night, or
<i>“Saul, Saul”</i> on the Damascus road—audible and visible to some extent to
his traveling companions.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  B.  Special guidance</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        Special guidance lies beyond
natural law and the outworking of generic Christian values in (1) biblical
teaching and (2) the Christian community together with (3) the sheer sense of
divine presence. It includes supernatural intervention that is invisible to the
human senses. (See elsewhere materials under “Guidance” associated with “Gifts
of the Spirit” as well as materials in “Basic Christianity.”)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'> VI. Views of Canonicity</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  A.  Closed canon</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        1.   Not adding to or taking
away: Revelation <b>22</b>:18-19; compare Deuteronomy </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              <b>4</b>:2;<b> 12</b>:32;
Proverbs <b>30</b>:6</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              We must take care in
using the Revelation passage to argue for a closed canon, because (a) the words
deal with the Book of Revelation itself. Besides, (b) the Old Testament
passages say the same thing; yet they do not eliminate the continued revelation
from God through the succession of Israelite prophets, to say nothing of the
New Covenant as a whole. Carried to the extreme, this position could become a
kind of Christian deism.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              According to Eusebius, in
the ancient church there were five New Testament books that some people
questioned as to their place in the canon: Hebrews, 2 Peter, James, Jude, and
Revelation. In all of church history, there have been four books that even the
most liberal scholars have never questioned as to genuineness and placement in
the canon: Romans, 1 Corinthians, 2 Corinthians, and Galatians.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        2.   Faith once and for all
time delivered to the saints: Hebrews<b> 7</b>:27; <b>9</b>:12, 28; </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              <b>10</b>:10; Jude 3</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        3.   No apostolic succession</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              a.   Where there are no
prophets/apostles/special messengers, there is no expansion of the canon. Regarding
the period after Malachi, Josephus said there was no exact succession of
prophets; so no scripture comes from that era in Israel; hence, the Old
Testament Apocrypha does not count as scripture.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              b.   Judas was replaced
when he left the apostolic office; but James, John’s brother, was not replaced
when Herod had him killed. Scripture always speaks of twelve apostles, not an
expanding number as pictured in apostolic succession.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        4.   When what is perfect has
come, the imperfect is removed (1 Corinthians<b> 13</b>:10; Hebrews<b> 8</b>:4-13;
<b>9</b>:23-28; <b>10</b>:8-14); when the perfect has come, it is not
superseded (Hebrews <b>7</b>:18-24). The principle in these texts does not deal
specifically with canonicity, but the idea relates to whether there will be
ongoing normative revelation that supersedes what has already come.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  B.  Open canon</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        1.   Having an open canon was
the situation of the Old Testament system in which Moses as normative prophet was
followed by others with additional communication from God (Deuteronomy <b>18</b>:15-19:
Jeremiah<b> 31</b>:31- 34).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        2.   This position describes
quasi-Christian groups that attempt to add further revelation to the New
Covenant. They claim their new revelation is to the New Testament what the New
Testament was to the Old.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  C.  Closed canon with continued
revelation</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:0in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify;text-indent:.75in'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>1.<span style='font:7.0pt "Times New Roman"'>&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;
</span></span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>Roman Catholicism belongs here or
under “open canon” since the <u>pope speaking <i>ex cathedra</i></u> adds to
the fundamental New Covenant corpus. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        2.   Tradition mediated
through the councils—and perhaps otherwise—expands the normative literature for
the church. The New Testament plus tradition is comparable to the Pentateuch
plus the prophets and the tradition of the elders. The verdicts of the councils
together with the proclamations of popes are functional equivalents of the New
Testament for what the tradition of the elders was for the Old Testament.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        3.   Pentecostalism also takes
this position regarding continued revelation at least on matters of guidance
and Christian practice.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>VIII. Canonicity Distinguished from Related Matters in
Bibliology</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  A.  Inerrancy </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        Inerrancy deals with the
character of scripture; determining what books belong in scripture represents a
prior task. Just because someone denies that, say, 2 Peter belongs in the
canon, he would not necessarily be denying inerrancy.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  B.  Literary genre </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        All literary genres have
potential for canonical status: history, poetry, devotional writing, wisdom
literature, apocalyptic, didactic materials.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  C.  Hermeneutics </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        There are no peculiar
principles for interpreting canonical literature beyond what operate in human
language generally.</span></p>
