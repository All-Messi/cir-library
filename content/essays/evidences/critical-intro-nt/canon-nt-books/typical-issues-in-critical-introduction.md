<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
.5in'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'> Critical introduction addresses several
issues related to the study of documents. In this setting, “introduction” does
not mean getting acquainted with a book’s contents for the first time. “Critical”
introduction does not “criticize” the book as if to degrade it. Critical
introduction evaluates the <u>nature</u> of a book and matters surrounding its <u>origin</u>.
</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
.5in'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'> Two classifications of information critics
draw on in this endeavor are <u>internal evidence</u> and <u>external evidence</u>.
Internal evidence refers to data in the writing; external evidence refers to
information from elsewhere. In critical introduction to the New Testament, <b>(a)</b>
extant early Christian literature provides most of the external evidence. <b>(b)
</b>Other resources related to the origins of Christianity help evaluate the
records of early Christianity. They have been assembled in writings like F. F.
Bruce’s <i>Jesus and Christian Origins Outside the New Testament </i>(Grand
Rapids, MI: Wm. B. Eerdmans Publishing Co., 1974) or Gary R. Habermas’ <i>Ancient
Evidence for the Life of Jesus: Historical Records of His Death and
Resurrection</i> (Nashville, TN: Thomas Nelson Publishers, 1984). A final, more
distant source of introductory information comes from extra-biblical records
that preserve <b>(c)</b> the historical background in the contemporary biblical
world.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                     The items below are major
subjects in critical studies. They tend to have a bearing on one another.</span></p>
