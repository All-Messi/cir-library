1.	Along with Luke, Acts has short paragraphs that summarize periods of time the author

does not handle in detail: <strong><sup>1</sup></strong>:12-14; <strong><sup>2</sup></strong>:43-47; <strong><sup>4</sup></strong>:32-35; <strong><sup>6</sup></strong>:7; <strong><sup>8</sup></strong>:1-3, 25, 40; <strong><sup>12</sup></strong>:24-25; <strong><sup>15</sup></strong>:33-35, 41;<strong> 19</strong>:21-22; <strong><sup>28</sup></strong>:30-31. They serve as links between the high points the author has highlighted.

2.	On some occasions the author introduces a brief note about someone that will play a major role later in the account: Saul at the stoning of Stephen <strong>(7</strong>:58;<strong> 8</strong>:1), Barnabas as among those who sold land to support for the needy (<strong><sup>4</sup></strong>:36-37), John Mark as the one whose mother owned the upper room where the disciples awaited Peter’s fate (<strong><sup>12</sup></strong>:12).

3.	In the mouth of Jesus, Luke states what could become the geographical outline of Acts (<strong><sup>1</sup></strong>:8): Jerusalem, Judaea, Samaria, the ends of the earth.

4.	The only New Testament document to refer to Christianity as “The Way”: <strong><sup>9</sup></strong>:2; <strong><sup>19</sup></strong>:9, 23; <strong><sup>22</sup></strong>:4; <strong><sup>24</sup></strong>:14, 22; cp. <strong><sup>18</sup></strong>:25, 26 (note John <strong><sup>14</sup></strong>:6; also Romans <strong><sup>5</sup></strong>:2; Ephesians <strong><sup>2</sup></strong>:18; Hebrews <strong><sup>10</sup></strong>:20)*

5.	The only writing besides 1 Peter <strong><sup>4</sup></strong>:16 to refer to followers of Jesus as “Christians” (<strong><sup>11</sup></strong>:26; <strong><sup>26</sup></strong>:28)*. Note also the <em>“of Christ”</em> expression in 1 Corinthians<strong> 1</strong>:12; <strong><sup>3</sup></strong>:23.

6.	Contains the only dominical saying outside the gospels (<strong><sup>20</sup></strong>:35; note also 1 Corinthians <strong><sup>11</sup></strong>:24, 25-26)

7.  	Has all eleven usages of the expression “together” (ὁμοθύμαδον) except one (Romans <strong><sup>15</sup></strong>:6): <strong><sup>1</sup></strong>:14; <strong><sup>2</sup></strong>:1, 46; <strong><sup>4</sup></strong>:24; <strong><sup>5</sup></strong>:12; <strong><sup>7</sup></strong>:57; <strong><sup>8</sup></strong>:6; <strong><sup>12</sup></strong>:20; <strong><sup>15</sup></strong>:25; <strong><sup>18</sup></strong>:12; <strong><sup>19</sup></strong>:29.*

8.	Except for Matthew <strong><sup>12</sup></strong>:41, Luke-Acts has the double-noun <em>“men _____ ”</em>: Luke <strong><sup>7</sup></strong>:37 (sinner woman?); <strong><sup>11</sup></strong>:32; <strong><sup>17</sup></strong>:12; <strong><sup>24</sup></strong>:19; Acts <strong><sup>1</sup></strong>:16;<strong> 2</strong>:22, 29, 37;<strong> 3</strong>:12, 14; <strong><sup>5</sup></strong>:35;<strong> 7</strong>:2; <strong><sup>8</sup></strong>:27; <strong><sup>11</sup></strong>:20;<strong> 13</strong>:6, 15, 16, 26, 38; <strong><sup>15</sup></strong>:7, 13; <strong><sup>16</sup></strong>:9; <strong><sup>17</sup></strong>:22; <strong><sup>21</sup></strong>:28; <strong><sup>22</sup></strong>:1; <strong><sup>23</sup></strong>:1, 6; <strong><sup>28</sup></strong>:17*

9.	Luke-Acts is the only two-volume work in the New Testament.

10.	Provides the only historical framework for tracing the activities in the first thirty-five years of the church. It supplies the historical framework for at least a majority of Paul’s epistles.

11. 	The only New Testament writing perhaps addressed to a government official (Theophilus; <strong><sup>1</sup></strong>:1).

12.	Except for Ephesians <strong><sup>5</sup></strong>:18, Luke-Acts alone uses the filling imagery for the Spirit.

13.	Frequently (79 times) uses <em>“Jews”</em> especially in chapters <strong>9-28</strong>, dedicated more to non-Judaean territories. Only 2 appear in chapters <strong>1-8</strong>: <strong><sup>2</sup></strong>:5, 10). John’s gospel has 68.
