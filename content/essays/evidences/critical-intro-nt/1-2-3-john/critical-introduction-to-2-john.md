<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:center'><b><span
style='font-size:14.0pt'>CRITICAL INTRODUCTION TO 2 JOHN</span></b></p>

<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:center'><b><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></b></p>

<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:center'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>Virgil Warren, PhD</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:center'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:center'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'> I. Authorship</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            A.  External</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        The external attestation to 2
John is not so strong as that for 1 John. Absence of citations from the book
may have been due to its subject matter. It is significant that the objections
to apostolic authorship come from the later Christian writers.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            B.  Internal</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        The term “the Elder” (<i>v</i>.
1) is appropriate for any older man of any office, especially since by the time
of its writing, the apostle John was probably the only living apostle. “Elder” among
the Jews was applied to leaders at (1) the synagogue level, but also at (2) the
city level and (3) the national level. By analogy it appears that “elder” could
have been applied to local units but also to the whole “nation” (1 Peter<b> 5</b>:1;
2 John 1; cp. Philemon 9?). So it could designate the ultimate leadership
within any level (John, Peter, Paul?). The expression “apostles and elders”
(Acts <b>15</b>:2) is significant in this respect. Could it mean elders in
general with the apostles as specially marked among them? A slightly different
arrangement terminologically would have the apostles as “apostles” of the
universal church and at the same time (some of) the “elders” of the Jerusalem
church. The passage in Eusebius that contains Papias’ statement (<i>Ecclesiastical
History</i><b> 3</b>:39:4) introduces all the apostles as “elders.” So a
distinction between the apostle John and an elder John is not necessary in view
of Papias’ use of the words. Note also 1 Peter<b> 5</b>:1.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>II. Destination: “the Elect Lady”</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            A.  Person (on the analogy of 3 John)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        1.   Hermeneutical principle
of preferring the literal interpretation (? <i>vv</i>. 1, 13)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        2.   Reference to children is
quite intelligible if they are grown.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            B.  Church (or community)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        1.   <i>“Lady loved by all who
know the truth” </i>implies a rather widespread knowledge of her; this is more
likely true of a church than an individual woman.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        2.   No name is given to her (<i>v</i>.
1) or her nephews (<i>v</i>. 13), detracting from the personal character of the
letter if a person is meant.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        3.   The subject matter is
more suitable for a church.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        4.   Second person plurals
predominate.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        5.   Personification of the
church as feminine is compatible with other instances in scripture: Ephesians <b>5</b>:29ff;
2 Corinthians <b>11</b>:2ff; 1 Peter<b> 5</b>:13. Compare also the “bride of
Christ” imagery in Revelation <b>18</b>:23; <b>21</b>:2, 9; <b>22</b>:17.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        6.   The greeting in verse 13
is more natural if sent from one church to another than from a group of people
to their aunt by means of a third party.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>III. Purpose</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>Same as 1 John. Warns against false teachers. They
were traveling among the churches.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>IV. Date</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            There is little to go on except the
lifetime of the apostle John and the general time of heretical docetic
teaching. The time of writing may compare with that of the gospel, first and
third epistles, and Revelation.</span></p>
