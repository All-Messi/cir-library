<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;

golden lamps, while Sardis has notes about </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify;text-indent:-31.5pt'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                       2.   Ephesus was the practical
center and leading city of Asia, and Sardis was the </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify;text-indent:-31.5pt'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>           ancient capital of Lydia and historical
center of the Asian churches.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>      J.   Parallels between Philadelphia and Smyrna</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  1.   </span><span style='font-size:
12.0pt;color:windowtext'>No</span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'> condemnation
is offered to either.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify;text-indent:-31.5pt'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                       2.   The writer invests himself
with the same character that he praises in the church addressed.  The
Philadelphia letter combines “he that is true” (Christ) with <i>“did not deny
my name”</i> (Christians).  The Smyrna letter refers to Christ as <i>“he who
was dead and lived”</i> and to the persecution and death of Christians in
Thyatira.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify;text-indent:-31.5pt'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                       3.   These two churches are
praised far more than the others.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify;text-indent:-31.5pt'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                       4.   They both contend with
especially serious difficulties.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify;text-indent:-31.5pt'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                       5.   Jews are mentioned only in
Smyrna and Philadelphia (<b>2</b>:9; <b>3</b>:9) probably because in wealth and
numbers they were strongest there.  In keeping with their wealth is the fact
that the Christians’ weakness and poverty are mentioned only in these two
churches.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:10.0pt'>      </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            These observations show that (1) the message
of Revelation was relevant. It was expressed in the everyday concerns of its
readers. (2) The historical and geographical allusions in these two chapters
were verisimilar to a book whose message is “sign-i-fied.” (3) The historical
and geographical emphases in these chapters should turn one away from
spiritualizing these short letters into something remote. (4) Revelation was a
practical book.  It was not written to the insular vacuum of the isle of
Patmos, but to the social fullness of seven bustling cities. It did not present
the subjective visions of an introverted mystic, but an “objective” message for
pragmatists influenced by what they saw and heard and felt.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>III. 
Special Points of Interpretation</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>      A.  Ephesus </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify;text-indent:-31.5pt'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                       The church is a “lampstand.” It
is not the light itself but that from which the light shines—a more apt figure
even than “candlestick.” These seven “lampstands” are not like the
seven-branched candlestick; they are seven individual stands.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify;text-indent:-31.5pt'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            B.  Smyrna  </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify;text-indent:-31.5pt'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                       Being cast into prison was not
the punishment itself, because the Romans put a person in prison only to await
trial or execution. The “true Jews” are those that accepted Christ, sent to be
received by God’s people.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify;text-indent:-31.5pt'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>      C.  Pergamum  </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            1.   <i>“The white stone”</i>  </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  a.   There was a relationship
between permanent validity of the message and the imperishable material used
for recording it. The validity was as permanent as the record was imperishable.
The name was written on imperishable material.  </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  b.   There was a suitable
relationship between the subject matter and the material it is written on. Lead
was used for curses, but white signified good fortune.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:10.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            2.   <i>“The new, unknown, name”</i></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  a.   The new name perhaps reflects
the custom of name-changing at baptism. In Acts Saul is increasingly called
Paul after his conversion (although there are probably other factors that
produced that phenomenon in Acts).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  b.   Eastern cultures give names
appropriate to the person and occasion. Names were often changed as an
indication of one’s entering a new capacity of new life, for example, after
severe sickness. Christians receive a new name in Christ because they become
new persons.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  c.   The true name of the god in
certain religions was kept secret except from the devotees lest an enemy could
gain influence over the god by knowing the god’s name. The name guaranteed and
even gave existence and life.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        <i>Augustus</i> was a name not
previously used for individuals, but only in the language of the priests. Receiving
it indicated the man’s elevation to a higher level, from which he transmitted
the divine power to his descendants. Christians are given their divine name to
indicate their association with the divine realm and to pass on that name to
their spiritual children.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        Ramsey recounts the case of
the poet Aelius Aristides, whose new name <i>Theodorus</i> was given in a
vision in which Aesculapius sent him to be conspicuously grand in the
oratorical delivery of philosophical and moral teaching. The name <i>Christian</i>
is a constant reminder to the wearer that he is supported and sent by the one
whose name he wears.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:10.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:10.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:10.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:10.0pt'>            </span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>3. 
Balaam</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  Being unable to satisfy Balak by
cursing Israel, Balaam in the Old Testament suggested that he corrupt them and
bring them under the curse of their God. The point here may be that some were
advocating a compromise between Christian allegiance and political religion.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;

only may he backslide; he may apostatize as well.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;

style='font-size:12.0pt'>            1.   The author tends to move forward

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  d.   Sardis relates to a pagan
atmosphere.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  e.   Philadelphia relates to a
Jewish atmosphere.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  f.    Laodicea (along with
Philadelphia) compares with the consummation </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        atmosphere.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  Does the writer draw on
chronological Old Testament history to bring the reader up to present-time
prophecies that begin in chapter <b>4</b>? If so, John may not be looking so
much at distant future events in Revelation as at present recurring processes
and imminent trials for his original audience—which may, of course, have
counterparts later.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;

style='font-size:12.0pt'>            2.   The letters seem to be directed
toward <i>“the things that are”</i> (<b>1</b>:19), while the rest of the book
concerns <i>“the things that will shortly be hereafter”</i> (<b>1</b>:19).</span></p>
