style='font-size:12.0pt'>   II. Critical Implications of the Use of Amanuenses</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            A.  May q<u>ualify the validity of <b>style</b>
studies as bases for denial of traditional authorship</u>. The scribe could
have been responsible for a rewrite after dictation so that his style affects
the final wording. He could have made an even greater contribution if the
author had simply given a line of thought, an outline, or a statement of the
gist of his concern; the scribe would then have been largely responsible for
generating the verbal expression of the letter. The author would presumably
read over and approve the piece before sending it. The effect of amanuensis on
style should not be overly pressed, however, since Paul’s various letters do
not show highly divergent styles even though some were written by his own hand
(Galatians and Philemon) while Tertius wrote Romans and perhaps still different
writers penned other epistles of Paul.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            B.  <u>Could explain <b>digressions</b> in
Paul’s letters</u> because digressions more naturally occur in oral
presentation (<b>structure</b>).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            C.  <u>Raises an option for the <b>collection
of the Pauline corpus</b></u> (<b>canon</b>). One of the scribe’s
responsibilities could have been duplicating the letter as a record of
correspondence or as a back-up copy in case the original was destroyed or lost
(cp. 2 Timothy <b>4</b>:13).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            D.  <u>May explain loosely attached
paragraphs at the end</u> of letters, where Paul picked up the pen to add his
customary postscript (<b>structure</b>; see 2 Thessalonians <b>3</b>:17-18).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            E.   <u>Affects the total range of
constructs possible within “God-breathedness</u>.” The Spirit serves a
protective and confirmatory role for what had significant human input in
generating ideas and ways of expressing them (<b>manner of inspiration</b>).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            F.   <u>Fosters a less rigid approach to <b>hermeneutics</b></u>.
Fine nuances in terminology and phraseology are probably not at the right level
to match the author’s intent and concern.</span></p>
