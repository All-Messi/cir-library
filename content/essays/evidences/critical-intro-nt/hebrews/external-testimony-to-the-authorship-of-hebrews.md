margin-bottom:0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:center'><b><span
style='font-size:14.0pt'>EXTERNAL TESTIMONY TO THE AUTHORSHIP OF HEBREWS</span></b></p>

<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;
margin-bottom:0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:center'><b><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></b></p>

<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;
margin-bottom:0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:center'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>Virgil Warren, PhD</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;
margin-bottom:0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:center'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>  </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;
margin-bottom:0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:center'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>    I.      Presentation of Evidence</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            A.  Early Christian writers</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  1.   Eastern tradition</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        a.   Direct statement
regarding authorship</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              (1)    Pantaenus</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                       In order of
time, Pantaenus is the earliest witness to the Eastern Tradition about the
authorship of Hebrews. He flourished about A.D. 180 as president of the catechetical
school in Alexandria, Egypt.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                       Photius says
that Pantaenus heard men who had seen the apostles.   Eusebius tells us that
Julian, bishop of Alexandria, sent Pantaenus eastward as far as India as a
missionary because of his piety and scholarship (<i>Ecclesiastical History</i> <b>5</b>:10).
Alexander, bishop of Alexandria about A.D. 212, highly commended him. (See
Eusebius’ <i>Ecclesiastical History</i> <b>6</b>:14.) Eusebius himself and
Jerome also speak of him as a learned, influential Christian leader.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                       Unfortunately,
the original works of Pantaenus are not extant, but Eusebius has preserved
thirdhand the Alexandrian tradition that Pantaenus perpetuated. In his <i>Ecclesiastical
History</i> <b>6</b>:14, Eusebius gives an extract from <i>Hypotyposis</i>, a
lost writing of Clement of Alexandria, in which Clement refers to the teaching
of the “blessed elder.”  “Blessed elder” is usually taken to mean Pantaenus,
Clement’s immediate predecessor as head of the Alexandrian school (cp. <i>Eccelesiastical
History </i><b>5</b>:11;<b> 6</b>:13): </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-left:.75in;text-align:justify'><i><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>“But now as the blessed Presbyter used to say, ‘Since
the Lord, who was the Apostle of the Almighty, was sent to the Hebrews, Paul,
by reason of his inferiority, as if sent to the Gentiles, did not subscribe
himself an apostle to the Hebrews, both out of reverence for the Lord, and
because <u>he wrote of his abundance to the Hebrews, as a herald and Apostle to
the Gentiles</u>.’”</span></i></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              (2)    Clement of
Alexandria</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                       Clement succeeded
Pantaenus as leader of the Alexandrian school after A.D. 187. In his <i>Stromateis</i>
Clement says he was educated in Italy, Greece, in the East, as well as in Egypt
(see also <i>Ecclesiastical History</i> <b>5</b>:11). Although he stands in an
Alexandrian current of tradition, he was aware of the tradition in other areas
where he studied. If he knew other traditions at his time, he makes no mention
of them. His agreement with Pantaenus was at least a view he took in preference
to the others he may have known, and so he gives comparatively greater credence
to the Alexandrian belief.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                       (a)     Eusebius’
<i>Ecclesiastical History</i> <b>6</b>:14 records Clement’s personal view of
Hebrews as expressed in <i>Hypotyposis</i>: </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-left:.75in;text-align:justify'><i><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>“In his work called <u>Hypotyposis</u>, he </span></i><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>[Clement]<i> affirms that Paul is the author of the
Epistle to the Hebrews; and that, as it was addressed to the Hebrews, it was
originally written in their language, and afterward  translated by Luke for the
Greeks—which is the reason why the coloring of the style is the same in this
Epistle and in the Acts of the Apostles. The reason why Paul did not affix his
name to the head of it probably is that the Hebrews had conceived a prejudice
against him, and were suspicious of him. Very prudently, therefore, did he not
place his name at the head of the Epistle, so as to divert them from the
perusal of it.”</i></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                       (b)   In his
other works Clement testifies several times to the same </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                                effect
about the authorship of this letter.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                       (c)     In <i>Stromateis</i><b>
2</b>:22; <b>6</b>:8 he cites Hebrews as Paul’s.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              (3)    Origen</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                       Origen, born in
Egypt about A.D. 185, became head of the Alexandrian school at the age of eighteen.
He visited Rome in A.D. 213 and later went to Greece, Arabia, and Asia Minor. In
A.D. 231 he moved to Caesarea.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                       (a)     The
broadest statement of Origen on the authorship of Hebrews is recorded by
Eusebius in <i>Ecclesiastical History</i><b> 6</b>:25 from Origen’s <i>Homilies
on Hebrews</i>, written after A.D. 245: </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-left:.75in;text-align:justify'><i><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>“Origen decides thus in his Homilies upon it: The
character of the style of the Epistle to the Hebrews has not the unpolished
cast of the Apostle’s language, who professed himself to be a man unlearned in
speech; that is, in phraseology. Besides, this Epistle, in the texture of its
style, is more conformed to the Greek idiom, as everyone must confess who is
able to distinguish differences in style. Moreover, the ideas in the Epistle
are admirable, and not inferior to those that are confessedly apostolic: and
that this is true, everyone must concede who has attentively read the writings
of the Apostles. A little further on he </span></i><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>[Origen]<i>
adds, ‘If I were to give my opinion, I would say the phraseology and the
texture belong to someone relating the Apostle’s sentiments, and, as it were,
commenting on the words of his master. If any church, therefore, holds this to
be an Epistle of Paul, let it receive commendation on account of this; for it
is not without reason that <u>the ancients have handed it down as Paul’s</u>. Who
wrote the Epistle, God only knows with certainty: but the report which has
reached us, is that some affirm it to be written by Clement, Bishop of Rome;
and some, by Luke, who wrote the Gospel and the Acts.’”</i></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                       (b)    In his <i>Homilies
on Joshua</i><b> 7</b>:100:1 he ascribes fourteen epistles </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                                to
Paul.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                       (c)     The <i>Letter
to Africanus</i> 9, written in A.D. 240, says, </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-left:.75in;text-align:justify'><i><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>“But possibly someone pressed with this argument will
have recourse to the opinion of those who reject this Epistle as not written by
Paul. In answer to such a one, we intend to write a separate discourse to show
that Epistle to be Paul’s.”</span></i></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                       (d)    He cites
it as Paul’s in <i>De Principiis</i> <b>3</b>:1:10; <b>4</b>:13; <i>De Oratione
</i><b>100</b>:27, 28; <i>Exhortatio </i></span><i><span style='font-size:12.0pt;
color:windowtext'>ad</span></i><i><span style='font-size:12.0pt'> Matyrium</span></i><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'> 44; <i>I Joann.</i> 6, 11 (three times);<i> Numer</i>.;
<i>Homiles </i><b>3</b>:3; <i>Epistle to the Romans </i>1, 36</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              (4)    Synodical letter
of the Council of Antioch</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                       The council
first met in A.D. 264, a gathering of some seventy-five delegates of the most
influential churches in Western Asia. In A.D. 269, it issued the letter at the
close of its second session, in connection with the trial and condemnation of
Paul of Samosata. It ascribes the Epistle of Hebrews to the same apostle that wrote
the Corinthian epistles: </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-left:.75in;text-align:justify'><i><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>“Now the Lord is that Spirit, according to the Apostle
</span></i><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>[2 Corinthians <b>3</b>:17]. <i>And
according to the same, ‘For they drank of the spiritual rock . . .’ </i>[1 Corinthians
<b>10</b>:4]<i>. And of Moses the Apostle writes, ‘Esteeming the reproach of
Christ greater riches . . .’</i> [Hebrews <b>11</b>:26].<i>”</i></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              (5)    Alexander of
Alexandria, A.D. 212, in writing to Arius (who probably agreed with Alexander)
uses the epistle as Paul’s.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              (6)    Dionysius of
Alexandria quotes the epistle as Paul’s (see Eusebius’ <i>Ecclesiastical
History</i><b> 6</b>:41).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              (7)    Theognotus, head
of the catechetical school in Alexandria, quotes </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                       Hebrews <b>6</b>:4
as Paul’s.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              (8)     Peter of
Alexandria quotes the epistle as Paul’s.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              (9)    Didymus of
Alexandria in <i>De Trin.</i> quotes Hebrews as by Paul. See </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                       also <i>Ecclesiastical
History</i> <b>6</b>:41.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                               (10)  Isidore of
Pelusium quotes Hebrews <b>4</b>:13 as Paul’s (<i>app. Lib.</i> </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                       <b>1</b>:7;
94).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              (11)  Jacob of Nisibis
gives fourteen letters to Paul.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              (12)  Eusebius, early
church historian and bishop of Caesarea from about A.D. 320, gives repeated
evidence that Hebrews is Paul’s.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                       (a)     <i>Ecclesiastical
History</i> <b>3</b>:3: </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-left:.75in;text-align:justify'><i><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>“Fourteen epistles are clearly and certainly Paul’s: although
it is proper to be known that some have rejected that written to the Hebrews,
alleging that it is spoken against as not belonging to Paul.”</span></i></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                       (b)    <i>Ecclesiastical
History</i> <b>3</b>:38: </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-left:.75in;text-align:justify'><i><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>“In which </span></i><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>[Epistle
of Clement of Rome]<i>, inserting many sentiments of the Epistle to the
Hebrews, and also using some of the very words of it, he </i>[Clement]<i>
plainly manifests that that Epistle is no modern writing, and hence it has not
without reason been reckoned among the other writings of the Apostle. For Paul
having written to the Hebrews in their own language, some think that the
evangelist Luke, and others that this very Clement translated it </i>[into
Greek]<i>; which last opinion is the more probable of the two, there being a
resemblance between the style of the epistle of Clement and of that to the
Hebrews, nor are the sentiments of these two writings very different.”</i></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              (13)  Cyril of Jerusalem,
bishop of Jerusalem A.D. 350-386, in <i>Cat.</i> <b>4</b>:36 </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                       gives fourteen
letters to Paul (</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>τὰς</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>Παῦλον</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>δεκατεσσάρας</span><span

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#0070C0'>                              (2)    “The
Epistle to the Hebrews” is the subscription.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            D.  Stichometry of Codex Claromontanus</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  The stichometry gives 850 for “The
Epistle of Barnabas.”</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  1.   There are only two possible
references: the Epistle to the Hebrews and <i>Epistle of Barnabas</i> (an apocryphal
work).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  2.   The apocryphal work is
one-third longer than Hebrews.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  3.   Hebrews is roughly equivalent
to Ephesians, Galatians, and Titus. Codex Sinaiticus has these three in 41
columns and Hebrews in 40½ columns.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  4.   Evidence</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        </span><span style='font-size:
12.0pt;color:#0070C0'>a.   Claromontanus gives 850 for “The Epistle of
Barnabas” and 865 (350 + 375 + 140) for Ephesians; hence, <i>Barnabas</i> is <i>Hebrews</i>.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#0070C0'>                        b.   Stichometry
of Nicephorus gives the apocryphal <i>Epistle of Barnabas</i> 1360 and <i>Revelation</i>
1400, a little less than <i>Revelation</i>. Codex Claromontanus gives <i>Revelation</i>
as 1200. <i>Barnabas</i> would then be 1150 since <i>Barnabas</i> is in
proportion to <i>Revelation</i> 1360 to 1400, but it gives 850. <i>Hebrews</i>
is proportioned to <i>Barnabas</i> 3 to 4, proportionally correct if <i>The
Epistle of Barnabas</i> is <i>Hebrews</i> (850 to 1150 is approximately 3 to
4). This identification was first made by Martianay.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>II. Observations on the Evidence</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            A.  The evidence on authorship is,
generally speaking, divided between the East and the West, with the East
maintaining Pauline authorship and the West not maintaining it. However,
Tertullian, a Montanist in North Africa, may have derived his beliefs on the
authorship of Hebrews (that it was by Barnabas) as a part of the Montanist
beliefs, and Montanism arose in Phrygia. Furthermore, Irenaeus, a bishop in
Gaul, came originally from Asia and had studied under Polycarp, and probably
did accept Pauline authorship.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            B.  Over a hundred years pass before any
information is preserved on the authorship in either the East or West,
Pantaenus being the earliest in the East (<i>cir.</i> 180) and Irenaeus in the
West (<i>cir</i>. 180), each taking opposite views.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            C.  The earliest citation of Hebrews in
the West, by Clement of Rome in A.D. 96, does not mention authorship, but it is
not fair to assume he did not know who the author was since Clement never
specifies authorship for his quotations except in quoting the Corinthian
epistles evidently because he was writing to Corinth. Similarly, the earliest
traces of Hebrews in the East do not mention it, that is, in Justin Martyr and
Polycarp.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            D.  The reasons Pantaenus gives for the
omission of Paul’s name and the tradition that Paul wrote the letter are not necessarily
of equal validity. The authorship of Hebrews is a historical matter that could
have been passed down faithfully from the original readers, but the writer’s
motives for omitting his name would be known only to the writer. So, to reject
Pantaenus’ explanation is not to reject the tradition of Pauline authorship,
which Pantaenus passed on.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            E.   In the above tradition of East or
West before Jerome and Augustine, the Pauline authorship was maintained
primarily from Alexandria; ten of the twenty-one witnesses for Paul are from that
city. But it is not legitimate to say, as does Zahn, that this was an Alexandrian
tradition, because the Council of Antioch in A.D. 269 were Christian leaders
from all the Eastern areas.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            F.   The Eastern writers maintained their
belief in Pauline authorship despite their knowledge of other beliefs, but
frequently in the West the writers seem ignorant of what was believed elsewhere
(for example, Tertullian), were unaware of <i>Hebrews’</i> canonicity at all,
or were changing when they knew of Pauline authorship (Jerome and Augustine). Irenaeus
and Hyppolytus may be exceptions.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            G.  The Eastern tradition is solid for
Pauline authorship all the way to the time when some of the Arians were denying
it for doctrinal reasons. In the West the tradition is diversified or less
sure.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            H.  The West eventually yielded to Eastern
tradition.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            I.    Augustine and Jerome quote Hebrews
as Paul’s, but in their direct statements they are not so decisively about the
fact.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            J.   The questioning of Hebrews in the
West may be due in part to the problem of second repentance, one of the issues
involved in the Montanist and Novatian heresies.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            K.  Irenaeus’ not using Hebrews in <i>Against
Heresies</i> may be because Marcion had rejected the book. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            L.   The Western expression that Paul
wrote to seven churches may have been perpetuated by a superstitious reverence
for that number. The first person who made that observation may not have meant
to exclude Hebrews, whose destination may not have been <u>a</u> church.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            M. The headings of the epistle that
include Paul’s name are not earlier than the ninth century, but the
classification of Hebrews among Paul’s letters is <u>from the first</u>. We may
say that in the manuscripts, if it was not included in connection with Paul’s
letters, it was not included at all. Most earlier manuscripts are of the
Alexandrian text type.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            N.  The testimony of Origen has frequently
been used to make it one-sided. Zahn, for example, repeats Origen’s expression
that <i>“God above knows” </i>who wrote the epistle, whereas Origen said, <i>“God
above knows certainly.”</i> It is not necessarily that Origen lacks confidence
in the Pauline tradition and says in effect that no one knows. Rather, he is
personally confident in the Alexandrian tradition, but his emphasis is on “certainly.”</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            O.  Problems come up in trying to harmonize
Origen’s testimony to Africanus and that recorded about him in Eusebius. How is
it that Origen is ready to contend that it is Paul’s and yet say that God alone
knows certainly?</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  1.   Perhaps he changed his mind
between A.D. 240 and the time of his <i>Homiles</i>, which Eusebius quotes
(after A.D. 245).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  2.   Perhaps he is saying God knows
certainly, but he believed Paul wrote it and was ready to give reasons.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  3.   Some have suggested that he
meant amanuensis when writing to Africanus, but this is not certain since he
used <i>writing</i> in the immediate context with respect to primary authorship
(Luke’s writing of his gospel). This objection cannot be pushed too far,
however, since there is no other word for amanuensis than </span><span lang=EL
style='font-size:12.0pt'>γράφω</span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'> in the
