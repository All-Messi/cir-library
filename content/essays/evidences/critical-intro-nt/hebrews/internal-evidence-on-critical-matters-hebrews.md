margin-bottom:0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:center'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>Virgil Warren, PhD</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>  </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>I. Authorship</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            Hebrews makes no claim to authorship. Certain
points about the book may have </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            relevance to the authorship question.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            </span><span style='font-size:12.0pt;
color:windowtext'>A.</span><span style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#0070C0'>  <b>2</b>:3-4:
<i>“How will we escape if we neglect such a great salvation, which having at
the first been spoken through the Lord, was confirmed to us by them that heard,
God also bearing witness with them both by signs and wonders and by many powers
and by gifts of the Holy Spirit according to his own will.”</i></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  Since the text says that the word of
the Lord was confirmed to “us” by those who heard the Lord himself,
commentators have inferred that t<u>he author was not an eyewitness</u> of the
Lord’s (cp. Ephesians<b> 2</b>:1-10; 2 Peter <b>3</b>:2). The inference would
be that the book was not written by anyone among the original apostles. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  The writer could be identifying
himself with his readers here and not necessarily be implying anything about
himself.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  Scholars often use this reference as
part of their denial of the Alexandrian tradition that Paul wrote Hebrews. But
Paul may not have been an eyewitness of the Lord’s ministry anyway, even though
he may have been among the “scouts” that Pharisees sent out to keep tabs on
Jesus’ activities. The text does not indicate that the <u>message</u> was
received second hand by the author but only that its confirmation was through
signs by eyewitnesses. In Galatians <b>1</b>:11-12, Paul is adamant that he did
not get his message from other people.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            B.  </span><b><span style='font-size:12.0pt;
color:#0070C0'>13</span></b><span style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#0070C0'>:23:  <i>“Know
that our brother Timothy has been set at liberty, with whom, if he come
shortly, I will see you.”</i></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  This verse could mean that the
writer was somewhat secondary to Timothy and that <u>his own movements would depend
on Timothy’s</u>. Such a reading is possible; if correct, it does not sound
like Paul, who would have considered Timothy a beloved helper that he directed
rather than one he followed around.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  A different reconstruction could
make it that the author intended to visit his readers anyway. If Timothy came
soon enough, they would be coming together.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            C.  </span><b><span style='font-size:12.0pt;
color:#0070C0'>13</span></b><span style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#0070C0'>:22: <i>“But
I encourage you, brothers, listen to the exhortation; I have written to you in
few words.”</i></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  The writer states that his purpose
is to “exhort” the readers. <i>Barnabas</i>, meaning “son of exhortation,” was
a nickname/surname given to Joseph (Acts <b>4</b>:36-37), who later became one
of Paul’s missionary companions. If the name was given to him because of his
tendency to encourage, then this book fits with Barnabas’ personality. But that
may have been appropriate to others among the apostles or sub-apostles.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            D.   </span><span style='font-size:12.0pt;
color:#0070C0'>A large number of <u>verbal allusions and conceptual parallels</u>
exist between Hebrews and Paul’s writings of Paul (<b>13</b>:19 and Philippians<b>
22</b>; <b>13</b>:22 and Galatians<b> 6</b>:11, <i>etc</i>.), but such
parallels exist with Peter (<b>13</b>:22 and 1 Peter <b>5</b>:12), Luke, and
Barnabas as well. Style studies abound that compare the rhetoric in Hebrews to that
of other New Testament writers.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            E.   Since <u>the rhetorical style of
Hebrews is more polished</u> than in any other New Testament writing, many scholars
have been enamored with Luther’s suggestion that Apollos wrote this epistle. </span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#0070C0'>Luke calls him (1) an eloquent man (Acts
<b>18</b>:24-28). In that same paragraph Luke says he was (2) mighty in the
scriptures, which fits nicely the observation that Hebrews makes more citations
of the Old Testament than does any other New Testament book.</span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'> In fact, every major point in Hebrews come from an
Old Testament text. Finally, Apollos was (3) fervent in spirit, which fits with
Hebrews as <i>“a word of exhortation”</i> (<b>13</b>:22). Modern scholarship
has also sought to identify the book with an Alexandrian origin, because it
supposedly uses the allegorical method of interpretation as did Philo, who came
from that city. According to the Acts statement, Apollos was an Alexandrian
Jew.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            F.   </span><b><span style='font-size:
12.0pt;color:#0070C0'>11</span></b><span style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#0070C0'>:32
shows at least that Hebrews was written by a man (contra Harnack):  </span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#0070C0'>ἐπιλείψει</span><span lang=EL
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#0070C0'> </span><u><span lang=EL
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#0070C0'>με</span></u><span lang=EL
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#0070C0'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:
12.0pt;color:#0070C0'>γὰρ</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt;
color:#0070C0'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#0070C0'>διηγούμεν<u>ον</u></span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#0070C0'> </span><span style='font-size:
12.0pt;color:#0070C0'>[mas. ptc.] </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt;
color:#0070C0'>ὁ</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#0070C0'> </span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#0070C0'>χρόνος</span><span lang=EL
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#0070C0'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:
12.0pt;color:#0070C0'>περὶ</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt;
color:#0070C0'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#0070C0'>Γεδεών</span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#0070C0'>. . . (<i>“For time will fail me <u>telling</u>
about Gideon,”</i> <i>etc</i>.)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            G. </span><span style='font-size:12.0pt;
color:#0070C0'> In <b>1</b>:1 </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt;
color:#0070C0'>τοῖς</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#0070C0'>
</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#0070C0'>πατράσιν</span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#0070C0'> is translated “our fathers” in some
versions, thus implying that the author is a Jew.</span><span style='font-size:
12.0pt'> But, while a possible translation, it is not a required rendering.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify;text-indent:-1.0in'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                    H.  Reasons given
for Paul’s omitting his name from the beginning of the letter if he </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify;text-indent:-1.0in'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                          wrote it:</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  1.  He omitted it so the surrounding
<u>unbelieving Jews</u> would not know that Paul was the author; he was not
concerned that the readers knew, but that the letter might be destroyed if it
fell into the hands of unbelieving Jews.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  2.  The salutation and statement of
authorship were contained in a cover letter rather than in the text itself.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  3.  He did not want to appear
presumptuous to speak to the Jewish Christians because that was Peter’s arena
of endeavor.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  4. If the letter was designed for
Greek-speaking Jewish Christians in Palestine/Jerusalem, he may not have wanted
it to fall into the hands of Hebrew-speaking Christians in Palestine lest they
disregard it or destroy it.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>II. Destination</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            A.  </span><span style='font-size:12.0pt;
color:#0070C0'>The subjects that  Hebrews treats are most appropriate to Jewish
believers</span><span style='font-size:12.0pt;color:windowtext'>,</span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'> who are under pressure to lapse back into Judaism. <i>“The
heavenly Jerusalem”</i> and <i>“the assembly of the firstborn who are enrolled
in heaven”</i> (<b>12</b>:22-23) combine with exhortation to stay in <i>“the
kingdom that cannot be shaken”</i> (<b>12</b>:27-28) and to look beyond their
familiar homeland and Holy City to something everlasting. They were to remain
faithful to the end that they might enter the <i>“rest of God”</i> that lay
beyond Canaan (<b>4</b>:6-9). The writer argues that the Mosaic Law, the
Levitical priesthood, the tabernacle, and the sacrificial system were shadows
of a later reality found in the Messiah (<b>10</b>:1-4, <i>etc</i>.). The
readers were to go outside the gate, where he had suffered, and meet him there,
bearing his reproach (<b>13</b>:12).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            B.  </span><span style='font-size:12.0pt;
color:#0070C0'>The extensive use of Old Testament scriptures</span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'> makes a Jewish audience natural.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            C.  Hebrews was originally written in
Greek since it bears no marks of being translated from a Semitic original. Palestine
used Aramaic as its main language although Greek as the <i>lingua franca</i> of
the Mediterranean world was used as a second language by a much of the
populace. The trilingual superscription above the cross indicates the language
situation in the land (John <b>19</b>:20). Likewise does the Greek-language
encyclical sent to Antioch by the apostles and elders at the Jerusalem
conference (Acts <b>15</b>:23-29; <b>16</b>:4). A Greek letter written to
Jerusalem would be especially appropriate for Hellenistic Christians residing
there. It is obvious from Hebrews <b>10</b>:32-36 and elsewhere that the
readers were a socially ostracized, persecuted minority in a hostile
environment. If they were Hellenistic Jews in Palestine, their plight may have
been doubly severe because of the language and cultural difference that tended
to separate them even from the larger Christian community there (Acts <b>6</b>:1-6).
If by chance Paul did write the letter, the great apostle to the Gentiles may
have had a special regard for these Greek-speaking brothers since he himself,
as a Jew from Cilicia, was likely a member of the Hellenistic synagogue in
Jerusalem from which many of these believers hailed (Acts <b>6</b>:9). More
than that, as perhaps an elder in that synagogue (if not a member of the
Sanhedrin), he had borne some special responsibility for the death of the first
martyr of the church, a member of that synagogue—Stephen, a saint whose wisdom
even Saul of Tarsus could not withstand (Acts <b>6</b>:8-8:1ff).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            D.  </span><b><span style='font-size:12.0pt;
color:#0070C0'>13</span></b><span style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#0070C0'>:24: <i>“the
(brothers) of/from Italy greet you.”</i></span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'> (See
under “Place of Writing.”)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            E.   </span><b><span style='font-size:
12.0pt;color:#0070C0'>6</span></b><span style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#0070C0'>:18:
<i>“Those who have fled,”</i></span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'> if
identified with Roman Jews, might indicate those who had to flee because of the
edict of Claudius in A.D. 49. The difficulty with this might be that the author
identifies himself with the readers in this situation: “we.”</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            F.   </span><span style='font-size:12.0pt;
color:#0070C0'>(<b>13</b>:12):<i> “Suffered outside the gate”</i></span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'> would have added punch for people living in Jerusalem
itself. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                 </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            G.  The writer mentions </span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#0070C0'>confiscation of property</span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'> at an earlier time in their faith (<b>10</b>:32-34). Such
a thing would have accompanied Claudius’ edict in A.D. 49, which banned Jews
from Rome. But that ban was against Jews in general, not just Christian Jews. Furthermore,
the persecution apparently had come from Jews or there would have been no
temptation to revert to Judaism to get relief.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            H.  Since </span><span style='font-size:
12.0pt;color:#0070C0'>the readers had not yet suffered unto blood (<b>12</b>:14),</span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'> were they other than Palestinian Christians, who <u>did</u>
suffer death because of the faith?</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>III. Date</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            A. </span><b><span style='font-size:12.0pt;
color:#0070C0'>10</span></b><span style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#0070C0'>:33ff.:
<i>“Call to remembrance the former days in which, after you were </i></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><i><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#0070C0'>                  enlightened, you
endured a great conflict of sufferings . . .”</span></i><span style='font-size:
12.0pt;color:#0070C0'> (cp.<b> 5</b>:12).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  <u>The readers had been Christians
for some time</u>, as “the former days” indicates. Significant time had passed
since their conversion. Considering Palestine as the destination moves that
beginning back to the beginning and moves with it the subsequent time Hebrews
would be talking about. If the letter was sent into the Gentile territories,
the date of the letter moves later, toward the time indicated by the
considerations below. That factor shortens the range of time within which the
letter would have originated.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            B.  </span><b><span style='font-size:12.0pt;
color:#0070C0'>10</span></b><span style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#0070C0'>:11: <i>“Indeed,
every priest stands day in and day out ministering and offering time and time
again the same sacrifices, which can never take away sins”</i> (cp. <b>8</b>:4-5;
<b>9</b>:11-9; <b>13</b>:10).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  <u>The Levitical priesthood is still
operating, fulfilling its responsibilities in the temple, which is still
standing</u>. All the texts in chapters <b>7-13 </b>picture the priesthood
system, the temple, the sacrificial system, and the Law still active.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            C.  </span><b><span style='font-size:12.0pt;
color:#0070C0'>8</span></b><span style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#0070C0'>:13: <i>“In
that he says, ‘A <u>new</u> covenant,’ he has made the first old. But what is
becoming old and growing aged is near to vanishing away”</i> (cp. <b>4</b>:6-11;
<b>10</b>:25; <b>12</b>:18-29; <b>13</b>:14).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  <u>The destruction of the land, the
city, and the temple lies not too far in the future</u>. The Hebrew writer has
just appealed to a lengthy citation from Jeremiah <b>31</b>:31-34 in which the
prophet predicts the coming of a “new covenant.” Hebrews explains that remark
as implying that the Mosaic covenant—made with the fathers when Yahveh brought
them out of Egypt—was going to be replaced rather than amended. The author
follows with the comment that this “old covenant” was near to vanishing away. There
are three times he could refer to in saying that the Mosaic Law was ready to
end. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        The first time the Law could
be considered taken away is the (a) “theoretical time”—as regards salvation
particularly—it was taken away in <u>the crucifixion-resurrection</u>: <i>“. .
. he has taken [the bond written in ordinances] out of the way, nailing it to
the cross . . .”</i> (Colossians<i> </i><b>2</b>:14-15). </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        The next time, soon afterward,
we could call the (b) “practical time” when the Law was taken away—on <u>Pentecost</u>,
when the Spirit came and the gospel was preached for the first time as a
historical reality. Neither of these past times qualifies for the future
reference in Hebrews. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        That leaves the (c) “historical
time” when the Law was taken away—at <u>the fall of Jerusalem</u>. Historically
speaking, the fall of the Jewish state and the accompanying destruction of the
Jerusalem temple meant that Mosaism could no longer operate as the Torah laid
out. Even with the partial restoration of Israel in 1948, Mosaism was not
restored because temple rituals and the priesthood system that officiated in
them were not restored, even though <i>“the times of the Gentiles”</i> (Luke <b>21</b>:24)—when
Jerusalem was trodden underfoot by the Gentiles—in a sense came to an end then.
God allowed the Jewish people about one generation to see that Jesus of
Nazareth was not another fly-by-night Messianic claimant, and then he removed
his protecting hand and let the “old” vanish away (cp. Gamaliel’s speech in
Acts <b>5</b>:33-40). Historically, there was about a generation’s overlap
between the beginning of Christianity and the end of Judaism. Jerusalem fell to
the Romans in A.D. 70 after a four-year war that began on the Mediterranean
coast in A.D. 66. Hebrews evidently precedes this date a little while, because
the recipients are still in the land.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>   D.     </span><b><span style='font-size:12.0pt;
color:#0070C0'>6</span></b><span style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#0070C0'>:18: <i>“We
. . . who have fled for refuge to lay hold of the hope set before us”</i></span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'> is sometimes taken as referring to Jews fleeing from
Rome at the edict of Claudius in A.D. 49. Maybe the imagery relates to fleeing
to the altar and laying hold of its horns as an analogy to reprieve from
punishment for sin. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            E.   </span><b><span style='font-size:
12.0pt;color:#0070C0'>13</span></b><span style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#0070C0'>:23:<i>
“Timothy has been set free” </i></span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>shows
that the letter is within Timothy’s lifetime.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>  IV. Place of writing: </span><i><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#0070C0'>“the (brothers) of/from Italy greet you”</span></i><i><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span></i><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>(<b>13</b>:24b)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            A.  Rome</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  The phrase </span><span lang=EL
style='font-size:12.0pt'>ἀσπάζονται</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>
</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ὑμᾶς</span><span lang=EL
style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>οἱ</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ἀπὸ</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>τῆς</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>Ἰταλίας</span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'> has traditionally been taken to mean </span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ἀπό</span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'> in
the weak sense “of,” implying that they are with the writer as he writes from
their city, Rome. Palestine becomes a natural destination, and Paul is the
author who writes from the Eternal City in anticipation of a visit to Palestine
with Timothy, who has been recently released from prison (too? Acts <b>28</b>:30-31?).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  The prepositional phrase </span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ἀπὸ</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>
</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>τῆς</span><span lang=EL
style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>Ἰταλίας</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>(“from
Italy”) is not adverbial—“salute from Italy.” “Of Italy” <i>versus</i> “away
from Italy” is broader (hence, minimum affirmation), which means it could be
either from Rome or from elsewhere back to Rome. The more restricted meaning “away
from” has to be the idea in </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ἀπό</span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'> if we claim that it means away from Rome.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            B.  Alexandria</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  The same phrase </span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ἀσπάζεται</span><span lang=EL
style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ὑμᾶς</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>οἱ</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ἀπὸ</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>τῆς</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>Ἰταλίας</span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'> has more recently been taken to mean </span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ἀπό</span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'> in
the more original sense “away from,” implying that they and the writer are
somewhere else, and they are sending greetings back to their home in Rome. Alexandria
is then picked as this other place because of the allegorical method of
interpretation supposedly used in Hebrews. Philo of Alexandria pioneered the
use of allegory among the Jews as a way of trying to show that the ideas of
Greek philosophy, many of which he had accepted, originated from the Jewish Old
Testament. Apollos, who came from Alexandria, becomes then a candidate for
authoring this book.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  A comparable example appears in Acts
<b>17</b>:13: </span><i><span style='font-size:12.0pt;color:#0070C0'>“When
those from Thessalonica knew that the word of God was preached by Paul in Berea
. . .”</span></i><span style='font-size:12.0pt'> (</span><span lang=EL
style='font-size:12.0pt'>ὡς</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>δὲ</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>
</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ἔγνωσαν</span><span lang=EL
style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>οἱ</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ἀπὸ</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>τῆς</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>Θεσσαλονίκτης</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>Ἰουδαῖοι</span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'> . . .). They were not so much “away from”
Thessalonica as “from/of” Thessalonica. A parallel exists also in the case of
Joseph of Arimathea in Matthew <b>27</b>:57 and John <b>19</b>:38 (</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ἀπὸ</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>
</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>Ἀριμαθαίας</span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>). The point is not that Joseph was away from
Arimathea at that time, but that he originated in that town. Likewise, John <b>1</b>:44
says that Philip was from (</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ἀπό</span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>) Bethsaida, the city of (</span><span lang=EL
style='font-size:12.0pt'>ἐκ</span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>) Andrew and
Peter; this case is like the one with Joseph of Arimathea. In these cases, the
person is away from his place of origin at the time of writing.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            C.  The author is not in prison and has no
immediate prospect of imprisonment because he plans to travel (<b>13</b>:23).</span></p>
