<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:center'><b><span
style='font-size:14.0pt'>INTERPRETATION IN HEBREWS CONTRASTED TO</span></b></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><b><span
style='font-size:4.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></b></p>

<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:center'><b><span
style='font-size:14.0pt'>THE ALLEGORICAL METHOD OF PHILO</span></b></p>

<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:center'><b><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></b></p>

<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:center'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>Virgil Warren, PhD</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>  </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            Historical backgrounds are important
because they <u>illuminate</u> scripture. They often <u>confirm</u> the
biblical record and may help <u>prove</u> its trustworthiness on things that
can be tested. So we view historical backgrounds as a valuable resource. </span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;color:windowtext'>This presentation,</span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'> however, stresses another observation. Like all
sources of information, historical backgrounds misused can harm rather than
help biblical studies. It can detract from hermeneutical and apologetic
concerns.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            Two related presuppositions often cause
professional theologians to misconnect or equivocate biblical and
extra-biblical phenomena: (a) their anti-supernatural bias prompts them to
develop (b) an evolutionary, naturalistic explanation for Christian origins. If
they can weave historical backgrounds into a developmental scheme, supernatural
explanation becomes gratuitous, the truthfulness of the faith is compromised,
and Christ’s claim on human allegiance is nullified.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            The concern </span><span style='font-size:
12.0pt;color:windowtext'>here</span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'> is to distinguish
the use of the Old Testament in the Book of Hebrews and that found in the
writings of Philo of Alexandria (30 B.C.-A.D. 45). Commentators try to parallel
the two. Drawing on the earlier work of an Alexandrian Jewish philosopher named
Aristobulus (fl. 160 B.C.), Philo used allegory to find in Holy Writ the
concepts of Greek thought, thereby uniting Greek philosophy and Jewish
revelation. Having claimed that the Hebrew writer employs Philo's allegorizing
practices, scholars proceed to integrate other items with this claim to make
the case more plausible. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  (1) They propose Apollos as the
author of Hebrews because he was an eloquent man from Alexandria (Acts <b>19</b>:1),
Philo’s native city. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  (2) The earliest, strongest
attestation to the apostolicity of Hebrews comes out of the catechetical school
in that same city during the second and third centuries. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  (3) <i>“Those of/from Italy”</i> who
send salutations (</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>οἱ</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ἀπὸ</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>τῆς</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>Ἰταλίας</span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>; <b>13</b>:24) were “away from” Italy—in Alexandria
perhaps. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  (4) The Septuagint was the Bible of
the Hebrew writer and presumably his audience as well. He consistently uses it
throughout his <i>“word of exhortation”</i> (<b>13</b>:22) and even bases
arguments on its distinctive wording (Psalm <b>40</b>:6-8 in Hebrews <b>10</b>:5-14)
and contents (Deuteronomy <b>32</b>:43 in Hebrews <b>1</b>:6). The Septuagint
significantly differs in these places from the standardized Hebrew text of the
Masoretes as well as other forms of the Old Testament text that have come down
to us. The Septuagint translation was made in Alexandria. Since the allegorical
method is artificial, New Testament writers’ use of it on Old Testament
revelation would be artificial, their inspiration would become suspect, and the
implied apologetic from prediction-fulfillment would be undercut. Trying to
equate the hermeneutics of Hebrews and Philo represents a significant misuse of
biblical backgrounds. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            Historically, the use of allegory became
important in the early church by working its way into early Christian writings
like <i>The Shepherd of Hermas</i> and <i>The Epistle of Barnabas</i> and into
the theologizing of Christian leaders like Clement of Rome, Polycarp, Irenaeus,
Hippolytus, Lactantius, Hilary, Origen, Jerome, Augustine, and a host of others
down into the Middle Ages. We wonders how Christianity fared as well as it did,
given the weakness of church leaders as judged from their lack of facility in
exegesis. Even as the <u>Stoics</u> had used allegory to harmonize Homer with
their own later views and <u>Philo</u> had used it to read Greek philosophy
into Old Testament revelation, so also <u>Christians</u> tried to use allegory
to solve the problem of covenant distinctions: they spiritualized the Old
Covenant in terms of the New. <u>Gnostic</u> heretics used it to bend biblical
teaching in the direction of their philosophical speculations. Allegorizing
plagued biblical interpretation down into the time of the Reformation. Today,
there appears to be a revival of allegory among those who use it to claim
special insight, further revelation, or predictive prophecy, all of which lie
beyond hermeneutical correction because they do not depend on hermeneutical
method to start with. A negative influence so long-lasting and all-pervasive as
allegorizing has been, must surely be distinguishable from the manner in which
scripture demonstrates how to rightly divide the word of revealed truth.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            (1) We illustrate the contrast between
Philo and Hebrews first by making some general observations and then by
offering a test case from the first chapter of the book. The Hebrew writer did
his work <u>from the vantage point of fulfilled prediction</u>. He was in a
position to make more precise correlations between the Messianic age and
prophecies about it than if he had not seen the fulfillment of the predictions
he adduces. Such circumstances differ from reading unfulfilled prophecies and
deriving such ideas from them or from taking historical narratives and
allegorically assigning cryptic spiritual messages to them. The accomplished
Messianic anticipations show that the ideas are true that Hebrews presents in
connection with Genesis, Psalms, 2 Samuel, Isaiah, Jeremiah, and Amos. The
later historical fulfillments had greater exactness than the earlier predictive
utterances had clarity, a pattern characteristic of fulfillment in comparison
to prediction. It only remains for us to work out the connection if it is not
immediately obvious.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            (2) The Hebrew writer makes his
observations <u>from the explicit contents of the Old Testament</u> Psalms and
prophets and from earlier historical events they allude to. He deals with the
text in a historico-grammatical sense rather than in an allegorical,
analogical, or spiritual manner that could not be obtained by means of the Old
Testament verbal communication itself.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            (3) The Hebrew writer sometimes makes his
observations <u>by necessary inference</u> from the express contents of Old
Testament scripture—mixed with explicit statements and experienced fulfillment.
Psalm <b>100</b>:4 plus Genesis <b>14</b>:18-20 is a prime example. Its
prediction about a <u>new</u> <b>priesthood</b> implies <u>change of priesthood</u>,
which implies a <u>change of the Law</u> at least about priesthood
qualifications, which further implies that the Levitical priesthood was <u>deficient</u>
and the new one <u>better</u>. He reasons similarly from statements in Psalm <b>40</b>:6-8
about dissatisfaction with <u>Mosaic</u> <b>sacrifices</b>, concluding that
they were <u>ineffective</u>, hence <u>temporary</u>, and in turn <u>replaced</u>
by what they only foreshadowed as the <u>permanent</u> and <u>final</u> answer
to the sin problem. By implication the <b>temple</b> in which temporary priests
offered ineffective sacrifices could itself be only a passing feature of the
divine economy. The same thing happens with the <b>covenant</b> itself on the
basis of Jeremiah <b>31</b>:31-34. A <u>new</u> covenant must mean a <u>different</u>
covenant as well as a different <u>kind</u> of covenant. The first one must be <u>annulled</u>
and <u>replaced</u> by the second because two covenants cannot govern the same
constituency simultaneously. The second covenant is different in kind, better
in quality, and effective for the forgiveness of sin (<b>31</b>:34); its
personal sacrifice is effective for removing a person’s self-consciousness
about his sinfulness (Psalm <b>40</b>:6-8), and the Melchizedekian priest is a
better mediator between sinful people and a holy God (Psalm <b>110</b>:4).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            (4) The Hebrew writer makes his
observations also <u>from cues within the texts themselves</u> that certain
comparisons are appropriate. The parallels are not made allegorically via the
resourcefulness of the Hebrew writer or even his inspiration, but because the
texts themselves <u>say</u> parallels exist. The wilderness wanderings correlate
with the Christian life and hope as per Psalm <b>95</b>. Melchizedek and
Messiah correlate according to Psalm <b>110</b>:4 + Genesis<b> 14</b>:18-20. Isaiah’s
children and the Messiah’s brothers are comparable by being humans <i>vs</i>.
angels because Isaiah’s children <i>“were for signs and wonders”</i> in the Emmanuel
chapters (Isaiah <b>8</b>:18). Coupled with express content, necessary
inference, and observed fulfillment, these cues within the texts yield
legitimate commentary in the hands of the Hebrew writer. In fact, every major
point he makes derives in this manner from an Old Testament text.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            Now to deal with one case for illustrative
purposes. The first instance where Hebrews does something surprising with the
Old Testament occurs almost immediately in chapter <b>1</b>. In verse 5b he
cites 2 Samuel <b>7</b>:14 as a reference to the Son of God despite the fact
that the original setting recounts God’s promise to David that his seed would
build the temple that he had wanted to build—an aspiration fulfilled by David’s
son Solomon. The first three chapters of Hebrews emphasize the superiority of
the “<u>Son</u> of God” (<b>1</b>:2, <u>4</u>, 5, 8; <b>3</b>:3-<u>6</u>; <b>4</b>:14)
over angels as <u>ministers</u> (<b>1-2</b>; <b>1</b>:7, 14) and over Moses as <u>servant</u>
(<b>3</b>; <b>3</b>:5). The introduction to Hebrews centers on the word <i>Son</i>
(<b>1</b>:1-4). Immediately after this opening sentence, the writer couples
Psalm <b>2</b>:7 (<i>“You are my Son”</i>) with 2 Samuel<b> 7</b>:14 (<i>“I
will be his Father and he will be my son”</i>) and other texts (Deuteronomy <b>32</b>:43
Septuagint [LXX]; Psalm<b> 95</b>:6-7; <b>102</b>:25-27; <b>110</b>:1) to show
that the Messianic Son is superior to angels (Deuteronomy <b>32</b>:43
Septuagint [LXX]; Psalm <b>104</b>:4). He does not explain how these references
relate to the Son or to angels, nor does he show how they contribute to his
argument that the Son is superior to the angels. He either leaves that for the
reader to figure out or he assumes it as common ground with his original
audience. It remains for us to work out the connections that verify the
relevance of his citations and the exegetical validity of his argument.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            Even though an initial reading of that
original context in 2 Samuel might lead us to suppose it was referring to
Solomon (cp. 1 Chronicles <b>17</b>:13; <b>22</b>:10), upon closer examination
anyone can see that there is more to it than that. <b>(a)</b> 2 Samuel<b> 7</b>:13-14a
must refer to Solomon; Solomon himself understood it that way (1 Kings<b> 6</b>:11-13;
<b>8</b>:17-21). </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  <b>(b)</b> The context cannot refer
just to Messiah, because God promises to discipline “him” if he sins (<b>7</b>:14b),
but it must refer to more than Solomon since it is an <u>eternal</u> seed that
contrasts with that of Saul (2 Samuel <b>7</b>:15-16). God will discipline “him”
rather than take away his loving kindness from “him” as he had done earlier
with Saul. So in 2 Samuel <b>7 </b>“seed” envisions the Davidic line, not just
Solomon—or Messiah, for that matter. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            One background observation is important: there
can be only one universal eternal kingdom. A universal eternal kingdom is
prophesied in Daniel <b>2</b>:35 (universal) + 44 (eternal) and in Psalm<b> 89</b>:27
+ 4, 36. So the <u>universal eternal</u> kingdom of Psalm <b>2</b> (the first
quotation in Hebrews 1) and the eternal kingdom of Psalm<b> 110</b> (the last
quotation in Hebrews <b>1</b>) must refer to the same thing as the <u>eternal</u>
kingdom of 2 Samuel <b>7</b>:16 (as well as Psalm <b>89</b>; Isaiah <b>9</b>:6-7,
<i>etc</i>.). Furthermore, the Daniel <b>2</b> prediction describes the
universal eternal kingdom as made “without hands” (Daniel <b>2</b>:34, 45),
meaning “from/by God” (cp.<b> 8</b>:25). This unique Old Testament expression
in Daniel together with its context is probably in the Hebrew writer’s mind,
because he uses this relatively rare terminology elsewhere in his own book (<b>9</b>:11,
24; cp. Mark <b>14</b>:58; Acts <b>7</b>:48; <b>17</b>:24; 2 Corinthians<b> 5</b>:1;
Ephesians<b> 2</b>:11; Colossians <b>2</b>:11). If Psalms and Daniel <b>2</b>
are Messianic, then 2 Samuel <b>7</b> must have Messianic connections as well.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  <b>(c)</b> <u>Seed</u> is a
collective term for the whole Davidic dynasty as a series, rather than an individual
reference to Solomon. All the third person singulars in 2 Samuel <b>7</b> are
grammatical consequents of the antecedent <u>seed</u> in <b>7</b>:12. <i>He</i>,
<i>his</i>, and <i>him</i> are grammatically third-person singulars referring
back to the collective noun <i>seed</i> (</span><span lang=HE dir=RTL
style='font-size:12.0pt'>זרע</span><span dir=LTR></span><span style='font-size:
12.0pt'><span dir=LTR></span>, <i>zeraʻ</i> m.), rather than to “he” as in
Solomon. Substituting the word <u>seed</u> for each third-person pronoun after<b>
7</b>:12 clarifies the broader force of the original Hebrew, a nuance lost in
translation by the requirements of English pronoun idiom. In confirmation of
these observations, Psalm <b>89</b>:29-30ff., which parallels 2 Samuel <b>7</b>,
interprets <u>seed</u> as descendants. The successive descendants of David in
turn fill the slot called “seed,” Messiah being the son <i>par excellence.</i> The
“seed,” in the person of Solomon, did build the first temple (<b>7</b>:13); the
Seed in the person of Messiah built the last temple “without hands” (John <b>2</b>:13-22;
cp. Matthew <b>26</b>:61 = Mark<b> 14</b>:58; Matthew<b> 27</b>:40 = Mark <b>15</b>:29;
Acts<b> 6</b>:13-14; note also<b> 24</b>:12; <b>25</b>:8?). The writer’s
approach so far is exegetical; the meaning derives from the Old Testament text
by normal principles of interpretation. It takes 2 Samuel <b>7</b>:14 as a
class prediction rather than as an allegory, or a double-reference prophecy, or
a type-antitype, or an accommodation of an individual person. 2 Samuel <b><i>7 </i></b>does
include Messiah in its purview.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            It remains to be shown how that text
relates to an argument for the superiority of the Son over angels. If “son” in
2 Samuel refers to the Davidic dynasty, which was comprised almost entirely of
mere mortals, the reader of Hebrews might still question the relevance of that
evidence to the claim that the title “son” correlates with Messiah’s
superiority over “angels.” <u>Solomon</u> would also be superior to angels in
that case. Some interpreters have in fact entertained the idea that eventually
human beings will rise to a status higher than that of angels: <i>“Don’t you
know that we will <u>judge</u> angels?”</i> Paul says (1 Corinthians <b>6</b>:3).
Angels are <i>“ministering spirits sent to <u>serve</u> the heirs of salvation”</i>
(Hebrews <b>1</b>:14). Humankind was <i>“made a little</i> [<i>-<u>while</u>?</i>
</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>βραχύ</span><span lang=EL
style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>τι</span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>] lower than the angels” (Psalm <b>8</b>:5 + Hebrews<b>
2</b>:7). Nevertheless, Peter comments that angels are more powerful than
humans (2 Peter<b> 2</b>:11); so the proposal about their future inferiority to
mankind is moot if not doubtful (unless “more powerful than humans” means
during this human life). </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            Another approach seems preferable even
though it is more complex and subtle. The primary basis for arguing the
superiority of the Son over angels really lies in Psalm <b>2</b>:7, the
immediately preceding citation in Hebrews <b>1</b>. 2 Samuel <b>7</b>:14, where
the theocratic king line is called son of God, is then added in confirmation,
which reduces the need for the second citation itself to serve as primary
evidence. The crux of the issue is that the theocratic king could be called “son
of God” most particularly by virtue of the descendant Son, not the other way
around. Jesus was not son of God because of a lineage that came down from
David, the son of God; rather, David could be called “son of God” because his
lineage led up to Messiah, the Son of God. David and his lineage got their
title metonymically from Messiah, not <i>vice versa</i>. According to the flesh,
Messiah did descend from David, but by natural rank he was Lord of David (Psalm
<b>110</b>:4 + Matthew <b>22</b>:41-45). </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            The <u>descriptive title</u> “son” lies
most basically in Messiah, whose designation is applied by extension to his physical
dynastic predecessors. For this reason, as well as others, Old Testament
scriptures could speak of the monarch of Israel as the “son of God” without
ascribing deity to him or in any way saying something inappropriate (note John <b>5</b>:18;
<b>10</b>:30-33; <b>19</b>:7). The <u>actual rank</u> of Messiah over angels is
indicated by (a) the actions of angels toward him: worship (Deuteronomy <b>32</b>:34/Hebrews
<b>1</b>:6); by (b) direct statements about his eternal rank above others
(Hebrews <b>1</b>:7-9 &lt; Psalm <b>95</b>:6-7; Hebrews <b>1</b>:13 &lt; Psalm <b>110</b>:1);
and by (c) the different activities assigned to each: the Son’s creating
(Hebrews <b>1</b>:10-12 &lt; Psalm <b>102</b>: 25-27) <i>vs</i>. the angels’ ministering
(Hebrews <b>1</b>:7 &lt; Psalm <b>94</b>:4). </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            Our conviction is that exegetical
legitimacy can be demonstrated for the several cases in Hebrews where the
validity of the writer’s interpretation may escape us at first, not because it
is artificial, but because it is profound.<span style='position:relative;
top:-3.0pt'>2 </span></span></p>
