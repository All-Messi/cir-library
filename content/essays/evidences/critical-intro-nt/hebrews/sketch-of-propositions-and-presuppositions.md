<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-left:.25in;text-align:center'><span

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-left:.25in;text-align:justify'><span

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-left:.25in;text-align:justify'><span

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-left:.75in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
-.5in'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>    I.      While 2 Samuel <b>7</b>:14
must refer to Solomon because Solomon so understood it (1 Kings<b> 6</b>:11-13;
<b>8</b>:17-21), it cannot refer exclusively to him since the context speaks of
an <u>eternal</u> seed (2 Samuel <b>7</b>:16).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-left:.75in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
-.5in'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>   II.     By the nature of the case, <u>there
can be only one universal eternal kingdom</u>.  So Daniel <b>2</b>:(35) 44;
Psalm <b>89</b>:27 + 4, 36; and therefore the universal kingdom of Psalm <b>2</b>
(first quotation) and the eternal kingdom of Psalm <b>110</b> (last quotation)
must all refer to the same thing as the eternal kingdom of 2 Samuel <b>7</b>
(as well as Psalm <b>89</b>; Isaiah<b> 9</b>; <i>etc</i>.).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-left:.25in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>      </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-left:.75in;text-align:justify'><u><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>Without hands</span></u><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>
(Daniel <b>2</b>:34-35) means &quot;from/by God&quot; (cp. <b>8</b>:25). This
expression and this context in Daniel is probably in the Hebrews writer's mind,
because he uses it elsewhere (<b>9</b>:11, 24; cp. Mark <b>14</b>:58; Acts <b>7</b>:48;
<b>17</b>:24; 2 Corinthians <b>5</b>:1; Ephesians <b>2</b>:11; Colossians <b>2</b>:11).
The phraseology occurs nowhere else in the Old Testament.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-left:.75in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
-.5in'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>III.       <u>Seed</u> is collective for
the whole Davidic dynasty as a series, rather than individual for Solomon
alone. Psalm <b>89</b>:29-30ff, a Psalm parallel to 2 Samuel <b>7</b>,
interprets <u>seed</u> as descendants. All the third personal singulars in 2
Samuel <b>7</b> are grammatical consequents of the word <u>seed</u>. <i>He</i>,
<i>his</i>, and <i>him</i> are grammatically third person singular references
to the collective noun <u>seed</u>, rather than <i>he</i> as in <u>Solomon</u>.
The successive collective descendants of David in turn fill the slot called
&quot;seed&quot; with the Messiah as son <u>par excellence</u>.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-left:.25in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>       Note: Biblical interpretation uses no principle
that is not found in human language generally (<i>contra</i>, <i>e.g</i>.,
Milligan<i>, New Testament Commentary on Hebrews</i>, p. 60). A person, for
example, may intend more in a statement than a hearer can derive from it (as in
type-antitype), but that is not the case in this use of 2 Samuel <b>7</b>:14. The
point made can be derived from the Old Testament through the principles of <u>hermeneutics;</u>
so <u>revelation</u> is not necessary to understand this Old Testament text in
the way the Hebrew writer takes it.</span></p>
