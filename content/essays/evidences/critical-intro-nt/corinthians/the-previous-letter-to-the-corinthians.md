style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  3.   Epistolary aorists were a
phenomenon in Greek, in New Testament Greek, in Paul, and in this letter (<b>9</b>:15).
They were probably more natural in the ancient world, where the postal system
was slow, or messages had to go by personal delivery.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  4.   Some usages of <i>write</i> in
letters do not employ an epistolary aorist in the original Greek. The authors
of the New Testament letters do use present tense <i>write</i> as in 1 Timothy <b>3</b>:14.
The usage of epistolary aorist rather than present tense might reflect
differences in the occasions of writing where a longer or shorter time period
intervenes between time of writing and time of reading. Use of epistolary
aorists may be a function of distance from the readers or inefficiency of the
delivery mechanism in given cases.</span></p>
