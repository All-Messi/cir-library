style='font-size:12.0pt'> I.   Destination Geographical </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            A.  The whole of Asia Minor except in
Cilicia</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  1.   1 Peter <b>1</b>:1 addresses
all the geographical names of Roman provinces in Asia </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        Minor.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  2.   Provincial Galatia included
Lycaonia and thoe parts of Phrygia and Pisidia </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        that did not belong to Asia.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            B.  Not addressed to Cilicia</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  1.   not because overlooked</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  2.   Cilicia was associated with
Syrian Antioch.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            C.  Lycia and Pamphylia are not mentioned.
They were two small districts whose early church history is vague (Acts <b>24</b>:25),
especially since they were not organized into an independent province until A.D.
74. Biggs says that they were still, in a sense, part of Galatia.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            D.  Route of delivery equals the order of
provinces named in the salutation. Pontus, Galatia, Cappadocia, Asia, Bithynia
equal a clockwise circuit, which does not mention the cut through (southern)
Galatia the second time, because Galatia was already mentioned. The route might
be from Caesarea, Cappadocia, along the great highroad leading westward through
Phrygia through Laodecia to Ephesus. Another great route would go past Smyrna,
Pergamos, Cyzicus in Mysia, thence to Pontus.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            E.   Perhaps the northern half of Asia
Minor is in view. Pontus and Bithynia, comprising one administrative province,
are mentioned first and last. Perhaps the general direction of the epistle is
in the parts where Paul had not evangelized (Acts <b>16</b>:6-7), but these
readers knew Paul and his letters. Beare says, </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-left:1.0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            <i>“In a general way, it may be said that
these provinces included the whole of the peninsula north of the great divide
of the Taurus mountains”</i> = </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>τὰ</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ἐντὸς</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>τοῦ</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ταύρου</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>(see
Strabo, <i>Geography</i> <b>2</b>:5:31).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            F.   Beare comments, </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-left:1.0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            <i>“The letter was addressed primarily to
the Christians of Pontus-Bithynia (since the province list begins and ends with
this area) and secondarily to the rest, because the persecution was most severe
in Pontus-Bithynia. The order of the other three provinces would not be
significant except that Cappadocia and Galatia are named jointly since they had
been under a single administrator for many years.”</i> </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-left:1.0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>(Beare is looking at this from the viewpoint of A.D.
110.)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  G.  Reason for circulatory letter</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  1.   General address</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  2.   Interpreter’s lack of direct
personal relations between the writer and the </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        readers</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  3.   Does not enter into questions
raised by a particular community</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>       II. Readers</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            A.  Churches not individuals</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  1.   <b>5</b>:14: <i>“<u>all</u>
that are in Christ”</i> (in places of <b>1</b>:1)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  2.   <b>5</b>:1-4: cared for by
their presbyters; they are the flocks of Christ, the Chief </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              Shepherd in Asia Minor.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  3.   Spoken of only in contrast to </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify;text-indent:-.25in'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        a.   their heathen neighbors:<b>
2</b>:12, 15; <b>3</b>:1, 13-17; <b>4</b>:3ff.; <b>4</b>:12ff. Christians are
dispersed among the non-Christians and Jews were dispersed among the Gentiles.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        b.   the whole church upon the
earth: <b>5</b>:8, 9</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        c.   a single local church
outside Asia Minor: <b>5</b>:13</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            B.  <u>Diaspora</u> signifies spiritual
dispersion, not <u>the</u> Jewish Dispersion.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  The letter was addressed to <u>churches</u>
not individuals; there were no purely or predominately Jewish churches in Asia
Minor. It was not sent to Ephesus:</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  1.   Acts <b>18</b>:19; <b>19</b>:8,
(1-7): it was <u>because</u> there was no church in Ephesus that Paul could
find individuals without baptism and apart from a congregation of a “developed
church,” yet these were “believers.”</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  2.   Paul began preaching in
synagogues wherever he went, including the whole of </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>Asia where he evangelized.    </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  3.   There were fewer Jews in
Northern Asia Minor than in those parts that Paul evangelized. Where would be
the purely or predominantly Jewish churches in this area?</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  4.   These supposed Jewish churches
did not come into existence because of the dispersion associated with Stephen’s
death;they only went as far as Antioch and Cyprus (Acts <b>11</b>:19).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  5.   People of this area from the
Jerusalem Pentecostal preaching at Pentecost did not establish these churches
upon returning, because they were Jews from abroad <u>dwelling</u> at Jerusalem
(Acts <b>2</b>:5; <b>6</b>:9). But note also <b>2</b>:8-9: dwellers and <u>sojourners
were</u> there who became members of the Jerusalem church.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  6.   Paul addresses churches of
Galatia (Galatians <b>1</b>:2; <b>1</b>:8; <b>4</b>:13; 1 Corinthians <b>16</b>:1),
not individual Christians in Galatia (note Corinth as well: Galatians <b>1</b>:9;
1 Corinthians <b>15</b>:1-3). </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  7.   Paul uses the figure of mother
(Galatians <b>4</b>:19) to describe his relationship to </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        the Galatian churches.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  8.   Paul refers to Colossae and
Laodicea as churches (Colossians <b>1</b>:1, 7; <b>4</b>:16, </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        17).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  9.   Paul speaks to Epaenetus
(Romans <b>16</b>:5) as the first convert of Asia, and the previous connection
with Prisca and Aquila in <b>16</b>:3-4 suggests that it was through their influence
that he was converted. Aquila first came to Ephesus with Paul, so he could not
have been converted previously.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>              10.     All the churches of Asia Minor
were established directly or ultimately through the labor and influence of Paul.
Paul’s own testimony (Romans <b>15</b>:20; 1 Corinthians <b>3</b>:10; 2
Corinthians<b> 10</b>:15; note also Romans <b>15</b>:16-23; <b>16</b>:4, 16; 2
Corinthians <b>11</b>:28; 2 Timothy <b>4</b>:17)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>              11.     The vices of <b>4</b>:3-5 are
typically Gentile:<b> 1</b>:18, 14;<b> </b>4; <b>2</b>:9, 10; <b>4</b>:3<i>.</i></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><i><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            </span></i><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>  12.     1
Peter uses Septuagint (LXX) quotations, not fresh translations of the </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        Hebrew.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>              13.      The areas addressed were
predominately Gentile.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>              14.     The readers form a special group
of persons, and the letter deals with a very </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        specific situation.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>              15.     The readers were not intended as
all the Christians of the world, but as all the </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        Christians in a definite part
of the world.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            C.  Salmon suggests that <u>Diaspora</u>
is literal—dispersed from the Neronian </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            D.  Reasons for the misunderstanding now
and from the time of Origen at least that </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  referred to the Jewish dispersion</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  1.   Because the terms </span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>παρεπίδημοι</span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>,
</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>πάροικοι</span><span lang=EL
style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>(<b>2</b>:1),
and </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>παροικία</span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'> (2<b>:11) </b>had been with reference to Israel and
Jewish conditions. The combination </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>παρεπίδημ<b>ο</b>ι</span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'> and </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>πάροικοι</span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'> (<b>2</b>:11) is only one time in the Septuagint—LXX—(Genesis
<b>23</b>:4) as a translati</span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>on of</span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=HE dir=RTL style='font-size:14.0pt'>ותושב</span><span
dir=LTR></span><span lang=HE style='font-size:12.0pt'><span dir=LTR></span> </span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>(<i>v<sup>e</sup>tōshāb</i>; note Psalm <b>39</b>:13 [LXX
= <b>38</b>:12]; Genesis <b>47</b>:9; 1 Chronicles <b>29</b>:15; Leviticus <b>25</b>:23
[where Hebrews is the same as Genesis <b>23</b>:4, but LXX has </span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>προσήλυποι</span><span lang=EL
style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>καὶ</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>πάροικοι</span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>—used to designate the Israelites in their own land in

lang=HE dir=RTL style='font-size:14.0pt'>גר</span><span dir=LTR></span><span

lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>πάροικος</span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>

40; as </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>προσήλυτος</span><span

 </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  2.   In themselves the words are
quite secular.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        a.    </span><span lang=EL
style='font-size:12.0pt'>Παρεπίδημοι</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>
</span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>refers to a stranger from a foreign land
temporarily resident in a place. It emphasizes the merely <u>temporary
character of the residence</u> (in distinction from </span><span lang=EL
style='font-size:12.0pt'>ἐπιδημεῖν</span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>: Acts <b>2</b>:10).
</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        b.   </span><span lang=EL
style='font-size:12.0pt'>Πάροικος</span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>
(practically synonymous with </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>μέτοικος</span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>, more commonly used by older writers) means resident
(stranger), not a citizen who lives by tolerance of the state under state
protection. It may also mean a tenant as opposed to property holder.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        c.   </span><span lang=EL
style='font-size:12.0pt'>Ξένος</span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'> is
practically synonymous with the previous words, that is, a general word that
can include all their meanings.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        d.   </span><span lang=EL
style='font-size:12.0pt'>Παρεπίδημος</span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'> and </span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>πάροικος</span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>
do not refer to persons who before their conversion were Jewish proselytes since
the passage is from the Septuagint—LXX (Genesis <b>23</b>:4 and Psalm <b>39</b>:13),
where </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>παρεπίδημος</span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'> and </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>πάροικος</span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'> are used does not refer to strangers in Israel
(proselytes), but to patriarchs in foreign land (<i>i.e</i>., the relation is
reversed from heathen in Israel to Israel among the heathen) without fixed
abode or permanent possessions and to pious Israelites who later emulated their
forefathers’ pattern of life.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        e.   From inscriptions and
literary sources, we know the existence of Jewish communities in these
districts.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        f.    The author makes extensive
use of the Old Testament in direct quotation and indirect allusions (<b>2</b>:9).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            E.   In conscious imitation of Old
Testament usage, the idea was used to speak of the Christian’s relationship to
this world.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  Note Hebrews <b>11</b>:13-16; <b>13</b>:14;
Philippians <b>3</b>:20; James <b>1</b>:1. Christians as members of a heavenly
commonwealth are temporary residents of this world without rights of
citizenship, remaining here only by the suffrage of the possessors and rulers
of this world. Even when they possessed land and citizenship, they still
regarded themselves as strangers who sought a fatherland not on this earth. These
attitudes fit well with 1 Peter <b>2</b>:11, where they supply motives for a
distinct manner of life in contrast to that led by the heathen. They have no
compulsion to conform to whatever does not accord with Christian morality.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            F.   Contrary to the Christian attitude,
the Jews, who were the literal Diaspora, made every effort either (1) to become
citizens with full rights in Gentile communities or (2) to secure legal recognition
as a separate community. (But do these practices deny their religious ancestry,
and were not the foreign Jews still called <u>the dispersion</u>? What is the point,
then, of saying that this differs from Christians who, though having no
homeland, also became citizens and held property?)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            G.  In <b>1</b>:17 Peter used </span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>παροικία</span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>
to describe their whole manner of life as a sojourning; so if they were Jewish
Christians dispersed from their Palestinian ancestral homeland, all they would
have had to do to get release from their moral obligation in <b>1</b>:17 and <b>2</b>:11
was to return to Palestine.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            H.  Language shows that Peter’s readers
are sojourners in the world because they were chosen by God, not because of
external circumstances.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  1.   The words of <b>1</b>:1
describe readers simply as Christians, and the names of the provinces that
follow in the genitive could have been added directly as in <i>“the churches of
Judaea”</i> (Galatians <b>1</b>:22) in the sense of <i>“the ones being in
Judaea”</i> (1 Thessalonians <b>2</b>:14).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  2.   The connection between
sojourners and the place names is not broken by the intervening </span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>διασπορά</span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>.
</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>Διασπορά</span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'> is generic without the article and cannot further
describe the readers as those elect strangers (Christians) who belong to the
Jewish dispersion, because then it would be a definite dispersion and/or
definite group of sojourners, so the modifying phrase would become </span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>παρ</span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>’ </span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ἐπιδήμοις</span><span lang=EL
style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>τοῖς</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>(</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ἐν</span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>) </span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ἐκ</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>
</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>τῇ</span><span lang=EL
style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>διασπορᾷ</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  3.   The connection between verses 1
and 2 is related to character.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  4.   The insertion of simple place
names does not preclude modification by clauses that follow (<i>e.g.</i>, 1
Corinthians<b> 1</b>:2; 1 Thessalonians <b>1</b>:1).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  5.   Hence, </span><span lang=EL
style='font-size:12.0pt'>διασπορᾶς</span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'> is
purely attributive (like </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ἐπιλησμονῆς</span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'> in James <b>1</b>:25; </span><span lang=EL
style='font-size:12.0pt'>ὑπακοῆς</span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'> in 1
Peter <b>1</b>:14; </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>τῆς</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ἀδικίας</span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'> in Luke <b>18</b>:6) equaling </span><span lang=EL
style='font-size:12.0pt'>διεσπαρμένοις</span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>,
and could as easily be used to refer to any Christians anywhere.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  6.   </span><span lang=EL
style='font-size:12.0pt'>Διασπορᾶς</span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'> is an
intensifying element for </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ἐπιδήμιοις</span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            I.    The contrast of the disbursement is
not a return to an earthly home, but their gathering into the kingdom of Christ
when the chief Shepherd shall appear, a fitting conclusion to an epistle thus
begun (1 Peter <b>5</b>:4ff).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            J.   The contrast to the Diaspora is not
the Jewish, but all disbelievers and unbelievers.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            K.  The contrast of what the readers are
to what they were is not to a people who had </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>lost their divine rank, but to a people who had never
possessed it. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            L.   <b>2</b>:10: Peter <u>uses the
language</u> of Hosea<b> 1</b>:9; <b>2</b>:1, 23 but modifies the expression </span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>οὐ</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>
</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>λαὸς</span><span lang=EL
style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>τοῦ</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>θεοῦ</span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'> (<i>“not a people of God”</i>) to read </span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>οἵ</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>
</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ποτε</span><span lang=EL
style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>οὐ</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>λαός</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>(<i>“who
in time past were not a people”</i>). Zahn seems to be playing on the
background of Hosea’s children (note especially <b>1</b>:2; <b>6</b>:11), and
saying that not <u>my people</u> refers to a repossessed Israelite race. He says
of Paul who quotes the same passage in Romans <b>9</b>:25, <i>“He <u>quotes the
substance of the passage</u> </i>(Hosea<b> 2</b>:10)<i> accurately with
reference to ‘the ultimate pardon of Israel’”</i>; and he notes Romans <b>11</b>:26-31
along with Romans <b>9</b>:25. The later passage strikes the same literal note
of Hosea that God’s former people, who being lost, were repossessed; but in <b>9</b>:24
Paul used Hosea <b>2</b>:10 to argue for a prophecy about <u>Gentiles</u> entering
the church—the same thing Peter is doing.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            M. Peter addresses the readers as distinct
from himself, especially in <b>1</b>:10-12, where the contrast is more than
between the ancient prophets and present Christians; because Peter himself
would also be in the same class and would include himself with them as “we,”
but he does not.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            N.  Difficulties in the ethical sense</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  1.   <u>Dispersion</u> is never
connected with the Christian church in early Christian </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        literature.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  2.   It is not feasible to suggest
that it was borrowed from the Old Testament, because then it was always linked
with the wrath of God against his disobedient people (but see<b> 1</b>:14: “obedient
children”).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            O.  The Jewish Dispersion is sometimes
thought to be more definitely meant in that the article is omitted from </span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>διασπορά</span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>,
a questionable distinction.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>III. Purpose</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>      </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            To encourage believers he had not seen,
but who knew him because of his prominence in the church. He accomplishes this
as the most distinguished apostle of the <u>circumcision</u> bears great
testimony to the Christian character of these called the <u>uncircumcision</u>.
In 2 Peter<b> 1</b>:12-15; <b>3</b>:1-2 (cp. 8) the two letters are books of
remembrance, reminding the readers of what they already knew.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>IV. Date of Writing</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            A.  <i>a quo</i>: In that Mark was not in
Paul’s company on any of his three journeys through Asia Minor, for the mention
of Mark (<b>5</b>:13) to be intelligent to them, it would have had to have been
written after such a time that Mark is known to have come in contact with at
least some of the individuals or churches in the area. Mark is not known to
have joined Paul until the latter’s Roman imprisonment in 62/63, at which
general time (as we know from Colossians <b>4</b>:10) Mark had not yet been
known to people in and around Colossae. In Colossians <b>4</b>:10 a proposed
visit by Mark is mentioned. Silvanus, on the other hand, was known to them (<b>5</b>:12).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            B.  <i>Ad quem</i>: Lycia and Pamphylia
are omitted perhaps because they were not organized into independent provinces
until A.D. 14, and</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            C.  Perhaps <i>Pontus</i> stands for
Pontus Polemoniacus, which was a principality until A.D. 63. Afterward, it was
part of Galatia. Pontus and Bithynia, both mentioned by Peter, were organized
into one province in 65 B.C. Perhaps Bithynia stands for the whole region. (Tacitus,
<i>Annals</i> <b>1</b>:74; <b>16</b>:18)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            D.  Naturally after Paul had written
letters to them for 2 Peter (<b>3</b>:15-16)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            E.   The persecutions spoken of in 2 Peter
seem to stop short of death with the possible exception of<b> 4</b>:15, but not
necessarily. The evidence from within the epistle could fit with a time when
the Roman government </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  1.   Sought them out.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  2.   Under Nero began punishing
Christians on the basis of certain acts that Christians were popularly believed
to have practiced, hence before Apocalypse, Hebrews, and the Neronian
persecutions. The three varieties (extents) of persecutions were (a)
persecutions, (b) persecutions for the name, (c) going to seek them out. They
burned Christians because arsonists were burned as punishment.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            F.   After 64; note <b>4</b>:12ff.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            G.  The persecution for the name was
prepared for even by the Lord himself. The persecutions under Pliny do not
suggest a new phenomenon. The kind of persecutions that were befalling Peter’s
readers were likely to befall Christians anywhere. Peter’s persecution remarks
imply that it is a new experience (<b>4</b>:12?).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            H.  Guthrie gives main proposals: (a)
90-100 (Domitian’s reign), (b) 111 (Trajan’s reign), and (c) 62-64 (Nero’s
reign).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            I.    Ramsey argues against a Neronian
time because</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  1.   Persecution by Nero was not “for
the name,” that is, simply because they were Christians, but trials were held.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  2.   Christianity cannot reasonably
have reached Pontus before A.D. 65. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  3.   Dependence on Romans and
Ephesians suggests a time had elapsed for their </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        recognition.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            J.   But if persecution “for the name” had
been a Roman policy for thirty years (since Vespasian), why would Pliny have
had any doubt about it? “Persecution for the name” is a set expression <u>we</u>
use; that does not mean Peter is using it as a technical term (<b>4</b>:14).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            K.  Lightfoot remarks that it was not
necessary that any formal edict against the Christians should be issued. The
mere negative fact that their religion had not been recognized as lawful would
have been ample justification for proceeding against them as soon as it was
recognized that Christianity was something distinct from Judaism. In Palestine
this distinction was clear from the time of the murder of James.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            L.   Arguments against Trajan time (Unnils
in <i>Interpreters</i>’ <i>Dictionary of the Bible</i>)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  1.   The sufferings were throughout
the world (<b>5</b>:9), but all the persecutions before the third century were
more or less local. Therefore, the sufferings must have been of another kind.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  2.   In <b>2</b>:13ff. a very
positive relation with the state authorities is implied, which is important in
that the governors and emperors are not decried (as in Revelation); and it is
supposed that they will praise the Christians.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  3.   The sufferings belonged to a
personal sphere rather than against Christians <i>per se</i>. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-left:1.25in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>               <i>“Once we rule out the possibility of
identifying these sufferings with some particular persecution, we are left with
no direct indication of date. The situation reflected in the letter could have
happened at any time in the first or second century wherever a Christian group
was found.”</i></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>   V. Author (Internal) </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            A.  Of particular note is <b>5</b>:12.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  1.   As far as words are concerned, </span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>γραφεῖν</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:
12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>διά</span><span lang=EL
style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>τινος</span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'> might refer to</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        a.   Amanuensis (see below)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        b.   Bearer (note Acts <b>15</b>:23:
</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>γράψαντες</span><span lang=EL
style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>διά</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>τινος</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>  </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>             </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  2.   The statement (</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>διὰ</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>
</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>Σιλουανοῦ</span><span lang=EL
style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ὑμῖν</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>τοῦ</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>πιστοῦ</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ἀδελφοῦ</span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>, </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ὡς</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>λογίζομαι</span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>, </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>δι</span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>’ </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ὀλίγων</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ἔγραψα</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>παρακαλῶν</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>κτλ</span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>.: <i>“Sylvanus to you the faithful brother, as I
account [him], through few [words] I wrote, exhorting,”</i> <i>etc</i>.) is of
utmost importance.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        a.   Peter could not express
his deliberate opinion as to the trustworthiness requisite for the task without
implying a degree of suspicion as to the person in question (?). </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        b.   He could have satisfied
that suspicion by reading the letter.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        c.   The order putting
Silvanus first may be significant for emphasis. Perhaps </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              it means that Silvanus’
part in the writing was more significant than usual.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        d.   If he were only the
bearer of the letter, it is questionable whether he needed to be mentioned, for
they would know the bearer on his arrival. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  3.   Peter must be considered
primary author instead of Silvanus or the point of writing would be destroyed: the
circumcision apostle’s praising the Christian Gentiles.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  4.   Perhaps Silvanus knew Greek
better than Peter did.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        a.   Peter did not speak his
own language with refined precision—Matthew <b>26</b>:73; Mark <b>14</b>:70;
Luke <b>22</b>:59.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        b.   He struck his own
countrymen as ignorant and unlearned (Acts <b>4</b>:13), a view that Luke seems
to agree with.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        c.   Primitive tradition
represents him as making use of Mark as interpreter (Eusebius, <i>Ecclesiastical
History</i> <b>3</b>:39:15; Irenaeus<b> 3</b>:1:1; <b>10</b>:6).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        d.   Basilides the Gnostic
pretended to have learned some part of his teaching from Glaucias, “the
interpreter of Peter” (Clement of Alexandria, <i>Stromateis</i> <b>7</b>:17:106).
This, though fiction, testifies to the early belief of the church that Peter
shrank from, or was rather inept at, literary composition in Greek.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        e.   The epistle shows no
trace of Hebraisms; so it is not a translation.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        f.    But the fact that Mark
is called the interpreter of Peter is clearly intended to authenticate Mark’s
gospel, and it is straining the language to suppose that Papias meant to imply
Peter’s linguistic inability. The objection that Peter got his Greek knowledge
from books is interesting in that <u>any</u> modern-day scholar who should so
assert is himself in that position and ought to be careful about judgments of
superior Greek style.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  5.   “Through Silvanus”</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        a.   Can mean more than bearer</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              Dionysius of Corinth
(Eusebius, <i>Ecclesiastical History</i> <b>4</b>:23:11) speaks of the <i>Epistle
of Clement</i> as </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ἡμῖν</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>διὰ</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ἐκδήμεντος</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>γραφεῖσαν</span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>, meaning clearly that Clement was the mouthpiece or
interpreter of the church of Rome.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        b.   Silvanus could have been
both bearer and amanuensis.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        c.   An interpreter might at
least be more than an amanuensis:</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:135.0pt;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify;text-indent:-27.0pt'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>(1)<span style='font:7.0pt "Times New Roman"'>&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;
</span></span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>Peter dictated in Aramaic with the
interpreter expressing the </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:135.0pt;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>substance in his own Greek.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:135.0pt;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify;text-indent:-117.0pt'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              (2)    Peter may have
dictated in Greek in the extent to which he could speak it and the interpreter
corrected and polished the expression. (Note Josephus in <i>Contra Apion</i> <b>1</b>:9.)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:135.0pt;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify;text-indent:-117.0pt'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              (3)    The interpreter
received only general instructions with a free hand to carry out the work
subject to the author’s approval.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  6.   Perhaps the extensive use of an
amanuensis has something to do with the </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        literary difference between 1
and 2 Peter.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        a.   A different amanuensis</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        b.   Peter wrote 2 Peter
himself (?).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  7.   The writer was not a forger.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        a.   Why would he choose such
out-of-the-way epistles as these? hence, the </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              authenticity of the
salutation.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        b.   A later pseudonymous
writer would have been more inclined to lay stress on the relationship of Peter
and Jesus as later Petrine apocryphals do to give them an air of
authentication. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        c.   As in later Petrine
apocryphals, we would expect a forger to quote more of the words of Jesus for
authentication.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  8.   Ignorance makes it impossible
to say what kind of relations existed between him and the communities he wrote
to, because we know nothing of Peter’s activities from the time of the Conference
on Circumcision till his being in Rome. We know he made missionary tours with
his wife (1 Corinthians <b>9</b>:5), and we find him in Antioch (Galatians <b>2</b>:11ff).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.75in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>      9.   He does not express his affection, as Paul
does.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>          10.    Peter mentions Mark in<b> 5</b>:13,
who is known in external literature as </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  connected with Peter.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                11.   “As I account him,” and so on,
may have been inserted to allay any suspicion on the part of the readers of a
letter from a Palestinian Jew with such perfect literary style.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            B.  General arguments against Petrine
authorship.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  1.   If he were a disciple of the
Lord, he would refer more to the words of Jesus.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        But the writer is more concerned
with the cross and resurrection, because they created the new situation of
grace, than with the earthly career of Jesus. It is the crucified, risen Lord,
rather than the teacher of Galilee who is of prime importance. The words of
Jesus are not the point so much as the significance of his most important deed—where
He was not verbally teaching. The whole presupposition is that the Apostle
would have written as we would expect him to.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  2.   Too Pauline rather than Judaic</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        The common elements between 1
Peter and Paul are better explained as the common ground of the preaching: the
truth. Liberals are ever too impetuous to judge the true life of Christianity
in terms of its apostates. But as a matter of fact, Peter the person of the New
Testament did not stress obedience to the Mosaic Law. The conference on
circumcision makes this fact evident.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  3.   The Greek is too good.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        Silvanus is the amanuensis.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  4.   The writer’s habit of quoting
the Old Testament in ways that point to the Septuagint (LXX) rather than to the
Masoretic Text is peculiar for a Palestinian Jew (?).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        a.   The Masoretic Text was one
of many texts (note Dead Sea Scrolls), but was chosen for its value in the
second century.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        b.   Peter’s text may have
resembled the LXX more closely than the Masoretic </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              Text.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        c.   Peter as a foreigner
writing in another language should suit his readers, not himself, especially
when it comes to literal quotations. Their text would have been the LXX.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'> VI.      External Testimony to Authorship</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            A.  2 Peter <b>3</b>:1</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  1.   Even if denied Petrine
authorship, it is nevertheless the earliest testimony of 1 Peter’s existence
and that it existed under the name of Peter. (Some want to make 2 Peter written
first, however, with the reference to a lost epistle).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  2.   Eusebius (<i>Ecclesiastical
History</i> <b>3</b>:25:2) puts 1 Peter among the </span><span lang=EL
style='font-size:12.0pt'>ὁμολογούμενα</span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>,
books placed in the canon without any doubt.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  3.   No book of the New Testament
has earlier, better, or stronger attestation, though Irenaeus is the first to
quote it by name (<i>ca</i>. A.D. 170): <b>4</b>:9:2; <u>16</u>:5; <b>5</b>:7:2.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  4.   All the catalogues of the New
Testament given by Westcott in Appendix D of his <u>Canon</u> and in the
Cheltenham List contain the book.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  5.   Absent from the Muratorian
Canon</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  6.   Absent from Marcion’s list</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        a.   Even though Marcion was
from Pontus</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        b.   Probably he knew of the
work but did not receive it because Peter was a </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              Jew.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  7.   Origen (Eusebius <i>Ecclesiastical
History</i> <b>6</b>:25:8) shows that by his time the epistle formed an
undisputed part of the canon.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  8.   1 Peter was omitted from the
Syriac at first though admitted later when other catholic epistles were not.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span lang=ES
style='font-size:12.0pt'>VII. General Doctrinal Content </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span lang=ES
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span lang=ES
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            A.  </span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>Peculiar
material</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  1.   The preaching of Christ in
Hades (= Matthew <b>27</b>:51, 52; Ephesians <b>4</b>:9?)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  2.   The inspiration of prophecy by
Christ (= 2 Corinthians <b>3</b>:7ff?)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            B.  It is the same as the Pauline
materials as to content.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.75in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify;text-indent:-.5in'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>VIII. Rome as the Place of Writing (<i>Babylon</i> = a
cryptogram for Rome: Revelation <b>14</b>:8; <b>16</b>:19; <b>17</b>:5; <b>18</b>:2,
10, 21)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            A.  We do not know of Peter being in this
direction except for the word <i>Babylon</i>.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            B.  Babylon itself was a desolate place at
this time.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            C.  From sources we know that Peter,
Silvanus, and Mark were associated.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            D.  We know that <i>Babylon</i> is elsewhere
used to mean Rome (Revelation <b>17-18</b>; <i>Apoc. Bar</i>. 1:1; Origen, <i>Sib</i>.
<b>5</b>:158).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            E.   Peter would want to go to Rome: all
roads led to Rome.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            F.   That <i>Babylon</i> meant Rome was
the early universal interpretation from the time of </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  Papias (<i>cir</i>. 60-130).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            G.  There is no local tradition that any one
of the apostles except Thomas was associated with those parts.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            H.  The earliest Syriac tradition connects
Peter, not with Babylon, but with Rome.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            I.    Perhaps for reasons of prudence “Babylon”
was used for “Rome” to protect the writer from police efforts, since Rome was a
center of vicious action against Christians.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            J.   The Eastern church until a late date
did not claim Peter in its origin.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            K.  The place was sparsely populated,
especially in the period after the migration of A.D. 41 (earlier than 1 Peter) and
the resultant massacre of large numbers of Jews at Seleucia (Josephus, <i>Antiquities
of the Jews</i> <b>18</b>:9). The Jews moved because of persecutions and
pestilence. Besides, about 50,000 were slain, which was the greater part.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            L.   Very early tradition associates Peter
with the West, not the East.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            M. Mark and Silvanus are known to have
been in the West at the time of Paul’s imprisonment (Col</span><span lang=EL
style='font-size:12.0pt'>ο</span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>ssians <b>4</b>:10),
but nothing suggests their ever being in the East.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            N.  A Jewish settlement in Mesopotamia
Babylon, until much later in the Christian era, was their home from the time of
the Babylonian captivity till the times of the Talmud (Josephus, <i>Antiquities</i>
<i>of the Jews </i><b>15</b>:3:1; Philo, <i>De Legatio ad Caium</i>, p. 1023).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            O.  If the order in which the name of the
provinces is given refers to the messenger’s order of travel, it is of note
that the travel ends in the West.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            P.   In <b>2</b>:5 Eusebius speaks of 1
Peter <b>5</b>:13, saying that <i>Babylon</i> is a metaphor for Rome. In
connection with so stating, he mentions Papias as giving also the story of the
writing of Mark’s gospel.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>  IX. Reasons Given for Babylonian Place of Writing</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            A.  Peter tended to be literal.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            B.  <i>Babylon</i> was not used that way before
the time of the Apocalypse.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>   X. Purpose </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>      </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            A.  It is an exhortation to live in
accordance with the hope they had received in Christ (see 2 Peter <b>3</b>:1-2).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            B.  Tenney says, <i>“The occasion for this
letter was the ominous shadow of persecution. The letter was a warning and an
encouragement preparatory to the coming emergency.”</i></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'> XI. Beare’s Arguments Against Petrine Authorship</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            A.  It was written during the time of
Trajan; hence, not Petrine.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            B.  A Jew did not write it, because to the
Jew the Torah was the heart of religion, but to this writer the substance of
the earlier writing lay in its testimony to the sufferings of Christ. Instead,
his expressions, which can be found to parallel certain aspects of mystery
religions, mean that the writer knew them as a devotee (!)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            C.  It shows dependence on Paul instead of
the words of Christ, with whom Peter was especially associated.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            D.  Peter would have known the Septuagint
only if he had learned Greek later in life, and he would have known it only in
a literary fashion, but the writer of this epistle knew the Old Testament only
in literary fashion and only in Greek. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            E.   The letter is the work of a man of
letters.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>XII. Concerning the Break Between<b> 4</b>:11 and <b>4</b>:12</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            A.  Perdelivitz said that there was a
break.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  1.   Doxology and a new address in <b>4</b>:11
and <b>4</b>:12 respectively</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  2.   A certain amount of repetition
before and after the break</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  3.   The persecutions of <b>4</b>:12
are actual facts; in <b>1</b>:6; <b>3</b>:13ff they are only </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        possibilities.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  4.   Are not sufferings both present
and future? Furthermore, what prohibits the view that the present sufferings
exist but in the future will grow worse? </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:
12.0pt'>Τ</span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>he difference is in degree. May
we ask whether the possibility of suffering in <b>1</b>:6 and in <b>3</b>:13
are possibilities by chance rather than by time (not that there are none until
the future, but that a given individual has a possibility of suffering now and
a likelihood later)?</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  5.   Even a definite break means
nothing. Perhaps we may regard <b>4</b>:12 as a postscript with <b>5</b>:12-14
as the salutary close.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            B.  Arguments against such a break as
indicating that the first was a baptismal catechism (<b>1</b>:3-<b>4</b>:11)
and the second an exhortation (<b>4</b>:12-<b>5</b>:14)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  1.   It is hard to see how baptism
fits in the structure of the epistle.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  2.   The distribution of various
sections according to a liturgical pattern appears </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        forced.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  3.   Some supposed parallels on
closer inspection turn out to be superficial.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  4.   Sufficient to say that the
connection here between Petrine materials and </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        baptismal language testifies
only to the common Christian vocabulary</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  5.   A certain amount of repetition
belongs to this writer’s style.</span></p>

margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
