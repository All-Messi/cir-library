<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>3.   Omission of chapters (<b>15</b>-)<b>16</b></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.75in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify;text-indent:-.25in'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>a.<span style='font:7.0pt "Times New Roman"'>&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;
</span></span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>Liturgical purposes originated
texts without chapter <b>16</b> and perhaps <b>15</b> as well. (See below “The
long list of greetings.”)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>      b.   Marcion omitted them because <b>15</b>:8
calls Christ a <i>“minister to the circumcision.”</i>  Other places in Romans
would apparently be as unacceptable to him as this phrase, notably the Olive Tree
Parable in chapter <b>11</b>. He would have needed to reject the book
altogether if this were a significant point and if he were consistent in his
mutilation of canonical writings.  Nevertheless, there is evidence from Origen
and Tertullian that Marcion used or originated a text that omitted these
chapters. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.75in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify;text-indent:-.5in'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>      c.   Paul (via Tertius?) wrote the letter in
more than one form (without the greetings so it could also serve as an
encyclical).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>      d.   Recopied during circulation without these
chapters</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.75in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify;text-indent:-.5in'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>      e.   Defective archetype (An early copy could
have lost its last sheet or two, especially if it was a codex; cp. the similar
possibility in the Gospel of Mark)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>      Codices Amiatinus and Fuldensis, whose chapter
headings can be traced to a text before the time of Jerome (fl. A.D. 400), seem
to indicate that these chapters were missing. Although Paul’s argument does not
properly end until <b>15</b>:13, there is a fairly definite break after chapter
<b>14</b>, as illustrated by the decision to make a chapter division here. Some
critics have said that the language beginning at chapter <b>15</b> is more
personal, although previously Paul has been speaking in the first and second person
singular and plural in<b> 14</b>:7-22.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>4.   The long list of greetings</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>      a.   Paul added the long list of greetings as
justification for writing a letter to a church he did not found. He wrote from
Corinth before he had been to Rome. But he had met, worked with, and even
converted a number of those who had founded or furthered the church in Rome. The
long list showed that he had some basis for doing what on his own principles may
have appeared presumptuous—teaching them despite not having converted them.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>      b.   He added the list because he knew <u>fewer</u>
in Rome than in other churches he addressed. In most other churches, it would
have been impractical to greet everyone he knew. His acquaintances in Rome were
limited; so rather than naming just the closest two or three, he names them
all. The letter to the Colossians also has a longer-than-normal list of
salutations (<u>from</u> individuals with the writer rather than <u>to</u>
individuals at the destination as in Romans), and Colossians was another church
Paul addressed but evidently did not found directly (Colossians <b>2</b>:1-5)—although
he may have founded it in the sense that members of his circle did the
evangelizing there.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>      c.   Similar salutation lists may normally have
been sent in a cover letter that the epistle itself was wrapped in. With Romans,
that cover letter could have been copied into the main manuscript at an early
date. Under this reconstruction, the long list would not have been abnormal;
copying that list onto the end of the letter would have been what was unusual.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>      d.   A long list of greetings may have been in
order since there were at least three congregations (<b>16</b>:4-5, 14, 15). But
this was probably true as well for other churches in larger metropolitan areas.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>      a.   The list of greetings may have been the
originating factor that occasioned all this textual instability. Leaving out
the greetings could have allowed the doxology or benediction to collapse onto
the end of chapter <b>15</b>.  </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>      b.   If Marcion had qualms about the final two
chapters, his influence may have additionally complicated the textual condition
of Romans manuscripts.  </span></p>
