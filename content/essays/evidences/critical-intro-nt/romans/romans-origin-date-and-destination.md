<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'> I. Origin</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            Paul wrote Romans after the acquaintances
previously known from elsewhere had moved to the imperial city (<b>16</b>:3-16),
after he had preached from Jerusalem to Illyricum (Romans <b>15</b>:19, cp.
23), while he was in route to Jerusalem with the offering for the saints
(Romans <b>15</b>:25-28, 31), and at a time when he anticipated preaching the gospel
in Spain (<b>15</b>:24, 28-29). According to Acts<b> 19</b>:21-22, while Paul
was still in Ephesus on the third tour, he planned to go to Jerusalem and then
to Rome. On this third tour, Paul organized the collection for the poor Christians
in Jerusalem (cp. Acts <b>20</b>:4; <b>21</b>:15; 1 Corinthians <b>16</b>:1-9;   
 2 Corinthians <b>8-9</b>). Having left Ephesus after the riot, Paul journeyed
north through Troas, across through Macedonia, and down to Greece (<b>20</b>:1-6)
before returning through Macedonia and down the east coast of the Aegean Sea to
Miletus and on to Palestine. He had not planned to minister again in Asia (Acts
<b>20</b>:25, 38). His three-month stay in Corinth during this trip would have
given him time to compose Romans.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            In Romans <b>16</b>:1, Paul calls Phoebe <i>“<u>our</u>
sister . . . in Cenchrea,”</i> implying that she was nearby at the time of
writing. Cenchrea lay across the narrow isthmus from Corinth.  Phoebe’s
proposed trip to Rome may have provided the occasion for Paul to write to
Christians he had wanted to see for so long.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>II. Date</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            The date of writing Romans depends on the
date for Paul’s third journey. He wrote the epistle then somewhere between A.D.
55 and 57.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>III. Destination</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            <i>In Rome</i> (</span><span lang=EL
style='font-size:12.0pt'>ἐν</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>Ρόμῃ</span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>) is
omitted at <b>1</b>:7 and 15 in a few sources. In <b>1</b>:7 it is omitted from
Gg, Origen, and minuscules 1739 and 1908. The destination is omitted from Gg in
<b>1</b>:7. Consequently, in only one manuscript, the bilingual Gg, are the
destination notes omitted altogether. Since all manuscripts except Gg contain
indications of destination, there is no problem about where the epistle was
originally sent, whatever may be said for circulation theories that attempt to account
for the shorter forms of the epistle.  Those theories pertain only to the last
two chapters.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            The reason <i>in Rome</i> is omitted in <b>1</b>:7
from Origen and the two minuscules is unintentional error in copying. With Gg
the question is more difficult, because only a deliberate act would seem to
account for leaving out both references to destination.  </span></p>
