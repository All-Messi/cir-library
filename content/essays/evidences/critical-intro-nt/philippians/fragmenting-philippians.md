<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            C.  The phenomenon in <b>3</b>:1-2 may not
represent a mood shift. Paul may be deliberately making the point that his
readers need to rejoice even in a circumstance like the one the circumcision
party was creating. He goes on to give them reasons for not accepting that
party’s message, which could have the effect of helping them not to get
discouraged over the circumcision advocates’ message. The same pattern occurs
again in <b>4</b>:4, where the commandment to rejoice leads to an exhortation
not to be anxious and to some advice on how to stay on top of anxiety: they
should <u>pray</u>, be <u>thankful</u>, and <u>think about positive things</u>.
Paul gives them his own example of rejoicing over the <u>good things that good
people have done for him</u> so that in any state he is in, he can be free
(from anxiety in imprisonment) and live above the situation.</span></p>
