<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;
margin-bottom:0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:center'><b><span
style='font-size:14.0pt'>THE PROBLEM OF THE PASTORALS</span></b></p>

<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;
margin-bottom:0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:center'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;
margin-bottom:0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:center'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>Virgil Warren, PhD</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;
margin-bottom:0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:center'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;
margin-bottom:0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:center'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>   </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'> I. Historical Problem</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            Those who  reject  the  Pauline  authorship
 of  the  pastoral  epistles  (1  Timoty,  2 <br>
Timothy, Titus ) contend that <i>there is no time in Paul’s life as recorded in
Acts when he could have written these epistles</i>. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            We cannot show, however, that the end of The
Acts account is also the end of Paul’s life. Arguments for a release from Paul’s
Roman imprisonment in the closing chapter of Acts are as follows:</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            A.  The epistles, written from a lengthy
imprisonment, expect Paul’s release: the ending of Philemon as well as
Philippians <b>2</b>:19-24; hence, liberal scholars are interested in getting
Paul away from Rome in writing the “prison epistles” (Ephesians, Philippians,
Colossians, and Philemon)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            B.  The ending of the Book of Acts <i>(“two
whole years”</i>) is the extent of waiting without accusers appearing. That
fact explains why Paul could be imprisoned that long and yet expect release. Roman
law imprisoned for awaiting trial or sentencing. Imprisonment itself was not a
punishment.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            C.  The <u>tradition</u> that Paul went to
Spain (not whether he actually went there) militates against his death at the
end of the Acts account. Clement of Rome (A.D. 96) says that Paul went to “the
bounds of the west.” The Muratorian Canon (A.D. 170-180) is the earliest source
indicating that Paul was released at the end of Acts.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            D.  Eusebius dates Paul’s death in July of
67. So according to him, Paul had 62/64-67 for activity.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            E.   The strong tradition that Paul did
write the pastoral epistles implies that early Christians believed he was released
from the Roman imprisonment.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            F.   The imprisonment and imminent death
of Paul pictured in 2 Timothy <b>4</b>:6-8 speaks differently from the
imprisonment at the close of Acts and the one presupposed in the prison
epistles. In 2 Timothy, accusers were at Paul’s trial; here no accusers appear.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>II. Theological Objections</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            A.  <i>The pastorals have a moral tone
rather than an evangelistic one</i>. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  The tone of a letter cannot
determine authorship. The circumstances and purpose of an epistle are responsible
for these matters. In other letters, Paul mixes comments about Christian living
with doctrine and outreach—a natural combination for the Christian faith in the
real world.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            B.   Faith<i> (</i></span><i><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>πίστις</span></i><i><span style='font-size:
12.0pt'>, pistis) is used as a deposit of doctrine </span></i><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>(1 Timothy <b>4</b>:1, 6).</span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  But there is a similar usage in Jude
3. <i>Faith</i> can serve as a label for the Christian system, which is based
on faith/trust. It is an example of metonymy (part put for whole). It is not
quite accurate to say that it means a deposit of doctrine if it serves
metonymically for the Christian system.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            C.  The objections against Pauline
authorship on <i>the absence of characteristic Pauline subject matter (fatherhood
of God, work of the Holy Spirit, mystic union with Christ)</i> lacks force.
Paul’s intent in these epistles is different and presumably so in that, having
never written about such matters, he would want to write for posterity because
of his imminent death (2 Timothy <b>4</b>:6-8).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>III. Ecclesiastical Problem</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            <i>Ecclesiastical arrangements are too
advanced for Paul’s time.</i></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><i><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></i></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            A.  <i>Paul did not favor organizing the
church</i>. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  On the contrary, Paul had seen that
there were those who could rule in Ephesus. Philippians <b>1</b>:1 speaks of
the same organizational pattern. From earliest times, the churches had elders: Jerusalem,
Antioch (“teachers”; Acts <b>13</b>:1ff). The simplicity of the church’s
organization may appear to be non-existent organization compared to later
developments in organization, but local autonomy, for example, leaves personal
connection with believers at large. The interpersonal character of the
Christian faith, in fact, predicts modest organizational structure.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            B.  <i>Elders in the pastorals are
essentially tradition bearers</i>. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  However, the matter of their
authority is also considered.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            C.  <i>A situation of lengthy
establishment may be reflected in the injunction not to appoint a new convert</i>.
</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  But the Ephesian church had existed
for more than fifteen years. “New” is relative. Besides, there would be ongoing
conversions that would bring new converts into the church.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            D.  <i>The directive to Titus to appoint
elders in every church (<b>1</b>:5) is taken to represent the monarchical
episcopate of the second century</i>. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  This supposes that “appoint”
signifies putting into office without selection by the congregation. Acts <b>6</b>:1-6
shows that selection and appointment could be alternatives. It also forgets
that Titus is serving as an extension of Paul’s own apostolic work. Titus and
Timothy are precisely <u>not</u> doing this work as an expression of whatever
office they themselves we can say they have.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            E.   <i>The use of </i>bishop<i> and </i>elder<i>
is supposed to represent a later distinction.</i> </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  In the text here no distinction is
evident. Titus<b> 1</b>:5-10 shows that <i>elder</i> and <i>bishop</i> are interchangeable
terms. Acts <b>20</b>:17 + 28 illustrates the same interchange of terms, and
Acts is surely not a mid-second-century work.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'> IV. Literary Objections</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            <i>The words of Paul not found elsewhere
in the New Testament bear a close association </i></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><i><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            to the second-century fathers.</span></i></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><i><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></i></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            A.  Ninety percent of the <i>hapax
legomena</i> (</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>ἅπαξ</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt'>λεγόμενα</span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>, “once-spoken”/“one-use” words) of the pastorals can
be explained as (1) words in the LXX, (2) compounds of common words, (3) words
that appear in lists.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            B.  Other differences</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  1.   Perhaps the second-century Church
fathers used these words because they were used in the pastorals.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  2.   Differences in purpose, subject
matter, destination, type of writing also have a </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>bearing on the vocabulary.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  3.   There is more material in the Church
Fathers than in the rest of the New Testament; so the greater likelihood that <i>hapaxes</i>
in the pastorals would show up there.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  4.   The second-century vocabulary
is closer to 1 Corinthians than to the pastorals.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  5.   Making vocabulary arguments
against authorship requires a substantial body of literature—10,000 words, for
example. The pastorals as a set have only about 3325 words.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  6.   The “higher” number of words
per page raises the number of <i>hapaxes</i> per page. The pastorals have more
words per page than do some of Paul’s other letters. When the “percentage” of <i>hapaxes</i>
per page is taken, it is not much different from Paul’s other writings.</span></p>

margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>
