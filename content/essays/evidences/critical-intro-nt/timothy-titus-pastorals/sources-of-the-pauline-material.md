<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;
margin-bottom:0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:center'><b><span
style='font-size:14.0pt'>SOURCES OF THE PAULINE MATERIAL</span></b></p>

<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;
margin-bottom:0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:center'><b><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></b></p>

<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;
margin-bottom:0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:center'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>Virgil Warren, PhD</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;
margin-bottom:0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:center'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>Introductory Guidelines</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>  </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            The kinds of sources found in Pauline
material are not only those that appear in literature generally, but those that
fit the nature of scripture. This observation is not meant to prejudice
research but to point out a consistency Christians expect to find in this
study. So the first guideline is that <b>(a)</b> <u>scripture is like what God
himself would exhale</u>. Presumably 2 Timothy <b>3</b>:16 teaches that
scripture <u>as such</u> is God-breathed as to its <u>intended content</u>. Sources
and processes of origination for Pauline material correlate with the nature of
scripture that issues from them. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            <b>(b)</b> <u>Supernatural agency is not
invoked unnecessarily</u>. The aim here is to make minimum affirmation, not to
account for Christianity without divine agency. Avoiding overkill lets God do
more than necessary without claiming he <u>did</u> do more. God need not have
done for Paul what Paul could do for himself.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  With content as well as manner of
expression, we can allow for both <u>the protective and positive roles of the
Spirit</u>. To conform to God-breathedness, scripture need only <u>result</u>
in trustworthy text; God need not be the immediate <u>cause</u> of every text. God
may indeed operate in <u>prescriptive-positive</u> fashion by giving even the
very <i>words</i> and writing them himself, as when he wrote on the wall of
Belshazzar’s palace (Daniel <b>5</b>) or engraved the ten commandments on stone
(Exodus <b>31</b>:18); and he may directly reveal <i>ideas</i>, as he did to
Moses at the burning bush (Exodus <b>3</b>:1-<b>4</b>:17). </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  But the Spirit may also operate in
more <u>permissive-protective</u> fashion and achieve the same level of
trustworthy results. He may give <i>abilities </i>(1 Kings <b>3</b>:4-15), <i>experiences
</i>(Acts <b>10</b>:9-<u>34</u>-48), or <i>offices </i>(2 Samuel <b>7</b>:1-17);
and then <u>permit</u> a man like Paul, not only to generate ways of
communicating God’s revelation, but to generate the revelation itself. If the
ideas or ways of saying them are incorrect or inadequate, the Spirit can
intervene to correct his prophet and <u>protect</u> him from error (cp. Nathan
in 2 Samuel <b>7</b>). Under either model, the quality of Paul’s material would
be the same as if God himself had said it.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            <b>(c)</b> <u>Jewish backgrounds take
precedence over Gentile ones</u>. Even on naturalistic grounds, we would expect
Christianity to have come primarily from Jewish sources. Paul’s worldview is
neither Greek nor Roman, but Jewish. Even in ambiguous cases, presumption
favors a scenario that emphasizes Jewish revelation, thought form, teaching
style, and literary custom.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            All things being equal, <b>(d)</b> <u>Pauline
materials are as likely to have come from Paul himself as from unknown persons</u>.
Everything creative and artistic in his writings need not be anonymous products
of the early church rather than from a man we know was a driving personality
behind the initial spread of Christianity. The New Testament was not produced
in a vacuum; neither did it just evolve.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>    I. Content Sources for Pauline Material</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            A.  The Old Testament canon</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  Paul uses an array of Old Testament
materials in his writings and uses them in several ways. The passages he cites
(1) g<u>ive content</u> that he reiterates to his readers. But more than that,
those texts (2) <u>provide authority</u> for what he says. They often (3) <u>establish
bases for inferences</u> that he makes beyond what they directly affirm. Paul
uses incidents and persons in scripture that (4) <u>illustrate</u> theological
points not necessarily implied by them. Those points rest on Paul’s own
authority as Christ’s spokesman, but he makes them memorable by attaching them
to striking images his Jewish audience knew.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'> </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            B.  Direct revelation </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  In Galatians, Paul is adamant about
not getting his message from other people but through revelation from Jesus
Christ (<b>1</b>:11-12). In chapters <b>1-2,</b> he claims to be a primary
witness for Christ, one who did not depend on previous apostolic testimony or
the witness of men like Ananias, who baptized him; Paul apparently refused to
be classified as a tradition bearer.<span style='position:relative;top:-3.0pt'>1</span> Not
only was his message true; it was directly revealed truth. This revelation was
from Christ in a way distinct from information learned through other people and
from his own thoughts as well (see I F). In Ephesians <b>3</b>:3 he says that “the
mystery” was made known to him by revelation (cp. 1 Corinthians <b>2</b>:10? <b>12</b>:1-4?
Galatians <b>2</b>:2). He tells the Corinthians, <i>“The things I am writing to
you . . . are an epistle of the Lord”</i> (1 Corinthians <b>14</b>:37).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  Matthew <b>16</b>:17 may serve as a
control text for the wording of Paul’s claim in Galatians. Jesus tells Peter
that he did not get his Good Confession from other people (“flesh and blood”),
but from the Father. Jesus may have meant a general idea—that Peter’s
confession did not represent what people were saying about the Son of Man (<b>16</b>:13).
It was a viewpoint that came mediately from the Father through the Son rather
than immediately from the Father to Peter. Back in Galatians, Paul’s words
similarly had a more general import, referring to something that originated in
his own thinking—as based on Old Testament exegesis, for example. It was
tantamount to direct revelation because the Spirit who worked manifestly with
Paul confirmed his understanding by not correcting it.<span style='position:
relative;top:-3.0pt'>2</span></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            C.  Jewish Apocrypha</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  Paul’s use of the armor figure in
Ephesians <b>6</b>:13-18 has antecedents in Wisdom <b>5</b>:17-23. Most
connections with apocryphal works are general ideas and verbal reminiscences
rather than intentional references; they are not necessarily any more than
cross references between different books within the New Testament. Even then
they are few, being mostly from <i>The Book of Wisdom</i> and <i>The Book of
Sirach</i> (<i>Ecclesiasticus</i>).<span style='position:relative;top:-3.0pt'>3</span> Paul
never uses the apocrypha as an authoritative basis for anything doctrinal or
factual. In comparison to canonical references, even verbal similarities with
the apocrypha are sparse</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            D.  Jewish Pseudepigrapha</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  The principal example of this source
in Paul appears in 2 Timothy <b>3</b>:8, where he refers to the magicians who
withstood Moses, calling them Jannes (“Seducer”) and Jambres (“Insurrectionist”).
Exodus <b>7</b>:11, 22; <b>8</b>:7 does not name these men; the names appear in
several later Jewish works as well as early Christian and even pagan sources,
the earliest reference being in the Essene work known as the <i>Damascus
Document</i>. (See <i>The Old Testament Pseudepigrapha</i> [Doubleday, 1985],
Vol. II, pp. 427-36; and “Jannes and Jambres,” <i>The New Schaff-Herzog
Religious Encyclopedia</i>, p. 95.) The fictitious “names” apparently derive
from their role in opposing Moses in front of Pharaoh. It is sufficient here to
suppose that Paul designated these men by traditional labels much as we refer
to the Magi as Melchior, Balthasar, and Gaspar. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  Citation by an inspired writer does
not mean the whole work is inspired, nor necessarily that the specific item is
true. It may illustrate the author’s point, much the way a preacher might
illustrate honesty with Parson Weems’ story about George Washington cutting
down a cherry tree with his hatchet.<span style='position:relative;top:-3.0pt'>4</span></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            E.   Pagan citations</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  All four pagan citations in the New
Testament occur in Paul. The first is an altar inscription in Athens: <i>“To an
Unknown God” </i>(Acts <b>17</b>:23). The next comes from Aratus in Acts <b>17</b>:28:
<i>“For we are also his offspring.”</i> The third is from Menander/Euripides in
1 Corinthians <b>15</b>:33: <i>“Evil companions corrupt good morals.” </i>The
last appears in Titus <b>1</b>:12, <i>“Cretans are always liars, evil beasts,
idle gluttons,”</i> a quotation from the Cretan poet Epimenides.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  Beyond these few clear cases,
originally pagan materials could be reworked for Christian purposes. Some Old
Testament psalms resemble Near Eastern counterparts; they were “baptized” into
Jewish thought much as we sing “Bridge over Troubled Waters” with Christian
meaning (For a brief discussion and bibliography, see R. K. Harrison, <i>Introduction
to the Old Testament</i> [Eerdmans, 1969], pp. 997-99). Some of the proverbs in
the Book of Proverbs originated from wise men of the East who are not likely to
have been Jewish (Proverbs 2<b>4</b>:23; <b>30-31</b>). There is no clear case
of this in Paul or the New Testament, although such a proposal has been made
for John <b>1</b>:1-14, <i>The Ode to the Logos</i>. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            F.   Christian tradition</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  In light of Paul’s claim to be a primary
witness for Christ (Galatians <b>1</b>:11-12), we wonder whether <i>“received
[from the Lord]”</i> in 1 Corinthians <b>11</b>:23; <b>15</b>:3-8 can mean <i>“received
ultimately from the Lord”</i> as an item of tradition or must mean <i>“received
directly from the Lord”</i> as an item of special revelation. The first passage
is especially interesting because it cites an event in the private life of
Christ during his ministry (note Hebrews<b> 5</b>:7). Neither in his letters
nor in The Acts does Paul indicate that he witnessed Christ’s ministry; so we
do not know how he knew about what happened. He would have been a prime
candidate for the Jews to send out to investigate the work of John the Baptist
and Jesus (John<b> 1</b>:19; Matthew <b>15</b>:1-2; Mark <b>3</b>:2 +<b> 2</b>:24;
Matthew <b>22</b>:15ff; John <b>7</b>:32-46; <i>etc</i>.). From personal
observation Paul could have known about public events in Jerusalem, especially
during the final week (Luke<b> 24</b>:18; Acts <b>26</b>:26) and if he was a
member of the Sanhedrin (Acts <b>7</b>:58; <b>26</b>:10). We do not know how he
knew about earlier events. According to Acts<b> 20</b>:35 Paul refers to the
only saying of the Lord outside the gospels: <i>“It is more blessed to give
than to receive.” </i>He did know about these things.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  Since only by The Twelve witnessed the
occurrences in the upper room, how did Paul know what Jesus said at the
institution of the Supper? (1) He could have been told by previous apostles, a
solution that accords with the principle of minimum affirmation, or unnecessary
supernaturalizing. <i>“Received</i> (from the Lord)<i>”</i> in the Corinthian
texts would mean <i>“received ultimately from the Lord.”</i> (2) He could also
have <i>“received directly from the Lord,”</i> say, in Arabia, a solution that
fits the verbiage of Galatians <b>1</b>. Like John the Baptist in the
wilderness, Elijah beside Brook Cherith, and Moses on Mount Sinai, Paul in
Arabia would have had a time of special encounter before entering his apostolic
ministry.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  Did Paul receive from previous
apostles information about Christ’s life, or did he receive his message
directly from Christ? One solution is to say that he received many facts
indirectly but their meaning directly. In Galatians <b>1</b>:11-12 he addresses
the Judaizing controversy that centered around circumcision, which had both a
salvation and a political association. Paul may mean that he got directly from
Christ the message/ that salvation was by faith rather than by keeping the Law
perfectly (Galatians <b>3</b>:10-14)—which is what circumcision implicitly
committed a person to do by identifying him with Mosaism—and that under Messiah
God’s people no longer had a national identity marked by circumcision. Relationship
to God was available to Gentiles as Gentiles—the “mystery,” as Paul calls it in
Ephesians <b>3</b>:1-13ff. This latter insight Paul would not have gotten from
Peter because Paul had no contact with him and Peter himself did not understand
that point until Cornelius’ conversion (Acts <b>10</b>:<u>34-35</u>-47). Cornelius
was converted after Paul (Acts <b>9</b>) and before the 14/17 years elapsed
when Paul finally had some significant contact with the Jerusalem apostles
(Galatians <b>1</b>:18-<b>2</b>:10). His indirect knowledge of what <i>“Jesus
began to do and to teach”</i> (Acts <b>1</b>:1) would fit with the fact that
Paul’s companion Luke interviewed <i>“eyewitnesses and ministers of the word”</i>
(Luke <b>1</b>:1-4) in writing his gospel (? while Paul was incarcerated in
Caesarea for two years) instead of depending simply on the testimony of Paul. “Tradition,”
then, may have been a source of Pauline material as to facts, but the <u>meaning</u>
of those facts did not come to him that way.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            G.  Christian hymns</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  The following texts in Paul are
among those identified as early Christian hymns or parts of hymns: Romans <b>1</b>:2-4;
<b>11</b>:33-35; Ephesians <b>1</b>:3-14;<b> 2</b>:19-22;<b> 5</b>:14;
Philippians<b> 2</b>:6-11; Colossians <b>1</b>:15-20; 1 Timothy <b>1</b>:17;<b>
3</b>:16;<b> 6</b>:15-16; 2 Timothy<b> 2</b>:11-13; Titus<b> 3</b>:4-7 (Hebrews
<b>1</b>:1-3/4; cp. John <b>1</b>:1-14; 1 Peter <b>1</b>:3-5, 18-21; <b>2</b>:21-25;
<b>3</b>:18-22;<b> 4</b>:6;<b> 5</b>:5-9; 2 Peter <b>2</b>:21-25a; see New <i>ISBE</i>,
Vol. II, pp.788-90 + bib.; Ralph Martin, <i>Worship in the Early Church</i>,
pp. 39-52 + fnts.). Scholars consider stylistic construction, theological
content, and unusual vocabulary as marks of Christian hymnody (see Martin, <i>Worship</i>,
p. 51). </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  Incorporating early Christian hymns
into scripture presents no difficulty, and musical creations were springing up
in the Christian community of Paul’s day as has happened in every Christian
renewal movement since then (“hymns” and “spiritual songs” in Ephesians <b>5</b>:19;
Colossians <b>3</b>:16-17; cp. ? Acts <b>16</b>:25 + 29-31). But caution is
appropriate for other reasons. <b>(1)</b> The fourteen hymnic texts in
Revelation are often too specialized for general use and too integral to their
settings to have originated separately and later been incorporated into the
Apocalypse. The book presents them as what John heard, so they must represent
his experience in the “spirit” (<b>4</b>:8, 11; <b>5</b>:9-10, 12, 13; <b>7</b>:12;
<b>11</b>:15, 17-18; <b>12</b>:10-12; <b>14</b>:7; <b>15</b>:3-4; <b>19</b>:1-2,
5, 6-8). If such a thing can occur in Revelation, it can occur in Paul.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  <b>(2)</b> Furthermore, specialists
have enough trouble identifying the limits of Paul’s hymns that we wonder how
easily they can be distinguished from their surroundings. Conceptually and even
grammatically, they flow so well out of and back into the context that it seems
easier to credit one author with writing the whole thing. 1 Timothy <b>6</b>:15-16
is hardly separable from or more stylistic than <b>6</b>:11-14, with which it
forms a seamless expression. Paul shifted into poetic phrasing to give an
aesthetic touch for more striking effect. <b>(3)</b> Some hymns are not very
complete units that could work as free-standing pieces. <b>(4) </b>If they <u>do</u>
form a unit, they are often too short to be songs (1 Timothy <b>1</b>:17; <b>3</b>:16).
</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  <b>(5)</b> Internally the hymns do
not represent a highly structured genre. They have no rhyme scheme and little
that could be called meter. Their balanced construction is not much more than
the Hebrew parallelism Paul grew up with and studied under Gamaliel. The format
of these hymns is free enough for a writer like Paul to produce. This kind of
expression could probably have been spontaneous as a good preacher does on
occasion. Spontaneous rap and traditional African-American oratory show that
the “hymn” phenomenon in the writings of Paul is not something more than what
he could have inserted with his own hand.<span style='position:relative;
top:-3.0pt'>5</span> They are hardly more elaborate than the <u>salutations</u>
(Galatians <b>1</b>:3-4; cp. Revelation <b>1</b>:4b-6), “<u>prayers</u>” (1 Thessalonians
<b>3</b>:11-13), <u>benedictions</u> (2 Corinthians <b>13</b>:14; 1
Thessalonians<b> 5</b>:23; 2 Thessalonians <b>2</b>:16-17; cp. Hebrews <b>13</b>:20-21),
and <u>doxologies</u> that mark culmination points he punctuates with “Amen”
(Romans <b>11</b>:33-36; <b>16</b>:25-27; Ephesians<b> 3</b>:20-21; 1 Timothy <b>1</b>:17;<b>
6</b>:15-16; cp. Jude 24-25). How is Ephesians <b>2</b>:19-22 more stylistic,
theological, and linguistically peculiar than <b>4</b>:4-7? Not everything that
has an artistic flair comes from another source.<span style='position:relative;
top:-3.0pt'>6</span> Grosheid, <i>1 Corinthians, </i>66, ¶3, suggests that 1
Corinthians <b>2</b>:9 is a hymn quoted by Paul rather than a fresh combination
of Old Testament phraseologies. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            H.  Christian sayings</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  Five times in the pastorals Paul
speaks of faithful sayings: 1 Timothy <b>1</b>:15; <b>3</b>:1; <b>4</b>:(8-) 9 (-10);
2 Timothy <b>2</b>:11-13; Titus <b>3</b>:8. Commentators typically understand
these as references to slogans circulating in the church. That could be so, but
it may be that Paul means something less formal, perhaps a saying of his own or
a comment that just came to mind. These comments could have the force of
saying, <i>“A good way to say it is . . . .”</i></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            I.    Catechisms/creeds/confessions of
faith/baptismal liturgies</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  Some texts called “hymns” above are
also considered parts of confessions. Double classification can happen because
confessions can be put to music. In some cases, these hymn-confessions commentators
consider baptismal liturgies because confessions relate to becoming a
Christian. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  Ralph Martin considers 1 Timothy <b>3</b>:16
<i>“a clear instance of an early confession of faith”</i> (<i>Worship</i>, p.
53). 1 Corinthians <b>15</b>:3-5 is regularly considered a creedal statement
along with Ephesians <b>5</b>:25-26, Philippians <b>2</b>:11, and a number of
similar passages, including Acts <b>8</b>:37. While the evidence brought
forward to support that thesis is interesting, we come away wondering whether
it would not be more natural to suppose that later confessions and baptismal
ceremonies formalized these earlier descriptions instead of supposing that the
earlier material itself was formal.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            J.   Unknown sources</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  Paul introduces Ephesians<b> 5</b>:14
with a citation formula—the same one he uses in <b>4</b>:8, but the source is
unknown despite a similarity to several Old Testament references (Isaiah <b>26</b>:19;
<b>51</b>:17; <b>52</b>:1; <b>60</b>:1; Malachi <b>4</b>:2): <i>“Wherefore
he/it says, ‘Arise, sleeping-one, and arise from the dead, and Christ will
shine on you.’”</i> Martin considers it a snatch from an early baptismal hymn (<i>Worship</i>,
p. 104), an assessment cautiously approved by F. F. Bruce (<i>Epistle to the
Ephesians</i> [Pickering &amp; Inglis, 1961], p. 108).<span style='position:
relative;top:-3.0pt'>7</span> </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>II. Non-Content Sources of Pauline Material </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            A.  Creative Thought</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  1.  Paul uses creative thought to
interpret. He derives theological insight from previous scripture. Earlier we
noted his extensive use of the Old Testament and his use of those texts for more
than information he repeats. Beyond direct exegetical data, he infers things
that are not said in the text itself and combines truths from different places
to produce inferences. Galatians <b>3</b>:7-14 combines five passages to make
his most succinct statement of salvation by faith as per Habakkuk<b> 2</b>:4. Direct
revelation is not necessarily occurring here; yet the Old Testament is not the
direct source either. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  2.    Paul uses creative thought to
make practical applications. He gives inspired advice in 1 Corinthians <b>7</b>:8-9,
12-40 <i>vs.</i> divine commandment in <b>7</b>:10-11 (cp. Peter’s “I perceive”
in Acts <b>10</b>:34). As used here, “advice” differs from “commandment” in
that advice does not necessarily imply disobedience if it is not taken. Like
wisdom literature in the Old Testament, advice concerns what is best, not just what
is morally mandated. Sometimes there is more than one way to carry out what fundamental
truth requires. Choosing any one of these options is good; the role of the
Spirit under such circumstances is not positively to command the one way to
operate, but to prevent a writer like Paul from giving directives that would
fall outside the boundaries set by first principles (cp. ? Matthew <b>16</b>:19;
<b>18</b>:15-20).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            B.  Rhetorical Style</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>      </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  Many times, Paul uses Old Testament
concrete items to picture New Testament spiritual truths. In Jewish style he
often presents the abstract in concrete form. Some of his work has the shape of
Jewish midrash, where Old Testament events and persons are used to picture
spiritual truths they do not themselves necessarily mean: </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            the Hagar-Sarah allegory (Galatians <b>4</b>:21-31),
</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            the Christ-Adam parallel (Romans <b>5</b>:12-21;
1 Corinthians <b>15</b>:21-23, 45-59), </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            episodes in the lives of the patriarchs
(Romans <b>9</b>:6-13), </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:1.0in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify;text-indent:-.25in'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>several items associated with the exodus from Egypt
and the wilderness wanderings (Romans <b>9</b>:14-18; 1 Corinthians <b>10</b>:1-11;
2 Corinthians <b>3</b>:3-18; <b>8</b>:15). Similarly, he appeals to </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:1.0in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify;text-indent:-.25in'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>Christ’s death-burial-resurrection-ascension (Romans <b>6</b>:1-14;
Galatians <b>2</b>:20; <b>6</b>:14; Colossians <b>2</b>:12-15; <b>3</b>:1-5). </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:1.0in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify;text-indent:-.75in'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>There is an immense amount of other illustrative
material as well: </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:1.0in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify;text-indent:-.25in'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>the Olive Tree Parable (Romans<b> 11</b>:16-24), </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:1.0in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify;text-indent:-.25in'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>the building imagery (Romans <b>9</b>:32-33; <b>15</b>:20;
1 Corinthians <b>3</b>:9-15; Ephesians <b>2</b>:10; <b>3</b>:20-22), </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:1.0in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify;text-indent:-.25in'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>the Greek games (1 Corinthians <b>9</b>:24-27; 2 Timothy
<b>4</b>:5-7), </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:1.0in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify;text-indent:-.25in'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>adoption (Romans <b>7</b>:15, 23; Galatians <b>4</b>:5-6),
</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:1.0in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify;text-indent:-.25in'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>marriage (Romans <b>7</b>:2-4), </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:1.0in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify;text-indent:-.25in'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>the family, </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:1.0in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify;text-indent:-.25in'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>agriculture (1 Corinthians <b>3</b>:6-8; Galatians <b>6</b>:7-10),
</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:1.0in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify;text-indent:-.25in'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>baptism (1 Corinthians <b>6</b>:11; Ephesians <b>5</b>:26;
Titus <b>3</b>:5), </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:1.0in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify;text-indent:-.25in'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>the physical body (Romans <b>12</b>:3-8; 1 Corinthians
<b>12</b>:12-30; <b>10</b>:16-17; Galatians<b> 2</b>:9; Ephesians <b>3</b>:6; <b>4</b>:4,
16-17; 1 Timothy <b>3</b>:15), and much more. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>The amount of such imagery is overwhelming, and it is
interwoven in ways that nearly forbid analysis. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            The use of Greek rhetorical antecedents in
Paul is a study in itself. For details and proposals, see George A. Kennedy, <i>Classical
Rhetoric and Its Christian and Secular Tradition from Ancient to Modern Times</i>
(U. of N. Carolina Press, 1980, especially pp. 120-60). </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>III. Reasons for the Use of Sources in Pauline
Material </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            Christians hesitate to suppose that New
Testament writers used sources because using sources suggests (a) <u>gaining
information</u>. Except for previous revelation and direct revelation, that
could remove the origin of the Christian message from New Testament writers to somewhere
else and reduce it from divine to human. Even if sources were used for that
reason, there would be no problem as long as the Spirit is exercising his
protective-permissive role.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            Most non-canonical “sources”—even the
questionable ones— Pauline does not use for getting content so much as for (b) <u>establishing
a point of contact with the audience</u>. His references to the Athens altar
inscription and to Aratus in the Areopagus speech are examples.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            Furthermore, such “sources” are not for
getting content, but for (c) <u>expressing content in memorable ways</u>. A
good example is Paul’s adaptation of Gnostic imagery and terminology when he
contrasts Christianity with Gnosticism in Colossians <b>1-2</b>. He uses the
gnostic terms for the elements that most closely correspond to Gnosticism.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;
margin-bottom:0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:center'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>Concluding Observations</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            The content, hermeneutics, and form of
presentation in scripture should fit the claims scripture makes for itself. We
find no sources, proposed sources, or ways of using them in Paul that are
incompatible with God-breathedness. The content sources for his letters are
primarily Old Testament scripture, direct revelation, and creative thought
protected by the Spirit. He makes secondary use of non-canonical Jewish
writings and a few pagan citations. He appeals to canonical materials and
direct revelation for content and authority, and uses extra-canonical sources
as vehicles of expression. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            The Jewish and pagan writings could be
called “Class 1” sources, because they are <u>extant</u> pieces that can be
verified. We hesitate, however, to buy into the plethora of other “sources”
behind the finished products. “Class 2” sources are <u>imaginary</u>: tradition,
hymns, sayings, confessions, catechisms, liturgies. Virtually every notable
comment Paul makes could be taken from him. This strikes us as literary method “gone
to seed.” When we compile all the Old Testament quotations, hymns, sayings, and
so forth, Paul’s letters become conglomerates of sources more than living
compositions. Somewhere between extremes ought to lie a “golden mean.” The
apostle could have incorporated references to any such source, but affirming
that he did so here or there, is another thing, especially in the frequency that
scholars often suggest.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            A prominent plague in biblical studies is
the attempt to claim more than the evidence warrants, manifested in the
tendency to technicalize and formalize. As linguistic studies load biblical
terms and phrases with too much precision, so also literary criticism is too
wooden. It undersells creativity and spontaneity. (a) <u>Modern literary
critics tend to project onto their objects of study the procedures they use
when they write</u>, and they are largely “combining minds” rather than “creative
minds.” But creative minds are what launch new movements like Christianity. (b)
<u>The style of criticism correlates with developmentalism more than revelation
as the explanation of Christian origins</u>. The evolutionary mindset that
pervades Western assumptions, when applied to literary criticism, overdoes the
notion that writings are products of process. Not enough credit goes to what
gifted people can do on their own “from scratch,” much less what revelation
might add. There were background influences on Paul’s letters, but that does
not mean he is reflecting a source at every juncture. Even if he were, we could
not detect that with much assurance.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            As a final note, not all the proposed “sources”
in Class 2 are equally probable. Early hymns and sayings are more likely than
baptismal liturgies, catechisms, and confessions. In regard to the latter, (c) <u>scholars
are projecting back into first-generation Christianity the forms that emerged a
couple generations later in the second century and beyond</u>. These are apt to
be anachronisms. We have no reason to suppose that such usages were in the
church in the apostolic era. They belong to an age of crystallization and
formalization rather than to the flexible period of a vital new movement.</span></p>

style='font-size:12.0pt'>            <span style='position:relative;top:-3.0pt'>1</span>In
1 Corinthians <b>11</b>:2 and 2 Thessalonians<span style='position:relative;
top:-3.0pt'> </span><b>2</b>:15; <b>3</b>:6, Paul does talk about delivering “traditions”

where Tarsus became his home base until moving to Antioch (Acts <b>11</b>:25-26).
From Paul’s comments in Galatians <b>1</b>, we could conjecture that his time
in Arabia was spent in study, thought, and receiving revelation from Christ
because he puts his flight to Arabia in contrast to conferring with earlier
apostles. Even before fleeing to Arabia, he could integrate his new experience
with Old Testament teaching and show that Jesus was the Messiah, the Son of God
(Acts <b>9</b>:20 + 22).</span></p>
