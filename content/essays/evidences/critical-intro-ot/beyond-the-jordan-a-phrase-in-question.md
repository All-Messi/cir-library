<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                   <i>“Beyond
the Jordan”</i> is a recurring biblical phrase that requires detailed study. It
has relevance to locating unknown places like <i>“the threshing floor of Atad”</i>
in Genesis <b>50</b>:10, 11. In this reference <i>“beyond the Jordan” </i>occurs
twice as an explanatory note for the position of the threshing floor.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                  Questions
arise as to whether the geographical note carries a consistent meaning, what
that usage is, whether more than one consistency may be involved, and whether
there are sufficient clear usages to determine its significance in ambiguous
cases.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                  In
narrative sections, <i>“beyond the Jordan”</i> could be opposite the author,
opposite the anticipated destination of the writing, or opposite the preceding
event. In dialogue it could mean opposite to the speaker. Narrative or dialogue
could use the term in a semi-technical way for just one side like Transjordan
in more modern times. A survey of the data may determine whether any of these
possibilities hold true.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                  Although
several words in the three biblical languages involve <i>beyond</i> in their
translation,</span><b><sup><span style='font-size:16.0pt;line-height:115%;
font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>1</span></sup></b><span style='font-size:
12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'> only prepositions
belong to this study. Verbs with <i>beyond</i> in their English equivalents are
verbs of motion; so “beyond” means direction rather than location. Since this
inquiry deals with the “location” of what is <i>“beyond the Jordan,”</i> only
prepositions appear here.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                  This
analysis provides the following conclusions. First, </span><i><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>“beyond
the Jordan”</span></i><span style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;
font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'> may refer to territories on the east or
west side of El Ghor. Numbers <b>34</b>:15 refers to Transjordan; Deuteronomy <b>11</b>:30
designates the western side. So, in the Old Testament </span><span lang=HE
dir=RTL style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>ע</span><span
lang=HE dir=RTL style='font-size:14.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>ֵבֶר־הַיַּרְדֵּן</span><span
dir=LTR></span><i><span lang=HE style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;
font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'><span dir=LTR></span> </span></i><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>is
not a quasi-technical term for one side.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                  Secondly,
the phrase does not speak relative to the location of the writer or reader.
Deuteronomy<b> 4</b>:41 speaks of </span><i><span style='font-size:12.0pt;
line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>“beyond the Jordan”</span></i><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>
toward the East; <b>11</b>:30 uses it for territory west of Palestine. The same
is true of Joshua <b>1</b>:15 and <b>5</b>:1, and 1 Chronicles <b>6</b>:78 and <b>26</b>:30.
In fact, every book with more than three usages has references to both sides.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                  The
presence of special direction indicators</span><b><sup><span style='font-size:
16.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>7</span></sup></b><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'> may
imply that the term is ambiguous. Of the forty-four times </span><i><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>“beyond
the Jordan”</span></i><span style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;
font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'> occurs, eighteen add direction notes.
However, Hebrew tends to be redundant, as evidenced in Numbers <b>34</b>:15: </span><i><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>“eastward,
toward the sunrising.” </span></i><span style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:
115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>Nevertheless, these cases should be
kept in mind.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                  Besides
the fact that </span><span lang=HE dir=RTL style='font-size:14.0pt;line-height:
115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>עֵבֶר־הַיַּרְדֵּן</span><span
dir=LTR></span><i><span lang=HE style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;
font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'><span dir=LTR></span> </span></i><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>can
label either side of the Jordan (and so is not quasi-technical or opposite to
the writer or reader), Isaiah <b>18</b>:1 speaks of </span><i><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>“the
land . . . beyond the rivers of Ethiopia”</span></i><span style='font-size:
12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>; hence, </span><span
lang=HE dir=RTL style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>עֵבֶר</span><span
dir=LTR></span><span lang=HE style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;
font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'><span dir=LTR></span> </span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>may
have an east-west or north-south orientation, depending on some viewpoint.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                  </span><i><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>“Beyond
the Jordan”</span></i><span style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;
font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'> could be governed by considerations
within the writing itself. The viewpoint of persons in the text might inform a

writings can be divided between narrative and dialogue. The following summary
organizes the forty-four occurrences of </span><span lang=HE dir=RTL
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>ע</span><span
lang=HE dir=RTL style='font-size:14.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>ֵבֶר־הַיַּרְדֵּן</span><span
dir=LTR></span><span style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'><span
dir=LTR></span> to determine whether the expression indicates the location of
events in the narrative and whether the speakers in dialogue reflect their
position by how they employ the phrase.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                  This
chart shows twenty-six occurrences of the phrase in narrative sections and
eighteen in dialogue. In the narrative portions, twelve times the phrase refers
to the side opposite the events recorded and twelve times to the same side,
with two uncertain examples. There is also no uniformity in the dialogue
sections: seven designate the same side as the speaker and ten designate the
opposite side, with one unclear example. Nothing evidently feels incongruous
about Moses’ standing on the east side of the Jordan and saying of Bashan and
Gilead, </span><i><span style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:
"Times New Roman",serif'>“. . . the two tribes and the half-tribe have received
their inheritance beyond the Jordan at Jericho eastward, toward the sunrising”</span></i><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>
(Numbers <b>34</b>:15; American Standard Version). Most striking is Numbers <b>32</b>:19,
where the Reubenites, Gadites, and the half-tribe of Manasseh tell Moses, </span><i><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>“.
. . we will not inherit with them beyond Jordan, and forward; because our
inheritance has fallen to us beyond Jordan eastward.”</span></i><b><sup><span
style='font-size:16.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>9</span></sup></b><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>
Here the term is used in the same verse to indicate the west side then the east
side.  </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                  </span><span
lang=HE dir=RTL style='font-size:14.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>עֵבֶר־הַיַּרְדֵּן</span><span
dir=LTR></span><span lang=HE style='font-size:14.0pt;line-height:115%;
font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'><span dir=LTR></span> </span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>is
used for both sides, and so is not quasi-technical nor spoken from the
viewpoint of the reader or writer. It occurs in both dialogue and narrative for
the opposite side as well as the same side. Consequently, </span><i><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>“beyond
the Jordan”</span></i><span style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;
font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'> is not the prime meaning of this phrase.
In keeping with this fact, Gesenius gives “region beyond” as the meaning of </span><span
lang=HE dir=RTL style='font-size:14.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>עֵבֶר</span><span
dir=LTR></span><span style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'><span
dir=LTR></span>, because the word is first considered a masculine <u>noun</u>,
which when put in the construct becomes </span><i><span style='font-size:12.0pt;
line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>“region of the Jordan,”</span></i><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>
or </span><i><span style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>“region
of the Arnon.” </span></i><span style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;
font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>The word becomes a <u>preposition</u>
meaning </span><i><span style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:
"Times New Roman",serif'>“at, by, near, around.” </span></i><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>The
cognate Hebrew <u>verb</u> </span><span lang=HE dir=RTL style='font-size:12.0pt;
line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>עָבַר</span><span
dir=LTR></span><i><span lang=HE style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;
font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'><span dir=LTR></span> </span></i><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>means
“to cross over,” being related to the Arabic root meaning </span><i><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>“to
cross a river.”</span></i><span style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;
font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'> All the evidence from related words and
popular usage indicates that “near” or “beside”—rather than “beyond”—is the prime
value of the word.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span

</span><span lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>Ὶορδάνου</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>
</span><span style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>does
show consistent usage. It always designates the eastern side</span><b><sup><span

<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='text-align:center'><span

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify'><span lang=HE dir=RTL
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                   
עֵבֶר־הַיַּרְדֵּן</span><span style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;
font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>is a recurrent Old Testament expression

the Jordan River. The phrase designates both sides of the Jordan in narrative
and dialogue. The corresponding New Testament phrase uniformly means
Transjordan except in Matthew<b> 4</b>:15, which quotes the Greek translation
of Isaiah <b>9</b>:1</span></p>
