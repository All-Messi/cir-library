style='font-size:14.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>THE
DATE OF THE EVENTS IN JOB</span></b></p>

<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='text-align:center'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>Virgil

<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:center;
line-height:normal'><span style='font-size:12.0pt;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='text-align:center'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>Introduction</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='text-align:justify;text-indent:.5in'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>Attempting
to determine the date of the events in Job proceeds assumes that the book is
more than a piece of literary art. The poetic form of most of the book has led
some to regard it as less than historical, but form does not speak to
historicity. Writers use poetry to tell the events at the Red Sea (Exodus <b>15</b>)
and the story of Sisera’s defeat (Judges <b>5</b>). Prose fiction, on the other
hand, appears constantly in modern authors. Whereas skillful writers of
contemporary literature do produce remarkable works about fictitious people and
happenings, it is doubtful whether that happened among the ancients. Their
literature appears to have been at least a development of some kernel of
historical truth, including the sophisticated poetry extant from the later
Greek tragedians. Furthermore, Job is both poetry <u>and prose</u>, so the idea
that poetry points to fiction and prose to non-fiction does not apply
consistently.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
.5in'><span style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>A
study of the date of the events in Job, however, proceeds on more than the
supposition that Job contains only a kernel of truth, because without the
evidence from internal <u>details</u> no solution of the dating problem is
possible.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
.5in'><span style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>Fiction,
when written with a judicious choice of details, can attain a high degree of
verisimilitude, and someone uninformed about the author’s intention may be
unable to distinguish it from a serious account of actual occurrences. But
anyone who views Job from a biblical perspective must view it, not only in a
way consistent with its own contents, but in conformity with the attitude
toward it by inspired writers. From Ezekiel <b>14</b>:14, 20 it seems clear
that Job was as historical a person as Noah and Daniel, with whom he appears in
the words of God himself:</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;text-align:justify'><span lang=HE dir=RTL style='font-size:
12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>            </span><span
dir=LTR></span><i><span style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:
"Times New Roman",serif'><span dir=LTR></span>“</span></i><i><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>And
the word of Jehovah came unto me, saying, </span></i><i><span style='font-size:
12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>‘</span></i><i><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>Son
of man, when a land sins against me by committing a trespass and I stretch out
my hand upon it and break the staff of the bread thereof and send famine upon
it and cut off from it man and beast, though these three men, Noah, Daniel, and
Job were in it, they would deliver but their own souls by their righteousness,’
says the Lord Jehovah . . .’ Or if I send a pestilence into that land and pour
out my wrath upon it in blood to cut off from it man and animal, though Noah,
Daniel, and Job were in it, as I live,’ says the Lord Jehovah, ‘they should
deliver neither son nor daughter; they should but deliver their own souls by
their righteousness’”</span></i><span style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;
font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'> (Ezekiel <b>14</b>:12-14, 19-20; cp.
Jeremiah <b>15</b>:1).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
.5in'><span style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>With
these passages as the deciding factor, an interpreter observes that Job names
an appropriate number of places and peoples, and speaks of several events, so
the historical setting is fairly definite, especially in light of the fact that
the book consists almost completely of poetic dialogue devoted to a religious
and philosophical problem: the suffering of the righteous. Extra-biblical
evidence from Babylonian and Arabic literature and tradition confirms the
conclusion that an ancient historical figure named Job lived somewhere close to
the Arabian Desert.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
.5in'><span style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>The
mention of a nation or tribe must succeed the ancestor it descended from. By
investigating the biblical date for the sources of the people Job names, we can
establish a <i>terminus a quo</i> for the date of the person Job within a
reasonable span of years after the least ancient forefather. We can establish something
of a <i>terminus ad quem</i> by the great age of Job, since he lived much
longer than people in the later millennia. Confirmation for the solution those
arguments yield appears in the cultural situation evident in the book and in
the ancient additions to the book in the Septuagint and Arabic translations. The
study concludes with an evaluation of alternate suggestions. See the summarizing
charts below.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify;text-indent:

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                  B.  Conclusion</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                        1.   The
latest names come from the fourth generation after Abraham: Teman and Uz. If we
allow two generations after Teman for the writer to speak of Temanites, the <i>terminus
a quo</i> for Job is some sixty or seventy years before the second generation
after Teman. He must have been about seventy when Eliphaz the Temanite came to
visit him. (See Chart A.)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                        2.   Zerah
in Genesis <b>36</b>:33 may equal Zerah son of Reuel in <b>36</b>:13, 17. Keil
and Delitzsch note that the kings of Edom listed in <b>36</b>:31-39 are
probably contemporary rulers over the princes listed previously.</span><b><sup><span
style='font-size:16.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>5</span></sup></b><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'> Teman
was then a cousin to Zerah, whose son Jobab was a king of Edom succeeded by
Hushan <i>“of the land of the Temanites”</i> (<b>36</b>:34; cp.  1 Chronicles <b>1</b>:45);
consequently, by about two generations after Teman there were not only
Temanites, but a <i>“land of the Temanites.”</i></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                        3.   The
conclusion seems to be that Job was an early contemporary of the seventh
generation after Abraham. (See Chart B.) That conclusion accords with a
possible mention of the flood (<b>38</b>:8), the existence of writing (<b>13</b>:26;
<b>19</b>:23; <b>31</b>:35), and the absence of anything associated with the
kingdom period in Israel. Job<b> 18</b>:15 may refer to the destruction of
Sodom and Gomorrah.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>         II.
<i>Terminus ad quem </i>(latest time)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                  A.  Evidence</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                        1.   Job’s
great age</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoListParagraph style='margin:0in;text-align:justify;text-indent:
-.25in'><span style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>a.<span
style='font:7.0pt "Times New Roman"'>&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; </span></span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>Masoretic
text</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                                    (1)    <b>42</b>:16</span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>.</span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'> The
Hebrew original closes the account of Job by saying that he lived 140 years
“after that.”</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                                    (2)    <b>29</b>:1-25.
In Job <b>29</b>, he <i>“went forth to the gate into the city . . . I prepared
my seat in the street.”</i> In other words, he was an elder of the city, a
position held by the older men of a town (cp. 11-17). Verse 8 speaks of even
the aged men rising up and standing when he arrived; the princes did the same. Since
these words refer to a time before his calamities, a number of years add to the
140 in <b>42</b>:16. (Note in contrast <b>14</b>:1ff).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                                    (3)    <b>12</b>:1-25
and<b> 30</b>:1? Were Job’s friends also judges (<b>12</b>:20), but younger
than he (<b>30</b>:1)?</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                                    (4)    <b>1</b>:2-4.
At the time of Job’s affliction, he already had ten grown children. If the
youngest of these was fifteen, the oldest would have been nearly thirty. If Job
married by age thirty (note: there is nothing about his own sons’ being married
at that age), he would have been sixty or more by the time Satan tried him. According
to this evidence, Job must have been over two hundred years old when he died
(140 + 60).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                              b.   Septuagint</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                                    (1)    The
Septuagint reads “170” instead of “140” in <b>42</b>:16. The reason for the
difference is that the Hebrew says, <i>“After <u>that</u> he lived 140 years,”</i>
which immediately follows the account about receiving double all the cattle he
had before as well as seven more sons and three daughters. The Septuagint says,
<i>“After that he lived 140 years,”</i> which immediately follows the account
about receiving double all the cattle he had before as well as seven more sons
and three daughters. The Septuagint says, <i>“After <u>the plague</u> he lived
170 years.”</i> The time for regaining his property and ten grown children was
thirty years, a realistic figure.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                                    (2)    An
added statement says that <i>“he lived two hundred (and) forty-eight years.”</i>
These figures make him seventy-eight at the time of the tragedy (248 minus 170),
a conclusion in accord with the internal evidence of the Masoretic text. Job
lived nearly 250 years. In other words, he received double in all things
(except the number of children), including the length of his life.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                        2.   Job’s
priestly function</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                        Job
acted as a priest for his sons (<b>1</b>:5) and for his friends at God’s
command (<b>42</b>:7-10). Such an activity confirms that he lived during the
patriarchal dispensation, where the family head carried out the priestly
function. In this respect he resembles Noah, Abraham, Isaac, Jacob, Jethro, and
Melchizedek. (Note the enigmatic statement in <b>12</b>:19, where God leads “priests
away stripped”; could he be referring to himself as a judge-king-priest?)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                              a.
  absence of a central shrine</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                              b.   absence
of a priesthood</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                        3.   God’s
direct speaking</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                              God’s
speaking to Job out of the whirlwind (<b>38</b>:1) is reminiscent of his
speaking to Moses from the burning bush. To Moses God spoke directly, but to
others he spoke in a dream or a vision (Numbers <b>12</b>:5-8). Is it possible
to say that after the time of Moses God ceased speaking “face to face,” but
always through visions, dreams, Urim and Thummim, and so on, and that before
this time divine communication was more direct? Was Moses the last patriarch
defined in terms of direct speaking? If so, the events in Job suit a pre-Mosaic
era better.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                        4.   Job’s
faith</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                              a.   God’s
description of him in<b> 1</b>:18, <i>“none like him in all the earth,”</i>
seems not only to be a note of tremendous praise, but</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                              b.   an
indication of the breadth of scope in God’s religious dealings with people: <i>“<u>all</u>
the earth,”</i> not <i>“in all Israel” </i>(Luke <b>7</b>:9); and</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                              c.   may
imply that there were relatively few people that God could use as examples of
righteousness (<b>1</b>:18; 2:3). Perhaps such a circumstance fits better a
time before the servants of Yahveh were greatly multiplied by his dealings with
Israel incorporated as a theocracy.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                        5.   
 </span><span lang=HE dir=RTL style='font-size:14.0pt;line-height:115%;
font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>קְשִׂיטָה</span><span dir=LTR></span><span
lang=HE style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'><span
dir=LTR></span> </span><b><span style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;
font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>42</span></b><span style='font-size:12.0pt;
line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>:11; this monetary unit
is mentioned elsewhere only in Genesis <b>33</b>:19 and Joshua <b>24</b>:32.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                        6.   Representation
of Sabeans and Chaldeans as marauding nomads</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                  B.
The age of men gradually reduced about 70% between the flood and the confusion
of tongues. Noah lived 950 years (Genesis <b>9</b>:29) while Peleg, at the
confusion of tongues, lived 293 years (Genesis <b>11</b>:18). The reduction
continued so that Terah, Abraham’s father, was the last man recorded in
scripture to live more than two hundred years. Abraham lived 175 years (Genesis<b>
25</b>:7-8), Isaac 180 (Genesis<b> 35</b>:25), Jacob 147 (Genesis <b>47</b>:28),
Levi 137 (Exodus<b> 6</b>:16), Joseph 110 (Genesis <b>50</b>:26), Kohath 133
(Exodus <b>6</b>:18), Amram 137 (Exodus <b>6</b>:20), Moses 120. Psalm <b>90</b>:10,
written by Moses, says that a person’s life is x eighty years at most. The
conclusion from the evidence for Job’s living at least two hundred
years—perhaps as much as 248—dates him as early as other evidence allows.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'> III.      
Septuagint addition to the <i>Book of Job</i></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                  A.  Translation</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.5in;text-align:justify'><span style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:
115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>            <i>“This book
is-translated from a-book of the Syrian dwelling in the Land of Ausitis by the
mountains of Idumaea and Arabia. And his name was-formerly Jobab. But taking an
Arabian wife, he begat a son whose name [was] Ennon, and his father was Zerah,
a son of the sons of Esau, and from the city of Bozrah so that he was fifth
from Abraham. And these are the kings who reigned in Edom, where also he-ruled
a country. First [was] Balak, son of Beor, and [the] name of his city was
Dennaba. And after Balak was Jobab, called Job. And after him [was] Asom who
was ruler of the Temanite country. And after him [was] Hadad son of Barad who
cut off Midian in the plain of Moab, Geththaim [was] the name of his city. And
the friends who came to him were Eliphaz, a Temanite king of the sons of Esau,
Bildad the Shuhite, Zophar the Minaian king.”</i></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                  B.  Evaluation
of the testimony</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                        1.
We cannot determine the credibility of this appendix. It appears in the
earliest copies of the Septuagint text; so it traces back to the manuscript
tradition the Septuagint translators used during the second to third centuries
before Christ.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                        2.   There
are some peculiar affinities with Genesis <b>36</b>:31-25:</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                              a.   That
Balak (= Bela son of Beor) was the first king of Edom, a fact not essential to
the apparent purpose of the appendix: the establishment of the source of the <i>Book
of Job</i>. The same might be said of those kings who succeeded Jobab, the man
with whom Job is identified: Asom (= Husham) and Hadad.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                              b.   The
historical notations attach to the kings that are mentioned:</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                                    (1)    The
name of Bela’s city: Dinhabah;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                                    (2)    The
fact that Hadad smote Midian in the plain of Moab</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                              We
might be inclined to write off this supplement as something dependent on the
Genesis account, but</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                        3.   There
are also marks of independence.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                              a.
Whereas the Masoretic text names Avith (</span><span lang=HE dir=RTL
style='font-size:14.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>עֲוִית</span><span
dir=LTR></span><span style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'><span
dir=LTR></span>) as Hadad’s city, the Septuagint reads Geththaim (</span><span
lang=EL style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>Γεθθαιμ</span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                              b.   It
supplies the name of Jobab’s son Ennon.   </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                              c.   It
calls Zophar a Minaian king rather than a Naamathite (see also <b>2</b>:11).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                        4.   Favorable
notations</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                              a.   It
provides a plausible association between Zerah the father of Jobab in Genesis <b>36</b>:33
and Zerah the grandson of Esau in <b>36</b>:13. That identification fits with
Keil and Delitzsch’s contention that the kings of Edom were contemporary
over-rulers of the princes in the earlier verses.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                              b.   It
presupposes that the period had ended during which the listed kings of Edom
ruled prior to the monarchy in Israel, not by the reign of Saul, but by the
time of writing.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                  C.  Testimony
regarding the identity of Job: that he was the Jobab of Genesis <b>36</b>:33.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                        1.   Linguistic
assessment</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                              a.   “Job”
is spelled in Hebrew </span><span lang=HE dir=RTL style='font-size:14.0pt;
line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>אִיּוֹב</span><span
dir=LTR></span><span style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'><span
dir=LTR></span>, one persecuted, as if from the root </span><span lang=HE
dir=RTL style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>אָיַב</span><span
dir=LTR></span><span style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'><span
dir=LTR></span>, to persecute, on the analogy of </span><span lang=HE dir=RTL
style='font-size:14.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>יַלּוֹד</span><span
dir=LTR></span><span lang=HE style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;
font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'><span dir=LTR></span> </span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>one
born, from </span><span lang=HE dir=RTL style='font-size:14.0pt;line-height:
115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>יָלַד</span><span dir=LTR></span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'><span
dir=LTR></span> to bear; or perhaps from </span><span lang=HE dir=RTL
style='font-size:14.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>אוֹב</span><span
dir=LTR></span><span style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'><span
dir=LTR></span>, to turn.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                              b.   “Jobab”
is spelled in Hebrew </span><span lang=HE dir=RTL style='font-size:14.0pt;
line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>יוֹבַב</span><span
dir=LTR></span><span style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'><span
dir=LTR></span>, outcry, from the root </span><span lang=HE dir=RTL
style='font-size:14.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>יָבַב</span><span
dir=LTR></span><span style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'><span
dir=LTR></span>, to cry </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                                    aloud.</span><b><sup><span
style='font-size:16.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>6</span></sup></b></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                              c.   Possible
interpretations of evidence</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                                    (1)    That
the man had two names</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                                    (2)    That
the Genesis and Job accounts are independent transliterations of an Aramaic
original, and hence, do not to trace to seemingly unrelated roots in Hebrew </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                        2.   The
time factor</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                              a.   From
the birth of Jacob and Esau to the death of Moses is 600 years. (Jacob lived
147 years; it was 413 years from his death to the exodus;</span><b><sup><span
style='font-size:16.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>7
</span></sup></b><span style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:
"Times New Roman",serif'>Moses died forty years after the exodus; 147 + 413 =
600). Hadar may be the last-mentioned king of Edom because he was ruling when
Moses wrote. (Tt does not say he died.) If so, the period from Esau’s birth
till the midst of Hadar’s reign must also be 600 years.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                              b.   Reuel
was born to Esau sometime after Esau was forty years old (Genesis <b>36</b>:24).
The time between Reuel’s and Zerah’s births is not given in the scripture, nor
is the time between Zerah’s and Jobab’s (Job’s) births. The generations may,
however, be tentatively set at forty-year intervals, so there are approximately
120 years from the birth of Esau to the birth of Jobab. If Jobab lived 250
years, he would have died 370 years after Esau’s birth, leaving 230 years to be
filled out by the six kings who succeeded him in Edom (Genesis <b>36</b>:34-39).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                              c.   Only
by an extremely long reign like Jobab’s would have been in this scheme, could we
account for the time period that transpired between the birth of Esau and the
death of Moses; that is, if Zerah the father of Jobab is to be identified with
Zerah the grandson of Esau and if Hadar was the king of Edom at Moses’ death (see
Numbers <b>20</b>:14ff), it may be that the two Zerahs are not the same, and
Keil and Delitzsch do not equate them. The Septuagint addition identifies them
because it speaks of Job as being the “fifth from Abraham” (Abraham-Isaac-Esau-Reuel-Zerah-Jobab;
see Chart B).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                  3.   Advantages
and disadvantages of Job’s being identified with Jobab</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                        a.   If
the kings of Edom in Genesis <b>36</b>:34-39 were contemporary rulers over the
princes mentioned 20-30, and if </span><span lang=HE dir=RTL style='font-size:
14.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>שַר</span><span
dir=LTR></span><span style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'><span
dir=LTR></span> (Job <b>29</b>:9) is roughly equal to </span><span lang=HE
dir=RTL style='font-size:14.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>אַלּוּף</span><span
dir=LTR></span><span lang=HE style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;
font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'><span dir=LTR></span> </span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>in
Genesis <b>36</b>, a situation exists where Job is above the princes like Jobab
was (Genesis<b> 36</b>:33).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><i><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>“When
I </span></i><span style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>[Job]<i>
went forth to the gate of the city . . . The princes </i>(</span><span lang=HE
dir=RTL style='font-size:14.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>שַר</span><span
dir=LTR></span><span style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'><span
dir=LTR></span>)<i> refrained from talking and put their hands on their mouths”
</i>(Job <b>29</b>:7a, 9).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                        b.   The
date of Jobab is in harmony with the <i>a quo</i> established by genealogies
and the <i>ad quem</i> established by his age.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                        c.   The
Midianites are smitten in Moab by the second king after Jobab, a fact implying
perhaps an allegiance between the Midianites and the Moabites as in Numbers <b>22</b>.
Hadad’s smiting them would be about 150 years before the event in Numbers <b>22</b>.
On the other hand, the Midianites at the time of Gideon (Judges <b>8</b>:28)
vanished from history. Their presence at the time of Job helps set an <i>ad
quem</i> for the events associated with him.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                        d.   One
disadvantage is the forty-year average reign of the last six kings of Edom:
this number seems high. The judgeships in Judges averaged about half that long.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                        e.   Job
had brothers and sisters (<b>42</b>:11). Genesis and 1 Chronicles do not
mention any brothers of Jobab. The importance of this may not be great,
however, since Jobab is not listed in the genealogical list, which comes down
only to Zerah his father, but mentions him separately, connecting him with the
line of Esau only by naming his father Zerah.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'> IV.
Evaluation of the suggestions of some scholars</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>            </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                  A.  Suggestions</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                        1.   In
the descriptive note beside the references to Job, Robert Young in his <i>Analytical
Concordance</i> speaks of Job as a contemporary of Abraham.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                        2.   <i>The
Pulpit Commentary</i> on Job places him between Eber and Jacob.</span><b><sup><span
style='font-size:16.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>8</span></sup></b></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                        3.   Eusebius
placed him “two ages before Moses,” that is, about the time of </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                              Isaac.</span><b><sup><span
style='font-size:16.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>9</span></sup></b></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                        4.   Gleason
Archer feels that Job could have lived as late as the time of the monarchy in
Israel.<sup>10</sup></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                  B.  Criticisms</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                        1.   Placing
Job before Abraham, or contemporary with him, is difficult to fit with the
mention of later tribal peoples, especially the Temanites, who came four generations
after the great Hebrew patriarch.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                        2.   A
time after Moses does not take due account of Job’s extreme age, which at the
very least would be slightly over two hundred years. Some adjustment to the
force of the argument from age might come if Job’s receiving double included
the length of his life, for in such case he would normally have lived only 120
years, a lifetime compatible with the time of the exodus. Still, he would have
been much older than David, who lived 70 years (1 Kings <b>2</b>:11).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:center'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>Conclusion</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:center'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-bottom:0in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt;line-height:115%;font-family:"Times New Roman",serif'>                  If
the Septuagint’s association of Job with Jobab is valid, Job must have lived
from <i>cir.</i> 1890 to 1640 B.C., barely making him a late contemporary of
Jacob, who lived down to <i>cir</i>. 1860 B.C. At any rate, Job’s birth would
have to come at least as late as the first generation after Teman, that is,
during the fifth generation after Abraham. That is the case, because at the
time of his afflictions (when he was about seventy) Eliphaz the “Temanite”
visited him, and the text would probably not call Eliphaz a Temanite if Teman
was his father. A generation or more probably lies between these two men. A
date during the Egyptian bondage seems safe from these considerations.</span></p>
