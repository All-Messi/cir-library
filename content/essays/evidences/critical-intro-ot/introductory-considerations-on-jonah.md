<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-27.0pt;margin-bottom:

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-27.0pt;margin-bottom:

style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  1.  Usage elsewhere in scripture</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-27.0pt;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-27.0pt;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify;text-indent:
-85.5pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                                     a.
Jesus’ comparison of Jonah’s experience to his own (Matthew <b>12</b>:34-42; </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-27.0pt;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify;text-indent:
-85.5pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                                           <b>16</b>:1-4;
*Luke <b>11</b>:29-<u>32</u>)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-27.0pt;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify;text-indent:
-85.5pt'><span style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-27.0pt;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              (1) <u>Ninevites will
stand up in the judgment</u> and condemn Jesus’ generation because they
repented at Jonah’s preaching, but Jesus’ generation did not. The existence of
men repentant over Jonah’s preaching would have to be the real case, for they
could not otherwise rise up in the judgment with their predicted function.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-27.0pt;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              (2) <u>Jesus used Jonah
as a “sign” of his own resurrection</u>. Depending on the meaning of his
statement, <u>the three-day experience of Jonah</u> in the fish’s stomach would
have to be real to serve as a sign to the contemporaries of our Lord. Jonah’s
experience was primarily a sign to his own generation. Another understanding of
the expression is to see “the sign of Jonah” as a label for the Messiah’s <u>resurrection</u>
rather than a reference to the Old Testament event. Such labeling was used
because of the loose similarity between the two cases. A third approach makes <u>the
Ninevites’ repentance</u> the “sign” (A. J. Glaze, Jr., <i>The Broadman Bible
Commentary</i>, Vol. VII, p. 157).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-27.0pt;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              (3) Jonah’s preaching
and his being in the fish are compared with Jesus' ministry and his being in
the grave.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-27.0pt;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              (4) Jonah is listed
along with the queen of Sheba and King Solomon (Matthew <b>12</b>:42; Luke <b>11</b>:32)
with the implication that he and his experiences were as real as they and their
experiences were. Though points (3) and (4) often appear in literature on this
question, it seems that a comparison does not require both sides of the analogy
to be of the same character. The <u>likeness</u> of the things compared is the
point of a comparison, not necessarily their <u>character</u> (as history,
story, etc.) as well.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-27.0pt;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-27.0pt;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        b. Jonah is mentioned with
Elijah the prophet in 1 Kings <b>17</b>:9-24.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-27.0pt;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        c. He is named alongside
Elisha the prophet (2 Kings <b>5</b>:1-27), who was from the same period with a
mission to the foreign city of Sidon. In response to the implication of
conjoining Jonah with Elijah and Elisha, however, see the note above in (4).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-27.0pt;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        d. There was a historical
prophet by the name of Jonah from the time and place assumed in the Book of
Jonah (2 Kings <b>14</b>:25).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-27.0pt;margin-bottom:

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-27.0pt;margin-bottom:

style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        a. Classification among the prophets</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-27.0pt;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-27.0pt;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              (1) Jonah is classified
among the prophets in the prologue to Ecclesiasticus, written <i>ca</i>. 180 B.C.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-27.0pt;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              (2) In the organization
of the Old Testament canon, Jonah is listed among the prophets, not in the
writings. Historical forces are at work among the prophets since the identity
of God’s spokesman was necessary for evaluating his credentials as a divine
spokesman. The historical principle takes priority in canonicity. A book by an
unknown author (which is likely the case if Jonah did not write it) would
create a problem for acceptance into the Jewish canon.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-27.0pt;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-27.0pt;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        b. Regarded as historical in
antiquity</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-27.0pt;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-27.0pt;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              (1) Tobit <b>14</b>:4</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-27.0pt;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              (2) 3 Maccabees<b> 6</b>:8</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-27.0pt;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              (3) Josephus’ <i>Antiquities

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-27.0pt;margin-bottom:

rationalists and deists in the last century or so.</span></p>

style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-27.0pt;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        a. The account of his call, as
with other prophets (<b>1</b>:1)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-27.0pt;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        b. Historical, geographical,
and other particulars</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-27.0pt;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-27.0pt;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              (1) Tarshish (<b>1</b>:3;
42)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-27.0pt;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              (2) Joppa (<b>1</b>:3)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-27.0pt;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              (3) Nineveh (<b>1</b>:2;
<b>3</b>:2, 5, 7; <b>4</b>:11)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-27.0pt;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              (4) Jonah’s genealogy (<b>1</b>:1)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-27.0pt;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              (5) The population of
Nineveh (<b>4</b>:11)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-27.0pt;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-27.0pt;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              Myth emphasizes
philosophical rather than historical truth. Its ideas are timeless and
placeless because they do not apply to a given setting. As a result, those
matters that localize, such as names and dates, appear more sparingly than they
do in a discrete episode. Jesus’ parables of the Good Samaritan and perhaps the
Rich Man and Lazarus do show that non-historical forms of presentation are not
completely devoid of concretizing elements.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-27.0pt;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-27.0pt;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        c. Under the higher critical
reconstruction of Israel’s religious development, the Book of Jonah as a
literary piece might have been opposed by the “priestly school,” whose
authority it seems to attack (Glaze, <i>Broadman Commentary</i>, Vol. VII, p.
157).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-27.0pt;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-27.0pt;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  4. Literary objections</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-27.0pt;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-27.0pt;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        a. As a “parable” the book is
too long. As an allegory the elements do not line up individually with truths
they might represent. Jonah could perhaps be placed in the form of myth.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-27.0pt;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        b. Nothing indicates the
intention of the author in writing the piece if it is not historical, although
that is true of short stories generally in modern literature.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-27.0pt;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-27.0pt;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            C. Difficulties in Jonah as history</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-27.0pt;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-27.0pt;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  If we consider Jonah as an extended parable
or short story, we are relieved of (a) defending the abundance of miraculous
elements in the book, hence, one motivation for wanting to consider it parabolic.
The main question here is the extent of miraculousness in life. Although God
may intervene from time to time, he does not necessarily cause miraculous
things to happen all around all the time. We could accept the events in Jonah
without supposing that such experience came to everybody throughout the history
of Israel.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-27.0pt;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  Parabolically understood, Jonah does
not commit us to (b) defending a moralizing view of history. As in the previous
matter, however, the kind of events Jonah records do not have to be generalized
as the author’s view of what happens to everybody usually. Whether Jonah
teaches such a view is itself open to question. A thoroughly historical
hermeneutic does not universalize these discrete events. In fact, generalizing
them may reflect an unconscious tendency to apply a literary hermeneutic to
create problems for the historical view. Getting rid of the problem in the case
of Jonah does not eliminate having to deal with the moralizing view itself,
because other prophets are as reasonably charged with teaching the same idea. The
treatment below sketches different ways of handling difficulties urged against
the historical approach to Jonah.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-27.0pt;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-27.0pt;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  1. The gourd that grew up overnight</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-27.0pt;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-27.0pt;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        a. The gourd was a real plant.
Some plants grow rapidly, and a few have been suggested as fulfilling the
requirements here. The <i>Ricimus communic</i> (a castor oil plant) grows as
much as a foot a day.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-27.0pt;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                      *b. <i>“Grew up in a night”</i>
is a hyperbole that stresses its unimportance as indicated by ephemeralness. The
point would be something like Jesus’ statement about <i>“the grass of the
field, which today is and thrown in an oven tomorrow”</i> (Mathew <b>6</b>:30).
A person might say, <i>“He changed overnight.”</i></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-27.0pt;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>      </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-27.0pt;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  2. Expressions involving God’s <i>“preparing
_________________”</i></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-27.0pt;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-27.0pt;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        a. God created a great fish, a
gourd, a worm, a wind (<b>1</b>:17; <b>4</b>:6, 7, 8) as a special creative
act.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-27.0pt;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                       *b. Hebrew idiom assigns God as
the source of things that were naturally produced, although some providential
oversight is evident as to timing and placement. Semitic idiom often assigns
the explanation of effects to their ultimate cause, especially when the more
immediate causes are not evident or visible. <i>“God hardened Pharaoh’s heart”</i>;
<i>“Satan entered into Judas”</i>; Satan filled the heart of Ananias to lie;
yet he conceived in his own heart to lie (Acts <b>5</b>:3-4). God hardened
Pharaoh’s heart; yet Pharaoh hardened his own heart (Exodus <b>10</b>:1; <b>8</b>:15).
The Lord opened Lydia’s heart; yet Paul opened her eyes (Acts <b>26</b>:17b-18).
Quite a few other examples show that such expressions mean ultimate inspiring
source, not miracle.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-27.0pt;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-27.0pt;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  3. The fish’s swallowing Jonah</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-27.0pt;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-27.0pt;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        a. Although most types of
whales could not swallow a man, a sperm whale might [G. Macloskie, <i>Bibliotheca
Sacra</i>, LXXII (1915), 36ff]. Whales, however, are not common in the
Mediterranean Sea.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-27.0pt;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        b. In 1771, a man was reported
to have been swallowed and soon recovered with some damage to his skin [A. J.
Wilson, <i>Princeton Theological Review</i>, (Oct. 1927), pp. 636ff].</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-27.0pt;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        *c. Keil and Delitzsch reported
an incident of a shark (<i>squalus carcharias</i>) that had swallowed a
fifteen-hundred-pound horse. See the Keil-Delitzsch commentary on Jonah for
other examples.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-27.0pt;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                         d. Another incident was
reported by Francis, <i>Sixty-Three Years of Engineering</i>. London: J.
Murray, 1924, pp. 298-300 [cp. <i>Expository Times</i>, XVIII (Feb., 1907), p.
239].</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-27.0pt;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-27.0pt;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  4. The prophet’s unlikely behavior</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-27.0pt;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-27.0pt;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        a.   Prophesying something
that did not happen made him look like a fool: <i>“Sure, sure, we would have
been destroyed.”</i> He knew ahead of time that God would likely do just what
he did (<b>4</b>:2). Announcing the destruction ahead of time may have implied
that it was a conditional prophecy, as in fact the Ninevites interpreted it (<b>3</b>:7-10).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-27.0pt;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        b. Jewish nationalism and
repugnance for the heathen Assyrians might sufficiently explain his disgust for
their not being destroyed.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-27.0pt;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        c. Elijah’s flight to Mount
Horeb (1 Kings <b>21</b>) for fear of Jezebel ended in the same kind of
despondency Jonah manifested.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-27.0pt;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-27.0pt;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  5. Unlikely conversion of the
Ninevites by a foreign prophet</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-27.0pt;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-27.0pt;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        a. The king commanded the
people to repent (<b>3</b>:7); how many would otherwise have displayed that
behavior is another matter.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-27.0pt;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        b. Being a foreign prophet may
have scared the Assyrians more than if he had been one of their own. What
reason would a stranger have for coming from a foreign (enemy) country to make
this prediction unless he was highly driven? The comments of Solzhenitsyn in
his address to the graduating class at Harvard in the spring of 1978 probably
made more of an impression on the American people than if a native “prophet”
had critiqued American culture.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-27.0pt;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        c. The people of Nineveh may
have learned something of the previous calamities that had befallen Jonah. In
the case of the sailor rescued from a whale’s stomach, his face, neck, and
hands were bleached to a deathly whiteness by the chemicals of the whale’s
stomach. If this discoloration occurred with Jonah, his ghastly appearance
would have added effect to his threat.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-27.0pt;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        d. Depending on the date of
Jonah’s mission to Nineveh, the populace of the area would have been
psychologically prepared to heed a message of destruction. During the reign of
Assurdan III (771-54 B.C.) there was a serious plague in 765, a total eclipse
on June 15, 763, and another plague in 759. Jonah’s coming to Nineveh anytime
soon afterward would have had a context of dread to help sensitize the hearts
of the people. Somewhat earlier during the rule of Adad-nirari III (810-783 B.C.),
the king practiced monolatrous worship of the god Nebo, revealing a tendency
toward monotheism that could answer to the setting pictured in the Book of
Jonah.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-27.0pt;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-27.0pt;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  6. Silence regarding such repentance
in Assyrian history</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-27.0pt;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-27.0pt;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        a. Perhaps the occasion may be
subsumed under the religious reforms of Adad-nirari III, 805-782 B.C., as
mentioned above. He restricted worship to one god during his reign.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-27.0pt;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        b. We wonder whether such their
records would omit such an episode because it made the Ninevites look bad.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-27.0pt;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        c. Many events in the ancient
world are not known to moderns from the relatively scanty evidence that remains
(King Sargon, <i>etc</i>.).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-27.0pt;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-27.0pt;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  7.  Nineveh, a city of three days’
journey</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-27.0pt;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-27.0pt;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        a. <i>“An exceedingly great
city of three days’ journey”</i> means that was its size relative to the
mission of Jonah there. It would take three days to go throughout its precincts
and deliver the threat. The time does not indicate how long it would take to
walk straight through the town. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-27.0pt;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        b. The “city of Nineveh”
refers to the administrative district of which it was the center. The Assyrian
term for the district (<i>alninua</i>) would probably translate into Hebrew by
the same word as city (<i>ninuaki</i>) would (R. K. Harrison, <i>Introduction
to the Old Testament,</i> p. 909). The distance across the district was between
thirty and sixty miles, while the walled area of the city proper was about
eight miles in circumference.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-27.0pt;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-27.0pt;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  8. “King” of Nineveh</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-27.0pt;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-27.0pt;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        Such an expression occurs
elsewhere, so it is not objectionable. Ahab, king of Israel, is called “king”
of Samaria in 1 Kings <b>21</b>:1; Benhadad, king of Syria, is referred to as
“king of Damascus” in 2 Chronicles <b>24</b>:23.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-27.0pt;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-27.0pt;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  9. Nineveh <u>was</u></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-27.0pt;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><u><span

0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        a. Inferring that Nineveh no
longer existed is not legitimate in light of Luke <b>24</b>:13: of Emmaus Luke
comments, <i>“. . . which was from Jerusalem about seven miles.”</i></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-27.0pt;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        b. If the Book of Jonah was
written in retrospect by the prophet himself, what he says of it was in keeping
with its size, population, and the like, at the time he was there.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-27.0pt;margin-bottom:

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-27.0pt;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>              10. Population of Nineveh</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-27.0pt;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-27.0pt;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        a. The population is 120,000,
not 600,000 or so. Not knowing their left hand from their right (<b>4</b>:11)
does not have to mean babies so as to imply such a large a number for the total
population. The expression refers to their spiritual ignorance of the true God.
Solomon speaks of himself as but a child (1 Kings <b>3</b>:7) because he felt
unequal to being king over Israel. Similarly, Jeremiah felt overwhelmed by his call
to be a prophet for God: <i>“I am a child”</i> (Jeremiah <b>1</b>:6; cp. Moses
in Exodus <b>4-5</b>).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-27.0pt;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        b. Not only the walled city,
but the administrative district may have been included, especially since the note
is added <i>“and a lot of cattle.”</i> A certain amount of suburbs with
temporary housing could have surrounded the city wall and contributed to the
population count.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-27.0pt;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        c. Ancient cities did not have
the wide streets, open park areas, and so on, that characterize modern cities. The
seemingly large population is not unrealistic. Evidence available from Nimrud,
a city half the size of Nineveh, indicates a population of 69,574 in 879 B.C. (Harrison,
<i>op. cit.,</i> p. 909). <i>New Bible Dictionary</i>, p. 889, estimates from
archaeological remains that Nineveh could house 175,000. Robert T. Boyd, in <i>Tells,
Tombs, and Treasures</i>, p. 140, recounts that 70,000 aristocrats from Nineveh
attended the dedication banquet of King Ashurbanipal II. </span></p>
