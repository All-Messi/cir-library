<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.25in;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:-.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify;text-indent:
.5in'><u>Can we put in sequence the origin of reciprocating parts that are
necessary for the viability of the organism and the parts themselves</u>? If we
can put their origin in sequence, they are not reciprocal. But the parts of
plants and animals are in fact reciprocally interdependent unto viability. All
parts have to be present and functional at all times. Disorganization destroys
functionality.</p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.25in;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:-.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify;text-indent:
.5in'>      </p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.25in;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:-.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'>      Reciprocation
is related to the concept of <u>irreducible minimums</u>, but it adds to it the
origin of complexity and change between levels of complexity; hence, the
mechanisms of change plus hoistic harmony.</p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.25in;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:-.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'>      There
must be a full realization of the degree of complexity involved in viable forms
within individual systems, of the number of such systems, and of the
hierarchical interconnectivity of such interdependent systems. Change at any
level within systems or between systems renders them dysfunctional. The degree
of complexity must not get lost in the thought process; it is typically underappreciated
in evolutionary thought.</p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.25in;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:-.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'>      The
greater the complexity the more difficult the origin of it and the more
difficult the change between complexities with constant viability during the
evolvement.</p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.25in;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:-.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'>      Graduated
change from one to another is not possible whether in the direction of infinity
as with gradualism or with fewer steps as with punctuated equilibrium. Transitional
forms between viable points are not possible because, by definition, they are
not viable. Forms must be continuously viable at every point in the theoretical
process. </p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.25in;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:-.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'>      A
systemic reality exists here. The whole in each case is systemic, comprised of
interedepentdent systems that individually are interdependent systems at many
levels.</p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.25in;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:-.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'>&nbsp;</p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.25in;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:-.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'>      Naturalism
is simpler in having fewer factors, but simpler must also be adequate.</p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.25in;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:-.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'>      Time
itself is not a cause.</p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.25in;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:-.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'>      Random
causation does not create the higher levels of order the biosphere exhibits. </p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.25in;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:-.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'>      Entropy
is in the opposite direction of evolution (endogenous complexification).</p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.25in;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:-.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'>      The
tendency toward selection against (vestigial and) and nascent organs works against
viability and therefore complexification, that is, evolution.</p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.25in;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:-.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'>      Evolution
exhibits a confusion of metaphysics and physics.</p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.25in;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:-.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'>      Evolutuion
confuses science and history. Science deals with repeatable, natural,
observable events. Science and history are overlapping circles/disciplines.</p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.25in;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:-.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'>      Methodological
naturalism has no power to deny causation outside its own realm.</p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.25in;margin-bottom:
0in;margin-left:-.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'>      By the
nature of the case, what is not possible by endogenous causation must be made
possible by exogenous causation. What is not within natural is outside nature;
it is “supernatural.”</p>
