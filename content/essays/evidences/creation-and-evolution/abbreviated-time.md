of scripture as in fiat creationism.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;

style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  1.   Proposed relationships between
Genesis <b>1</b>:1 and <b>1</b>:2ff</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        a.   Genesis <b>1</b>:1 as a caption
for the subsequent creation account</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;
margin-bottom:0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:center'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'><img width=69 height=62
src="/cir-library/images/essays/abbreviated-time/image001.png"></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              Under this view, the
account may provide no statement of absolute origin in the “book of
beginnings.” That would leave open the eternality of matter and, when taken
together with a parabolic understanding of Genesis <b>1-11</b>, would pose no
difficulties for a theistic evolutionary approach. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              The answer to that
concern, however, is that Day 4 seems to cover the origin of everything beyond
earth—sun, moon, and stars; so an absolute beginning <u>does</u> appear in the
account if <b>1</b>:1 serves as a title for everything that follows. Besides,
taking <b>1</b>:1 as a title does not require taking <b>1-11</b> as parabolic.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              Observations against a
title view include the following:</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              (1)    The rest of
scripture teaches creation from nothing. We would expect it here in the Genesis
statement of origins.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify;text-indent:-63.0pt'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                                   (2)    “Bara<b><i>’</i></b>”
(</span><span lang=HE dir=RTL style='font-size:14.0pt'>בָּרַא</span><span
dir=LTR></span><span style='font-size:12.0pt'><span dir=LTR></span>) means to
create something new kind. In reference to the universe, the word implies
creation from nothing.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify;text-indent:-1.5in'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                                                  (3)
   Taking <i>“in the beginning”</i> (</span><span lang=HE dir=RTL
style='font-size:14.0pt'>בְּרֵשִׁית</span><span dir=LTR></span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'><span dir=LTR></span>) as a construct form causes a
rather complex syntax for Hebrew grammar, a syntax that seems out of place in
the simple style in the rest of Genesis <b>1-2</b>.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              (4)    The Masoretic
Hebrew text does not point the verse to have it read “<i>In the beginning of
God’s creating the heaven . . .”</i></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify;text-indent:-1.5in'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                                                  (5)
   All the ancient translations understand the Hebrew as an absolute reference,
notably the Septuagint Greek version that Jewish scholars prepared a couple
centuries before Christ.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>      </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        b.   Gap between Genesis <b>1</b>:1
and <b>1</b>:2 (gap theory; ruin-reconstruction; pre-</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              Adamic cataclysmic
theory, creative re-iterations)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;
margin-bottom:0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:center'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'><img width=159 height=45
src="/cir-library/images/essays/abbreviated-time/image002.png"></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              After a previous form of
existence, the earth <i>“<u>became</u> waste and void</i>” so God started over.
The approach removes all difficulty over the geological remains because it
assigns virtually all that stratigraphy to pre-Adamic circumstances. The
ruin-reconstruction hypothesis makes Adam and Eve comparable to Noah and his
family at the end of the flood. Gap theory rests on observations like the
following:</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify;text-indent:-1.5in'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                                                  (1)
   The expression <i>“the heavens and the earth”</i> appears elsewhere in</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify;text-indent:-1.5in'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                                                           reference
to the completed creation: Isaiah <b>45</b>:18.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              (2)    <i>Hayah</i> (</span><span
lang=HE dir=RTL style='font-size:14.0pt'>הָיָה</span><span dir=LTR></span><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'><span dir=LTR></span>) can mean “became.”</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        c.   Genesis <b>1</b>:1 is a
part of the first day. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              Exodus <b>20</b>:11 and<b>
31</b>:17 speak of God’s creating the heavens and the earth in six days. Were <b>1</b>:1
to precede Day 1, this later statements would be unlikely. The verses from
Exodus, however, may be “loose” statements. (a) Within Genesis <b>1</b> itself,
all the other days begin with the formula <i>“And God said.”</i> That clause
does not appear until verse three, which would lead the reader to make God’s
six days of work a matter of adjusting <i>“waste and void”</i> into a situation
inhabitable for mankind. (b) The rest of the days do not have amounts of
activity comparable to the large-scale creation of the whole universe. Including
<b>1</b>:1-2 in day one would create a disparity in the order of magnitude
within the days.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              How crucial this last
point is depends on whether we feel free to take the “days” of Genesis as topics
of divine activity instead of times of activity. If that appears reasonable,
the “days” do not deal with duration of time.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        d.   Genesis <b>1</b>:1 is
antecedent to <b>1</b>:2ff., yet inclusive of them. </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              Under this schematic
Genesis<b> 1</b>:1 states the completed creation act and then laps back to the
point at which the earth was waste and void, and elaborates the account in a
six-day arrangement; <b>2</b>:4-25 becomes another such lapsing back to
elaborate the last half of what took place in Day 6.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:8.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal align=center style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;
margin-bottom:0in;margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:center'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'><img width=250 height=168
src="/cir-library/images/essays/abbreviated-time/image003.png"></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              (1)    Isaiah <b>45:</b>18
uses <i>“the heavens and the earth”</i> to indicate the mature </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>creation; so we may take Genesis <b>1</b>:1 to mean
the same.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              (2) Genesis<b> 2</b>:4-25
elaborates the last part of Day 6.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              Consequently, the
literary pattern of these two chapters can allow for an indefinite period of
time prior to the “waste and void” condition. Those stages of cosmological
development that preceded <b>1</b>:2 could be of any length. That period could
account for chemical evolution as well as solve the difficulty created by light
traveling for millions of years.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            B.  Periods of time for the “days” of
Genesis <b>1</b></span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  A summary of constructions for the
Genesis days appears below with some of the reasons for them. A helpful study
is E. J. Young, <i>Studies in Genesis One </i>(Philadelphia, PA: Presbyterian
and Reformed Publishing Co., l964). See also Richard Niessen, <i>Acts &amp;
Facts </i>IX:3 (March l980), pp. i-viii.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  1. <u>Twenty-four-hour periods</u>. Actually,
earth’s rotation has retarded somewhat over the millennia. The original
rotation period was somewhat less than at present. If we were to accept Donald
Patten’s theory about Martian fly-bys from 701 B.C. back, alteration by 2% or
so rotation rate would cause other deviations in day length.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify;text-indent:-81.0pt'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                                   a.   The
correlation of the normal week with the creation activity: Exodus <b>20</b>:11.
Where does the idea of a seven-day week come from if not from the creation
account? Our proposal is that God inspired Moses to set up a seven-day week in
contrast to the Egyptian ten-day week. That move was part of a larger
reconstruction of life elements to dissociate Israelites from the cultural
circumstance they lived under for some centuries. In God’s creating Israel, he
provided them with an account of his creative work in a sevenfold breakdown.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify;text-indent:-2.25in'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                                                              b.
  The plants of the previous day would have died if the sun had not been
created soon (Day 4). <b>1</b>:14-19 may not be the creation of the sun and
moon.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify;text-indent:-81.0pt'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                                   c.   The
<i>“evening and morning”</i> expression. Contrast Psalm <b>90</b>:4-5, where
the typical Jewish view of day starting in the evening is set aside for <i>“morning
and evening”</i> when a figure is meant: <i>Acts &amp; Facts</i>, (November 1978),
p. 11.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify;text-indent:-2.25in'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                                                              d.
  With an associated term/ordinal-number, </span><span lang=HE dir=RTL
style='font-size:12.0pt'>יוֹם</span><span dir=LTR></span><span lang=HE
style='font-size:12.0pt'><span dir=LTR></span> </span><span style='font-size:
12.0pt'>(day) always means a period of twenty-four hours. But the difficulty is
that Hebrew has no word for a time period that it can put in ordinal series. Using
“day” for that unusual purpose would make sense.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-right:-.5in;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              e.   “Day and night”</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        f.    Genesis <b>1</b>:14
distinguishes between days, years, and seasons.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        g.   Symbiosis requires
short-term days (?).                                 </span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        h.   Survival of plants and
animals requires a twenty-four-hour day.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify;text-indent:-81.0pt'><span

style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  1.   Considerations legitimizing
expansion</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        a.   Schematizing is suggested
by having ten antediluvian generations to Noah and ten more to Abraham (note
the schematizing by fourteens in Matthew <b>1</b>:2-17). This schematizing may
be qualified in that only eight generations appear in the Cainite line before
the flood. On the other hand, Peleg would have predeceased five generations
before him. Noah would also have been a contemporary of Abraham for fifty-eight
years, and Shem would have survived Abraham by thirty-five years. Genesis <b>11</b>:26
is somewhat ambiguous. Some people for this reason identify Shem with
Melchizedek.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        b. Known examples of
genealogical abbreviation show the versatility of the “begot,” “bare,” and “son
of” expressions. See William Henry Green, <i>Bibliotheca Sacra </i>XLVII
(1890), pp. 285-303, reprinted in Newman and Eckelmann<i>, Genesis One and the
Origin of the Earth</i> (InterVarsity, 1977), pp. 105-23.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              (1)    Matthew<b> 1</b>:
(<b>1</b>:8: Joram [Ahaziah, Joash, Amaziah] Ozias), (<b>1</b>:11: Josiah
[Jehoiakim] and Jechoniah), (<b>1</b>:1: the whole genealogy of Jesus, David, Abraham)</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              (2)    1 Chronicles <b>23</b>:15-16;
<b>26</b>:24: Moses, Gershom [. . .] Shebuel, a contemporary of David</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify;text-indent:-1.5in'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                                                  (3)
   1 Chronicles<b> 27</b>:31: Levi, Kohath, Hebron [. . .] Jerijah, a
contemporary of David</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              (4)    Ezra <b>8</b>:1-2:
(David [. . .] Hattush) (Aaron, Eleazar, Phinehas [. . .] Gershom), Hattush and
Gershom being contemporaries of Daniel</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              (5)    1 Chronicles<b> 6</b>:3-14
+ Ezra <b>7</b>:1-5, where Ezra omits six generations between Meraioth and
Azariah</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              (6)    Exodus <b>6</b>:16-22
has <u>four</u> generations from Jacob to Moses; but         1 Chronicles <b>2</b>:18-20
gives <u>seven</u> generations from Jacob to Bezaleel, while 1 Chronicles <b>7</b>:23-27
gives <u>eleven</u> to Joshua, all for the same period.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              (7)    Numbers <b>3</b>:19,
27-28: 8,600 males only from Moses’ grandfather to Moses if no abridgment</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                              (8)    See Kenneth A.
Kitchen, <i>Ancient Orient and Old Testament</i>, p. 55, for extra-biblical
examples.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        c.   Scripture never uses a
long date based on its genealogies. Their purpose evidently was for
establishing identity, lineage, and inheritance rather than time length. There
is no summation from creation to the flood as there is from Egypt to the exodus
(Exodus <b>12</b>:40) or from the exodus to the first temple (1 Kings <b>6</b>:1).</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        d.   Egyptian chronology
(known to Moses, author of the Pentateuch) presumably goes back to 3,000 B.C.
and probably more.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                  2.   Limiting considerations</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        a. Enoch is called &quot;the
seventh from Adam&quot;: Jude 14 (Genesis <b>5</b>:18, 21-24). Perhaps that
reference is literary only. Luke 3, Genesis <b>5</b>, and 1 Chronicles <b>1</b>
all have the same generations from Adam to Noah.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        b. As they stand, the
antediluvian life spans form a transient decay curve. But that curve does not
necessarily prove no gaps exist in the genealogy, because the decay curve would
still appear as such.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        c. Methuselah dies the year of
the flood; that is, no pre-flood person arithmetically succeeds the flood date
of A.M. 1656.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        d. Long ages of pre-diluvian
patriarchs have been considered as transcriptionally erroneous. See <i>Biblical
Archaeologist</i>, XLIII:52, pp. 69-70, for example.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify;text-indent:-.5in'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                                    e. Recently, studies
have been conducted on the Y-chromosome in ethnically distinct humans. The
projections backward in time for the antiquity of mankind based on the variety
within the gene pool give surprisingly recent dates for the origin of <i>Homo
sapiens</i>. See <i>Reasons to Believe</i> (X:1), 4.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify;text-indent:-.5in'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>                        F. The best alternative seems
to be to take the “days” of Genesis <b>1</b> as topics instead of time
segments. The rationale for that understanding appears elsewhere on the website
under the title “The Rationale for the Structure of Genesis <b>1</b>” under
“Critical Introduction to the Old Testament.”</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'> II.  Interpretative Inference</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>&nbsp;</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            Certain elongations may be possible, but
there seems to be no way to expand the time allowed by the biblical framework
to accommodate the millions of years posited for the antiquity of the human
race in evolutionary thought.</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            One way to avoid that conclusion is to
understand Genesis as talking about Adam and Eve, not as the first pair, but as
in effect the first pair. The opening chapters of Genesis pick up the story
with a particular man’s clan and trace it on through to its connections to the
Israelite nation. It is not dealing with the whole human race nor with the
whole history of the race, but with one family at some point along the way,
however long it had been since some beginning point. Eve would in effect be
“the mother of all living” from the time viewpoint of Moses</span></p>

<p class=MsoNormal style='margin-top:0in;margin-right:-.5in;margin-bottom:0in;
margin-left:.25in;margin-bottom:.0001pt;text-align:justify'><span
style='font-size:12.0pt'>            Eve is, in effect, <i>“the mother of all
living”</i> because those who end up living—from the perspective of the post-diluvian
world—were all descendants of her by virtue of being descended from Noah her
descendant. Cain’s wife was not a sister or some other relative, but a woman
from another clan besides the Adam-Eve clan. The fossil remains of ancient people
were ancestors of Adam or one of the other clans contemporary with him.</span></p>
